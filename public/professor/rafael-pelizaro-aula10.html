Chapter 3 -- The Code

Grammar Discovery

Indirect questions: a more polite way to ask

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

The orange words begin a polite, indirect question. What happens to the word order after them?

"Could you tell me when the call starts?"

"Do you know where the client is based?"

"I was wondering if we could move the meeting."

"Can you explain what this acronym means?"

Grammar Rule

Indirect Questions

DirectIndirect (polite)
When does the call start?Could you tell me when the call starts?
Where is the client based?Do you know where the client is based?
Can we move the meeting?I was wondering if we could move it.

Key idea: Start with an introducer like Could you tell me, Do you know, or I was wondering. After it, use statement word order (subject + verb) and drop do, does, and did. For a yes/no question, add if.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"Could you tell me when does the call start?"
"Could you tell me when the call starts?"

After the introducer, use statement order and no do/does/did. Say "when the call starts", never "when does the call start".

Grammar Practice

Make It Indirect

"Could you tell me when the call _____starts?" (statement order, no does)
"Do you know where the client _____is based?" (verb to be after the subject)
"I was wondering _____if we could reschedule." (yes/no question)
"Can you explain what this acronym _____means?" (statement order, no does)

About the Call Opening

1. What two simple rules does Rafael set for the call?

First, ask him to clarify if anything is not clear. Second, he will avoid jargon and acronyms and paraphrase any new word.

2. Why does he want to avoid jargon and acronyms?

Because the people come from different teams and different countries, so simple words prevent a misunderstanding.

3. How long is the overlap, and what does he want to build?

They only have a two-hour overlap, and he wants to build a good rapport as a global team.
