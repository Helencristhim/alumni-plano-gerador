Read for the Main Idea

Reading the Future in the Numbers

Reading the Future in the Numbers

Every quarter, Emmanuele presents the Brazil results to the Global team. For a long time, she only talked about the past: what happened, what the numbers were. That is important. But the Global team always wants one more thing -- the future. They ask: "And next quarter? What do you predict?" A good manager does not only report results. She gives a projection.

A projection is not a promise. It is your best estimate of what will happen, based on the data you have. "Sales will increase in the second half." "The public market will grow slowly, but it will grow." "I think this year will be our best year." Notice the small word in every sentence: will. Will is the language of the forecast. You use it to say what you expect, what you predict, what you think will happen -- not what is already decided (that is "going to"), but what the numbers point to.

Of course, a good forecast is honest. Some months are a peak; then the numbers decrease a little and stay steady. And sometimes you predict an increase, but you also have to report a shortfall -- a number below the plan. "We will probably miss the target by a small amount this quarter." That is not weakness; that is control. The manager who can say "here is where we are, and here is where I think we will be" is the manager the Global team trusts. The past is the story. The forecast is the value.

Chapter 4: The Pattern

One Small Word for the Whole Future

the easiest grammar in English — and you already read it

Grammar Discovery

Find the Word

"Next quarter will be stronger than this one."

"I think Brazil will reach the target."

"The public market will grow slowly."

"This will probably be our best year."

Every line is about the future. Look at the purple word — what is the same in all four?

UseStructureExample
A predictionwill + base verb (same for I / you / it)"Sales will increase."
Negativewill not (won't) + base verb"We won't miss the target."
QuestionWill + subject + base verb?"Will Brazil reach the goal?"
will vs going togoing to = a plan you already decided ("We are going to launch in Q3"). will = what you PREDICT from the numbers ("I think it will be a success").

One word, no traps: will never changes, and the verb after it stays simple — will be, will grow, will reach.

Language Focus

Prediction, Plan, or Estimate?

AnalyseWhich is it -- a prediction, a decided plan, or an estimate?
ASales will increase next quarter. -- a prediction
BWe are going to launch in Q3. -- a decided plan
CI think it will stay steady. -- a prediction
DThis is about five percent, more or less. -- an estimate
A and C use WILL -- they say what you PREDICT, what you expect from the numbers. B uses GOING TO -- a plan already decided, not a guess. D is an estimate -- an approximate number, not an exact one. A real forecast mixes all three: the plan you decided, the numbers you predict with will, and the honest "more or less".
Common Mistake

After "will", the Simple Verb

"Sales will to increase next year."
"Sales will increase next year."
"I think it will grows slowly."
"I think it will grow slowly."
"Next quarter is better, I predict."
"Next quarter will be better, I predict."

One rule covers all three: after will, use the simple verb — no to, no -s. And for a prediction about the future, use will, not the present.

Dialogue

You Give the Forecast

M
Thanks for the Q3 numbers, Emmanuele. They look solid. Now the question everyone wants: what do you predict for Q4?
E
Good question. My projection is positive. I think sales will increase by about eight percent.
M
Eight percent. Will that be steady, or just one strong month?
E
Steady, I believe. After the peak in September, the numbers will stay steady — not up and down.
M
That's good to hear. And the public market? Will it reach the target?
E
Honestly, I predict a small shortfall there — maybe five percent below the plan. But it will recover in Q1.
M
Thank you for being clear about that. An honest forecast is more useful than a perfect one.
E
I agree. So, my estimate: total growth will be strong, and the shortfall will not change the full-year result.
