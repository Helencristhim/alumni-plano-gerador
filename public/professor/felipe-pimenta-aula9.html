Defining What Matters

Chapter 1: The Pitch

Why Would Anyone Invest in Your Company?

One sentence. Not a list of features — an argument.

One Comma. Two Companies.

The Comma Changes the Fact

"The analysts who left..."

SOME left. Others stayed. (defining)

"The analysts, who left, ..."

ALL of them left. (non-defining)

In a contract

that difference is a number — and a lawsuit

The clause without commas DEFINES which ones you mean. The clause with commas is an aside — it applies to all of them.

Chapter 3: The Code

Defining vs Describing

The comma that changes the fact

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"The analysts who left took the model with them." (some left)

"The analysts, who left, took the model with them." (they ALL left)

"The assumption that underpins the model is optimistic."

"Our lead investor, who has backed us since 2019, is in."

Sentences 1 and 2 have the same words. So tell me: in each one, how many analysts do we still have?

FormUseExample
Defining (no commas)Tells you WHICH ones. Essential — remove it and you no longer know who you mean.The analysts who left took the model. (only those)
Non-defining (commas)Extra information about ALL of them. Remove it and the fact survives.The analysts, who left, took the model. (all of them)
that / which / whothat only in DEFINING clauses. which and who in both. Never that after a comma.the metric that matters / the metric, which matters,...
Reduced relativeDrop that is / which are entirely. Sounds far more native and saves you two words.the risks driving churn = the risks that are driving churn
whosePossession — for people AND for companies.a company whose exit nobody can describe
Preposition at the endNatural, correct English. Do not force from which unless you are writing a contract.the fund we raised from (not: from which we raised)

The test that never fails: take the clause out. If you still know WHICH ones you mean, the clause was extra — put the commas back. If you no longer know, it was essential — no commas.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Our lead investor, that has backed us since 2019, is in."
"Our lead investor, who has backed us since 2019, is in."
"The analysts, who left, took the model. (when only SOME left)"
"The analysts who left took the model. (no commas = only those who left)"
"The assumption, that underpins the model, is optimistic."
"The assumption that underpins the model is optimistic."

that never follows a comma. And the comma is not decoration: without it the clause DEFINES which ones; with it, the clause describes ALL of them. In a contract, that is a number.

Chapter 4: The Investor

The Investor Who Already Backed You

She remembers what you promised

Dialogue

Defending the Thesis

I
Felipe, I backed you in 2022 on a thesis that turned out to be half right. Give me the new one, in one sentence.
F
The thesis is the licence. Our differentiator is a regulatory licence that took four years to win and that a competitor cannot simply buy.
I
That is a moat. What about the churn that worried me last time?
F
The churn that worried you in March has halved. And the unit economics, which improved every quarter this year, are now positive — that is the number I would attack if I were you.
I
Then I will. What is the weakest part of your case?
F
The addressable market, which nobody has measured properly. The assumption that underpins our model is a market size that I cannot prove. I would rather tell you that now than have you find it in the data room.

Quick Fire

The Investor Asks. You Define.

1 / 6

"Give me the thesis, in one sentence."

  • Our differentiator is a licence that took four years to win.
  • An argument, not a list. Define, do not describe.

"Why is that a moat?"

  • Because a competitor cannot simply buy it.
  • A moat that a rival can buy is not a moat.

"What about the churn?"

  • The churn that worried you in March has halved.
  • Defining clause: WHICH churn. No commas.

"Are the unit economics positive yet?"

  • The unit economics, which improved every quarter, are now positive.
  • Non-defining: there is only one set. Commas.

"What is the weakest part of your case?"

  • The addressable market, which nobody has measured properly.
  • Name it before she does. That is what buys credibility.

"Why should I believe you this time?"

  • Because I would rather tell you now than have you find it in the data room.
  • Track record + candor. Nothing else works.

Make the Case

Role-Play

The Thesis in One Sentence

Scenario: An investor asks for your thesis in one sentence. Define the company — do not list its features. Name the moat, and say precisely why a competitor cannot copy it.

Keywords:

The thesis is...Our differentiator is a ... that...The moat is the ... which...The assumption that underpins it is...
Role-Play

She Attacks Your Weakest Number

Scenario: The investor goes straight at the addressable market: "That number is a guess." She is right. Concede it precisely, then rebuild the case on what you CAN prove — the licence, the churn, the unit economics.

Keywords:

You are right, and the addressable market, which...What I can prove is the ... that...The churn that worried you...I would rather tell you now than...
Role-Play

The Whole Pitch

Scenario: Three minutes. Define the company in one sentence. Name the moat and why it cannot be copied. Give the traction and the unit economics. Then name your own weakest number before the investor finds it — and say what you will do about it.

No keywords. The floor is yours.

Role-Play

Now Define Yourself

Scenario: Same structure, different subject: define your own career the way you just defined the company. What is the thing you have that took years to build and that a rival cannot simply buy? What is your track record, and what is your weakest number?

No keywords. The floor is yours.
