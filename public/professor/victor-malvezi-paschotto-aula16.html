No Line on the Org Chart

Aligning a room you run is one skill. Moving people who do not report to you is another. When you cannot order, you have to make them want it — and own it.

Chapter 3

The Grammar

Get people to act — and give the reason why

Grammar Discovery

Spot the Pattern

Three lines — which ones move a peer you cannot order?

"Do the forecast by Friday." (an order — fails on a peer)

"I got Sarah to own the forecast." (get + person + to + verb)

"I'll have IT review it so that we avoid surprises." (have + person + verb / so that + reason)

Don't command. Get them to act — with a reason.

The Rule

The Influence Toolkit

StructureFormExample
Get + person + to + verbpersuade / lead someone to act"I got finance to back the rollout."
Have + person + verbarrange for someone to do it (no "to")"I'll have IT review the plan."
… so that + clausethe reason / result — the "why""I framed it so that they owned the how."
… in order to + verbthe purpose (formal)"I met them early in order to enlist support."

The key thing to keep clean:

When you can't order, you get people to act, not tell them to. Use get + person + to + verb ("I got them to agree") and have + person + verb with NO "to" ("I'll have IT review it"). And always attach the why: so that + a clause ("so that they own it") or in order to + a verb ("in order to win support"). The reason is what turns a request into something people actually want to do.

Common Mistake

Order vs Influence

✗ ORDER TO A PEER

"Do the forecast and send it to me by Friday."

✓ GET THEM TO ACT

"Could I get you to own the forecast, so we're aligned by Friday?"

✗ WRONG CAUSATIVE

"I'll have IT to review the plan." (have + to = wrong)

✓ HAVE + BARE VERB

"I'll have IT review the plan." (have + verb, no "to")

Get someone TO do it. Have someone DO it. And give the reason with "so that".

Chapter 4

Winning Them Over

A peer you need, but cannot command

Dialogue

The Cross-Functional Ask

H
Victor, I'll be honest — my team is buried. Whatever this rollout is, we don't have the capacity for it.
V
Fair, and I'm not here to add work. Honestly, what's in it for your team is fewer last-minute fire drills — the rollout fixes the handoffs that keep burning your people.
H
That part I'd actually like. But I can't have my schedule blown up by someone else's plan.
V
Then you shape it, not me. What would have to be true for this to work for your team — so that it fits your schedule instead of fighting it?
H
If the rollout phased in over two months instead of all at once, I could absorb it. Okay — that I can work with.
V
Done — we phase it. And here's my real ask: could I get you to champion the phased version in Thursday's meeting? Your word carries more weight in that room than mine.
H
I can do that. If it's phased and it's framed as ours, I'll back it — and I'll bring finance along.
V
Then let's tie it to the regional target we all own — it stops being my project and becomes ours. And I'll back your warehouse upgrade next quarter, so this runs both ways.
H
Now that's a deal I'll actually advocate for. Phased, shared, and reciprocal — count me in.
Comprehension

Did You Catch It?

1. What is Renata's opening objection?
Her team is buried and has no capacity — she can't take on someone else's plan.
2. What condition makes Renata willing to work with it?
Phasing the rollout in over two months instead of all at once, so she can absorb it — and that it's framed as shared, not imposed.
3. What does Renata agree to do in Thursday's meeting?
Champion the phased version and bring finance along — she'll advocate for it because it's phased, shared, and reciprocal.

Stop trying to be convincing and start being useful — work out what the other side wants and lead with that; people act because they see what's in it for them.
