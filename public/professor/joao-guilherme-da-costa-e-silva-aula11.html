The Distinction

Recognize vs Use

Recognize

You read the word, or you hear a native use it, and you understand it completely.

The easy half. It feels like knowing.

Use

Under pressure, in a room, the word comes out of your mouth in the right place, without you reaching for it.

The hard half. It is a different muscle.

A milestone review is not about learning a fifteenth word. It is about moving the fourteen you have from the left column to the right one.

Chapter 3: The Code

Six You Own, One New Bridge

"If they had followed the protocol" (past)  →  "we wouldn't be behind now" (present)

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

If they had followed the protocol, we wouldn't be behind schedule now.

If Camille had escalated the shortage in March, the handover would be on schedule today.

If we weren't so exposed on compliance, I would have accepted the delay last week.

If I were an engineer who let scope creep slide, I would have signed that off months ago.

In two of them the cause is past and the result is now. In two of them the cause is a permanent truth and the result is past. The two halves never share a time.

StructureWhat it meansOn the call
if + past perfect, would + baseA PAST cause with a result you feel in the PRESENT. This is the new bridge: it takes the third conditional you know and lands the result in the room you are in."If they had followed the protocol, we wouldn't be behind now."
if + past simple, would have + participleA PRESENT, permanent truth that explains a PAST decision. The condition is who you ARE; the result is what you DID."If I were an engineer who cut corners, I would have signed that off."
vs. the third conditional (Lesson 2)Pure third conditional keeps BOTH halves in the past: "we wouldn't have been behind." Use the MIXED one the moment the result is now."...we wouldn't be behind now" (not "have been")
wish (the near cousin)wish + past perfect = regret about the past. wish + past simple = a present you want changed. wish + would = a complaint about someone's behavior."I wish they had flagged it earlier." / "I wish they would send a status update without being chased."
Consolidation

The Structures You Own

The structures you already own -- and the one new bridge between them
If they had followed the protocol, we wouldn't be behind now
MIXED CONDITIONAL -- the new one

if + past perfect, would + BASE verb. A PAST cause with a result you feel in the PRESENT. This is the new step today: it takes the third conditional you learned in Lesson 2 and lands the result in the room you are in now. "If Camille had escalated the shortage in March, the handover would be on schedule today."

If we weren't so exposed on compliance, I would have accepted it
MIXED CONDITIONAL -- the other direction

if + past simple, would have + participle. A PRESENT, permanent truth that explains a PAST decision. "If I were the kind of engineer who let scope creep slide, I would have signed that off months ago." The condition is who you ARE; the result is what you DID.

It was the missing checks that caused the delay
CLEFT -- Lessons 5 & 8, still yours

It was ... that / What we need is ... Puts one fact under a spotlight and pushes the blame or the request to the exact right place. "What I need from your side is a corrective action plan, not an apology."

Never have we seen a test logged so carelessly
INVERSION -- Lessons 4 & 7, still yours

Negative adverbial + auxiliary before subject. Formal, emphatic, and it makes a supplier sit up. "Not only did the checks get skipped, but the result was logged as a pass."

We are going to have the three units retested at their cost
CAUSATIVE -- Lesson 6, still yours

have + object + past participle. You arrange it; someone else does it; they pay. The language of a project engineer who directs work without doing it himself. "We will have the inspection protocol re-run before any sign-off."

By the end of the month we will have agreed a recovery plan
FUTURE PERFECT -- Lesson 9, still yours

will have + past participle. The tense of the deadline -- done BEFORE a date, and the one a contract can hold you to. "By the next milestone, every outstanding item will have been closed."

The mixed conditional is the ONLY new grammar today. Everything else on this card you have already met. The whole point of a milestone review is not to add a seventh structure -- it is to make these six come out of your mouth in a live call, in the right order, without you reaching for them. Recognition is the easy half. Use is the muscle we train today.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"If they had followed the protocol, we wouldn't have been behind now."
"If they had followed the protocol, we wouldn't be behind now."
"If I had been an engineer who cut corners, I would have signed that off."
"If I were an engineer who cut corners, I would have signed that off."

The mixed conditional refuses to keep both halves in the same time. When the result is now, drop the have been and say be. When the condition is a permanent truth, drop the had and say were.

"It was the three skipped checks that caused the delay, not our cable schedule — and the protocol both sides signed in February is what says so."
2. Link the February mistake to today's problem. (Mixed conditional)

Dialogue

The Debrief with Camille

C
João, before we start, I want to be clear: this milestone is behind because Motiva released the cable schedule late. My team could not test what it could not access. So let us not turn this into a review of my factory.
J
Understood, Camille, and I hear you. Before we get into who owns what, could you run one thing by me? The inspection protocol we both signed in February — it lists three checks as mandatory hold points. Were those three run before the test was logged as a pass?
C
Two of them were run. The third we deferred, because the cable data was not there yet. It was a practical decision under your delay.
J
Then let me name the elephant in the room myself: our procurement team released the cable schedule late, and I own that. That is real. But a deferred check is not a passed check — and what the protocol requires is that a skipped hold point is logged as open, not as a pass.
C
That is a documentation point. The units are fine. Reopening the test now costs my team two weeks we do not have.
J
If that check had been run in February, we wouldn't be having this conversation now. It was that one deferred check that put us here, not the cable schedule alone. On compliance I cannot sign off on a deferred hold point, so we are going to have the three units retested against the full protocol.
C
And if I say the retest is not in my budget? What then?
J
Then I escalate it, and neither of us wants that. So here is what I would rather do: I own the cable delay in writing, you retest the three checks at your cost, and by the end of the month we will have agreed a recovery plan. You get a written status update from me every Friday until this package is signed off. Fair?
Comprehension

About Camille

1. How does she open the call, and what is she trying to do?
She opens by putting the whole delay on Motiva's late cable schedule, and by framing any discussion of her factory as off-limits. She is trying to control the story before João can.
2. What does she admit about the three mandatory checks?
That only two were run. The third was deferred because the cable data was not available — and she calls that a "practical decision," not a failure.
3. What is her argument against reopening the test?
That it is only a documentation point, the units are fine anyway, and a retest costs her team two weeks they do not have.
4. How does her position change by the end, and why?
She moves from blaming Motiva to negotiating the cost of the retest. It shifts because João names Motiva's own fault first and anchors everything in the protocol both sides signed — so there is nothing left for her to attack.
Listening

Your Brief, One Hour Before

Marina, your program manager. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does the record show about the factory acceptance test, and why is that João's leverage?
Both sides signed the inspection protocol in February; Camille's team then ran the test without three of the required checks and logged it as a pass. It is leverage because it is in writing — the criteria were agreed, so the skipped checks are not a matter of opinion.
2. What is the elephant in the room on Motiva's side, and what does Marina tell him to do about it?
Motiva's procurement team released the cable schedule late. Marina tells him to name that himself, before Camille does — because if she names it first, Motiva loses the moral high ground.
3. What three things does she need from the call, and when should he escalate?
A corrective action plan, a weekly status update both sides can see, and a realistic date for provisional acceptance that factors in the retesting. He should escalate to her only if Camille refuses to retest the three failed checks.
