The Reframe

Story First vs Number First

Story first (the engineer)

"So, back in March the axle counters were reprioritised, then the firmware slipped, and after we expedited them… the package is four percent over."

By the time the number arrives, the board has stopped listening — and found it themselves on the dashboard.

Number first (the board's ear)

"Bottom line: one package is four percent over budget — a cost variance above threshold. Here's the cause, and here's when I close it."

The number you name first is a number under control; the number they discover is a number out of control.

A board reads the number first and the story second. Lead with the bottom line, own the deviation in your own words, then give the why. Same facts, opposite impression.

Chapter 3: The Code

Finished By, or In Progress At

"By Q3 we will have closed it / this time next month the crew will be running the SATs" — the grammar that turns a forecast into a promise.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

By the end of Q3, we will have completed the site acceptance tests.

This time next month, the crew will be running the SATs on nights.

By the time the board meets again, we will have closed the cost variance.

Throughout August, the commissioning team will be working two shifts.

Look at what follows will. Two say have + completed / closed — a past participle. Two say be + running / working — an -ing verb. One means finished by a point; the other means in progress at a point.

StructureWhat it doesIn the room
will have + past participle
future perfect		An action FINISHED before a future point. Answers "how much will be done by then?" It lands as a promise with a date on it."By Q3 we will have completed the SATs."
will be + verb-ing
future continuous		An action IN PROGRESS at a future point. Answers "what will you be doing at that moment?" It shows the work is already in motion."Next month the crew will be running the SATs."

In speech, will have reduces to "we'll've" — listen for the -ll-uv before a participle. The future perfect promises a completed STATE; the future continuous shows the plan already underway. Together they turn a forecast into something the board can hold you to.
Meaning

Two Tenses, One Commitment

The future perfect and the future continuous -- two lenses on the same future
will have + past participle (future perfect)
Finished BEFORE a future point

Use it for an action that will already be COMPLETE by a deadline. "By the end of Q3, we will have completed the SATs." "By the time the board meets again, we will have closed the variance." It answers the board's real question -- how much will be DONE by then -- and it lands as a promise with a date on it, not a hope. The verb after have is a past participle: completed, closed, finished -- not the base form.

will be + verb-ing (future continuous)
In progress AT a future point

Use it for an action that will be ONGOING at or around a moment in the future. "This time next month, the crew will be running the SATs on nights." "Throughout the commissioning window, the team will be working two shifts." It tells the board the recovery is already in motion -- something happening at that moment, not a plan on a slide.

BY, not UNTIL, with the future perfect
The deadline word

The future perfect takes BY for its deadline -- the point a thing is done by. "By December we will have closed the gap" (not "until December"). UNTIL is for something that CONTINUES up to a point ("we will keep testing until December"). Portuguese uses ate for both, so the reflex is to say "until" -- and it breaks the sentence. The deadline of a completed action is always BY.

Why it sounds senior in a numbers meeting
A commitment, not a wish

"We hope to finish by Q3" is a wish; "by Q3 we will have finished" is a commitment with a date. The future perfect lets you promise a completed STATE by a moment; the future continuous shows the work is already underway. Together they turn a forecast into something the board can hold you to -- which is exactly what a board wants to hear.

One reflex runs through both: look at what comes after will. will HAVE + a past participle (completed, closed, delivered) = finished by a point. will BE + an -ing (running, testing, working) = in progress at a point. The moment you hear yourself say "we will complete by Q3" for something that must be DONE by then, upgrade it to "we will have completed" -- the plain future says it happens sometime around Q3; the future perfect says it is finished by Q3. In a budget review, that difference is the difference between a hope and a commitment.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"By the end of Q3, we will complete the site acceptance tests."
"By the end of Q3, we will have completed the site acceptance tests."
"Until December, we will have closed the cost variance."
"By December, we will have closed the cost variance."
"This time next month, we will run the SATs on nights."
"This time next month, we will be running the SATs on nights."

A completed result before a deadline needs the future perfect (will have + participle) with by, not until. Ongoing work at a future point needs the future continuous (will be + -ing). Plain future says only that it happens sometime — not that it is finished by then, or in progress right then.

"will have" collapses to "we'll've". What follows it tells you which future it is.

1. She gives you one sentence. What is the bottom line you lead with?
"Bottom line: schedule is green, cost is amber — one package, the axle counters, is four percent over budget and above the two-percent threshold. Everything else is on plan." One KPI, one figure, the deviation named first.
2. The earned value is 62% against a plan of 66%. What does that actually tell the board?
Less work is truly finished than the plan expected at this point — we are slightly behind on progress, not just on money. Earned value keeps me honest: the calendar and the spend can look fine while the completed work lags.
3. Forecast completion is end of Q3. Say it as a commitment, not a hope.
"By the end of Q3 we will have completed the site acceptance tests, and by the time the board meets again we will have closed the variance. This time next month the crew will be running the SATs on nights to recover the two weeks."

Comprehension

About Ingrid

1. What does Ingrid ask for at the start, and what does it tell you about how she listens?
She already has the dashboard open and asks for where they stand in one line each — schedule and cost. She reads the numbers first and does not want to be walked through the whole project; the story comes second, if at all.
2. How does Ingrid react when João names the variance himself?
She says "you named it before I had to" — approval. Owning the deviation before she points at it turns it into a number under control instead of one she has to dig out.
3. When João starts to explain the cause, what does Ingrid cut him off to demand?
A date. "I don't want history, I want a date. When is this variance gone?" She wants the forecast as a commitment, not the backstory of how it happened.
4. What does Ingrid want to know about the smaller packages, and what is João's honest answer?
Whether any of them help her — offset the overrun. Two came in under budget and nearly break even on the minor scope, but he admits it doesn't move the needle on the total; the axle-counter variance is the number that matters.

1. In what order does the mentor say a board reads a report, and what should you lead with?
The number first, the story second — the opposite of how engineers build up to it. So lead with the bottom line: one KPI, one figure, where the project stands against plan and against budget, before any of the why.
2. What does he say about a deviation the presenter names versus one the board discovers?
Own the deviation before they find it. The number you name first is a number under control; the number the board discovers is a number out of control — even when it is the exact same number.
3. How does he say a forecast should sound, and which grammar carries it?
Not a hope but a commitment with a date. Don't say "we hope to finish by Q3"; say "by the end of Q3 we will have completed the tests" (future perfect). And for what's happening now to recover, the future continuous: "next month the crew will be running the SATs on nights."
Listening 2

Reading the Numbers

A project-controls advisor, twenty years of dashboards. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What is the difference between the burn rate and the run rate, and which one does a board want?
The burn rate is how fast you are spending right now — this month's drawdown. The run rate is that pace projected forward, what the whole year looks like if this month continues. The board wants the run rate, because it tells them where you land.
2. Why does he say earned value is the number that keeps you honest?
It is not how much you spent or how much time has passed — it is the budgeted value of the work actually finished. You can be halfway through the calendar and the money and still be badly behind, because only a third of the work is truly done. The cost variance is the gap between what that finished work should have cost and what it did.
3. How does he say you must treat a ballpark figure differently from a forecast?
A ballpark is a rough early number nobody should hold you to — and you must say out loud that it's a ballpark. A forecast is a commitment, updated with real data, stated in the future perfect ("by quarter end we will have closed the gap"). Present a ballpark as a forecast and you'll spend three meetings explaining why the number moved.
