Chapter 1: Counting the Cost

The Price Is Rarely the Price

Last class you learned to ask for help in an emergency. Many travel problems, though, are quietly about money -- a service charge you did not expect, a tip you were unsure about, a fee added by your bank. The number on the menu is rarely the number you pay, and the rules change the moment you cross a border.

In one word: how do you usually feel about handling money in a foreign country?

Chapter 3: Done To You

The Advanced Passive

When the action matters, not the doer

Discover the Code

Listen and Find the Pattern

"A service charge is added to the bill."

"Tourists are often overcharged."

"You may be charged a fee by your bank."

"It is said that tourists pay more."

FunctionStructureExample
State a rule (agentless)is / are + past participleA service charge is added.
Past event (agentless)was / were + past participleWe were overcharged.
Possibility / futuremay / will + be + past participleYou may be charged a fee.
Keep the doer (new info)... by + agent... a fee by your bank.

The passive is be + past participle. We drop the doer when it is obvious or unimportant (agentless), and keep by + agent only when the doer is new or important.

Common Mistake

Wrong vs Right

✗ WRONG

"A service charge added to the bill."

✓ RIGHT

"A service charge is added to the bill."

✗ WRONG

"We were overcharged by them at the market."

✓ RIGHT

"We were overcharged at the market."

Never drop be from the passive. And cut by + agent when the doer is obvious -- "by them" adds nothing here.

No -- a ten percent service charge is already added automatically.
2. Who might add a fee if Pricila pays by card?
Her own bank -- a small foreign fee, not the restaurant.
3. How does Pricila finally decide to pay?
In cash, and she tells the waiter to keep the change.

Read for the Main Idea

The Quiet Rules of Money

The Quiet Rules of Money Abroad

Few things are governed by such unwritten rules as money. A traveler who has mastered the language of a country can still be caught out by the way its bills are handled. In one city the service is added automatically and a tip would be refused; in the next, the same gesture is quietly expected and its absence is noticed at once. The price on the menu is rarely the price that is paid, and the difference -- a service charge here, a surcharge there -- is seldom explained until the bill arrives.

Tourists, it is said, are overcharged more often than they realize. A markup is added the moment an accent is heard, and a hidden cost is buried somewhere in the small print of the receipt. Cards are not always welcome, and when they are accepted, a foreign fee may be charged by the bank rather than the shop. The wise traveler is told, again and again, to carry a little petty cash and some loose change, and to ask one blunt question before paying: is service included, or is a gratuity expected on top?

None of this is meant to alarm. It is simply how the world works once you cross a border, and it is handled best with curiosity rather than fear. Budget a daily allowance, agree a flat rate where you can, and never be afraid to ask why a number is what it is. Money, after all, is only spoken about openly by those who are not embarrassed by it -- and those, it turns out, are the travelers who are rarely caught out.

Talking About Likelihood

How Likely Is It?

How likely is it? (talking about money rules)
Tips are always included.
Certain

A fixed rule with no doubt. The service charge is built into the bill, so nothing extra is needed.

A service charge is added automatically.
Certain

It happens every time, by default. You do not choose it; the restaurant adds it for you.

A fee may be charged abroad.
Possible

It might happen, it might not -- it depends on your bank. Use may or might when you are not sure.

Your card might be declined.
Possible

Not certain, but worth expecting. Many cards are blocked the first time they are used in a new country.

You won't be charged for the ticket again.
Impossible

It cannot happen because it is already paid. Use won't be for something that will NOT occur.

Usage Note

Agentless vs By + Agent

Agentless passive: when the doer is obvious or unimportant, we drop it. "A service charge is added" -- we don't care who adds it. Use by + agent only when the doer is new information: "You may be charged a fee by your bank" -- here the bank matters, so we keep it.
