Two Sides of the Same Road

Two Sides of the Same Road

Every year, more travelers reach places that were once off the map. It could be argued that this is a good thing. Tourism brings money, jobs, and attention to regions that need all three. In an emerging economy, a single popular valley can lift a whole town. Supporters point out that visitors also bring curiosity and respect, and that a village with guests has a reason to protect what makes it special.

There is another perspective, though. Critics claim that mass tourism can slowly damage the very thing people travel to see. Even though it creates income, it can raise prices for locals, strain fragile roads and services, and turn a living culture into a performance. It should be noted that the money does not always stay in the community; often it flows to large companies far away, deepening inequality rather than reducing it.

So who is right? Perhaps both sides are. That said, the honest answer has more nuance than either slogan admits. The places that do best are the ones with resilience: communities that welcome visitors on their own terms, that acknowledge the risks, and that challenge the idea that more tourists always means more progress. The road runs two ways, and how a town travels it may matter more than how many people arrive.

Adapted from a travel-affairs feature, The World and the Road https://www.theworldandtheroad.com

Notice the two tools of an intelligent opinion. HEDGES soften a claim so it does not sound absolute: 'It could be argued that...', 'There might be...', 'It should be noted that...'. Chapter 4: The Code

Hedge and Concede

It could be argued · That said

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"It could be argued that tourism helps."

"There might be a downside to consider."

"Even though it creates jobs, prices rise."

"That said, I see your point."

Why green to soften an opinion, but blue to admit the other side?

ToolUseExample
Hedge (modal)softens a claim so it is not absoluteIt could be argued that ... / There might be ...
Concessionadmits the other side before your pointEven though ... / That said, ... / On the other hand, ...

A strong opinion, softened and balanced, sounds far more convincing than a loud, absolute one.

Language Focus

Hedge or Concession?

AnalyseHedge (soften) or concession (admit the other side)?
AIt could be argued that mass tourism helps.
BEven though it creates jobs, prices rise.
CThere might be a downside to consider.
DThat said, I see your point.
Notice: the green phrases HEDGE -- they make an opinion softer and less absolute (could be argued, might be). The blue phrases are CONCESSION -- they admit the other side before you make your point (even though, that said). A strong opinion, softened and balanced, sounds far more convincing than a loud one.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ TOO ABSOLUTE

"Tourism is bad, period."

✓ HEDGED

"It could be argued that tourism does more harm than good."

✗ INCOMPLETE

"Even though it creates jobs."

✓ COMPLETE

"Even though it creates jobs, prices rise for locals."

Hedge an opinion so it is not absolute. And after a concession like Even though, finish the thought with your point.

Dialogue

A Dinner in Tirana

P
You have cycled through half the Balkans, Marcelo. Be honest, is all this tourism good for these little towns, or are we ruining them?
M
It is not simple. It could be argued that visitors bring money and jobs. In an emerging economy, that really matters.
P
True, but critics claim it raises prices and turns a real culture into a performance.
M
I acknowledge that. And I would point out that the money often flows away, deepening inequality. That said, some towns handle it well.
P
So what is your real perspective? Give me a straight answer.
M
The honest answer has more nuance than either of us wants. The towns with resilience, the ones that set their own terms, do fine. It depends on how you look at it.

Chapter 6: Your Turn

Give Your Opinion

From guided to free — say each answer out loud

Scenario 1

Give your opinion on mass tourism to remote places like Albania. Hedge it with 'It could be argued that ...' and add the other side with 'On the other hand, ...'

Scenario 2

You disagree with someone at dinner. Challenge their idea politely: 'I see your point, but ...' and 'It should be noted that ...'

Scenario 3

Talk about an emerging economy you know. Point out one benefit and acknowledge one risk. Use 'point out' and 'acknowledge'.

Scenario 4

Give a balanced view on a current issue you follow (neuroscience, technology, geopolitics). Hedge with 'There might be ...' and add nuance.

Check Your Work

Model Answers

Try the gap-fill and the scenarios first. Reveal the key only to compare.

+Reveal the answer key
A balanced opinion is not a weak one. Hedging and concession show you have considered the nuance -- they make a strong argument sound reasonable, not aggressive.
  1. Gap-fill: 1 = could be argued, 2 = Even though, 3 = point out, 4 = That said, 5 = nuance, 6 = resilience.
  2. Model (hedged): 'It could be argued that tourism helps, but it should be noted that the money does not always stay local.'
  3. Model (concession): 'Even though mass tourism creates jobs, it can raise prices. That said, communities with resilience can welcome visitors on their own terms.'
  4. Hedge vs concession: hedges (could be argued, might be, you could say) soften a claim; concession markers (even though, that said, on the other hand) admit the other side before your point.
