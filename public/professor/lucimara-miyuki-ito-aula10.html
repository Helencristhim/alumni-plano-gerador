Chapter 3: The Code

The Passive Voice

When the process is the star, and the person disappears.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

Our products are tested before distribution.

Regulations are set by the health authority.

Each batch is checked by our quality team.

The formula was approved last year.

In none of these do we start with the person. The product is the star. What two parts build every sentence?

StructureFormWhen you use it
Present passiveam / is / are + past participle
are tested, is checked, are set		A regular process, true now and always.
Past passivewas / were + past participle
was approved, were renewed		A finished action, done in the past.
The "by"… by + the doer
set by the health authority		Add "by" ONLY when who does it matters.
Why business loves itIn a company, the process matters more than the person. "Our products are tested" is stronger and more professional than "Someone tests our products" — the product is the star, and you never have to name who does the work.
The one rulePassive = a form of be (am/is/are/was/were) + the past participle (the third form: test → tested, set → set, approve → approved). Get the be right for the time, and never drop the -ed.

One idea holds it together: you talk about what is done, not who does it.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Our products are test before distribution."
"Our products are tested before distribution."
"The formula approved last year."
"The formula was approved last year."

Two rules, and only two: after be, always the past participle (tested, not test). And passive always needs the be verb — drop the "was" and the sentence falls apart.

Practice

Turn It into the Passive

Read the active sentence, say the passive out loud, then click to check

We test our products before distribution.  →  "Our products are tested before distribution."
The team checks each batch.  →  "Each batch is checked (by the team)."
We source the raw materials in Asia.  →  "The raw materials are sourced in Asia."
They approved the formula last year.  →  "The formula was approved last year." (past: was + approved)
The health authority sets the regulations.  →  "The regulations are set by the health authority." (use "by" — the doer matters)
Two Ways to Say It

Active or Passive?

When the doer matters, use active. When the process matters, use passive.

Active — the doer is the point
"I run production and regulatory affairs."
"My team monitors the whole line."
Passive — the process is the point
"Our products are made to a high standard."
"Each shipment is checked when it arrives."

Same company, two lenses: "I test…" points at YOU; "…are tested" points at the PRODUCT. In a brochure, the product wins.

Reading

Read for the Passive Voice

The Booth in Lyon

The fair in Lyon was held every November, and for years it had been the same quiet dread. Lucimara would stand at her company's booth, hand out brochures, and hope that no one stopped to ask the one question she could never answer in English: "So, what exactly does your company do?" She knew the answer perfectly in Portuguese. She was a director. She ran production and regulatory affairs for a chemical company in Guarulhos. But every time the question came in English, the words were scattered, and she would smile and point at the brochure instead of speaking.

This November was different. A man stopped at the booth, read the sign, and asked the question. This time she did not point. "We specialize in specialty chemicals," she began. "Our products are manufactured in Guarulhos, near São Paulo, and every batch is tested before it is released." She watched his face. He was still listening. "The whole manufacturing process is monitored by our quality control team, and the plant is certified to international standards." She had said it. Three sentences, in English, and the man was nodding, not because he was being polite, but because he had understood.

"Interesting," he said. "And your supply chain? Where are the raw materials sourced?" It was exactly the kind of question she had always feared, and now it felt easy, because she knew the words. "Most of our raw materials are sourced from vendors in Asia and Europe," she answered. "Each shipment is checked when it arrives, and nothing enters the manufacturing process until it is approved." She was not reciting a script. She was explaining her own company, the one she ran, in a second language, to a stranger who might matter.

The man handed her his card. His name was Daniel, and his company was a distributor of specialty chemicals across France and Belgium. "In our market," he said, "a product cannot be sold without proper certification and full regulatory compliance. From what you have told me, yours has both." He was right. Every regulation in her sector was set by the health authority, and she had spent her whole career making sure they were followed. Two minutes, one language, and a booth she used to dread. The director from São Paulo had just explained her entire company in English, and someone in Lyon had listened, understood, and asked for more.

Strategy

Field, Value, Human Detail

You will never say everything. Three clean sentences, and you are understood.

Field: I'm a director at a chemical company. We specialize in specialty chemicals.
Value: Our products are tested before they are shipped, and all regulations are followed.
Human detail: It's a fairly complex process, but basically we make what other factories can't make safely.
We work closely with distributors in Europe and Asia.

Quick Fire

Explain It Like You Are Already There

1 / 6

At a dinner in New York, someone asks, "So what do you do?" Give the one-sentence version -- your field, nothing more yet.

  • I'm a director at a chemical company. We specialize in specialty chemicals.
  • Start with the field in one clean sentence. You can always add more if they seem interested -- don't drown them in detail they didn't ask for.

They want to know if your products are safe. Use the passive voice -- the process is the star, not the person.

  • Every batch is tested before it is released, and the whole process is monitored by our quality control team.
  • 'is tested', 'is monitored' -- be + past participle. You never have to say WHO tests it. In business, the process matters, not the actor.

A potential distributor asks where your raw materials come from. Answer with the passive.

  • Most of our raw materials are sourced from vendors in Asia and Europe.
  • 'are sourced' -- passive, present. The focus is on the materials and the supply chain, not on who does the buying.

Explain, in the passive, that your factory follows the rules set by the regulator (Anvisa).

  • Regulations are set by the health authority, and every one of them is followed.
  • 'are set by the health authority' -- here you DO name the actor with 'by', because who sets the rules matters. Use 'by' only when the actor is important.

Tell a partner that last year's formula was officially approved. Use the PAST passive.

  • The new formula was approved last year, and the certification was renewed in the spring.
  • 'was approved', 'was renewed' -- past passive is was/were + past participle. A finished action, and again the process is the star.

Someone looks a little lost by the technical detail. Soften it with one human sentence.

  • It's a fairly complex process, but basically we make the raw materials that other factories can't make safely themselves.
  • 'It's a fairly complex process, but basically...' is your rescue line -- it signals 'here comes the simple version' and puts the listener at ease.
