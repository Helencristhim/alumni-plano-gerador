Chapter 3: What Would You Do?

If This Happened...

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Find the Pattern

"If I lost my passport, I would go to the embassy."

"If the flight were cancelled, I would find another route."

"What would you do if you missed your connection?"

"If I were you, I would stay calm."

Part 1: the imaginary conditionPart 2: the imaginary result
if + PAST SIMPLEwould (or wouldn't) + base verb
If I lost my passport (past form)I would go to the embassy

We use the second conditional for situations that are imaginary or unlikely now. The past form is hypothetical, not real past. Use were for all persons in the hypothetical: if I were you, if the flight were cancelled. When the if-clause comes first, add a comma.

Common Mistake

Wrong vs Right

If I would lose my passport, I would go to the embassy.
If I lost my passport, I would go to the embassy.

After if, use the past simple -- never would. The would goes only in the result clause.

If I was you, I would make a backup plan.
If I were you, I would make a backup plan.

In the hypothetical, use were for all persons -- "if I were you", "if the flight were cancelled".

Chapter 4: A Crisis Abroad

Midnight at the Airport

Dialogue

When the Flight Falls Through

P
I can't believe it. The flight is cancelled, and the hotel has no record of my booking. Everything has gone pear-shaped at once.
T
Take a breath. It's just a hiccup. If I were you, I would keep my cool and deal with it one piece at a time.
P
You're right. But what would you do if you had no room for the night?
T
I'd make do with the airport tonight and build a contingency plan. If the cheaper hotel fell through too, I'd have a backup plan ready.
P
That actually helps. I was caught off guard, but if I thought of it as a puzzle, I think I could roll with the punches.
T
Exactly. Take it in your stride. By morning we'll have weathered the storm, and this will be the best story of your trip.

Read for the Main Idea

A Trip That Went Wrong

The flight that fell through

Halfway through what was meant to be a relaxing trip, everything went pear-shaped at once. My connecting flight was cancelled, the airline had no record of my onward booking, and the only hotel I had reserved for that night had quietly given my room away. Standing in a foreign airport at midnight, jet-lagged and exhausted, I felt the panic rising. For a few minutes I could not think on my feet at all; I just stared at the departure board as if it might fix itself.

Then something my mother used to say came back to me: when a trip goes wrong, the journey is not ruined -- it just changes shape. So I made do with what I had. I took it in my stride, kept my cool, and worked the problem one piece at a time. I found a quiet corner, charged my phone, and built a quick contingency plan: a new route home, a cheaper hotel two streets away, and a backup plan in case that one fell through too. By the time the sun came up, I had weathered the storm, and the curveball had somehow become the best story of the whole trip.

Looking back, the trip taught me more about myself than any smooth holiday ever could. If it happened again tomorrow, I would not panic the way I did at first. I would breathe, accept the hiccup, and trust that I can roll with the punches. The unexpected is not the enemy of a good journey; very often, it is the part you remember most.

Say It Another Way

Make It Hypothetical

Turn each real situation into an imaginary one using the cue

  1. 1Real: If I miss the train, I will take a taxi. Make it hypothetical.(if I missed) 
  2. 2Real: If the hotel loses my booking, I will ask for the manager. Make it hypothetical.(if the hotel lost) 
  3. 3Real: If it rains, we will change our plans. Make it hypothetical.(if it rained) 
  4. 4Real: If I lose my passport, I will go to the embassy. Make it hypothetical.(if I lost) 
  5. 5Advice: You are in my situation. Give advice with a hypothetical.(if I were you) 
Check Your Answers

Rephrase Answer Key

+Reveal the rephrase answer key
Second conditional form: if + PAST SIMPLE, would + base verb. The past tense here is hypothetical, not real past. Never put would in the if-clause (NOT 'if I would lose'). Use were for all persons in the hypothetical ('if I were you', 'if the flight were cancelled').
  1. If I missed the train, I would take a taxi.
  2. If the hotel lost my booking, I would ask for the manager.
  3. If it rained, we would change our plans.
  4. If I lost my passport, I would go to the embassy.
  5. If I were you, I would stay calm and make a backup plan.
