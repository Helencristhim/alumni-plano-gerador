Chapter 3: The Code

How to Recommend

Four ways to give an idea — and leave the other person free to say no.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

I'd recommend the sea bass. It's the best thing tonight.

You might want to try the tasting menu.

Why don't you start with the soup?

Should I mention my dietary restriction now?

Not one of them is an order. Each one offers an idea. What are the four openers?

OpenerFormWhen you use it
I'd recommend...I'd recommend + a noun
or + verb-ing
...the salmon / ...trying it		A strong, confident suggestion. Careful: never "to try".
You might want to...You might want to + base verb
...try the tasting menu		A gentle, soft suggestion — very polite, never pushy.
Why don't you...?Why don't you + base verb
...start with the soup?		A friendly suggestion, as a question. No "to" after it.
Should I...? / Shall I...?Should I + base verbTo ASK for a recommendation, or to offer to do something.
The one ruleAfter recommend, use a noun ("the salmon") or -ing ("trying it") — never "to try". After why don't you and should I, use the base verb, no "to". Only want to keeps its "to".

One idea holds them together: you offer, you do not order. The other person can always say, "actually, I'll go with the soup."

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I'd recommend to try the salmon."
"I'd recommend the salmon." / "I'd recommend trying the salmon."
"Why don't you to start with the soup?"
"Why don't you start with the soup?"

Two rules, and only two: after recommend, a noun or -ing, never "to". After why don't you, the base verb, never "to". The word "to" only stays inside want to.

Practice

Turn It into a Recommendation

Read the situation, say the recommendation out loud, then click to check

The fish is the best dish tonight. Suggest it, gently.  →  "You might want to try the sea bass — it's the best thing on the menu."
Your guest is unsure where to start. Suggest the soup, as a question.  →  "Why don't you start with the soup?"
Recommend the steak, strongly and with confidence.  →  "I'd recommend the steak — or, if you prefer, I'd recommend trying the short rib." (noun or -ing, never "to")
Your guest is gluten-free. Ask if you should tell the kitchen now.  →  "Should I mention his dietary restriction to the kitchen now?"
Suggest sharing an appetizer before the main course.  →  "Why don't we share an appetizer first?"
Two Roles at the Table

Ask For It, or Give It

When you don't know the menu, you ask. When you do, you recommend.

Asking for a recommendation
"What would you recommend? It's my first time here."
"Should I go with the fish or the steak?"
Giving a recommendation
"I'd recommend the sea bass — it's excellent here."
"You might want to try the tasting menu — it's easier."

"What do you recommend?" is your best defense against fast English: the answer comes back short, and you always catch it.

Reading

Read for the Recommendations

A Table for Two in Midtown

The reservation was for eight o'clock, and Lucimara arrived first, on purpose. She wanted five quiet minutes with the menu before her guest sat down. Mr. Chen was flying in from Shanghai for two days, and this dinner mattered more to her than any meeting on the trip. For years, at tables exactly like this one, she had let other people order for her, because the menu was a wall of words and she did not want to get it wrong in front of anyone. Tonight she wanted to be the one who looked up and said, "I'd recommend the sea bass," and meant it.

The server, Marco, brought two menus and a small plate of warm bread. "This is complimentary," he said, "on the house." Lucimara opened the menu and, for the first time, she could read the shape of it: the appetizers on the left, the entrees down the middle, and a short list of side dishes at the bottom. She still did not know every word, but she knew where each thing lived. When Mr. Chen finally sat down, she did not hand him the menu in silence. "Why don't you start with the soup?" she said. "And you might want to try the sea bass. Marco says it's the best thing on the menu tonight."

There was one thing she had to get right. Mr. Chen had a dietary restriction: he could not eat gluten. So before anyone ordered, she turned to Marco. "He's gluten-free. Should I mention it to the kitchen now, or later?" Now, Marco told her, always now. It was the right question, and better asked early than sent back to the kitchen half-cooked. For herself, she ordered the steak, medium-rare, and a side dish of grilled vegetables. When the sommelier came over with a suggestion of his own, "I'd recommend a light red with the sea bass," she did not simply nod the way she always had. "Is it anything like a Pinot Noir?" she asked. It was.

At the end came the part she had always dreaded, and it was never the food, it was the paying. The tab arrived in a small leather folder. In the United States, the tip is not printed on the bill; you read the total, and you add eighteen to twenty percent yourself. She knew the tipping etiquette now, and she added twenty without a second of doubt. "It's on me," she told Mr. Chen, before he could reach for his wallet. He smiled and thanked her in careful English. Two hours, one language, and no help from anyone. The supplier from Shanghai had just watched a director from São Paulo host a whole dinner in English, from the first recommendation to the last.

Strategy

Let the Menu Come to You in One Sentence

You can't read a whole menu in ten seconds. You can ask one question and catch the answer.

What would you recommend? It's my first time here.
Is the fish or the steak better tonight?
My guest is gluten-free. What would you suggest for him?
So you'd go with the sea bass? Perfect, we'll have that.

Quick Fire

Order Like You Are Already There

1 / 6

The server hands you a menu full of words you half understand. Do not point. Ask the one question a local always asks.

  • What would you recommend? It's my first time here.
  • This question puts the server on your side -- and the answer is usually one short phrase you can follow.

Your guest is looking at the menu, unsure. Suggest the fish -- gently, not as an order.

  • You might want to try the sea bass. Marco says it's the best thing tonight.
  • 'You might want to try...' + base verb. It is a suggestion, so it leaves your guest free to say no.

Your business partner cannot eat gluten. Check with the kitchen -- and ask BEFORE you order, not after.

  • He's gluten-free. Should I mention it to the kitchen before we order?
  • 'Should I...?' asks for advice. Ask early -- a dietary restriction is easier to plan than to fix on the plate.

You want the steak, but not well-done. Tell the server exactly how you want it cooked.

  • I'll go with the steak, medium-rare, please. And could I get a side of vegetables?
  • 'I'll go with...' is how you place your order. 'Could I get...?' is the review request -- polite, from Lesson 7.

The sommelier is at the table and you have no idea about the wine. Turn it into a question you can follow.

  • What would you recommend with the sea bass? Is it anything like a Pinot Noir?
  • Ask for a recommendation, then compare it to something you know. Now you are steering the conversation, not drowning in it.

The tab arrives in a leather folder. Your Chinese supplier reaches for his wallet. Stop him -- warmly, and fast.

  • Please, it's on me. And don't worry about the tip -- I've got it.
  • 'It's on me' means you are paying. Say it early, before the folder even opens, and remember: in the U.S. you add the tip yourself.

Comprehension

What Did Marco Say?

1. What did Marco recommend for the main course, and what was his second option?
He'd go with the sea bass — the best thing tonight. Or you might want to try the tasting menu, and let the chef choose.
2. What did Marco say about the gluten-free guest?
That it was right to mention it now, before ordering — and that the sea bass is already gluten-free, so it was an easy one.
3. Why did Marco offer to send the sommelier?
Because the sommelier can suggest a light red wine that works with both the fish and the steak.
4. What did Marco remind Lucimara about the bill?
That the tip is not included, so she can add it herself at the end.
