Vocabulary

Stage 1.1: Key Email Vocabulary

Click on each card to listen and learn. These 10 words are essential for professional emails.

Subject Line

The title or topic of an email, shown before opening it.
"A clear subject line helps the recipient understand the purpose of your email."

Recipient

The person who receives the email.
"Make sure the recipient is correct before sending."

Attachment

A file sent together with an email.
"Please find the attachment with the updated contract."

Deadline

The latest date or time by which something must be completed.
"The deadline for the proposal is next Friday."

CC

Carbon Copy — sending a copy of the email to additional people.
"I will CC the legal team on this email."

Follow Up

To contact someone again about a previous topic.
"I am writing to follow up on our previous conversation."

Proofread

To read a text carefully to find and correct mistakes.
"Please proofread the document before sending it."

Regards

A polite closing word in professional emails.
"Best regards, Milton Sayegh."

Forward

To send an email you received to another person.
"Could you forward this email to the sales team?"

Greeting

The opening line of an email, such as "Dear Mr. Smith."
"The greeting sets the tone for a professional email."

Grammar

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip — Linking Words for Business Emails

Linking words connect ideas in emails. They make your writing clear and professional.

FunctionLinking WordExample
AdditionFurthermore / In addition"Furthermore, we offer free shipping."
ContrastHowever / Nevertheless"However, the deadline has changed."
ResultTherefore / As a result"Therefore, I suggest a meeting."
ReferenceRegarding / With regard to"Regarding your inquiry, please see below."
SequenceFirst / Then / Finally"First, let me summarize the terms."

Grammar Rule

Linking Words for Emails

FunctionWordsUse in Email
AdditionFurthermore, In additionAdd more info to support your point
ContrastHowever, NeverthelessIntroduce an opposite idea or exception
ResultTherefore, As a resultShow consequence or next step
ReferenceRegarding, With regard toIntroduce a topic being discussed
Common Mistake

Formal vs. Informal

✗ "The order is ready but shipping will be late."
✓ "The order is ready. However, shipping will be delayed."

In professional emails, use formal linking words instead of "but", "so", "and".

Milton's Email to Ms. Chen

From: milton.sayegh@sayeghjewelry.com
To: l.chen@chenretail.com
CC: legal@sayeghjewelry.com
Subject: Partnership Agreement — Sayegh Jewelry / Chen Retail

Dear Ms. Chen,

Thank you for your interest in our products. I am pleased to inform you that we have approved your initial order.

However, we will need to adjust the delivery timeline due to high demand this season.

Furthermore, our quality team will inspect all items before shipment to ensure they meet your standards.

Therefore, please confirm the shipping address at your earliest convenience.

In addition, I have attached our latest price list for your reference.

I look forward to a successful partnership.

Best regards,
Milton Sayegh
Export Director, Sayegh Jewelry

Chapter 5: Practice

More
Practice

Quick Fire

Quick Fire

Challenge 1 / 4

You want to introduce a topic in your email. Which phrase is best?

A) "About the thing..."
B) "Regarding the partnership agreement..."
C) "So about that deal..."

B) "Regarding the partnership agreement..." — formal and professional.
Challenge 2 / 4

You approved an order but need to mention a delay. Which linking word connects these ideas?

However — it introduces a contrast between the approval and the delay.
Challenge 3 / 4

You want to ask the client to confirm their address as a consequence of the deadline. Which word?

Therefore — it shows the consequence (need to confirm because of the deadline).
Challenge 4 / 4

How should you close a formal business email to a new partner?

"I look forward to hearing from you. Best regards, Milton Sayegh" — professional, warm, and complete.
