Chapter 2: Words of the Outcome

The Language of the Verdict

Vocabulary

Words 1-4

0 / 4 words revealed

Verdict
The formal decision on guilt or innocence reached by the court
"The jury returned a guilty verdict after two days of deliberation."
Outcome
The final result of the case, taken as a whole
"Whatever the outcome, I will explain exactly what it means for you."
Ruling
A formal decision made by the court on a particular point
"The judge's ruling on the evidence shaped the whole verdict."
Finding
A specific conclusion the court reaches on a fact or issue
"The court's finding was that the search had been unlawful."
Vocabulary

Words 5-8

Consequence
A result that follows directly from an action or decision
"The conviction has one serious consequence: a permanent record."
Aftermath
The difficult period that follows a serious event
"In the aftermath of the verdict, the family needs a clear plan."
Conviction
The formal recording that a person has been found guilty
"A conviction stays on your record, but it can later be sealed."
Costs
The financial and personal price the outcome carries
"The deal limits the costs you would face after a full trial."
Vocabulary

Words 9-12

Comply
To follow a rule, order or condition exactly as required
"If you comply with every condition, the sentence stays suspended."
Conditions
The requirements a person must meet under a sentence or order
"The conditions include regular reporting and no contact with the witness."

Chapter 3: The Language of Consequence

Linking Cause to Effect

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

The evidence was so strong that a conviction followed.

It was such a serious offence that custody was likely.

I will explain the conditions so that you can comply.

The ruling was final; consequently, we must look at the appeal.

LinkerPatternExample
so ... thatso + adjective + that + resultso strong that we lost
such ... thatsuch + a/an + adj + noun + thatsuch a serious offence that...
so thatso that + clause (purpose)...so that you can comply
consequently / as a result / thereforesentence link (result)...; consequently, we appeal.

Use so + adjective + that and such + a/an + noun + that to link a degree to its result (so strong that; such a serious offence that). Use so that for a purpose (I explain the conditions so that you can comply). Use consequently / as a result / therefore to open a new sentence with the result. These are the links that make an explanation of an outcome clear and professional.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"It was so a serious offence that..."

✓ RIGHT

"It was such a serious offence that..."

✗ WRONG

"The evidence was such strong that we lost."

✓ RIGHT

"The evidence was so strong that we lost."

Use "so" with an adjective alone ("so strong that") and "such" with a noun phrase ("such a serious offence that"). Do not mix them. Keep "so that" for purpose ("so that you can comply"), and use "consequently" only to open a result, never inside the same clause.

Chapter 4: Explaining the Result

After the Decision

Dialogue

The Verdict

C
I heard the word "guilty" and everything stopped. What does the verdict actually mean for me?
L
Let me explain the outcome plainly. The evidence was so strong that the court reached a guilty finding, and the ruling stands.
C
So what is the consequence? Do I go to prison?
L
No. The conviction carries a record and redress to the victim, but the sentence is suspended; consequently, you serve no time if you comply with the conditions.
C
So this record follows me forever?
L
Not forever. The costs are real now, but if you comply for the full period, we can apply to seal it. I will write everything down so that the road ahead is clear.
C
The aftermath is so heavy that I could not think before. Now, at least, I understand it.
Comprehension

The Outcome

1. Why did the court reach a guilty finding?
Because the evidence was so strong that a conviction followed; the ruling stands.
2. Will the client serve time in prison?
No — the sentence is suspended; consequently, no time is served if the conditions are met.
3. What does the conviction carry as costs?
A criminal record and redress to the victim.
4. Is the record permanent?
No — if the client complies for the full period, counsel can apply to seal it.

Real Document

Post-Verdict Client Letter

CONFIDENTIAL — CLIENT LETTER

RE: STATE v. R. ANDRADE — THE VERDICT

From: Luiz Bressane, Public Defender · To: The Client

The outcome: Guilty finding on the lesser charge. Suspended sentence; redress ordered. No custodial time.

Why this result:

1. The evidence was so strong that the conviction followed.

2. It was such a serious offence that the court ordered redress.

3. The sentence is suspended; consequently, you serve no time.

The road ahead:

→ Comply with every condition so that the sentence stays suspended. After the full period, we can apply to seal the record.

Counsel's note: in every section, link the fact to its consequence ("so strong that", "such a serious offence that", "consequently"), and use "so that" for the conditions, so the client sees both the result and the way forward.

Give a "so ... that" line explaining the conviction.
The evidence was so strong that the court had no choice but to convict.
Give a "so that" line for the road ahead.
Comply with every condition so that the sentence stays suspended and the record can later be sealed.
Chapter 5: Drill

Sharpen the Link

Detective · Transformation · Building

Spot the Error

Fix the Link

"It was so a serious offence that custody was likely."
It was such a serious offence that custody was likely. (such + a/an + noun)
"The evidence was such strong that we lost."
The evidence was so strong that we lost. (so + adjective alone)
"The ruling was final, consequently we appeal."
The ruling was final; consequently, we appeal. (semicolon, then result)
"I explain the conditions so you can comply." (purpose intended)
I explain the conditions so that you can comply. (so that for purpose)
Transformation

Link the Facts

"The evidence was strong. We lost." → so ... that
The evidence was so strong that we lost.
"It was a serious offence. The court ordered redress." → such ... that
It was such a serious offence that the court ordered redress.
"I will list the conditions. You can comply." → so that
I will list the conditions so that you can comply.
"The ruling was final. We turn to the appeal." → consequently
The ruling was final; consequently, we turn to the appeal.
