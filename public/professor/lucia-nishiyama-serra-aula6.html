Certain When You Can, Careful When You Must

How Leaders Use Modal Verbs to Sound Both Certain and Careful

In a board briefing, the smallest words carry the most authority. Modal verbs -- can, should, must, may, might, will -- let a leader control exactly how strong or how careful a statement sounds. 'We must act' is an obligation. 'We should act' is a recommendation. 'We might act' leaves the door open. The same idea, three different levels of force. Executives who master this spectrum sound decisive when they need to, and careful when the situation is not yet clear.

Imagine a leader briefing the board on AI safety. She does not say 'there is maybe a problem.' She chooses her modal on purpose. 'I can tell you with certainty that our exposure is rising,' she asserts. 'The board should prioritize this now, and every team must follow the new framework.' Then, where the data is still thin, she hedges: 'This might create exposure at the regulatory level, so I would recommend that we do not defer the decision.' Each modal is a signal. 'Must' is a wall; 'might' is a window. The board hears a leader who knows the difference.

The lesson for any presenter is simple. Before you speak, decide how certain you really are, and pick the modal that matches. Use 'must' and 'will' only when you can defend them. Use 'should' and 'ought to' to recommend. Use 'may' and 'might' to hedge honestly when you are not sure. If you clarify your level of certainty with the right modal, people will trust every word -- because they will know that when you say 'must,' you mean it.

Chapter 4: The Modal Spectrum

From Might to Must

Obligation, recommendation, possibility, certainty

The Certainty Scale

One Idea, Five Levels of Force

The certainty scale: might -> may -> should -> must -> will
might / may
Possibility -- a window

Hedge when you are not fully sure: 'This might create exposure at the regulatory level.'

should / ought to
Recommendation -- advice

Recommend the best action: 'The board should prioritize this now.'

must / have to
Obligation -- a wall

A duty or a rule: 'Every team must comply.' (must = your conviction; have to = an outside rule.)

can / could
Ability / polite request

What is possible, or a polite request: 'I can confirm the numbers.' / 'Could you clarify that?'

will / would
Certainty / hypothetical

'will' = a confident prediction: 'This will scale.' 'would' = a hypothetical: 'I would recommend a review.'

Detail

Must vs Have To

MUST = your conviction

It comes from inside you.

"You must align on this."

Negative: "must not" = it is forbidden.

HAVE TO = an outside rule

It comes from a rule or a law.

"You have to submit the report by Friday."

Negative: "don't have to" = it is not necessary.

Both are followed by the base verb: "must comply", "have to submit".

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Every team must to comply with the framework."
"Every team must comply with the framework."
"The board should prioritizes AI safety."
"The board should prioritize AI safety."
"This will maybe create exposure."
"This might create exposure."

After a modal, use the base verb -- no to, no -s. To sound careful, choose might or may; do not weaken will with maybe.
