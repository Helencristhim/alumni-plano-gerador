The Distinction

Provisional vs Final

Provisional acceptance

"The line goes into service with a list attached. The defects liability period starts, and the supplier still pays for what fails."

The trains run. The obligation does not end.

Final acceptance

"Every outstanding item is closed, the twelve months are over, and the last payment is released."

Now it ends. And it only ends once.

On a fast call, "acceptance" on its own is not a word: it is two words, twelve months apart. When somebody says it without the adjective, ask "Provisional or final?" — that question has been worth millions.

Chapter 3: The Code

Two Tenses Run a Project

"We will finish" is a hope.  →  "By the twelfth we will have finished" is a commitment.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

By the time the integration test starts, we will have energized the power supply.

This time next week, we will be running a dry run of the failover procedure.

The supplier will have submitted the look-ahead schedule before the next milestone review.

By September, the team will have been working nights for six weeks.

Every sentence points at a date. In two of them the work is finished before it. In one it is still happening on it. And one says how long it will have gone on.

StructureWhat it promisesOn the call
will have + past participleDONE before that point in the future. This is the deadline tense — the one the contract reads."By the twelfth we will have confirmed site readiness."
will be + -ingIN PROGRESS at that point. Not finished, not starting: happening, while other things happen around it."This time next month we will be running final integration tests on site."
will have been + -ingHOW LONG it will have gone on by that point. It measures the effort, and it is how you justify a cost."By September the team will have been working nights for six weeks."
by / by the timeby = the deadline (not later than). until = the whole stretch of time. They are not interchangeable, and only one of them belongs in a commitment."By Friday, not until Friday."
by the time + PRESENTAfter by the time / before / when / as soon as, the future is written in the PRESENT. Never will there."By the time the ramp-up starts..." (never "will start")
What it sounds likeNobody says these tenses the way they are written. will have collapses to /wɪləv/ ("will-uv"), we will have becomes "we'll've", and will be becomes /wɪlbi/, unstressed and almost gone. The tense is still there — it is just carried by the PAST PARTICIPLE at the end, not by the auxiliary. That is why you listen to the END of the phrase.
Meaning

Four Patterns, Four Promises

Four patterns that forecast a project
will have + past participle
DONE by the deadline

The tense of the commitment. It says the work is finished BEFORE that point, not on it. This is the sentence somebody can hold you to, and it is the one the contract reads. "By the twelfth we will have confirmed site readiness in writing."

will be + -ing
IN PROGRESS at that moment

Not finished, not starting: happening, while the world moves around it. It is how you tell somebody what the site will look like on a given day. "This time next week we will be running a dry run of the failover procedure."

will have been + -ing
HOW LONG it will have gone on

It measures the effort accumulated up to that point, and it is how a cost or an extension gets justified. "By September the crews will have been working nights for six weeks."

by / by the time + present
The frame that carries the tense

BY is the deadline; UNTIL is the stretch. And after by the time, before, when, as soon as, the future is written in the PRESENT. "By the time the ramp-up starts, the workarounds will have been phased out."

"We will finish before the test" is a hope, and everybody in the call knows it. "By the twelfth we will have confirmed site readiness" is a date with a name on it. Same fact, same crew, same site: only the tense changed, and only one of them can be enforced.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Until the twelfth we will have confirmed site readiness."
"By the twelfth we will have confirmed site readiness."
"By the time the ramp-up will start, we will phase out the workarounds."
"By the time the ramp-up starts, we will have phased out the workarounds."

Portuguese covers a deadline and a duration with the same little word, and it puts the future after quando. English does neither: by is the deadline and until is the stretch — and after by the time the verb goes into the present, however far away the date is.

Sound First

What You Read vs What You Hear

The words do not disappear. The auxiliaries do.

You hear: "we'll've closed'em by Friday"
"We will have closed them by Friday."

You hear: "we'll-be runnin' the dry run"
"We will be running the dry run next week."

You hear: "wotcha gonna do about the interfaces"
"What are you going to do about the interfaces?"

You hear: "we shoulda flagged it, kinda late now"
"We should have flagged that interface earlier."

The tense survives at the end of the phrase, in the participle: closed, running, flagged. Stop hunting for the auxiliary you were taught. Listen for the last word.

Comprehension

About Fiona

1. What are the four dates she gives, and what happens on each?
The twelfth: site readiness, power supply energized. The fifteenth: the integration test begins. The week after: the dry run of the failover. November: the ramp-up to full service — and that one does not move.
2. What is her worry about the interfaces?
That nobody is managing them, and that this only becomes visible during the test — when it is too late and too expensive to fix.
3. What does she say about workarounds, and why?
That they are phased out BEFORE provisional acceptance, never after. Her reason: she has seen too many projects where the temporary fix quietly becomes the design.
4. How does she react when he interrupts to check the deadline?
She says "Correct" and carries on. She does not lose patience — she confirms and moves. Asking cost him five seconds; getting it wrong would have cost the site three days.
Listening

She Calls You Back

Between two meetings, at her speed. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What has changed since the call, and when did she decide it?
Provisional acceptance has moved forward, from the thirtieth to the twenty-third of September. Her board asked for it that morning, after the call — and she is telling him before it appears in a document.
2. What does she want from him by tonight, and what will she NOT accept in it?
The three-week look-ahead schedule. She will not accept a workaround carried into provisional acceptance, and she will not accept a date that has not factored in the night-shift restrictions.
3. What does she admit, and what does she ask him to do about it?
That the interface document from the rolling stock supplier is three weeks late, and that it is her side's problem, not his. She asks him to keep the integration test date anyway and to flag it to her the moment it starts to hurt.
Listening 2

Three Rules for the Call You Cannot Follow

A commissioning manager, thirty years of it. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Why does he say the fast speaker is not testing you?
Because she is not thinking about your English at all. She is late, she has three projects, and she talks that way to everybody. Speed is not an exam — it is her Tuesday.
2. What does he say about the auxiliaries, and where does the tense survive?
The auxiliaries collapse: "will have" becomes /wɪləv/ and "will be" almost disappears. The tense survives at the end, in the participle — closed, energized, running. Listen to the last word, not the first.
3. What is the rule about dates, and what does it cost?
Never agree to a date you have not repeated back out loud. Saying "just to confirm, the twelfth, not the fifteenth" costs five seconds. Getting it wrong costs a crew, a night shift, and a slipped test.
