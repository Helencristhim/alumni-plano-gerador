Lesson 14 · The Coaching 1:1

Developing People, Not Managing Them

Anyone can hand a struggling person a plan. A senior leader asks the right question, stays quiet long enough to hear the answer, and guides — warm, but directive — so the person walks out able to solve the next one themselves.

Chapter 1: The Quiet Mentee

Two Ways to Open a 1:1

A. “I have looked at your deadlines. Here is what I am going to move off your plate, and here is the new plan.”
B. “Before I say anything — help me understand what got in the way these last two weeks.”

Which one develops the person — and which one just solves the task and leaves them exactly where they were?

Reading

Read for the Main Idea

The Hour That Kept Owen

Owen was a talented junior coordinator, and for two weeks he had gone quiet. Deadlines slipped, his replies got shorter, and the night before his one-on-one he left Danielle a voice note that all but said he was ready to throw in the towel. The easy move -- the one that would have felt kind and fast -- was to take the hard tasks off his plate and hand him a plan. Danielle did the opposite. She walked in with a coaching mindset, which is a fancy way of saying she led with a question instead of a verdict, and then did the single hardest thing for a fast, capable leader: she was quiet. Active listening is not waiting for your turn to talk; it is letting the silence sit until the person says the thing under the thing.

What Owen said, once the silence did its work, was not that he was lazy. He was drowning in tasks that did not play to his strengths, and he had been too proud to check in before things slipped. So Danielle reframed the story. "You are not failing," she told him. "You are on the steep part of the learning curve." That single move -- reframing -- did more than any instruction could have, because it changed what the problem was. And only then did she guide, carefully choosing the register of a leader who advises without commanding: "I would rather we solved the root cause together than patch the same thing next month. And honestly, you had better talk to your manager before the next deadline slips -- it is far easier now than later." When Owen admitted he would have to redo an entire report regardless, she smiled. "If you are redoing it anyway, you may as well use it to show me exactly what you have learned."

None of this was a scorecard. It was developmental feedback -- input aimed at growth, not a verdict on the past -- and it landed because she had earned the right to give it by listening first. She stayed a sounding board rather than a judge, so Owen could think out loud without defending himself. She named his real growth edge, which was not effort but asking for help early, and she set a stretch assignment just beyond his comfort: running the next round of interviews, work that pointed straight at his career aspirations. A task a size too big is exactly where growth happens, provided the person is not left alone in it.

The point of the hour was never to rescue Owen; it was to empower him to solve the next problem without her. She modelled boundary setting, too, telling him plainly what she could and could not take off his plate, so the help stayed honest and the ownership stayed his. He had walked in ready to quit and walked out with one clear task and a reason to stay. Sometimes leadership means you bite the bullet and have the difficult conversation. More often, as Danielle's own coach likes to remind her, it means you ask the right question and then keep your solutions in your pocket long enough to hear the answer.

Chapter 4: The Code

Guide, Don't Command

I would rather we solved it · You had better ask early · You may as well show what you learned

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

I would rather grow you than replace you.

I would rather we solved the root cause than patch it again.

You had better talk to your manager before the deadline slips.

If you are redoing it anyway, you may as well show what you learned.

Every modal is followed by the bare verb — grow, talk, show, no “to” — and each one carries a different force: would rather a preference, had better a warning, may as well a “no reason not to”. With a different subject, would rather pulls the verb into the past (we solved).

StructureFormWhat it does
would ratherwould rather + bare verbA preference. I would rather grow you than replace you. NOT “would rather to grow”.
would rather + subjectwould rather + person + past formA preference about someone else. I would rather you didn't promise it. I would rather we solved it.
had betterhad better (not) + bare verbStrong advice with a warning. You had better talk to them first. You had better not wait.
may / might as wellmay as well + bare verbNo better option, so do it. If you are redoing it anyway, you may as well show what changed.
Why it sounds senior“Do it” is a command that shrinks the other person. “I would rather we solved this, and you had better flag it early” guides with a preference and a warning — warm, but directive. The modal you choose IS the register.

Put them together and you guide a whole 1:1 without a single order: “I would rather grow you than replace you — but you had better ask for help early, and if you are redoing the report anyway, you may as well use it to show me what changed.”

Register

Suggestion, or Guidance

A weak suggestion — it sounds unsure

Soft to the point of vanishing. The mentee cannot tell if it matters, so nothing changes.

“Maybe you could, I don't know, mention it to your manager sometime?”

Executive guidance — warm, but directive

A warning without a raised voice. The mentee knows it matters and still keeps ownership.

“You had better talk to your manager before the deadline slips — it is far easier now than later.”

Same advice, two registers. The first invites the mentee to ignore you. The second is the sentence a senior leader says — clear enough to act on, kind enough to keep the relationship.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"You had better to talk to your manager today."
"You had better talk to your manager today."
"I would rather to reframe the problem than blame the tool."
"I would rather reframe the problem than blame the tool."

Both slips are Portuguese arriving on time. would rather, had better and may as well all take the bare infinitive — no “to”. And with a different subject, would rather takes the past: “I would rather you don't” → “I would rather you didn't”.

Quick Fire

Coach, Don't Rescue

1 / 6

"A mentee says: 'I think I'm just not good at this. Maybe I should quit.' Respond so you open the door instead of closing it."

  • Help me understand what got in the way. You are not failing -- you are on the steep part of the learning curve, and I would rather grow you than replace you. What would it look like if the next month went differently?
  • reframe the story first, then use 'would rather' to state your preference -- own it without ordering him to stay

"Your mentee keeps missing deadlines and only tells you afterwards. Give urgent advice that carries a warning, without raising your voice."

  • You had better talk to your manager before the next deadline slips, not after -- it is far easier now. And you had better not wait until it is a crisis to check in with me.
  • 'had better' is strong advice with a warning; keep it warm by pairing it with why -- it is easier now

"Owen admits he'll have to redo a whole report anyway and is dreading it. Turn the sunk cost into growth."

  • If you are redoing it regardless, you may as well use it to show me exactly what you have learned. Same work, better payoff -- let us make the redo the stretch assignment, not a punishment.
  • 'may as well' makes the decision feel easy; frame the redo as a stretch assignment that plays to his strengths

"A mentee is drowning in tasks that don't suit them. What is the coaching move -- in one or two sentences?"

  • Let us play to your strengths. I would rather move you onto the interviews, where you are genuinely strong, and empower you to run them, than keep you buried in spreadsheets you dread.
  • name the mismatch, then 'would rather' + a stretch assignment that fits their strengths and their career aspirations

"Your mentee opens up and is close to giving up. Name the real growth edge without it sounding like criticism."

  • What I am noticing is that your growth edge is not effort -- you have plenty. It is asking for help before things slip. I would rather you didn't carry it alone; that is what I am here for.
  • developmental feedback about the pattern, not the person; 'would rather you didn't' (past form, different subject)

"Summarize, for your own coach, how you kept a mentee who wanted to quit."

  • I led with a question, not a verdict, and I listened until he named the real problem. I reframed it as a learning curve, named his growth edge, and guided him -- I would rather grow him than replace him. I gave him a stretch assignment and empowered him to run it. He walked out with a reason to stay.
  • the close: listen, reframe, guide with the modals, empower -- rescue was never the point
