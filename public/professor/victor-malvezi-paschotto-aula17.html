Leading the Room

Chair and steer a meeting from start to finish

Warm-up

Attending Is Easy. Chairing Is the Job

Winning people over gets them in the room. Running the room is a different skill: you own the purpose, the floor, and the clock — and you leave with a decision, not just a discussion.

The Four Jobs

Open, Steer, Protect the Clock, Close

Open with purpose

"We are here to lock the date and leave with owners."

Steer the floor

"Carla, what's your read? Let's park that for now."

Protect the clock

"In the interest of time, can we come back to that?"

Close with a recap

"Here's what we decided, who owns it, and the date."

Chapter 3

The Grammar

Direct the room without sounding like a boss

Grammar Discovery

Spot the Pattern

Three lines — which ones steer a room without barking orders?

"Move on to the next item." (a bare order — sounds blunt in the chair)

"Shall we move on to the next item?" (shall we + bare verb)

"Would you mind keeping it brief, so we stay on time?" (would you mind + -ing)

Don't command the room. Invite it — and it follows.

The Rule

The Chairing Toolkit

StructureJobExample
Shall we / Why don't we + bare verbpropose the next step"Shall we move on to item two?"
Would you mind + -ingask politely to do / stop something"Would you mind keeping it brief?"
Could we + bare verbredirect gently"Could we come back to that later?"
In the interest of time / Let's move onsteering markers (guard the clock)"In the interest of time, let's move on."

The key thing to keep clean:

A chair steers with invitations, not commands. Use shall we / why don't we + bare verb ("Shall we start?") to propose, would you mind + -ing ("Would you mind holding that?") to ask politely, and could we + bare verb ("Could we park this?") to redirect. Drop in steering markers like in the interest of time and let's move on to guard the clock. Same control, far less friction — the room follows because you invited it, not ordered it.

Common Mistake

Order vs Steer

✗ BARE ORDER

"Stop talking and move on to the next point."

✓ SOFT IMPERATIVE

"Shall we move on to the next point, and come back to this?"

✗ WRONG FORM

"Would you mind to keep it brief?" (mind + to = wrong)

✓ MIND + -ING

"Would you mind keeping it brief?" (mind + -ing)

Shall we + bare verb. Would you mind + -ing. Could we + bare verb. Invite, don't bark.

Chapter 4

Running the Room

A drifting meeting, steered back to a decision

Dialogue

The Cross-Regional Review

V
Thanks for joining, everyone. We're here to lock the rollout date and leave with one owner per region — three items on the agenda, ten minutes each. Shall we start with the timeline?
B
Before that, Victor — we really need to talk about the budget cuts, because honestly none of this matters if my region loses two headcount next quarter.
V
That's a real concern, Beatriz, and I don't want to lose it. Would you mind parking it as a sidebar? Let's put budget right after this call so it gets proper time, and keep this hour on the date.
B
Okay, fair — as long as it actually happens and not "next time". Put it on the sidebar list and I'll hold it there.
V
Done, it's on the list. Now, on the date — Carla's been quiet and she runs the busiest corridor. Carla, what's your read on the fifteenth? And in the interest of time, could we keep it to the headline?
B
Carla just dropped off, but she sent me her note: the fifteenth works if her team gets the new vans by the tenth. So the date holds, with that one condition.
V
Perfect. Let me recap before we adjourn: rollout date is the fifteenth, conditional on Carla's vans by the tenth, which I'll own. Beatriz owns the budget sidebar right after this. Anything I've missed?
B
Nothing missed — and we finished on time, for once. That's the first review this year that ended with an actual decision.
Comprehension

Did You Catch It?

1. What does Beatriz try to raise, and how does the chair handle it?
She tries to open the budget cuts, which aren't on the agenda; Victor parks it as a sidebar right after the call so it gets proper time and the hour stays on the date.
2. How does the chair get the quiet, best-informed person's view?
He brings Carla in by name because she runs the busiest corridor; Carla's note says the fifteenth works if her team gets the new vans by the tenth.
3. What is decided by the end of the meeting?
The rollout date is the fifteenth, conditional on the vans by the tenth (Victor owns it), and Beatriz owns the budget sidebar — and it finished on time with a real decision.

Listening 2

The Coach Voice Note

Listen first. No text. Sound only.

0:000:00
1. What is Beatriz's first rule?
The chair owns the purpose and the clock — open with why we're here and what a finished meeting looks like, put a time box on every item, and say it out loud.
2. Why does she say the chair's real job is the floor, not the content?
Confident voices dominate and the people with the answers stay silent, so you steer deliberately: park a digression, push side talk into a sidebar, and bring the quiet in by name.
3. What does she say about ending a meeting?
Never let it dissolve — recap the decisions, name an owner for each action, confirm the date, and adjourn cleanly; if people leave unsure, the meeting didn't happen.

Quick Fire

Run the Room

1 / 5

2. Someone digresses. Park it as a sidebar (soft imperative).

"That's a good point — would you mind parking it as a sidebar for after?"

"Why don't we hear from Carla — what's your read on this?"

"In the interest of time, could we move on and come back to that?"

"Let me recap the decisions and owners, then we'll adjourn on time."
