Chapter 3: Comparing Value

How Much
Cheaper?

Comparatives of degree + intensifiers

Grammar Discovery

Spot the Pattern

This stall is far more expensive than the next one.
The online price is slightly cheaper than in the shop.
The copy is not nearly as good as the original.
The more you haggle, the more you save.

How big is each difference? And what does the more... the more mean?

IdeaStructureExample
Big differencefar / considerably / much + comparative"far more expensive"
Small differenceslightly / a bit + comparative"slightly cheaper"
Much less thannot nearly as + adjective + as"not nearly as good as..."
Two things rise togetherthe + comparative..., the + comparative..."The more you haggle, the more you save."

Key: use far / considerably / much para uma diferença grande, e slightly / a bit para uma pequena. Not nearly as ... as = muito menos que. The more..., the more... = quanto mais..., mais....

Common Mistake

Watch Out!

"It is very cheaper here." / "Not so good than the real one." / "More you haggle, more you save."
"It is far cheaper here." / "Not nearly as good as the real one." / "The more you haggle, the more you save."

Use far / much (not "very") before a comparative, and keep as ... as and the two thes.

the Market

Dialogue

Haggling for a Souvenir

S
"Good morning! Feel free to browse. That leather bag is handmade — top of the range. Sixty euros."
P
"It is lovely, but sixty is a little pricey. The stall round the corner had a similar one for far less."
S
"Ah, but those are knock-offs. Mine is real leather — considerably better made. It will last for years."
P
"I can see the quality. But I am on a tight budget. Could you do forty?"
S
"Forty is too low, my friend. But because you have a good eye, I will say fifty. That is real value for money."
P
"Let us meet in the middle. Forty-five, and you have a deal."
S
"You drive a hard bargain! Forty-five it is. At that price, it is almost a steal."
P
"Wonderful. The more we talked, the better the price got. Thank you!"

3. Why did Pricila say she could not pay sixty?
She was on a tight budget.
4. What was the final price they agreed on?
Forty-five euros.

Read for the Main Idea

The Souvenir Trap

The Souvenir Trap: How Travellers Spend Smart Abroad

Every famous destination has its shopping streets, and most of them are designed to part you from your money. The closer you stand to the main attraction, the more overpriced everything becomes. A fridge magnet that costs one euro three streets away can be five euros in the square, and the so-called 'local craft' is, more often than not, a knock-off shipped in by the thousand.

The traveller who spends smart treats the first price as the beginning of a conversation, not the end. In many markets, the marked price is considerably higher than what the seller actually expects to receive, and a polite shopper who is willing to haggle can often talk the price down by a third. The trick is to stay friendly, to browse without rushing, and never to look too keen on any single item.

Yet smart spending is not only about paying less. It is about value for money. Sometimes the cheapest option is a false economy, and the slightly pricier, better-made piece is far more worth the extra. The wisest souvenirs are rarely the most expensive ones; they are the ones that still mean something long after the trip, when the bargain has been forgotten but the memory has not.

Adapted from a travel-money column, The Frugal Globe
