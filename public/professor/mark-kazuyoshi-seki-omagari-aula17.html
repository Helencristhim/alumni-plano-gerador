Chapter 3 -- The Pattern

Grammar Discovery

verb + -ing / verb + to + base verb

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

When is the verb -ing, and when is it to + verb?

"I enjoy playing the guitar." (liking → -ing)

"I decided to study harder." (planning → to + verb)

"I cannot stand waiting in lines." (disliking → -ing)

"I look forward to seeing you." (preposition → -ing)

Grammar Rule

-ing or to + verb?

FormAfter verbs likeExample
-ing (gerund)enjoy, avoid, fancy, can't stand, mind, dreadI enjoy playing.
to + verb (infinitive)want, hope, decide, manage, afford, tendI decided to study.
eitherstart, love, prefer, beginI love reading / to read.
-ing (after preposition)look forward to, give up, keep onI look forward to seeing.

Key idea: verbs of liking and disliking take the gerund (-ing); verbs of wanting and planning take the infinitive (to + verb). A few verbs take either with almost no change in meaning, and after a preposition you always use the gerund.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"I enjoy to play chess." (enjoy + to + verb)
"I enjoy playing chess."

After liking and disliking verbs use the gerund (-ing); after wanting and planning verbs use the infinitive (to + verb). "I want learning" is also wrong — say "I want to learn".

Comprehension

About the Tips

1. Which form do we use after verbs of liking, like enjoy?

The gerund, the verb plus -ing, for example "I enjoy playing chess".

2. Which form do we use after verbs of planning, like decide?

The infinitive, to plus the base verb, for example "I decided to study".

3. What form do we use after a preposition, like "to" in look forward to?

Always the gerund, so we say "I look forward to seeing you", not "to see".
