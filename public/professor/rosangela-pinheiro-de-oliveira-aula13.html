From a Lifetime to the Last Hour

What Have You Just Done?

It is almost midnight. Give me three sentences about these travelers: "The plane has just landed...", "They have already got their bags...", "They haven't found the hotel yet..."

In Lesson 12 you told a whole life — twenty-three cities, forty years. Three Words, and Where They Stand

Chapter 3: The Code

Two in the middle. One at the end.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I have just arrived. The flight has just landed."

"I have already unpacked, and I have already checked in."

"I haven't had dinner yet."

"Have you checked in yet?"

Two colors, two places. Where do the green words stand? And where does the blue word stand — and in which two sentences?

WordWhere it standsExample
justIn the middle: have + just + participleI have just arrived.
alreadyIn the middle: have + already + participleI have already unpacked.
yetAt the end — only in the negative and in questionsI haven't eaten yet. · Have you eaten yet?

Just and already are a sandwich: they sit in the middle, between have and the verb. Yet is a door: it sits at the end, and it only opens when the sentence is a "no" or a question.

You already know the base. In Lesson 12 you said "I have been to Italy." Today: same have, same participle — you only drop one word in the middle, or one at the end.

The Code

The Sandwich and the Door

Three little words that change where they stand
just
a moment ago -- it stands in the MIDDLE

"I have JUST arrived." It happened a very short time ago, almost now. Put it between have and the verb: have + just + past participle. Never "I just have arrived", never "I have arrived just now".

already
sooner than you thought -- also in the MIDDLE

"I have ALREADY unpacked." It is done, and it is done early -- it is good news, a small surprise. Same place as just: have + already + past participle.

yet
up to now -- and it lives at the END

"I haven't had dinner YET." "Have you checked in YET?" Only in questions and in the negative, and always at the end of the sentence. Yet is the thing that is still waiting to happen.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I have arrived just now, and I am very tired."
"I have just arrived, and I am very tired."
"I have already ate breakfast."
"I have already eaten breakfast."
"Did you check in yet?"
"Have you checked in yet?"

Just goes in the middle, never at the end. After have comes the participle — eaten, not ate. And with yet, the question is Have you...?, not Did you...?, because the thing can still happen.

The Sentence Kit

Three Sentences, Any Front Desk

You can answer a whole check-in with these three.

"I have just arrived, and I am exhausted." (just = a moment ago)
"I have already checked in online." (already = done, and done early)
"I haven't had dinner yet." (not yet = still waiting — a polite little ask)

One says what just happened. One gives good news. One asks for help without asking. Three lines, and the whole front desk is done.

Expression of the Day

So Far, So Good

So far, so good.

= up to now, everything is fine (the trip is not finished — "so far" is the present perfect in two words)

"Three days in Portugal, and no problems. So far, so good."
"The room is nice, the bed is soft, and I have already unpacked. So far, so good."

You have been in Europe for three days now. So far, so good?

The Hard Part

Same Desk. Which One Sounds Human?

Same information. Same midnight. Same person.

Survival English

"I arrive now. I am tired. Check-in yes, online. Dinner no. Room? Bags there."

Every word is a fact with no time on it. The receptionist understands, but he cannot help you, because he does not know what you still need.

Real English

"I have just arrived, and I am exhausted. I have already checked in online, and I have already dropped off my bags — but I haven't had dinner yet."

Now he knows exactly what to do: the room is set, and you need food. The three little words did that.

You have had the facts since Lesson 2. Tonight you learn the three words that make them sound like you.

Comprehension

About Tomás

1. What did Tomás say about the room? Is it ready, and what has the hotel already done?
Yes, the room is ready — room 402, on the fourth floor. They have already cleaned it, and Tomás has already put a spare key in the envelope for her husband.
2. Rosângela and her husband haven't had dinner. The kitchen is closed — so what did Tomás offer?
He offered to send some soup and bread up to the room, because it is midnight and they have not eaten. The kitchen has already closed, but he found a way.
3. What are the breakfast plans in the dialogue — and what did Tomás tell her to leave for tomorrow?
He told her to do nothing tonight: just drop off the bags, freshen up, and sleep. She can unpack and settle in tomorrow.
4. What did Rosângela ask for, for the morning, and why? What did Tomás confirm?
She asked for a wake-up call at eight, because she does not trust her phone with the jet lag. Tomás confirmed: a wake-up call at eight, room 402.
5. Tomás says she has already done the hard part. In his words, what is the hard part?
Arriving. "You have already done the hard part — you are here." Everything else (unpacking, settling in) can wait until tomorrow.
Listening

The Night Manager's Welcome

Almost midnight. A group of tired travelers has just arrived. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Henrique says the rooms are ready. What three things has the hotel already done?
They have already cleaned the rooms, they have already put out the water and the towels, and the beds are turned down. The travelers do not have to do anything tonight.
2. What are the breakfast times, and what is his advice for people who have just arrived from far away?
Breakfast is downstairs, from seven to ten. But he says: do not put an alarm for six. If you have just arrived from far away, your body does not know the time, so sleep. If you miss breakfast, they will find you a coffee.
3. What is the one thing he says people forget — and when should they do it?
The wake-up call. He says to call reception now, tonight, before going up — not in the morning. Half of the people who miss their tour have not asked for the wake-up call yet, and then jet lag wins.
4. How does Henrique finish? What does he say they have already done?
He says: "You have already done the hard part. You have arrived." Then he gives them their keys and welcomes them to Lisbon.
Listening 2

Margarida, 72, and the First Hour

The hardest hour of any trip, she says. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Margarida says the hardest part of a trip is not the airport or the plane. What is it?
The first hour in the room, alone. You have just arrived, you are exhausted and jet-lagged, it is late, and you open the door of a room you have never seen, and for one minute you want to go home.
2. She has a ritual of three small things, in the same order. What are they?
One: she drops off her bag and opens the curtains, even at night. Two: she unpacks one thing, usually a photograph. Three: she freshens up, washes her face with cold water, and says out loud, "I have arrived. I made it."
3. What is the one thing she says you must NOT do in that first hour?
Do not sit down on the bed. She says if you sit down, jet-lagged, at midnight, it is over. Do the three small things first, standing up.
4. Who used to do the talking for her, and what does she say the hard part really was?
Her husband used to do the talking, and he is not with her any more. She says the language was never the hard part — the doorway was. And she tells the listener: "You have already walked through worse doorways than this one."

Quick Fire

Answer the Front Desk

1 / 8

It is almost midnight. The receptionist looks at your face and asks: "Have you just arrived?" Answer, and say how you feel.

  • Yes, I have just arrived. The flight has just landed, and I am exhausted.
  • Yes, I have just arrived, and I am so jet-lagged I cannot think.

He asks if you have checked in online. You did it this morning, before the airport. Use the good-news word.

  • Yes, I have already checked in online, this morning.
  • I have already checked in. I did it on my phone before the flight.

He asks if you have had dinner. You have not. Use the word that waits at the end.

  • No, I haven't had dinner yet. Is the restaurant still open?
  • Not yet. I haven't eaten anything since the plane.

Your bags are already up in the room -- you dropped them off ten minutes ago. Your husband asks about them. Tell him.

  • I have already dropped off the bags. They are in the room.
  • Don't worry, I have already taken the bags upstairs.

You want a wake-up call for seven o'clock. Ask the receptionist for it, politely.

  • Could I have a wake-up call at seven, please?
  • Can you give me a wake-up call at seven tomorrow?

Careful now -- he asks WHEN you arrived in Lisbon, the city. You came three days ago. That is a finished time.

  • I arrived in Lisbon three days ago. (A finished time, so past simple, not "I have arrived three days ago".)
  • I came three days ago. Today I have just arrived at the hotel, and that is different.

It is early morning. You are hungry and you want to know if breakfast is open. Ask, with the end word.

  • Has breakfast started yet?
  • Excuse me, has the restaurant opened yet?

He asks if this is your first time in Portugal. It is not -- you came once in 2019. Answer with BOTH tenses.

  • No, I have been to Portugal before. I came in 2019.
  • I have been here once. I came in 2019, with my son.
