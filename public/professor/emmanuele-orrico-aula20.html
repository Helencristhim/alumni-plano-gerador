The Second Before the Answer

The Second Before the Answer

Every quarter, Emmanuele presents Brazil's results to the Global team. She prepares the slides for days, and she knows the numbers well. But there is one part she cannot prepare: the questions. After the presentation, someone from Global always asks something she did not expect. "What is the biggest risk right now?" "What should Brazil do differently in Q4?" "What could go wrong with the launch?" For a long time, these questions were her nightmare. Her mind went blank, and she switched to Portuguese.

Then a mentor gave her a simple idea: the answer is not the hard part -- the first second is. When a hard question comes, do not rush. Buy one second. Say, "That's a great question -- let me think about that for a second." That short pause is not a weakness. It shows you are thinking, and it gives your brain time to find the words. A calm approach is more professional than a fast, nervous one.

And there are three small words that make any answer sound clear and confident. Use should for a recommendation: "We should focus on the private market." Use could for an alternative or a possibility: "We could adjust the targeting," or "One option could be to start in the Northeast." And use might when there is uncertainty: "The rollout might take a little longer." These are modal verbs, and they all work the same way: the modal plus the base verb -- should focus, could adjust, might take. No "to", and no extra "s".

So now, when Global asks the unexpected, Emmanuele has a plan. She buys a second, she stays in English, and she answers with should, could, or might. She does not need a perfect answer. She needs a clear approach and a calm voice. "I don't know" becomes "That's a great question -- one option could be this, and another might be that." And that, in any language, is what confidence sounds like.

Chapter 4: The Pattern

Should, Could, Might

three words, one shape — and you already read them

Grammar Discovery

Find the Pattern

"We should focus on the private market."

"We could adjust the HCP targeting."

"The rollout might take a little longer."

"We should protect the top accounts."

After should / could / might — what form is the orange verb? And do you hear "to" or an "s"?

WordUseExample
shoulda recommendation (the best idea)"We should focus on the private market."
couldan alternative / a polite option"We could adjust the targeting."
mightuncertainty (possible, not sure)"The rollout might take longer."
The one shapeModal + the base verb: should focus, could adjust, might take. No "to", no "s" — not "should to focus", not "might takes".

One shape for all three. Only the meaning changes: should = advice, could = option, might = maybe.

Language Focus

Recommendation, Option, or Uncertainty?

AnalyseWhich is it -- a recommendation, an option, or uncertainty?
AWe should focus on the private market. -- a recommendation (should)
BWe could adjust the HCP targeting. -- an alternative / option (could)
CThe rollout might take a little longer. -- uncertainty (might)
DMy recommendation is that we should start small. -- a recommendation (should)
SHOULD (A and D) gives your recommendation -- the best idea, what you advise. COULD (B) offers an alternative or a polite option -- one possible way, not the only way. MIGHT (C) shows uncertainty -- something possible, but you are not sure. In a real Q and A, you mix all three: "We should focus on the private market (recommendation). We could adjust the targeting (option). And the timeline might change a little (uncertainty)."
Common Mistake

Modal + the Base Verb

"We should to focus on the private market."
"We should focus on the private market."
"The rollout might takes a little longer."
"The rollout might take a little longer."
"We could to adjust the targeting."
"We could adjust the targeting."

One rule covers all three: after should, could and might, use the base verb — no "to" and no "s".
