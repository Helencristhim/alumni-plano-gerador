Metodologia

1. Listening em TODA aula — o gap nº 1

Dois áudios longos por aula, com player completo e controle de velocidade (0.5x a 1.25x). Protocolo fixo: som primeiro, sem texto; “what did you catch?”; segunda escuta mais lenta; só então as perguntas. Nunca tradução.

2. Os 80 minutos de carro viram sala de aula

Ela mesma ofereceu a janela: “mesmo que eu vá ouvindo no carro, são quarenta minutos de ida, quarenta de volta”. Os Complementares priorizam podcast e áudio (ela assiste pouca série) e sempre com tarefa concreta: 3 frases entendidas, 2 não captadas.

3. Contexto de viagem, não de curso

Aeroporto, hotel, táxi, restaurante, feira internacional, jantar com parceiro chinês. O conteúdo é o que ela vai viver em setembro em Nova York — nunca inglês genérico. Business entra pontual: reuniões com chineses e atualidades.

4. Ritmo calmo, adulto-adulto (REGRA 14 — 50+)

Repetição generosa, pausas, tempo para pensar. Zero gamificação infantil e zero condescendência: ela é diretora de uma indústria. A vivência internacional dela é recurso pedagógico, não enfeite.

5. Vínculo profissional, NUNCA amizade

O ponto crítico do histórico dela: a professora anterior virou amiga e a aula virou conversa. Seja caloroso e mantenha a autoridade pedagógica. Cada aula precisa mostrar PROGRESSO TANGÍVEL — é o que sustenta a motivação dela.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Simple vs Present Continuous vs Present Perfect (for / since)

GRAMMAR

The three structures behind any personal introduction in English.

FormUseExample
Present Simple
I / you / we + verb (he/she + s)		Your routine, your job title, what is always true.I work in the chemical industry.
Present Continuous
am / is / are + verb-ing		What is happening NOW, this month — not the routine.Right now, I am preparing a registration.
Present Perfect
have / has + past participle		It started in the past and it CONTINUES today. Duration up to now (for / since).I have worked here for twenty years.
Negativehave / has + not + past participle. In speech, contracted: haven't.I haven't been to Lyon this year.
QuestionHow long + have + subject + past participle?How long have you been in the industry?
for vs sincefor + a period of time (for twenty years) · since + a starting point (since 2015)

Watch out -- the classic mistake: in your first language, duration up to now uses the PRESENT tense, so "I work here for twenty years" feels right. In English it is wrong: duration up to now never uses the present simple. The correct form is "I have worked here for twenty years" (in speech, I've worked). And remember: responsible FOR, never "responsible of".

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

It is nine fifteen at night at Terminal Four. Lucimara has just landed in New York. She landed at nine, walked to passport control and answered two questions: "Business or pleasure?" and "How long are you staying?" — that part is over, and the past simple tells the story. Now she is at baggage claim. Her suitcase has already arrived on belt six; her carry-on has never left her shoulder. She has not gone through customs yet, but she has nothing to declare. At the hotel, the front desk only needs her confirmation number, because she has already completed the online check-in. One problem: the room is not ready yet. So she leaves her bags, and the concierge recommends a small restaurant two blocks away. At eleven she is finally in her room. She has not unpacked yet — and she will not tonight. The jet lag is going to wake her at four in the morning, and her itinerary says the trade fair starts at nine.

1. "She has just landed." What does just add to the sentence?
A It happened a few minutes ago — very recently.
B It happened a long time ago and it is over.
C It is going to happen in a few minutes.
2. "She has not gone through customs yet." What does this sentence say?
A She has already gone through customs.
B She has not gone through customs so far — but she still will. yet = up to now, not.
C She will never go through customs.
3. Why is "She landed at nine, walked to passport control and answered two questions" in the past simple, and not in the present perfect?
A Because the action is still happening now.
B Because it is a SEQUENCE of finished actions, with the time said out loud (at nine) — it is a story.
C Because the result now matters more than the moment.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer. Some options are grammatically wrong; others are just too blunt for the moment.

You arrive early and want to know if you can check in. Which sounds best?
A "Is the room ready? Give me my key."
B "Do you know if the room is ready yet? I'm happy to wait if it isn't."
C "Do you know is the room ready?"
The heating is broken. You call the front desk. You say:
A "Would you mind to send someone to fix the heating?"
B "Would you mind sending someone up to look at the heating?"
C "You must send someone to fix the heating now."
There is a valet surcharge on your bill, but you brought no car. How do you speak up?
A "I was wondering if you could take a look at this — there seems to be a valet charge, and I didn't use the parking."
B "This charge is a lie. Remove it right now."
C "Sorry, sorry, it's probably fine, never mind."
You want to know what time breakfast starts. Which is correct?
A "Could you tell me what time does breakfast start?"
B "Could you tell me what time breakfast starts?"
C "Could you tell me what time starts breakfast?"
Your flight is late on Friday and you want to stay in the room longer. You ask:
A "I need a late checkout. Make it happen."
B "Could I possibly have a late checkout on Friday? My flight is not until the evening."
C "Do you know can I have a late checkout?"

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer — speak for about 90 seconds, with no script.

You are at a hotel front desk, and your bill has two charges that are wrong: a valet fee for a car you never had, and a minibar charge for a minibar you never opened. Speak up, out loud, as if the clerk were in front of you. Open politely, name both problems with the facts, ask for them to be removed, and confirm at the end. Use at least two of today's wrappers: "I was wondering if you could...", "Would you mind...", "There seems to be...", "Could you tell me...". Be firm and kind at the same time. Take your time and do not read from a script.
