Chapter 3: The Post-Mortem

The Memo Nobody Wants to Write

Read it the way the board will read it.

Read for the Main Idea

A Post-Mortem of a Failed Acquisition

Project Helios — Post-Mortem of a Failed Acquisition

Two years ago the company acquired a small payments processor. The valuation looked cheap, and cheap is persuasive. Under pressure to close before the quarter ended, the deal team ran the due diligence in six weeks, half the usual time. The auditor left a short note about an unresolved tax liability. Everyone read it. Nobody stopped.

The financial model rested on a single assumption: that revenue would double within a year. It did not. Within two quarters the burn rate had climbed sharply and the runway had fallen from eighteen months to nine. The tax liability surfaced exactly where the auditor said it would, and the company took a write-off of thirty million dollars.

The lesson is not that the team was careless. It is that nobody in the room was asked to model the downside. If the board had insisted on a downside case, it would have seen the risk. If the CFO had pushed back on the timeline, the deal would have died — and in hindsight, it should have. Arguably the strongest signal was the one that cost nothing to read: a two-line note from an auditor.

Three rules now govern every acquisition. No deal closes without audited numbers. Every proposal carries a modeled downside alongside the base case. And ten percent of the price is held back as a contingency until the first audit clears. The company overpaid once. It does not intend to overpay again.

Chapter 4: The Code

The Grammar of Hindsight

If we had known... we would have...

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"If we had run proper due diligence, we would have seen the risk."

"If the board had modeled the downside, we wouldn't have overpaid."

"We would have kept our runway if we hadn't closed that deal."

"In hindsight, the valuation may have been too optimistic."

The green verbs are in the past perfect. The blue ones are would have + participle. So — did any of it actually happen?

FormUseExample
Third Conditional
If + past perfect, would have + participle		An imaginary past. The condition did NOT happen, so the result did NOT happen either.If we had run diligence, we would have seen it.
Negative
hadn't / wouldn't have		The same imaginary past, the other way round.We wouldn't have overpaid if we hadn't rushed.
Order is freeEither half can come first. Comma only when the if-half opens.We would have kept our runway if we hadn't closed.
Hedging
in hindsight / arguably / may have been		Softens the blame so you stay senior instead of sounding defensive.In hindsight, the valuation may have been optimistic.

Say it out loud as a native does: If we'd run diligence, we'd have seen it. The contraction is not laziness — it is the sound of fluency.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"If we would have known the risk, we didn't buy the company."
"If we had known the risk, we wouldn't have bought the company."
"If I knew about the tax liability, I would not sign the contract."
"If I had known about the tax liability, I wouldn't have signed the contract."
"In the hindsight, we payed too much."
"In hindsight, we overpaid."

The if-half NEVER takes would. It takes the past perfect (had + participle). The other half takes would have + participle. And the expression is in hindsight — no article.

Quick Fire

The Board Asks. You Answer.

1 / 6

"Walk us through what went wrong."

  • We moved too fast on the due diligence.
  • The model rested on one assumption, and it did not hold.
  • State facts first — analysis second.

"What would you have done differently?"

  • If we had taken the full twelve weeks, we would have found the liability.
  • if + past perfect -> would have + participle

"There were red flags. Why did nobody stop?"

  • In hindsight, we underestimated the auditor's note.
  • Hedge, then own it — never blame the team.

"Whose decision was it, in the end?"

  • I signed off on it, so the decision was mine.
  • Own it in one short sentence. Then move to what changes.

"How do we know this will not happen again?"

  • No deal closes without audited numbers.
  • Every proposal now carries a modeled downside and a ten percent contingency.

"In an interview: tell me about a failure."

  • Two years ago I signed off on an acquisition...
  • In hindsight, we moved too fast.
  • Finish with the rule you built from it.
