Chapter 3: The Code

"Could You Upgrade My Room?"

Modal verbs — could, would, should: the small words that make you firm and polite at once.

Grammar Discovery

Firm, but Polite

Could you upgrade my room, please?

Would it be possible to check in early?

You should speak to the manager about it.

I would appreciate it if you could fix the Wi-Fi.

Two of the orange words make a polite request; one gives advice. Which is which?

ModalUse it forExample
could you...?a polite request (softer than can)Could you help me, please?
would it be possible to...?a very polite requestWould it be possible to move me?
you should...advice (a recommendation)You should ask for the manager.
The one ideaA modal softens your words: you sound firm about the problem, but kind to the person — and that is what gets a yes.

Two traps: after a modal use the base verb with no to — you should speak, never "you should to speak"; and use only one modal at a time — "Would you can help me?" becomes Could you help me?

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"You should to speak to the manager."
"You should speak to the manager."
"Would you can help me with the Wi-Fi?"
"Could you help me with the Wi-Fi?"

A modal already carries the meaning, so the next verb stays in its base form — should speak, could help — and you never stack two modals together. One modal, one base verb.

Listening Strategy

Catch the Modal

In fast speech the polite modal almost disappears — "could you" becomes "couldja". Catch it, and you catch the request.

"Could you help me with this?" — "couldja" = a request
"Would you mind moving my room?" — "wouldja mind" = a request
"You should ask for the manager." — "should" = advice
"Would it be possible to check in early?" — a very polite request

Catch the little modal, and ask yourself: is this a request or advice? The rest of the sentence follows.

Quick Fire

Answer at Full Speed

1 / 6

You arrive and your room is not ready yet. Ask the front desk to hold your bags -- politely.

  • Could you hold my bags for an hour while I wait? Thank you.
  • 'Could you...?' is the soft, polite request -- warmer and more natural than 'Can you...?' with a stranger who is helping you.

Your confirmation says superior room, but they gave you a standard. Point out the discrepancy and ask them to fix it.

  • There seems to be a discrepancy -- I booked a superior room, so would it be possible to move me?
  • 'There seems to be a discrepancy' is firm but not aggressive -- you name the problem without blaming the person in front of you.

The Wi-Fi code on your card does not work. Report it and ask them to resolve it.

  • The Wi-Fi code isn't working -- could someone help me resolve it?
  • You report the fact, then make a polite request with 'could' -- that combination gets things done at any front desk.

A friend at the hotel is unhappy with their room but too shy to say anything. Give them advice.

  • You should speak to the front desk -- they can't fix a problem they don't know about.
  • 'You should...' is the natural way to give advice -- no 'to' after it: 'you should speak', never 'you should to speak'.

There is a charge on your bill you do not recognize. Ask about it without accusing anyone.

  • I'm sorry, there's a charge here I don't recognize -- would you mind checking it for me?
  • 'Would you mind checking...?' keeps it light -- you assume it's an honest mistake, which makes staff want to help.

The front desk cannot help and you need a decision from someone higher up. Ask to escalate -- politely.

  • I understand it's not your decision -- could I speak with the duty manager, please?
  • You escalate without attacking the person: acknowledge their limit ('it's not your decision'), then ask calmly for the manager.
