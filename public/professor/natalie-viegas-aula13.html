Chapter 3 -- The Code

The Second Conditional

The grammar of imaginary deals

Discovery

What's the Pattern?

What do the orange words have in common?

"If they asked for less, we would offer a longer contract."

"If I lost my patience, I would lose the deal."

"If we found common ground, the price would feel fair."

"If I were you, I would make a counteroffer."

The Rule

If + Past, Would + Verb

If-clause (condition)Result clause
If + past simplewould + base verb
"If they asked for less,""we would offer more time."
"If I were you,""I would make a counteroffer."
"If we lowered the price,""we would lose our margin."

Key idea: the second conditional is for imaginary or hypothetical situations. Compare: "If they ask, we will offer" (first conditional = a real future possibility) vs "If they asked, we would offer" (second conditional = imaginary, polite, softer). After "if", use were for all subjects: "If I were you."

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"If they would ask for less, we would offer more time."
"If they asked for less, we would offer more time."

Never use would after if. The if-clause takes the past simple (asked, lowered, found); only the result clause uses would + base verb.

Grammar Practice

Complete the Conditional

"If they asked for a discount, we _____would offer a longer contract." (would + verb)
"If we _____lowered the price, we would lose our margin." (if + past)
"If I _____were you, I would make a counteroffer." (if + were)
"If we reached a deadlock, I _____would suggest a short break." (would + verb)
