to control for

to skew

to pull a distribution to one side, usually through an outlier or a bad sample

Reading

Read for the Main Idea

What a Number Cannot Say on Its Own

Every People and Culture leader eventually stands in front of a chart with two lines that move together, and feels the pull to say the sentence that will ruin the presentation: this caused that. Engagement went up, turnover went down, the mentorship program launched in between, and the story writes itself. The problem is that the story is data storytelling in the worst sense of the phrase. Two numbers moving together is a correlation, and a correlation is not yet a reason. Causation is one thing actually producing another, and a line on a slide can hint at it but never prove it.

The gap between the two is almost always a confounding variable, a hidden third factor quietly driving both numbers while you credit your own program. In this case the reorganization landed in exactly the same quarter as the mentorship launch, and it is thought to have shifted the workload of half the teams. Either event could be moving engagement, and no chart can separate them for you. The only honest move is to name the variable you could not control for out loud, before anyone in the room does it for you. It is believed, across the field, that the fastest way to lose a research audience is to skew a story toward the explanation that flatters you.

This is where the discipline lives. A single outlier, one team that tripled its scores after a change of manager, can drag an average far enough to look like a trend, so you check the sample size and you keep the margin of error on the slide even though it makes the number look smaller. Statistical significance is reported to matter less to executives than it should, precisely because it is the boring test that separates a real result from the noise of a small sample. You do not drop it to look confident. You keep it to look like someone who has done this before.

So what is left to say, once you have refused to overclaim? More than it seems. You can still tease out the one signal that survives every rival explanation, and you can call it what it is, a leading indicator rather than a proof, a thing that tends to move before the outcome does. And then you connect the dots into a single actionable insight, the sentence a manager can act on this Monday and not merely admire on a slide. The number never speaks for itself. Your job is not to make it say more than it can. Your job is to make it say the one true thing it can, clearly enough that the room can act on it.

Field note, People & Culture -- adapted for Maple Bear Canada

Chapter 4: The Code

State It Without Owning It

It is estimated that turnover rose... · Turnover is thought to be linked to onboarding

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

It is estimated that turnover among new managers rose by twelve percent last year.

Turnover is thought to be closely linked to how onboarding is run.

The drop is believed to reflect the reorganization, though it cannot be proven.

Retention is reported to have improved since the mentorship program began.

In none of these sentences does the speaker claim the fact as their own. In all of them, the claim is attributed to the evidence — which is exactly the register a researcher will trust.

StructureFormWhat it does
Impersonal reportIt is + estimated/reported/thought + that + clause“It is estimated that turnover rose.” Note: that + a full clause with a finite verb (rose), never “to rise”.
Personal reportsubject + is thought/believed to + base verb“Turnover is thought to be linked to onboarding.” After “to” comes the base verb — be, reflect, drive.
Perfect reportis + reported to have + past participle“Retention is reported to have improved.” The result already happened, in the past.
Data comparatives“New joiners are twice as likely as tenured staff to leave” · “a threefold increase” · “significantly higher than the benchmark”. Compare precisely, without overclaiming size.
Why it sounds senior“Onboarding causes turnover” claims causation as fact — and a researcher will destroy it. The reporting passive states the same finding while conceding it is a reading of evidence. In that room, the concession IS your credibility.

Pair it with the honest caveat and it becomes a claim a researcher can trust: “The drop is believed to be linked to the reorganization, which I could not fully control for — so I am reporting it as a leading indicator, not a cause.”

Register

Claim It, or Report It

Direct claim — you own the causation

Sounds confident, and a researcher can knock it down in one question about the confounding variable.

“The mentorship program raised engagement.”

Reporting passive — you attribute it to evidence

Says the same finding while conceding it is a reading, not a proof — so there is nothing to knock down.

“Engagement is thought to be linked to the mentorship program.”

Same finding, two futures. The first invites a fight you will lose. The second invites the researcher to help you find what the number actually means — which is the sentence Prescott repeats to the council when you are not in the room.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"It is estimated turnover to rise twelve percent last year."
"It is estimated that turnover rose twelve percent last year."
"The data proves the new manager caused the drop in scores."
"The drop in scores is thought to be linked to the change in management."

The first slip is Portuguese arriving on time: the impersonal report needs that + a finite clause — “is estimated that turnover rose”, never “estimated turnover to rise”. The second is the one that ends presentations: a correlation reported as a cause. Report the link; never claim it.

Quick Fire

Answer Like the Room Reads Data for a Living

1 / 6

"Your two lines move together. So the mentorship program raised engagement. Correct?"

  • It is tempting to read it that way, but the reorganization landed in the same quarter, so I would call this a correlation I cannot yet attribute. What I can defend is a leading indicator, not a cause.
  • concede the correlation, name the confounding variable yourself, then downgrade the claim to what the data can carry

"One team tripled its score. That is your evidence?"

  • That team is an outlier, and on a sample this size it skews the average more than it should. Drop it and the effect shrinks but survives, which is the version I would ask you to act on.
  • identify the outlier, admit it skews the mean, then show the signal that survives without it

"You did not control for tenure. New joiners always score differently."

  • You are right, and I should have said so first. Once we control for tenure, the gap narrows but does not close, and it is that residual gap I am reporting, not the raw one.
  • concede the missing control before defending, then report the residual effect, not the inflated one

"Is this statistically significant, or is it the noise of a small sample?"

  • At this sample size the margin of error is wide, and I would not present it as significant. It is thought to be a real signal, but I am flagging it as directional and asking for a second read in March.
  • state the margin honestly, refuse the word significant if it is not earned, and ask for the follow-up

"So what am I actually supposed to do with this on Monday?"

  • One thing: treat the onboarding window as a leading indicator and watch it monthly, rather than waiting for turnover to confirm the problem a quarter too late. That is the actionable insight, and it does not depend on the causal claim.
  • the close: hand over one action that holds even if the causation never does

"Every consultant I meet reads too much into a chart. Convince me you do not."

  • By telling you what this number cannot say before I tell you what it can. It is a correlation, the sample is small, and I could not fully control for the reorganization. What survives all of that is the one thing I am asking you to act on.
  • lead with the limits, not the conclusion -- the honesty is the argument
