Metodologia

1. Da Leitura à Fala

A Ana já lê inglês técnico. Cada aula converte esse conhecimento passivo em produção oral ativa, com muita fala e repetição em contexto real.

2. Contexto Real de Tech & QA

Todo conteúdo usa o mundo dela: testes, bugs, releases, reuniões remotas, entrevistas. Ela pratica exatamente as conversas que vai ter de verdade.

3. Listening Sob Pressão

Exposição gradual a sotaques variados e fala rápida, treinando a captar o CONTEXTO geral sem travar em cada palavra — a habilidade que mais a bloqueia.

4. Disciplina e Estrutura

A Ana prefere um professor com certa rigidez e gosta de anotar. As aulas têm estrutura clara, metas por bloco e espaço para registro no caderno.

5. Correção Adiada e Diagnóstica

Na conversa, fluência primeiro; correção depois. O professor anota padrões (perfect tenses, condicionais) e devolve feedback focado, sem interromper a produção.

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Ana lives in a small town in the countryside, about forty thousand people. She grew up in a busy neighborhood in Sao Paulo, but a few years ago she moved away because she wants a quieter life. Her street is so peaceful that she can hear the birds in the morning. Every day, her two dogs wake her up at six, and every afternoon they sleep in the backyard. Both of them were strays before she found them, and now she takes care of them like family. On Saturdays she does her chores in the morning and then she rests. Ana is very handy: this month she is restoring an old wooden cabinet, and right now she is painting the kitchen wall, because she wants the house to feel cozy. Her closest neighbor lives two hundred meters away, and she likes it that way.

1. Why do we say "she is restoring an old cabinet" and not "she restores"?
A It is in progress these days, not finished (present continuous).
B It is something she does every single day.
C It happened once and it is completely finished.
2. "Her dogs sleep in the backyard every afternoon" uses the present simple because:
A It is happening at this exact moment.
B It is a routine that repeats every day.
C It is a temporary situation, just for this month.
3. The text says "she wants a quieter life", never "she is wanting". Why?
A Because the sentence is about the past.
B Because "want" is an irregular verb.
C Because "want" is a state verb, and state verbs do not take -ing.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Simple vs Present Continuous

GRAMMAR

How to say what is always true and what is happening now.

FormUseExample
Present Simple
I / you / we + verb (he / she + -s)		Facts, routines and permanent situations - always true.I live in the countryside.
Present Continuous
am / is / are + verb-ing		Right now, today, these days - temporary or unfinished.I am restoring a cabinet.
Negativedo not / does not + verb · am not / is not / are not + -ingI do not drive to work. / She is not sleeping.
QuestionDo / Does + subject + verb · Am / Is / Are + subject + -ingDo you live here? / Are you working now?
State verbslike, love, want, need, know, believe, understand almost never take -ing: "I like this town" (not "I am liking").
Time wordsevery day, always, usually, never → simple · right now, today, this week, these days → continuous

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Past Simple vs Past Continuous

GRAMMAR

How to tell a story: the events, and the scene around them.

FormUseExample
Past Simple
verb + -ed (or irregular)		The events of the story, one after another. Finished.I decided to leave. We sold the flat.
Past Continuous
was / were + verb-ing		The scene: something already in progress around the event.I was sitting in traffic. It was raining.
Negativedid not + verb · was not / were not + verb-ingI did not know. They were not listening.
QuestionDid + subject + verb · Was / Were + subject + verb-ingDid you grow up here? / Were you living alone?
while vs whenwhile + the long scene · when + the short event: "While I was driving, the phone rang."
State verbshate, love, know, want, believe stay simple in the past too: "I knew everyone" (not "I was knowing").

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

People often ask Ana which life is better, and she never gives a simple answer. Sao Paulo was far more convenient. The public transport was better than anything she has now, and there was a much wider range of restaurants, cinemas and nightlife. But it was also the most stressful period of her life. Today her town is much quieter, the pace of life is slower, and the cost of living is less than half. The air is cleaner, there is almost no pollution, and a house with a backyard is affordable here in a way it never was there. She feels safer too: she walks home at night, which she never did before. It is not perfect. The facilities are worse, the hospital is smaller, and some people would call the town isolated. But for her, it is the most peaceful place she has ever lived, and that is the best answer she has.

1. Why is it "the most stressful period" and not "the more stressful period"?
A Because she is comparing it with all the other periods of her life, not just one.
B Because "stressful" is an irregular adjective.
C Because the sentence is in the past.
2. The text says "much quieter", not "very quieter". Why?
A Because "quiet" is a long adjective.
B Because comparatives take much / far / a lot to get stronger, never "very".
C Because "very" cannot be used in the present tense.
3. Which sentence about the facilities is correct?
A "The facilities are more bad than in the city."
B "The facilities are worse than in the city."
C "The facilities are the most worst than in the city."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Comparatives and Superlatives

GRAMMAR

How to put two things side by side, and how to crown a winner.

FormUseExample
Short adjective + -er ... thanTwo things. One or two syllables.quiet → quieter than
more + long adjective ... thanTwo things. Three syllables or more.convenient → more convenient than
the + adjective + -est
the most + long adjective		The winner out of all of them.the quietest / the most peaceful
Irregularsgood → better / the best · bad → worse / the worst · far → further / the furthest
Stronger / softermuch, far, a lot + comparative (much cheaper) · a bit, slightly + comparative (a bit smaller). Never "very cheaper".
Spellingbig → bigger (double the consonant) · easy → easier (y becomes i)
