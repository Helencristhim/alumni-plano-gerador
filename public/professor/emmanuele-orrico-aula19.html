Chapter 3: How Much, How Many

Count It, or Not?

many patients · much access · a lot of demand

Grammar Discovery

How Much, How Many?

"We have many patients." (you can count patients)

"There is a lot of demand." (you cannot count demand)

"Only a few hospitals are ready." (a small number you can count)

"We have a little time." (a small amount you cannot count)

Can you count it? → MANY / A FEW. Cannot count it? → MUCH / A LITTLE. Both? → A LOT OF.

WordUse withExample
many / a fewcountable (patients, hospitals)many patients · a few hospitals
much / a littleuncountable (access, time)not much time · a little access
a lot ofbotha lot of patients · a lot of demand

Ask How many? for countable, How much? for uncountable. Use much mostly in questions and negatives.

The Form

Countable · Uncountable

countable (how many?)We have many patients. Only a few hospitals are ready.
uncountable (how much?)We don't have much time. We have a little access.
bothThere is a lot of demand. We have a lot of patients.

First ask: can I count it? Then the word comes by itself — many for countable, much for uncountable.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"We have much patients."
"We have many patients."
"There are many demand."
"There is a lot of demand."
"We have a few time."
"We have a little time."

Use many / a few with countable nouns ("many patients", "a few hospitals"). Use much / a little with uncountable nouns ("a little time"). And a lot of works with both.
