The Present We Built

The Present We Built

Eliane rarely talks about luck. When she looks at the clinic today, she sees the result of a handful of decisions that were far more formative than they seemed at the time. One of them was pivotal: years ago, against the advice of nearly everyone, she turned down a secure hospital post to specialize in reproductive medicine. If she had taken that safe job, she would not be running her own clinic now. That refusal became the defining moment of her career -- the cornerstone the whole practice was later built on.

Small choices compounded. A decision to invest early in one difficult procedure had a ripple effect she could not have predicted: it attracted the exact colleagues she needed, and their arrival had a knock-on effect on everything from research to reputation. Not every decision was right the first time. There was a hard-won course correction in the third year, when she quietly closed a service that was draining the team. If she weren't the kind of person who admits mistakes early, she would have carried that failure for much longer.

What Eliane understands now is that the present is always built out of the past. If she hadn't played the long game -- choosing slow, durable growth over quick wins -- the clinic would not have the reputation it enjoys today. In a way, her story has come full circle: the young doctor who once felt she had nothing to offer now trains the residents who will carry the work forward. That, she says, is the real legacy -- not the building, but the decisions that still shape everyone who walks through the door. If those early choices had been different, everything standing here would be different too.

Adapted from a founder's essay on how the present is built, The Decisions Behind Us https://www.decisionsbehindus.example.com

Chapter 4: Mixed Conditionals

If I had taken it, I would not be here...

the grammar that connects two times

Discovery

Listen and Notice

"If I had taken the safe job, I would not be here now."

"If we hadn't played the long game, we would not have this reputation today."

"If she weren't so honest, she would have hidden the mistake."

"If those choices had been different, everything would be different now."

"If I didn't believe in slow growth, I would never have turned it down."

TypeIf-clause (condition)Main clause (result)
Past → PresentIf + past perfect (had taken)would + base verb (would not be) — a past condition, a result now
Present → PastIf + past simple / were (weren't)would have + past participle (would have hidden) — a present condition, a past result

Mixed conditionals join two different times, so the two halves use different tenses. Never put "would" in the if-clause: say "If I had known", not "If I would have known".

Practice

Complete the Sentence

Say it out loud, then tap to check

"If I had taken the safe job, I ___would not be here now." (present result — would + base)
"If we hadn't played the long game, we ___would not have this reputation today." (present result)
"If she weren't so honest, she ___would have hidden the mistake." (past result — would have + participle)
"If those choices ___had been different, everything would be different now." (past condition — past perfect)
"If I ___didn't believe in slow growth, I would never have turned it down." (present condition)
Language Focus

Name the Half

AnalyseMixed conditionals: name each half
AIf I had taken the safe job, I would not be here now.
BIf I had taken the safe job, I would not be here now.
CIf she weren't so honest, she would have hidden the mistake.
DIf she weren't so honest, she would have hidden the mistake.
EIf those choices had been different, everything would be different now.
These are mixed conditionals -- they connect two different times. A + B is past to present: an unreal PAST condition (if + past perfect: had taken) with a PRESENT result (would + base verb: would not be). C + D is present to past: an unreal PRESENT condition (if + past simple / were: weren't) with a PAST result (would have + past participle: would have hidden). The two halves point to different times, so the tenses do not match -- and there is never a 'would' in the if-clause.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

If I would have listened to them, I would be somewhere else now.
If I had listened to them, I would be somewhere else now.
If we had started earlier, we would have been bigger today.
If we had started earlier, we would be bigger today.
If she didn't care so much, she wouldn't build the clinic back then.
If she didn't care so much, she would never have built the clinic back then.

A mixed conditional joins two different times. Never use "would" in the if-clause -- use the past perfect (had listened). For a PRESENT result, use would + base verb (would be), not would have. For a PAST result of a present condition, use would have + past participle (would have built).

Chapter 6: Your Turn

Trace Your Decisions

Four rounds — say each one out loud

Round 1 -- The decision that still shapes you

Tell one formative, pivotal decision from your past and how it shapes your present. Use a past-to-present mixed conditional: If I had (not) ... , I would (not) ... now.

Round 2 -- Who you are, and what it changed

Describe a trait or value you have now and how it changed a past outcome. Use a present-to-past mixed conditional: If I weren't / didn't ... , I would (never) have ... .

Round 3 -- Ripple effects

Choose one choice and trace its ripple effect and knock-on effect on everything that followed. Name the cornerstone or defining moment.

Round 4 -- Full circle

Put it together in one minute: the formative choice, the hard-won course correction, the long game, and how the work has come full circle into your legacy.

Model Language

+Reveal the model language
Notice the two mixed patterns: past to present (If I had taken ... I would not be) and present to past (If I weren't ... I would have carried). The tenses in the two halves point to different times, and there is never a 'would' in the if-clause.

  3. Ripple effects: 'Investing in one difficult procedure was the cornerstone; it had a ripple effect that attracted the colleagues I needed, and a knock-on effect on our whole reputation.'
