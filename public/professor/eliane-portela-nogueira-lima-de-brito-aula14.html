Chapter 3: After the Pitch

The Room After the Pitch

Read for the Main Idea

What the Q&A Really Tests

What the Q&A Really Tests

The pitch ends and the real test begins: the questions. Every founder eventually learns that investors do not write checks because the slides were beautiful; they write them because the founder held up under pressure. When a room starts to grill you, each question is a small stress test. Some are genuine; others are designed to see whether you deflect, whether you hedge, or whether you can concede a fair point without losing the room. Eliane left her last pitch replaying every answer. If she had anticipated the hardest question, she told herself, she would have answered it in one calm sentence instead of three nervous ones.

The most common mistake under scrutiny is to treat every objection as an attack. A sharp investor is not trying to humiliate you; a good objection is almost a gift, because it shows you exactly where the red flag is. Founders who reassure a room do not pretend the risk does not exist. They name the worst-case scenario before the investor does, and they explain the contingency. Eliane still remembers one meeting that fell apart over a single number. If the team had shown a worst-case scenario in the first place, she now believes, the investors would not have pushed back so hard, and the follow-up questions would have felt like curiosity instead of doubt.

Learning to field hard questions is mostly learning in hindsight. After every Q&A, the strongest founders run a quiet post-mortem: which answer landed, which one they hedged, where they should have conceded and where they were right to hold firm. If I had deflected less and reassured more, one founder wrote, they would have trusted the plan sooner. None of this means having a perfect answer for everything. It means anticipating the pushback, welcoming the objection, and knowing that the room is not really testing your slides. It is testing whether, under real pressure, you are someone an investor would have backed.

Chapter 4: The Third Conditional

If we had known...

the grammar of hindsight and regret

Discovery

Listen and Notice

"If she had anticipated the question, she would have stayed calm."

"If we had shown the risk, they would not have pushed back."

"If I had conceded the point, the meeting would have moved on."

"They would have invested if the plan had addressed the red flag."

"Had we prepared for the follow-up, we could have closed the round."

PartFormExample
If-clause (condition)If + had + past participleIf we had shown the risk...
Result clausewould / could / might + have + past participle...they would not have pushed back.
Meaningan imagined past — it did NOT happenhindsight · regret
Inversion (formal)Had + subject + past participleHad we prepared, we could have...

The third conditional talks about an unreal past: what would have been true if the past had been different. Use it to learn from a Q&A that already happened.

Practice

Complete the Sentence

"If I ___had conceded the point, the meeting would have moved on." (if + past perfect)
"___Had we prepared for the follow-up, we could have closed the round." (inversion, no if)
Language Focus

Spot the Third Conditional

AnalyseThird conditional: spot the two halves
AIf she had anticipated the question, she would have stayed calm.
BThey would have invested if the plan had addressed the red flag.
CIf we had shown the risk, they would not have pushed back.
DHad we prepared for the follow-up, we could have closed the round.
EIf I had deflected less, they would have trusted me sooner.
A, B, C and E all follow one pattern: If + past perfect (had anticipated / had shown / had deflected) in the condition, and would / could / might + have + past participle in the result. This is the third conditional: an imagined past that did not actually happen. It is the grammar of hindsight and regret -- what would have been true if the past had been different. D shows the formal inversion: Had we prepared... means the same as If we had prepared... but drops the if.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

If I would have known, I would have prepared.
If I had known, I would have prepared.
If we had shown the risk, they would not pushed back.
If we had shown the risk, they would not have pushed back.
If she anticipated the question, she would have answered.
If she had anticipated the question, she would have answered.

The third conditional needs "had + past participle" in the if-clause and "would have + past participle" in the result. Never use "would have" after if, and never drop the "have" in the result.

Chapter 6: Your Turn

Handle the Q&A

Four rounds — say each one out loud

Round 1 -- The replay

Think of your last tough Q&A. In hindsight, say three things you would have done differently, using the third conditional (If + had + past participle, ... would have ...).

Round 2 -- The red flag

An investor names a risk you did not mention. Respond well: concede the fair point, name the worst-case scenario, and reassure the room with your contingency.

Round 3 -- Welcome the grilling

Role-play the moment the room starts to grill you. Show that you welcome the pushback: turn each objection into a follow-up that makes your case stronger -- without deflecting or hedging.

Round 4 -- The post-mortem

Run a one-minute post-mortem out loud: which answers landed, where you hedged, and what you would have changed. Then anticipate the questions you will face next time.

Check Your Work

Model Language

Try the rounds first. Reveal the key only to compare.

+Reveal the model language
Notice how each answer rides on the third conditional -- If + had + past participle, would have + past participle -- to turn a past mistake into a lesson, and how the pressure words (objection, red flag, pushback, reassure) frame the Q&A as a conversation, not an attack.
  1. The replay: 'If I had anticipated the question about year one, I would have named the worst-case scenario myself. If I had hedged less, they would have trusted the plan sooner.'
  2. The red flag: 'You are right, and I concede the point. The worst-case scenario is a slow first year, so we built a contingency for it -- let me reassure you with the numbers.'
  3. Welcome the grilling: 'Grill me on this. A sharp objection is a gift; it shows me the red flag. I would rather face the pushback now than discover it after we sign.'
  4. The post-mortem: 'The value proposition landed. I hedged on the numbers. If we had shown the contingency in the deck, they would not have pushed back so hard. Next time I anticipate that follow-up.'
