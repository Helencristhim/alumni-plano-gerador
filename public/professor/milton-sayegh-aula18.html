Chapter 3

The Emphasis Code

Point the spotlight at what matters

Discovery

Spot the Pattern

Each line takes a plain fact and points a spotlight at one idea. What do the orange words do?

Plain: "Our margin sets us apart." → What sets us apart is our margin.

Plain: "The bottleneck is holding us back." → It is the bottleneck that is holding us back.

Plain: "We need capacity now." → What we need now is capacity.

These are emphasis (cleft) sentences. You split one plain sentence into two parts to push one idea into the spotlight:

What + clause + is / are + the key idea — What sets us apart is the margin.

It is / It's + the key idea + that + clause — It is the bottleneck that holds us back.

Use them sparingly, at the moments that matter most: your edge, your proof, your ask.

The Structure

Two Ways to Emphasize

PatternFrameExample
What-cleftWhat + clause + is/are + focusWhat sets us apart is the margin.
It-cleftIt is + focus + that + clauseIt is the bottleneck that holds us back.
What we needWhat we need is + focusWhat we need is capacity.
The thing isThe thing that matters is + focusThe thing that matters is the return.
Watch Out

Common Mistake

What sets us apart the margin.
What sets us apart is the margin.

Never drop the linking verb: an emphasis sentence always needs is or are before the focus. And in the "It is..." pattern, the connector is that, not which, when you point at a person or a fact.

Practice

Add the Spotlight

Plain: "Our craftsmanship makes the difference." → What makes the difference is our craftsmanship.
Plain: "The repeat clients prove the traction." → It is the repeat clients that prove the traction.
Plain: "We need capacity, not more demand." → What we need is capacity, not more demand.
Plain: "The valuation reflects our margin." → It is our margin that the valuation reflects.
Chapter 4

In the Room

Milton pitches Diana, the investor

Dialogue

Milton & Diana

D
Karen tells me you export luxury jewelry and you're growing fast. I see a lot of pitches, so be direct with me. Why should I be your investor?
M
Happily. What sets us apart is the margin. It is our craftsmanship and our story that let us price above the market and still sell out. Last year revenue grew forty percent.
D
Forty percent is nice, but handmade rarely scales. How is this actually scalable?
M
Fair question. It is the bottleneck, not demand, that holds us back. We're raising one and a half million for a fifteen percent stake, at a valuation of ten million, and the money goes straight into capacity.
D
And the proof that this isn't just a good year?
M
More than half of our buyers came back to order again. That repeat traction is the real proof. For a backer who understands premium brands, the return is clear. I'd love to walk you through the numbers.
Did You Catch It?

About the Pitch

1. What is Diana's main doubt about the business?
That a handmade, luxury product rarely scales — she pushes Milton on how it can actually grow.
2. What exactly is Milton asking for?
One and a half million for a fifteen percent stake, at a valuation of ten million — with the money going into capacity.
3. What proof does Milton give that the growth is real?
More than half of his buyers came back to order again — that repeat traction is his real proof.

Spot the Error

Fix the Line

"What sets us apart the craftsmanship."
"What sets us apart is the craftsmanship." (emphasis needs the linking verb is)
"It is the bottleneck which holds us back."
"It is the bottleneck that holds us back." (use that, not which, in this cleft)
"We are raising for a fifteen percent stake one million."
"We're raising one million for a fifteen percent stake." (amount first, then the stake)
"What we need are capacity."
"What we need is capacity." (the verb agrees with the singular focus)
