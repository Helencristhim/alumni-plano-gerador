Done Through Others

Done Through Others

For the first few years, Eliane did everything herself. She had every chart reviewed personally, got every appointment confirmed in her own hand, and signed off on the smallest purchase. She told herself this was care; in truth, she had become the bottleneck. Nothing moved unless it moved through her. She was proud of how much she carried, and quietly exhausted. The clinic could not grow past the size of one very tired founder, because she refused to delegate a single decision she believed only she could make.

The shift came from an executive coach named Oliver, who asked her one hard question: what would happen to the clinic if she took a month off? She had no answer. Over the next year he taught her to lead a different way. Instead of doing the work, she learned to have it done: she had the billing system rebuilt by an outside firm, got the new doctors properly onboarded, and had her deputy run the morning rounds. She stopped trying to micromanage every case and started to empower the people around her. She got her team to take ownership, made each lead accountable for real outcomes, and learned to step back so that others could step up.

It was not comfortable. To entrust a decision to someone else, she first had to accept that they might do it differently -- and sometimes better. She had the worst of the red tape cut, outsourced what did not need her, and slowly freed up the bandwidth for the one thing no one else could do: the science, and the vision. Today the clinic runs whether she is in the room or not. When residents ask her what leadership is, she no longer talks about working harder. She says the real work of a leader is done through others -- and that is the only way anything you build outlasts you.

Chapter 4: The Causative

I had it done...

the grammar of getting things done through others

Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I had the clinic accredited last year."

"We got the system rebuilt by an outside firm."

"I had my deputy run the morning rounds."

"I got my team to take ownership of their cases."

"She had her proposal turned down twice before it passed."

UseStructureExample
Arrange a service (thing)have / get + object + past participleI had the clinic accredited. We got the system rebuilt.
Direct a person (have)have + person + base verb (no "to")I had my deputy run the rounds.
Persuade a person (get)get + person + to + infinitiveI got my team to take ownership.

The causative says someone else does the action for you. For a thing, put the object BEFORE the past participle: "I had the report translated", not "I had translated the report". With "have + person", use the base verb and no "to"; with "get + person", use "to".

Language Focus

Name the Pattern

AnalyseThe causative: name the pattern
AI had the clinic accredited last year.
BWe got the system rebuilt by an outside firm.
CI had my deputy run the morning rounds.
DI got my team to take ownership of their cases.
EShe had her proposal turned down twice before it passed.
These are causatives -- you arrange for someone else to do something. A + B use a thing: have or get + object + past participle (had the clinic accredited / got the system rebuilt). C + D use a person: have + person + base verb, with no 'to' (had my deputy run), but get + person + to + infinitive (got my team to take). E shows the same have + object + past participle used for something that happened to you (had her proposal turned down). The subject arranges or receives the action; someone else actually does it.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

I had translated the report by the agency.
I had the report translated by the agency.
I had my assistant to book the room.
I had my assistant book the room.
I got the team take ownership of their cases.
I got the team to take ownership of their cases.

For a thing, put the object BEFORE the past participle: "had the report translated", not "had translated the report". With have + person, use the base verb and no "to" (had my assistant book). With get + person, you need "to" (got the team to take).
