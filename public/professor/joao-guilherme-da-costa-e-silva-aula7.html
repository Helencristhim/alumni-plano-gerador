The Distinction

Float vs Critical Path

There is float

"The cable pulling has ten days of float, so a five-day slip is absorbed. The end date does not move."

A delay eats the spare time. Annoying, not fatal — and the supplier is right.

It is on the critical path

"Signal installation is on the critical path. The float is zero. Six weeks late here is six weeks late for the line."

Nothing absorbs it. Every day lost is a day the passenger waits.

"We will absorb it" is a technical claim, not a reassurance. Ask one question back: "Absorb it into what float?" If the answer is silence, the activity is on the critical path.

Chapter 3: The Code

The Record Speaks Louder

"You are late."  →  "On 3 June you assured us that the relay would ship on the 15th."

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

They said: "The relay will ship on the 15th."
On 3 June you assured us that the relay would ship on the 15th.

They said: "The installation is on track."
In the last review, your team told us that the installation was on track.

They said: "The cable has been dispatched."
Your planner claimed that the cable had been dispatched.

They said: "We will submit a recovery schedule."
Your company undertook to submit a recovery schedule within ten days.

Nobody is accused. Nothing is denied. Every verb simply took one step back in time — and the sentence stopped being your opinion and became the record.

They saidYou reportIn the meeting
will → wouldThe promise moves one step back, and the promise is now on the record."You assured us the relay would ship on the 15th."
is / are → was / werePresent becomes past: what was true for them, at that moment."Your team told us the installation was on track."
has been → had beenPerfect becomes past perfect. This is the one that dates a claim — and dating it is how you check it."Your planner claimed the cable had been dispatched."
can → could · must → had toModals step back too. "Must" is the exception: it becomes "had to"."They said they could recover two weeks, and that we had to wait."
say vs telltell needs a person; say does not. "He told us", "he said that" — never "he said us"."He told us that..." / "He said that..."
The verb chooses the weightsaid (neutral) · claimed (you doubt it) · assured us (they committed, with confidence) · acknowledged / conceded (they admitted it) · denied (they refused it)."They assured us" binds them. "They said" does not.
Time and place move toonow → then · today → that day · yesterday → the day before · next week → the following week. In a review meeting, replace them with the real date: "on 3 June" beats "the week before" every single time.
Meaning

Four Verbs, Four Weights

The verb you choose decides how much it binds them
said (that)...
Neutral. It happened, that is all

You are recording, not pressing. Nobody is bound by it. "They said that the cable was on its way."

told us (that)...
Neutral, but it names the audience

TELL always takes the person: told US, told ME. It puts the room in the sentence, so it cannot be denied privately. "Your team told us that the installation was on track."

assured us (that)...
A commitment, given with confidence

This is the one you use when you intend to hold them to it. To assure is to remove somebody's doubt on purpose. "On 3 June you assured us that the relay would ship on the fifteenth."

undertook to / committed to + verb
A formal obligation

The strongest of the four, and the only one with a to-infinitive. It is contract language: they took the duty on. "Your company undertook to submit a recovery schedule within ten days."

Three more you will need in the minutes: they CLAIMED it (you are signalling that you doubt it), they ACKNOWLEDGED or CONCEDED it (they admitted something against their own interest -- write this one down, it is gold), they DENIED it (they refused it outright). Choose the verb before you choose the tone.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"He said me that the relay will ship on Friday."
"He told me that the relay would ship on Friday."
"They assured us to send the recovery schedule within ten days."
"They assured us that they would send the recovery schedule, and they undertook to submit it within ten days."

Portuguese lets you say ele me disse and lets the tense stay where it is. English does neither: tell takes the person, say does not — and if the verb does not step back, you are no longer quoting them. You are promising it yourself.

Comprehension

About Elena

1. How does Elena open the meeting, and why?
With the good news: the team is working weekends and progress is being made. She puts the effort on the table before anybody puts the number on it.
2. Who does she say is responsible for the delay?
Motiva, in part: she says the interface drawings arrived late from João's side and her team could not start.
3. She denies promising the fifteenth. What ends that argument?
Not a louder voice: the minutes. She assured them on 3 June, the minutes were circulated the same afternoon, and her company undertook on 12 June to send a recovery schedule. Against a dated record, there is nothing to argue with.
4. What does she do once the record is read out?
She concedes: "That is fair. The schedule was not sent, and I should have called you." Then she asks what he needs — which is where the negotiation actually begins.
Listening

The Night Before the Review

Elena leaves a message. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Why is she calling the night before, and not in the meeting?
Because she wants him to hear the real reason from her, privately, before it is said in front of her director: a subcontractor lost the cable batch and she did not escalate it for three weeks.
2. What does she acknowledge, and what does she still not accept?
She acknowledges that the recovery schedule was never sent and that she should have called. She does not accept that the whole variance is hers: the interface drawings were eight days late.
3. What does she ask for — and what is the price she offers?
She asks him not to take it to the steering committee in April. In exchange she offers a recovery schedule by Friday, a second crew at her company's cost, and a weekly call.
Listening 2

Three Rules Before You Chair It

A planning director, twenty years of reviews. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does he say a review meeting is actually for?
Not to establish who is guilty. To establish what is true, what has changed, and who does what by when. Blame is a by-product; the date is the product.
2. Why must you never agree a new date before you know the cause?
Because a date agreed without a cause is a guess, and it will slip a second time — on your schedule and at your cost. Ask what happened, then ask for the plan, and only then talk about dates.
3. What does he say about float?
The float belongs to the project, not to the supplier. The moment you let somebody consume it to cover their own delay, you have paid for their mistake with time you will need later.

Quick Fire

Answer From the Record

1 / 6

"So what is the actual problem here?" Open the review with the number. No names, no adjectives.

  • The schedule variance on the installation is minus thirty working days.
  • The activity is on the critical path, so the float is zero and nothing absorbs it.

"I never promised you the fifteenth. I said we would do our best." Answer from the record.

  • On 3 June you assured us that the relay would ship on the fifteenth, and the minutes were circulated the same afternoon.
  • That is why I am reading from the minutes, and not from memory.

"The drawings arrived late from your side." She is partly right. Concede, then hold.

  • To some extent, both parties share responsibility: the drawings were issued eight days late.
  • The slippage we are discussing, however, is thirty days.

"Give us two weeks of float from the testing activity and we recover everything." Refuse it.

  • I am afraid the float belongs to the project, not to the package that is late.
  • If I hand it over now, I have nothing left when something unplanned happens later.

"What do you need from us today?" Three things, each with a date.

  • A recovery schedule by Friday, showing how the thirty days are won back.
  • A contingency plan if the retest fails, and a weekly call so the next slip reaches me in three days.

"Can we keep this between us, and not escalate?" Say yes on your terms, or no without closing the door.

  • If we can reach a consensus today, this does not have to go to the steering committee in April.
  • Without a recovery schedule, I have no position to defend, and the decision stops being mine.
