Objective: Learn strategies for contributing to meetings, interrupting politely, proposing ideas, and building consensus. Practice using polite interruption phrases to take the floor confidently in high-stakes business meetings.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Study each word, its definition, and the example sentence. Tap Listen to hear the pronunciation.

Contribute -- to offer an idea or opinion in a discussion
"I would like to contribute an idea on the integration timeline."
Interrupt -- to politely break into a conversation to share a point
"If I may jump in here, I think we are missing a key risk factor."
Propose -- to formally suggest a plan or course of action
"If I may, I would like to propose a different approach."
Address -- to deal with or bring attention to a specific topic or issue
"Could we address the budget issue before moving on?"
Elaborate -- to explain something in more detail
"Let me elaborate on that point for a moment."
Defer -- to postpone a decision to a later time or to another person
"I think we should defer this decision until we have more data."
Consensus -- general agreement among a group of people
"Can we reach a consensus on the next steps?"
Wrap up -- to bring a discussion or meeting to a close
"Let me wrap up the main takeaways from this discussion."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Polite Interruptions

Grammar

In business meetings, interrupting directly can sound aggressive. Using conditional structures and softening phrases makes your interruption sound professional and respectful.

Direct (too blunt)Polite (professional)
"I want to say something.""If I may, I would like to add something."
"Wait, let me talk.""If I may jump in here, I think..."
"We should do X.""I would like to propose that we do X."
"Go back to the other topic.""Could we circle back to the other topic?"

Common Mistake

I want to say something about that.

If I may, I would like to add something to that point. (conditional + softener = professional)

Key patterns: If I may + verb..., I would like to + verb..., Could we + verb...?, Would it be possible to + verb...?

Redirecting: Use "Could we circle back to..." or "Could we address..." to steer the conversation back on track.

Quiz
1. You have an important point to make but a colleague is talking. What is the most professional way to interrupt?
A "Wait, I want to say something."
B "If I may jump in here, I think there is a point we should consider."
C "Stop talking, it is my turn."
2. The meeting is going off-topic. How do you redirect the discussion?
A "This is not relevant. Move on."
B "We are wasting time."
C "Could we circle back to the main agenda item? I think we need to address the timeline first."
3. The team cannot agree on a strategy. What is the best way to suggest a decision?
A "I would like to propose a middle ground. What if we start with option A and transition to B later?"
B "Just pick option A. We do not have time for this."
C "I do not care which option we choose."

The Code

Polite interruption structures for meetings

Grammar Discovery

I would like to propose a different approach.
Could we address the budget issue first?
If I may, I would like to elaborate on the timeline.

Look at the beginning of each sentence. What makes them professional?

The Rule

Polite Interruptions

Use conditional structures and softeners to interrupt professionally

Direct (too blunt)Polite (professional)
"I want to say something.""If I may, I would like to add something."
"Wait, let me talk.""If I may jump in here, I think..."
"We should do X.""I would like to propose that we do X."
"Go back to the other topic.""Could we circle back to the other topic?"
Common Mistake

Spot the Error

"I want to say something about the budget."
"If I may, I would like to add a point about the budget."

Quick Practice

Convert: "Let me talk." → "___If I may jump in here, I would like to share a thought."
Convert: "We should change the plan." → "___I would like to propose that we change the plan."
Convert: "Go back to the timeline." → "___Could we circle back to the timeline?"

More Practice

From guided drills to free production

Oral Drilling

React to the Situation

1. The meeting is going off track. You want to refocus the discussion on the main agenda item.
"Could we circle back to the main agenda item? I think we need to address the budget first."
2. A colleague is dominating the conversation. You want to contribute your perspective.
"If I may jump in here, I would like to add a point from the strategy team's perspective."
3. The team is debating two options and cannot decide. You want to propose a compromise.
"I would like to propose a middle ground. What if we start with option A for Q3 and transition to option B in Q4?"
Oral Drilling (continued)

React to the Situation

4. Your manager asks for your opinion on the integration timeline. You think it needs more time.
"If I may, I would like to elaborate on the timeline. Based on our analysis, we need at least two additional months for the tech migration."
5. The discussion is running long and you need to close the meeting.
"We need to wrap up. Let me quickly go through the action items and make sure we have consensus on next steps."
6. Someone raises a complex topic that requires more research. You want to postpone the decision.
"That is a valid concern. I think we should defer this decision until the finance team has completed their analysis. Can we revisit this next week?"
Quick Fire

One at a Time

Challenge 1 / 6
You are in a meeting and two colleagues are debating a topic that has already been decided. You want to move the conversation forward. What do you say?
"If I may jump in here, I think we already reached consensus on this point. Could we address the next agenda item?"
Challenge 2 / 6
Your manager asks for ideas but nobody is speaking up. You have a suggestion about the integration approach. What do you say?
"I would like to contribute an idea. What if we create a dedicated integration team instead of splitting responsibilities across existing departments?"
Challenge 3 / 6
The meeting has been going for 50 minutes and no decisions have been made. You need to bring it to a close. What do you say?
"We need to wrap up. Let me go through what we have agreed on: the joint task force, the cost analysis deadline, and the follow-up meeting. Can we reach consensus on these three items before we leave?"
Challenge 4 / 6
A colleague proposes an aggressive deadline that you think is unrealistic. You want to push back diplomatically. What do you say?
"If I may, I would like to elaborate on why that timeline concerns me. Based on our current resources, I would like to propose extending the deadline by two weeks."
Challenge 5 / 6
Someone raises a legal question that nobody in the room can answer. You want to avoid making a decision without proper information. What do you say?
"I think we should defer this decision until the legal team has reviewed the contracts. Could we circle back to this at next week's meeting?"
Challenge 6 / 6
You have been silent for most of the meeting but you have a critical insight about the data. How do you break in?
"I would like to add something to that point. The data from Q2 actually shows a different trend, and I think we need to address this before making a final decision."
