Chapter 3: The Code

The Indirect Question

A soft opener in front, and normal word order behind it.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

Could you tell me where the elevator is?

Do you know if breakfast is included?

I was wondering if I could move to a quieter room.

Would it be possible to get a late check-out?

Every one opens with a soft phrase — and after it, the sentence relaxes into normal order. What are the two parts that build a polite question?

PartFormExample
The soft openerCould you tell me / Do you know / I was wondering if / Would it be possible toCould you tell me…
Wh- questionopener + where / when / how + normal order (subject, then verb)"Could you tell me where the elevator is?" (not "where is the elevator")
Yes / No questionopener + if / whether + normal order"Do you know if breakfast is included?"
A requestI was wondering if I could… / Would it be possible to + verb"I was wondering if I could move rooms."
The one rulePut a soft opener in front, then drop the inversion: the verb goes AFTER the subject, exactly like a normal sentence. "Where is it?" → "…where it is." "Is it ready?" → "…if it is ready."

One idea holds it together: the opener is polite; the rest is calm and normal.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Could you tell me where is the elevator?"
"Could you tell me where the elevator is?"
"Do you know what time is breakfast?"
"Do you know what time breakfast is?"

One rule, and only one: after the soft opener, drop the inversion. The verb comes AFTER the subject (the elevator is, breakfast is) — exactly like a normal sentence, never like a direct question.

Two Ways to Ask

Direct or Indirect?

Direct is fine with friends. Indirect opens doors with a stranger at the desk.

Direct — short and sharp
"Give me another room."
"Where is the gym?"
Indirect — soft and polite
"I was wondering if I could move to another room."
"Could you tell me where the gym is?"

Same request, two tones: "Give me…" is an order; "I was wondering if…" is a favor. At a front desk, the favor wins every time.

Reading

Read for the Polite Requests

The Front Desk on West 44th

The flight had landed at JFK a little after six, and by the time the taxi reached the hotel, Lucimara wanted only one thing: her room. She crossed the bright lobby, rolled her suitcase to the front desk, and gave her name. The young man behind the counter typed for a moment and frowned. "I'm sorry," he said, "I don't see an Ito. I have a reservation under Yto, Y-T-O." A year ago, this was exactly the moment that would have frozen her. This time she stayed calm. "Could you tell me if that reservation is for two nights, arriving today?" she asked. He checked. It was. "Then I think that's mine," she said gently. "It's Ito, I-T-O. There's often a mix-up with the spelling." He smiled, corrected it, and the problem was gone.

"You're in room three-oh-four," he said, sliding a key card across the counter. "First floor, facing the street." Lucimara had stayed in enough hotels to know what a first-floor room facing the street in Manhattan sounded like at night. She did not complain, and she did not demand. She simply asked. "I was wondering if it would be possible to move to a higher floor, somewhere a little quieter. Do you know whether anything is available facing the back?" The clerk looked at his screen. There was a room on the fourteenth floor, facing the courtyard, at the same rate for double occupancy. "That would be perfect," she said. "Thank you."

In the new room, one thing was wrong. The air conditioning was faulty: it hummed loudly and blew warm air, and after ten minutes the room was no cooler. She picked up the phone and called the front desk, and again she led with a soft opener instead of an order. "I'm sorry to bother you," she said, "but the air conditioning doesn't seem to be working. Would you mind sending someone up to take a look? And could you tell me roughly how long it might take?" Twenty minutes, he said, and an engineer was at her door in fifteen. While she waited, she noticed the minibar, the courtyard view, the quiet. She thought about her colleague, arriving Thursday, and made a note to ask the desk whether they could set up an adjoining room.

On the last morning, Lucimara went down early. She had one more request, and by now it felt like nothing at all. "Good morning," she said. "I was wondering if I could extend my stay by one night, and whether I could settle the whole bill when I leave on Sunday." The clerk checked, confirmed the room was free, and moved everything onto a single bill. Two years ago, in a hotel exactly like this one, she would have pointed, smiled, and hoped someone understood. This week she had corrected a reservation, changed her room, reported a problem, and extended her stay, all in English, all politely, all by herself. The front desk on West 44th had no idea it had just watched a small, quiet victory.

How to Ask for Anything, Politely

Fast, real, and full of soft openers. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What problem does the host say happens to polite people at a busy front desk?
In English, under pressure, they go too direct — "give me another room", "where is the elevator" — and it comes out sharp, even when they are lovely people.
2. What is the simple fix the host recommends?
The indirect question: put a soft opener in front — "could you tell me", "do you know", "I was wondering if", "would it be possible to".
3. What are the TWO things to notice after the opener?
First, the opener does all the polite work. Second, after it the sentence goes back to normal order — "where the gym is", not "where is the gym".
4. What is the host's challenge for your next trip?
Every time you ask for something at the front desk, start with "could you tell me" or "I was wondering if" — you ask in a way that makes the other person want to help.
Strategy

Four Openers That Open Doors

Pick one, then say the rest as a calm, normal sentence. That is the whole trick.

To ask for information: Could you tell me where the elevator is?
To check a fact: Do you know if breakfast is included?
To make a request: I was wondering if I could get a wake-up call.
To ask a big favor: Would it be possible to have a late check-out?

Quick Fire

Ask It Like You Are Already There

1 / 6

You reach the front desk and your name is not in the system. Ask -- politely and indirectly -- whether the reservation might be under a different spelling.

  • Could you tell me if the reservation might be under a different spelling? It's Ito, I-T-O -- there's often a mix-up.
  • Start with 'Could you tell me if...'. After the opener, normal order: 'if the reservation might be...', never 'if might be the reservation'.

Your room faces a noisy street. Ask if you can move to a higher, quieter floor.

  • I was wondering if it would be possible to move to a higher floor, somewhere a little quieter.
  • 'I was wondering if it would be possible to...' is the softest, most natural way to ask for an upgrade or a change. It never sounds like a demand.

You want to know whether breakfast is included in the rate. Ask indirectly.

  • Do you know if breakfast is included in the rate?
  • Direct: 'Is breakfast included?' Indirect: 'Do you know IF breakfast IS included?' -- the 'if' comes in and the verb goes to the end, in normal order.

The air conditioning is faulty. Report it politely and ask how long a repair might take.

  • I'm sorry to bother you, but the air conditioning seems to be faulty. Would you mind sending someone up, and could you tell me how long it might take?
  • 'Would you mind sending...' + 'could you tell me how long it might take' -- two soft openers. Notice: 'how long it might take', not 'how long might it take'.

A colleague arrives Thursday. Ask whether they can set up an adjoining room next to yours.

  • I was wondering whether it would be possible to have an adjoining room for my colleague, arriving on Thursday.
  • 'whether' works just like 'if' after the opener. An adjoining room is one connected to yours by an inside door.

It's your last morning. Ask to extend your stay by a night and to settle the whole bill on checkout.

  • Could you tell me if I could extend my stay by one night, and settle the whole bill when I leave?
  • To 'extend your stay' is to stay longer; to 'settle the bill' is to pay the final total. One soft opener carries both requests.
