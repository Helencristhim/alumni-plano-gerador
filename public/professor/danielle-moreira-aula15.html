Chapter 4: The Register

Every Structure, One Room

Future perfect · past perfect · used to · advanced modals · wish / if only · third conditional · cleft · hedging

The Grammar Map

One Job Each

StructureIts job in the roomExampleHear
Future perfect / continuousProject confidence about what you will have deliveredBy year one I will have run a full diagnostic.
Past perfectSequence a backstory — what had happened beforeHe had rolled out the framework before he had the buy-in.
Advanced modalsGuide and disagree without commandingI would rather build on it than start over.
Wish / if onlyVoice adaptation, not complaintI wish the objections came sooner, so I read the room instead.
Third conditionalOwn a past call without drowning in itIf I had prescribed, I would have lost the room.
Cleft sentencesPut the emphasis exactly where you want itWhat I am changing is the sequence, not the destination.
Hedging languageBe honest about uncertainty, then commitI am not fully sure yet — but here is what I would do.

Seven structures, one register. You will not think about any of these on Thursday — they are the sound your English already makes when you mean what you say.

Register

Hedge, Then Commit

Same uncertainty. Only the second half changes.

Hedge that dissolves

“Um, I'm not really sure, it depends, maybe it could work, I don't know, we'd have to see…”

All doubt, no anchor. In a second language this is the trap: the uncertainty is real, but nothing follows it, so it reads as junior.

Hedge that lands

“I'm not fully sure how I'd measure that yet — but here is what I am sure of, and here is how I'd find the rest.”

Names the doubt honestly, then commits to what you know and a path to the rest. That is the most senior move in any language.

Ruth's whole instruction fits here: reach for the true sentence, not the perfect one. “I don't have a complete answer yet, and here is how I'd find one” is not a weakness you're hiding — it is the strongest thing you can say in that room.

Quick Fire

The Room, One Question at a Time

1 / 6

"Grant opens: 'Before we start, tell the room in one minute what you were actually brought here to do.' Frame your mandate -- do not undersell it."

  • I was brought in to lead the cultural transformation of the Canadian division -- to design the employee journey, close the trust gaps our diagnostic surfaced, and build the People and Culture function the way I built it in Brazil. By the end of my first year I will have run a full cultural diagnostic, rolled out the new onboarding experience, and given every leader here a clear people strategy to work from.
  • own the mandate with future perfect ('I will have run / rolled out'); no hedging on what you were hired to do -- save hedging for uncertainty, not for your own remit

"A regional director asks: 'How is this different from what your predecessor tried and dropped?' Answer without trashing the predecessor."

  • My predecessor laid real groundwork, and I would rather build on it than start over. What I am changing is the sequence: he rolled out the framework before he had the buy-in, so it never gained momentum. I am running the listening tour first, earning credibility, and only then moving the needle -- same destination, a different order of operations.
  • 'would rather build on it than' (advanced modal, no blame); past simple for what happened, present continuous for your plan; land 'buy-in / move the needle / gain momentum'

"The academic lead, a doctor, pushes: 'With respect, a Brazil-designed framework may not transfer to a Canadian academic culture.' Disagree and hold your ground."

  • That is a fair challenge, and I would push back gently on one part of it. I am not importing a Brazilian framework; I am importing a diagnostic method and letting your context write the answers. I take the point about cultural distance -- it is exactly why I would rather read this culture first than prescribe to it. Where we may differ is on transfer: the method is culturally neutral, even if the findings will not be.
  • concede the fair part ('I take the point'), then 'where we may differ' (cleft/hedged disagreement); stand your ground without a flat 'no'

"Grant's uncomfortable question: 'What does success look like for you, personally, in this role -- not for the company?' No prepared answer exists."

  • Honestly, let me think on that out loud. I think success, for me, is the day a Canadian leader here disagrees with me directly, in a meeting, because the psychological safety is real enough that they can. If I have built a culture where people push back on me the way I am pushing back on you now, that is the whole job. I am not fully sure how I would measure it yet -- but that is the thing I would want to be true.
  • hedge honestly ('let me think on that out loud', 'I am not fully sure yet'), then commit to the true part; a real, personal answer beats a polished corporate one

"A director you have not won over yet says your plan is 'ambitious'. Read the understatement and respond."

  • I am reading 'ambitious' as a gentle way of saying you are not yet convinced it is realistic -- tell me if I have that wrong. If I have it right, that is exactly the conversation I want. I would rather you told me plainly where the plan is thin, because the fastest way to move the needle is for me to hear the real objection now, not after we have launched.
  • read the room -- 'ambitious' is an understatement/polite objection; surface it with 'tell me if I have that wrong', invite directness without imposing

"Close the meeting. In two or three sentences, tell the leadership team what you want them to remember about you after today."

  • Remember that I came to listen before I came to change anything, and that I told you the truth even when a more comfortable version was available. The English is newer for me than the work is -- but the work is the same work I have done for a decade, and I am going to do it here with the same clarity. Give me a year, and this division will feel like a place people are proud to belong to.
  • close on identity, not tasks; a touch of vulnerability about the language paired with total confidence about the work -- that contrast is the senior note
