From Doing It to Telling It

Who Tells Your Story?

This photo is last Tuesday. What were these people doing? Give me four sentences: "They were waiting...", "Somebody was running...", "The sun was going down..."

In Lesson 10 you crossed a whole day alone — the counter, the gate, the hotel, the bill. Nobody translated for you. And then you came home, and at the dinner table somebody else told everybody what happened to you. That is the last thing left. The day is already yours. Tonight we take the story back.

Today

Three Things, One Story

1. The Words

realize, rush, take off, break down, stuck, traffic jam...

2. The Code

I was waiting when it happened

3. The Spine

"All of a sudden..." · "At that point..."

A list is not a story. "The flight was late. We waited. We arrived." — nobody wants that. They want to know what you were doing when it happened. That one thing turns a list into a story.

The Long Line and the Cut

Chapter 3: The Code

Two tenses. One story.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I was waiting at the gate when they announced the delay."

"We were having dinner when the alarm went off."

"The agent was checking my passport when I realized my mistake."

"I was sleeping when the plane landed."

Two colors, two jobs. Which action started first? Which one is long, and which one is one second?

The long oneThe glueThe short one
I was waitingwhenthey announced the delay.
We were having dinnerwhenthe alarm went off.
was / were + verb-ing past simple (Lesson 2)

The long one is a line: it was already going on. The short one is a pair of scissors: it arrives and it cuts the line in half. And when is the hand that holds the scissors.

You already know this. In Lesson 4 you said "the flight is boarding" — now. Today it is "the flight was boarding" — yesterday. Same -ing, older day.

The Code

The Line, the Scissors, and the Hand

One long action, one short event, one word that glues them
was / were + -ing
the LONG action -- it was already happening

"I was waiting at the gate." You did not start it in that second and you did not finish it in that second. It was already going on, like a line on a timeline.

past simple
the SHORT event -- it cut the line in half

"...they announced the delay." One second. It arrives, it happens, it is over. This is the tense you have used since Lesson 2.

when / while
the glue -- it says which one is which

"I was waiting at the gate WHEN they announced the delay." After WHEN comes the short one. After WHILE comes the long one. Same story, two doors.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I was wait at the gate when they called my name."
"I was waiting at the gate when they called my name."
"We was having dinner when the alarm went off."
"We were having dinner when the alarm went off."
"I was realizing that my passport was in the other bag."
"I realized that my passport was in the other bag."

The -ing is not optional: was alone is nothing. We and they take were. And realize is one second, not a line — you cannot be realizing something for twenty minutes.

The Sentence Kit

Three Sentences, Any Story

You do not need a big vocabulary to tell a story. You need a spine.

"I was waiting at the gate when the screen changed." (the line and the scissors)
"All of a sudden, the taxi broke down." (wake up — here come the scissors)
"At that point, I was still calm." (the clock of the story)

Notice what the spine does: it tells the other person when to lean in. Without it, everything in your story sounds the same size — and then it is a report, not a story.

Expression of the Day

I Could Not Believe It

I could not believe it.

= it was true, and my head still said no (never "I cannot believe" — the story is in the past)

"We were rushing to the gate, and the door was already closed. I could not believe it."
"My alarm was set for seven in the evening. Seven P M. I could not believe it."

Twenty-three cities in Italy. Tell me one thing you could not believe.

The Hard Part

Same Day. Which One Is a Story?

Same trip. Same facts. Same person.

The list

"The alarm did not go off. We took a taxi. There was a traffic jam. The taxi broke down. We missed the flight."

Everything is the same size, so nothing is important. Five facts in nine seconds, and the person in front of you is already thinking about something else.

The story

"I was sleeping like a baby when my wife started shouting my name. We were sitting in that taxi for fifty minutes, and all of a sudden the engine died. At that point I was watching our plane take off. Without us."

The background is what makes the event land. Now the other person can see it — and they lean in.

You have been able to say the facts since Lesson 2. The facts were never the problem — the background was missing.

Dialogue

The Man in the Next Seat

M
Two hours. Two more hours in this airport. Sorry — I am talking to myself. It has been a very long day.
R
A long day? I have two hours too. What were you doing this morning?
M
This morning? At six o'clock I was sleeping in a very nice hotel in Porto. My alarm was set for six, and it did not go off. I still do not know why.
R
Oh no. And then? What happened when you woke up?
M
The sun woke me up. The sun! I looked at my phone and it was ten past eight, and my train was at nine. I was rushing around that room like a crazy man.
R
Did you catch the train?
M
I did! I was very proud of myself. And then we were sitting on that train for forty minutes, in the middle of nowhere, and nobody was saying anything. All of a sudden a man came and told us: the train in front of us broke down.
R
So you were stuck on the train. What were you thinking at that point?
M
Honestly? I was thinking about my daughter. It is her wedding on Saturday, in London, and I was sitting in a dead train in Portugal. I arrived here at two o'clock, I was lining up for three hours, and they put me on the eight o'clock flight. I could not believe it — but I am going to be there.
M
Listen to me, talking and talking. And you? Two hours here as well — what happened to you today?
R
Me? Nothing like your day. I was having a coffee downstairs when they changed my gate — that is all. But your daughter is getting married on Saturday, and you are going to walk in on time. That is a good story, Martin.
Comprehension

About Martin

1. What was Martin doing at six o'clock this morning, and what went wrong?
He was sleeping in a hotel in Porto. His alarm was set for six, and it did not go off — and he still does not know why. The sun woke him up at ten past eight.
2. His train was at nine. Did he catch it?
Yes, he did — he was rushing around the hotel room "like a crazy man", and he caught it. He says he was very proud of himself.
3. Then the train stopped. What was happening, and why?
They were sitting on the train for forty minutes in the middle of nowhere, and nobody was saying anything. Then a man came and told them the train in front of them broke down.
4. What was he thinking about while he was stuck on the train?
His daughter. It is her wedding on Saturday, in London — and he was sitting in a dead train in Portugal.
5. How does his day end? What did he do at the airport, and what did they give him?
He arrived at two o'clock and he was lining up for three hours. They put him on the eight o'clock flight, so he is going to be at the wedding. He could not believe it.
Listening

The Worst Day of Frank's Trip

Rome, two years ago. His wife still tells this story at every dinner party. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Their flight was at eleven. What was Frank doing at seven o'clock, and why did the alarm not go off?
He was sleeping "like a baby". The alarm did not go off because he set it for seven in the evening — seven P M, not seven A M. At half past eight his wife was shaking his shoulder and shouting his name.
2. Two things stopped them on the way to the airport. What were they, and how long were they stuck?
First a big traffic jam on the highway — they were sitting in the taxi for fifty minutes and they were not moving one meter. Then, all of a sudden, the taxi broke down, right there in the middle of the traffic jam.
3. What was he doing at the desk when he realized something about his passport?
He was lining up at the desk when he realized his passport was in the front pocket of the other suitcase — the one his wife was carrying.
4. How does the story end, and what two things did Frank learn?
They were rushing to the gate when the woman closed the door, and through the window they were watching their plane take off without them. He learned two things: read your alarm (morning or evening), and leave early.
Listening 2

Teresa, 71, in a Station in Florence

She started traveling alone at sixty-six. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Teresa started traveling alone at sixty-six. Why not before?
Her husband did the talking for forty years. Then her husband was gone, and she thought that was the end of Italy for her. It was not.
2. What was she doing in the station in Florence, and what happened all of a sudden?
She was standing in the station waiting for the train to Bologna, and she was very proud of herself because she was doing it alone. Then a voice said something in Italian and everybody stood up and walked away — her train was cancelled, and she did not understand one word.
3. What did she do instead of crying? What exactly did she say, and how many words was it?
She walked to the information desk, she lined up, and she waited twenty minutes. She said: "My train is cancelled, what should I do?" — six words. That was all she had.
4. What did the young man do, and what is still in her drawer at home?
He answered her slowly, because he saw her face. He put her on a train forty minutes later and wrote the platform number on a piece of paper: fourteen. She still has that piece of paper in a drawer.
5. What does Teresa say the real problem is?
Not the bad day — the bad day is going to happen. The problem is thinking that you need perfect English to survive it. She had six words, and they were enough.

1. Read the two departure times. How long is the delay, and why?
"It was 19:40, and now it is 21:55. That is two hours and fifteen minutes." The reason: the aircraft is arriving late from Madrid.
2. Two things changed on this screen, not one. What is the second one, and where do you have to go now?
The gate changed too: it was B14, and now it is C22. "The gate changed. We are not at B14 any more — we have to walk to C22."
3. Your husband does not speak English, and he is looking at you. Tell him what happened — start with what you were doing.
"We were having a coffee at gate B14 when my phone rang. All of a sudden the flight is at 21:55, and the gate is C22. We are not stuck — we just have to walk."
4. At 21:55 the plane takes off. Was it on time?
"No. It was not on time — it was delayed. On time is 19:40, exactly. Two hours and fifteen minutes late is not on time."
Quick Fire

Tell It Like You Were There

1 / 8

Somebody at the dinner table asks: what happened on your last trip? Do not give a list. Start the story with the long action -- where you were and what you were doing.

  • I was waiting at the gate in Lisbon when the screen changed.
  • We were having dinner at the airport when they called my name.

Your alarm did not work. Say it -- and say what you were doing at that moment.

  • I was sleeping when my alarm went off. It went off at seven in the evening, not seven in the morning.
  • All of a sudden it was half past eight, and I was still sleeping.

The taxi stopped working on the way to the airport. Tell that part.

  • We were sitting in a traffic jam when the taxi broke down.
  • The taxi broke down while we were going to the airport, and we were stuck for fifty minutes.

You were at the desk, and then you understood that your passport was in the other bag. Tell it with the two tenses.

  • I was lining up at the desk when I realized my passport was in the other bag.
  • While I was lining up, I realized I did not have my passport.

You ran, and you arrived, and the door was closed. The plane left without you. Say it.

  • We were rushing to the gate when the woman closed the door. We missed it.
  • We were watching our plane take off, and we were still inside the airport. I could not believe it.
  • At that point I was not relieved. I was exhausted, and I was still in Rome.

Now the good ending: the airline put you on the next flight, and it left on time. How did you feel, and what were you doing when they told you?

  • I was lining up at the desk when the agent said there was a seat at four o'clock. I was so relieved.
  • The next flight took off on time, and I slept for three hours.

Your friend says a story is only the facts: the flight was late, we waited, we arrived. You disagree. Say why.

  • I disagree. Nobody wants the list -- they want to know what you were doing when it happened.
  • In my opinion, a story needs the long part and the short part. The list is not a story.

Somebody asks you why you always tell the same story from Paris. Answer, and start it.

  • Once in a while something goes wrong, and that is the only part anybody remembers.
  • I was standing at passport control in London when the officer asked me a question, and I was not ready for it.

Production
