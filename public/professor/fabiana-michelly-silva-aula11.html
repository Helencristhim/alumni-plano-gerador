Chapter 3

The Code

Advanced relative clauses for precise description

Discovery

One Sentence, Two Ideas

"The manager who leads the call rarely says no directly." (defining -- person)

"Silence, which Westerners find awkward, is a sign of thinking." (non-defining -- extra info, commas)

"The deal that respects the rules is the deal that closes." (defining -- thing)

"The senior manager whose approval we need will speak last." (possession)

"This is the room where the final terms are agreed." (place)

"The colleague introducing you sets the tone." (reduced -- who is introducing)

Grammar Rule

Advanced Relative Clauses

PronounUseExample
who / thatFor people (subject or object)The manager who leads the call.
which / thatFor things and ideasThe deal that closes.
whoseFor possession (person or thing)The manager whose approval we need.
where / whenFor places and timesThe room where terms are agreed.
Defining (no commas)Essential -- identifies which oneThe buyer who led the meeting.
Non-defining (commas)Extra info; use which, never thatSilence, which they find odd, is normal.
Common Mistake

Watch Out

"Silence, that they find awkward, is normal." (non-defining clause cannot use that)
"Silence, which they find awkward, is normal." (non-defining = which + commas)

In a non-defining clause (extra information set off by commas) use which for things and who for people -- never that. The commas are your clue.

The Cultural Briefing

Marcus prepares the team for a high-context negotiation

0:000:00
1. Why does Marcus say a response that sounds like "maybe" is important?
It is often a polite refusal -- something you have to read between the lines.
2. What does silence signal in this high-context culture?
A sign of careful thinking, not discomfort -- so the team should not push for a quick decision.
3. What does Marcus say closes the deal?
The deal that respects the unwritten rules -- building common ground first, then the contract follows.
Artifact

Culture Map

Bloomberg APAC -- Negotiation Culture Map

JAPAN
High-context culture. Meaning is indirect; you must read between the lines. Etiquette and saving face are essential. The manager whose approval matters speaks last.
GERMANY
Low-context culture. Directness is a sign of respect. Little small talk. The buyer who leads goes straight to the numbers.
BOTH
Reciprocity applies everywhere: a concession that you accept should be returned. A mediator can help adjust pace.

Rule of thumb: The negotiator who reads the room and accommodates the other side's style is the one who finds common ground.

Comprehension

Clauses Spotted

1. who (defining person): "The partner _____ led the Japan call..."
"The partner who led the Japan call never said no."
2. whose (possession): "...the manager _____ approval we needed..."
"I let the manager whose approval we needed take his time."
3. which (non-defining): "The German call, _____ happened right after..."
"The German call, which happened right after, was the opposite."

Spot the Error

Find the Mistake

0 / 4 errors found

"Silence, that they find awkward, is a sign of thinking."
"Silence, WHICH they find awkward, is a sign of thinking." (non-defining clause uses which, never that)
"The manager who's approval we need will speak last."
"The manager WHOSE approval we need will speak last." (whose = possession; who's = who is)
"This is the room which the terms are agreed."
"This is the room WHERE the terms are agreed." (place = where, not which)
"The buyer which led the meeting got straight to the numbers."
"The buyer WHO led the meeting got straight to the numbers." (people = who, not which)
