Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip

Grammar
Present Perfect Simple vs Continuous

Present Perfect Simple — focuses on the result or completion of an action.

FormStructureExample
+Subject + have/has + past participleI have played FIFA since 2020.
-Subject + have/has + not + past participleHe has not finished the project.
?Have/Has + subject + past participleHave you seen this movie?

Present Perfect Continuous — focuses on the duration or ongoing nature of an action.

FormStructureExample
+Subject + have/has + been + verb-ingI have been playing for three hours.
-Subject + have/has + not + been + verb-ingHe has not been studying enough.
?Have/Has + subject + been + verb-ingHave you been waiting long?
Key Difference

Simple = How many? How much? (result/quantity)
"I have read five books this month." (focus on the number)

Continuous = How long? (duration/process)
"I have been reading all morning." (focus on the ongoing activity)

Note: State verbs (know, believe, want, like) do NOT use the continuous form. "I have known him for years." (NOT "I have been knowing")

Grammar Tip: Mixed Tenses Review

Grammar

When talking about personality and experiences, we often mix tenses:

TenseUseExample
Present SimplePermanent traitsHe is driven.
Past SimpleCompleted past actionsHe joined the club last year.
Present PerfectPast actions with present relevanceHe has participated in three debates.
Present Perfect ContinuousActions that started in the past and continueHe has been trying to be more confident.

Key rule: Use Past Simple with specific time markers (yesterday, last year). Use Present Perfect when the time is not important or the action connects to now.

Grammar Tip: Relative Clauses

Grammar

We use relative clauses to give more information about a noun:

TypePurposeExample
DefiningEssential info (no commas)The friend who I count on is Leo.
Non-definingExtra info (with commas)Leo, who is my best friend, plays soccer.
who / thatFor peopleThe person who/that I admire...
which / thatFor thingsThe game which/that I play...
whosePossessionThe friend whose brother plays...

Important: You cannot use "that" in non-defining relative clauses. Only "who," "which," or "whose."

Grammar Tip: Passive Voice

Grammar

We use the passive when the action is more important than who does it:

FormStructureExample
Present Passive (+)am/is/are + past participleContent is shared every day.
Present Passive (-)am/is/are + not + past participleFake news is not tolerated.
Present Passive (?)Am/Is/Are + subject + past participle?Is this content filtered?
Past Passive (+)was/were + past participleThe study was published last year.
Past Passive (-)was/were + not + past participleThe data was not deleted.
Past Passive (?)Was/Were + subject + past participle?Was the article written by AI?

Key: Active = who does it matters. Passive = what happens matters. Use "by" to mention the agent when needed.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Second Conditional

GRAMMAR

Situações imaginárias no presente/futuro. / Imaginary situations, present or future.

PartFormExample
ConditionIf + past simpleIf I found a wallet... (Se eu encontrasse)
Resultwould + base verb...I would return it. (eu devolveria)
QuestionWhat would you do if...?What would you do if you won?
to bewere (all persons)If I were you... (Se eu fosse você)

NEVER "if + would" / "if + will". The past form creates distance from reality — it is NOT about the past.

Chapter 3 -- The Grammar of Imagination

Second Conditional

If + past simple, would + verb

Discover the Rule

Listen and Notice

"If I found a wallet, I would return it."

"What would you do if you saw a friend cheating?"

"If I were you, I would tell the teacher."

"I would not hesitate if the outcome were clear."

PartFormExample
Condition (imaginary)If + past simpleIf I found a wallet...
Resultwould + base verb...I would return it.
Special: to bewere (all persons)If I were you / If she were rich

Past form, but NOT about the past — about an imaginary present or future. The past creates distance from reality.
