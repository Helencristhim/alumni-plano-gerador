Chapter 3 -- The Code

Grammar Discovery

The first conditional for advice and predictions

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

How do we give advice about what will happen?

"If you rest, you will feel better." (condition + result)

"If you take the medicine, you will get better." (present + will)

"If the fever does not go down, you should see a doctor." (advice with should)

"You will feel dizzy if you stand up too fast." (result + if)

Grammar Rule

The First Conditional

PatternExample
If + present simple, will + base verbIf you rest, you will feel better.
Will + base verb + if + present simpleYou will get better if you take the medicine.
Negative condition: if + do not + base verbIf the fever does not go down, see a doctor.
Advice result: if + present, should + base verbIf you cough a lot, you should drink water.

Key idea: the first conditional talks about a real, possible future. Use if + the present simple for the condition, and will + base verb for the result. Never say "if you will rest" -- the if part stays in the present.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"If you will rest, you feel better."
"If you rest, you will feel better."

After if, use the present simple (no "will"). The result part needs will + base verb.

Giving Advice to a Colleague

S

"Rubens, I feel awful. I have a fever, a sore throat, and a bad headache."

R

"I am sorry to hear that. You should rest today. If you rest, you will feel better tomorrow."

S

"I took a painkiller, but my whole body still aches. And I feel dizzy when I stand up."

R

"That is the fever. If you drink water and sleep, the ache will get better. Do not stand up too fast."

S

"Thank you. Should I see a doctor, or should I just wait?"

R

"If the fever does not go down by tomorrow, you should see a doctor. For now, rest and take sick leave."
