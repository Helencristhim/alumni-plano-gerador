Chapter 3: The Code

The Plan

I am going to · Are you going to?

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"We are going to take the metro to the city center."

"I am going to ask at the front desk for a map."

"On Thursday, Rosângela is going to take the tram to Belém."

"Are you going to come back today?"

All four are about later — today, Thursday, tomorrow. Two little words are in every sentence: going to. So look at the verb after it — take, ask, come. And look at the word before it: am, is, are. Where have you seen those three before?

FormUseExample
Yes
am / is / are
+ going to + verb		A plan: you decided it before, and you are going to do it later.I am going to take the metro. We are going to visit the tower.
The verb
stays normal		"Going to" already says it is later, so the verb does not change.We are going to take the tram. (never "going to took")
No
am / is / are + not		The "not" goes next to am / is / are, like in Lesson 4.I am not going to take a taxi. The traffic is terrible.
Question
Are you going to...?		Turn it around: am / is / are comes to the front.Are you going to come back today? — "Yes, I am." / "No, I am not."
You already
know this		Lesson 4 was "I am waiting" — now. Add going to in the middle, and it is a plan — later.I am waiting (now) · I am going to wait (later)

The pieces are the same as Lesson 4: am / is / are. You only put going to in the middle — and the verb after it relaxes.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I go to visit Belém tomorrow."
"I am going to visit Belém tomorrow."
"We are going to took the metro."
"We are going to take the metro."
"She going to buy a round trip."
"She is going to buy a round trip."

In Portuguese the present can talk about tomorrow. In English it cannot: a plan always needs am / is / are + going to. And after going to, the verb relaxes — take, never "took".

The Question Kit

Three Questions, Any City

You do not need to understand the whole city. You need three questions — and the courage to stop a stranger.

"Excuse me. Could you tell me how to get to Belém, please?"
"Is this the right platform for the airport?"
"How long does it take to get to the city center?"

And when the answer comes too fast, you already have the words from Lesson 3: "I am sorry, I did not catch that. Could you say it more slowly, please?"

Expression of the Day

I Would Rather

I would rather ... than ...

= I prefer this one (and the verb after it is normal: take, not "to take")

"Taxi or metro?" — "I would rather take the metro. The traffic here is terrible."
"At night I would rather take a ride-share than walk."

Now say two true sentences about your next trip with I would rather.

Dialogue

At the Metro Station

R
Good morning. Could you tell me how to get to Belém, please? My husband and I are going to visit the tower today.
M
Good morning. Belém — of course. You are not going to take the metro: you are going to take the tram. Number fifteen, from Praça da Figueira.
R
The tram, not the metro. Thank you. And where do I buy the ticket? Is there a ticket machine here?
M
Yes, behind you, on your left. The fare is one euro and eighty. Are you going to come back today?
R
Yes, we are. We are going to come back in the afternoon, after lunch.
M
Then buy a round trip, not a one-way ticket. Same price, and you are not going to stand in this line again.
R
I am sorry, I did not catch that. Could you say it more slowly, please?
M
Of course. Buy a round trip — one ticket, to go and to come back. Then walk to platform two. And do not transfer: tram fifteen goes all the way to Belém.
R
Now I understand. Thank you very much. And how long does it take to get to Belém?
M
About twenty-five minutes. And if you are tired tonight, take a ride-share back to the hotel — after eleven the metro is closed. Enjoy the tower!
Comprehension

About the Agent

1. Which transport did the agent say she is going to take to Belém, and which number?
The tram, not the metro. Number fifteen, from Praça da Figueira.
2. Why did the agent say to buy a round trip and not a one-way ticket?
It is the same price, and she is not going to stand in the line again.
3. What did the agent do when she did not catch what he said?
He said "Of course" and explained it again, more slowly. He was not annoyed.
4. How long does the trip take, and what did the agent say about the night?
About twenty-five minutes. At night, if she is tired, take a ride-share — after eleven the metro is closed.
Listening

The Station Information

A recorded message in the metro station. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Where are the ticket machines, and how do you get English on the screen?
In front of you, next to the entrance. Press the small flag, on the top right of the screen.
2. What is the fare for one trip, and what does a round trip give you?
One euro and eighty for one trip. A round trip is three euros and sixty — the same price, but you stand in the line only once.
3. Does your metro ticket work on the tram?
No. For the tram and the bus you buy a different ticket, at the bus stop or from the driver.

  • We are going to take a ride-share back to the hotel.
  • I would rather take a ride-share than walk at night.

  • Tomorrow we are going to take the tram to Alfama, early.
  • In the afternoon I am going to visit the tower, and we are going to walk by the river.

Stopping a Stranger

Scenario: You are on the street in Lisbon and you cannot find the tram stop. Your husband is behind you with the bags, and he does not speak English. Stop a local person: ask the way, ask how long it takes, and say what you are going to visit. He speaks fast — do not guess. Ask him to repeat, then say the way back to him to check.
