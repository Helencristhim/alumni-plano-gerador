Cracking the Code of Fast English

Cracking the Code of Fast English

Real American English does not sound like a textbook. In stores and markets, people link their words together and cut them short. 'What are you looking for' becomes 'whatcha lookin for', and 'let me' becomes 'lemme'. This is called connected speech, and it is full of reductions and contractions. If you stop to understand every single word, you fall behind and lose the thread of the conversation.

Many learners used to freeze at the first fast sentence. They used to try to translate every word in their head, and they used to tune out when it got hard. The real trick is to change that habit. Instead of chasing every sound, you listen for keywords and get the gist. Intonation and a few important words carry most of the meaning, and inference fills in the rest.

With practice, your ear starts to catch on. You tune in to the words that matter and tune out the noise. You pick up new phrases without ever studying them. What used to sound like a wall of noise slowly becomes a real conversation, and a busy New York market stops being scary.

Chapter 4: I Used To

Used To + Infinitive

past habit that changed · used to + base verb · drop the 'd' in questions and negatives

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I used to freeze when people spoke fast."

"She used to translate every word, but now she listens for the gist."

"We used to lose the thread, but now we catch on."

Do these things happen now, or did they happen in the past and change? And what verb form comes right after "used to"?

FormStructureExample
Affirmativeused to + base verb"I used to freeze when people spoke fast."
Negativedidn't use to + base"I didn't use to understand reductions."
QuestionDid ... use to + base?"Did you use to translate every word?"
Meaningpast habit, no longer true"I used to tune out, but now I tune in."

"Used to + base verb" = a past habit that changed. Keep the "d" in the affirmative (used to); drop it in negatives and questions (use to).

  • 'I think there is a sale today.'

  • Keywords carry the meaning.

  • A rising intonation makes it a polite question.
