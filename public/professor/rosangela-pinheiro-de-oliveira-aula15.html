One Journey, Four Tenses

Chapter 3: The Code

No new grammar today. The right tense for each moment of the day.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"The plane has just landed in Lisbon."

"I checked in in São Paulo this morning."

"Right now, I am looking for the train."

"Then I am going to take it to the hotel."

Same day, same person, four colors. Why is each sentence in a different tense? Look at WHEN each one happens.

WhenTenseExample
Just now, still freshPresent perfect: have/has + just / already / yetThe plane has just landed.
A finished time (you can say when)Past simpleI checked in this morning.
Right now, as you speakPresent continuous: am/is/are + -ingI am looking for the train.
The next step, the planGoing to + verbI am going to take the train.

A travel day is a line of moments, and each moment has its own tense. The question is never "which grammar?" — it is "when?" Answer when, and the tense chooses itself.

Nothing here is new. You learned the past simple in Lesson 2, the present continuous in Lesson 4, "going to" in Lesson 5, and the present perfect in Lessons 12 and 13. Today you only learn to switch between them, fast.

The Code

The Line of Moments

One journey, four tenses, working together
Just now -- present perfect
have / has + just / already / yet

For what has just happened or is already done, when the moment is still fresh: "I have just landed." "I have already gone through passport control." "I haven't collected my bag yet." This is the news of right now.

A finished time -- past simple
checked, took, was -- with a closed time

For a step that finished at a set time: "I checked in in São Paulo this morning." "The flight took off at ten." If you can say WHEN (this morning, at ten, yesterday), use the past simple, never the present perfect.

Right now -- present continuous
am / is / are + verb-ing

For what is happening this very second, in front of you: "I am waiting at the gate." "I am looking for the train." "They are boarding now." It is the thing you can see as you speak.

The next step -- going to
am going to + verb (and I'll for a quick decision)

For the plan and the next move: "I am going to take the train to the city." "I'll ask at the information desk." Going to for the plan you already have; I'll for a decision you make on the spot.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I have checked in this morning."
"I checked in this morning."
"Right now I wait at the gate."
"Right now I am waiting at the gate."
"Then I take the train to the hotel." (my plan)
"Then I am going to take the train to the hotel."

A finished time (this morning) takes the past simple. What is happening right now takes am/is/are + -ing. And the next step, the plan, takes going to. In Portuguese one form does all three — in English, each moment has its own.

Expression of the Day

One Step at a Time

One step at a time.

= do not look at the whole staircase; do one thing, then the next. It is how you cross a big airport without panic.

"Passport, then bag, then train. One step at a time."
"I do not look at the whole trip. I take it one step at a time, and it is smooth."

The airport is big. How are you going to cross it?

The Hard Part

Same Journey. Which One Sounds Human?

Same day. Same airport. Same person.

Survival English

"I land now. Check-in yes, morning. Passport control yes. Bag no. Train? Hotel after."

Every word is a fact with no time on it. The agent understands, but he cannot help, because he does not know what is done and what is still coming.

Real English

"I have just landed. I checked in this morning, and I have already gone through passport control — but my bag hasn't come out yet. Right now I am looking for the train, and then I am going to take it to the hotel."

Now he knows exactly where you are in the day: what is done, what is waiting, and what comes next. The tenses did that.

You have had the facts for fourteen lessons. Today you learn to move between the tenses without stopping.

R
Yes, please. I have just landed from São Paulo, and I am on my own. I am looking for the train to the city.

R

Then collect the bag first, belt four, and then come back this way for the train. Do you know which hotel you are going to?
R
Yes, the Hotel Baixa, in the center. I am going to take the train, and then I am going to walk from the station.

Good plan. The train is clean and cheap, and it goes straight there. One tip: keep track of your platform number, because it can change.
R
I will. One more thing — my phone has no signal. I need to sort out a SIM card right away, for the maps.
D
There is a phone shop just past the bags, on the left. Buy the SIM there, and they will set it up for you. Then you are ready.
R
Thank you. So: the bag, then the SIM card, then the train. Let me double-check — belt four, and the train is this way?
D
Exactly right. You are doing all of this on your own, senhora, and you are handling it beautifully. Welcome to Lisbon. Have a smooth trip.
Comprehension

About Daniel

1. Rosângela's bag hasn't come out yet. What did Daniel tell her to do first, and where?
He told her to collect the bag first, at belt four, and then to come back that way for the train.
2. What did Daniel say about the train — is it a good way to get to the city, and why?
Yes. He said the train is clean and cheap, and it goes straight to the center. It was a good plan.
3. Rosângela's phone has no signal. Where did Daniel send her to sort out a SIM card?
To a phone shop just past the bags, on the left. He said they would buy the SIM there and set it up for her.
4. Daniel gave her one tip about the platform. What was it, and why?
To keep track of the platform number, because it can change. So she should check the screen and not just trust the first one.
5. At the end, what did Daniel say about Rosângela doing this alone, and what did he wish her?
He said she was doing all of this on her own and handling it beautifully. Then he welcomed her to Lisbon and wished her a smooth trip.
Listening

Bruno Walks You Through the Arrivals Hall

A group has just landed, many traveling alone. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Bruno says the next hour is all in one line. What are the first three steps, in order?
One: follow the purple signs to passport control, with your passport ready. Two: go down to baggage and find your belt on the screen. Three: walk out through the green channel, unless you have something to declare.
2. What does Bruno say to do if your bag has not come out yet?
Do not panic. Wait — it always comes.
3. Bruno says the thing people get wrong is the taxi. What is his advice about getting to the city?
Do not take the first taxi that shouts at you. Keep track of your money. The train and the metro are cheaper, clean, and they go straight to the city.
4. For people who are worried about being on their own, what six words does Bruno say are enough — and how does he finish?
Passport, bag, train, hotel, please, and thank you — that is the whole journey. He finishes: "You have already flown across an ocean by yourself. You can handle the last hour."
Listening 2

Inês, 71, and Her First Trip Alone

Forty years following, and then one flight on her own. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. For forty years, who did everything on Inês's trips, and what did she do?
Her husband did everything — he checked them in, he talked at passport control, he found the taxi, he asked for the room. She just followed him, quiet, and saw the world through the back of his coat.
2. Her daughter told her she could not travel alone. What did Inês do about it?
She booked a flight to Porto, alone, and she did not tell her daughter until the morning she left.
3. Her hands were shaking at the airport. What little thing had she made to help her, and how did she use it?
A little card, with every step written in order: check in, go through security, find the gate, board. She did them one at a time, like climbing stairs, looking only at the next step, not the whole staircase.
4. When she closed the hotel room door, what did she feel — and what does she say was the only hard part?
She sat on the bed, cried, and then laughed, because the trip had been completely smooth. The only hard part, the whole time, had been believing she could. Her advice: "You have handled harder things than an airport. Make your little card. Take the next step. And go."

Quick Fire

Answer Across the Whole Day

1 / 8

You have just come off the plane in Lisbon after eleven hours. A ground agent asks how the flight was. Tell him you have just arrived, and how you feel.

  • I have just landed, and I am exhausted, but I am fine.
  • I have just arrived. It was a long flight, but a smooth one.

He asks if you checked in for this flight. You did it in São Paulo, early this morning. That is a finished time -- choose the right tense.

  • Yes, I checked in in São Paulo this morning. (finished time, so past simple)
  • I checked in early this morning, before the flight.

He asks if you have been through passport control. You have -- it is done. Use the good-news word.

  • Yes, I have already gone through passport control.
  • I have already gone through. My passport is stamped.

You are at the baggage belt, but your suitcase is still not there. Tell him, using the word that waits at the end.

  • My bag hasn't come out yet.
  • I haven't collected my suitcase yet -- I am still waiting.

He asks what you are doing right now, at this second. You are looking for the train. Use the tense for right now.

  • I am looking for the train to the city.
  • Right now I am trying to find the platform for the train.

He asks how you are getting to the hotel. It is your plan -- the train, and then a short walk. Tell him.

  • I am going to take the train, and then walk to the hotel.
  • I'm going to take the metro to the city, and walk from there.

Your phone has no signal, and you need internet to find the hotel. You want to fix this small problem now. Say it with today's words.

  • I need to sort out a SIM card right away.
  • I have to sort out my phone first -- I'll buy a SIM card here.

He smiles and says you are doing all of this alone. Tell him you are on your own this time, and that you can handle it.

  • Yes, I am on my own this time, and I can handle it.
  • My husband is not here -- I'm on my own, but I can handle the airport now.
