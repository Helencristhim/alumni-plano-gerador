Reading a business case and making an argument with emphasis.

Warm-Up

From Deciding to Making the Case

Last class you weighed the proposal and said what you decided to do. Today that decision has to be defended in writing: a business case the board will read line by line. To persuade, you separate fact from opinion, and you put the one idea that matters most in the spotlight.

When you argue for a decision, how do you make the one key point stand out?

Today

Three Missions

1

Read a written business case and separate fact from opinion.

2

Master cleft sentences: It was ... that ...; What ... is ...; The reason ... is that ...

3

Argue the case out loud: emphasize what matters and answer the objections.

The Case for the Deal

The Case for the Deal

This memo makes the case for acquiring Nordic Sense. What makes the proposal compelling is speed: it is their proprietary sensor technology that would put AVRT in the medical market a full year sooner than building the same capability in-house. That is the bottom line. The merits are clear on paper, but a strong case must also substantiate its claims and answer the obvious objections, not just assert them.

The first objection is price. It is the valuation, not the idea, that worries the CFO, and that concern is fair. We should not take the founders' revenue figures at face value; an independent audit will substantiate them before any number is agreed. A second caveat is integration. What could slow both teams down is a clash of engineering cultures, so the plan sets aside a full quarter to manage it.

One counterargument deserves a direct rebuttal. Some directors assume that a larger rival will simply copy the technology. That assertion rests on a bias toward size. What the record actually shows is the opposite: it was reliability, not scale, that won AVRT its own clients. The reason we recommend green-lighting the deal is that the upside is real and the downside is manageable. Read fairly, the case is close to watertight.

WhatItthatreasonis
Cleft sentences split one idea into two clauses to EMPHASIZE one part. It-cleft: 'It was the lead time that won the deal' (emphasis on the lead time). What-cleft: 'What convinced the board was the track record' (emphasis on what follows 'was'). The reason ... is that: 'The reason we recommend it is that the upside is real' (never 'is because'). Emphasis makes a written or spoken argument land harder.

Chapter 4: The Code

Cleft & Emphasis

It was ... that ... · What ... is ... · The reason ... is that ...

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"It was the lead time that won the deal."

"What convinced the board was the track record."

"It is reliability that clients pay for."

"The reason we recommend it is that the upside is real."

Each sentence takes one idea and puts one part in the spotlight. How does it do it?

StructureFormExample
It-cleftIt + be + X + that / who + restIt was the lead time that won it.
What-cleftWhat + clause + be + XWhat won it was the lead time.
The reason ... is thatThe reason + clause + is that + restThe reason we back it is that it saves a year.

Use "that" (or "who" for people) in it-clefts, and always start with "It". After "The reason ..." use "is that", never "is because".

Language Focus

Which Structure?

AnalyseCleft and emphasis: which structure?
AWhat impressed the board was the track record.

DWhat we need now is the board's approval.
It-cleft: It + be + emphasized part + that/who + rest ('It was reliability that won them'). What-cleft: What + clause + be + emphasized part ('What won them was reliability'). Use 'The reason ... is that', never 'The reason ... is because'. Cleft and emphasis let you put the most important idea in the spotlight.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"Was the reliability that won the deal."

✓ RIGHT

"It was the reliability that won the deal."

✗ WRONG

"What convinced the board it was the track record."

✓ RIGHT

"What convinced the board was the track record."

✗ WRONG

"The reason we chose them is because of their tech."

✓ RIGHT

"The reason we chose them is that they own their tech."

Always start an it-cleft with "It". In a what-cleft, do not add an extra "it" before "was". After "The reason ..." use "is that", never "is because".
