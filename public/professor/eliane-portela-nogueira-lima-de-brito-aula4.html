What Real Fluency Sounds Like

Read for the Main Idea

Halfway There

Halfway There: What Real Fluency Sounds Like

Reaching the middle of an intensive program is a good moment to stop and reflect. Fluency is not about speaking fast or never making a mistake. It is about communicating your ideas clearly, even under pressure: being able to articulate a complex plan, clarify a point when someone looks confused, and outline your vision in a way people remember. The professionals who sound most confident are rarely the ones with a perfect accent; they are the ones who organize their thoughts and keep the conversation moving.

One secret of fluent speakers is that they use small phrases to buy time and guide the listener. Instead of freezing when they need a second to think, they say things like 'To give you a bit of context...' or 'What I mean is...'. These discourse markers do two jobs at once: they give the speaker thinking time, and they signal to the listener how the ideas connect. When speakers want to soften a disagreement, they add 'That said...' or 'Having said that...' before making their point.

Reflection also means being honest about what still needs work. After an important call, strong communicators debrief: they ask what went well, what was only developing, and what is now a priority. They write down clear action items, agree on who will follow up, and wrap up with a short summary. The main takeaway is simple: fluency grows fastest when you practice under real pressure and then review honestly what happened.

Chapter 4: Fluency Markers

Buy Time, Guide the Listener

Five discourse markers for real conversations

Discovery

Listen and Notice

"To give you a bit of context, I am opening a clinic next year."

"What I mean is, I need reliable support."

"That said, the budget is not unlimited."

"The reason I bring this up is that I want a real partnership."

MarkerWhat it does
To give you a bit of context...Opens a topic and sets the scene before your main point
What I mean is...Clarifies or rephrases so the listener follows you
The reason I bring this up is...Explains why the point matters
That said... / Having said that...Softens a contrast or disagreement before you make your point

These markers are fluency tools: they buy you a moment to think and signal to the listener how your ideas connect.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"The reason is because I want a partner."

✓ RIGHT

"The reason I bring this up is that I want a partner."

✗ WRONG

"That said that, the budget is limited."

✓ RIGHT

"That said, the budget is limited."

Discourse markers are fixed phrases. Say the whole phrase cleanly: "The reason I bring this up is that..." and "That said, ..." Do not add an extra "because" or "that".

Dialogue

The Video Call

G
Hi Eliane, thanks for joining the call. I am Greg, from the medical-technology team in Austin. So, tell me a little about your project.
E
Thanks, Greg. To give you a bit of context, I am a gynecologist, and I am opening a fertility clinic in São Paulo next year.
G
That sounds exciting. And what are you looking for in a technology partner?
E
The reason I bring this up is simple. I do not just want a supplier. What I mean is, I need reliable equipment and fast support.
G
That makes sense. Our incubators are state-of-the-art, and our team follows up within twenty-four hours.
E
That said, I have to be honest about the budget. Quality matters more to me than the lowest price.
G
I appreciate that. Let me outline a plan and send you the action items after this call.
E
Perfect. Could you also clarify the timeline for installation?
G
Of course. To wrap up, I will email a full proposal, and we will debrief next week. Thank you, Eliane.
