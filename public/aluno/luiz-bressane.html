Grammar Tip

Grammar

Present Perfect Simple vs Present Perfect Continuous

Present Perfect Simple (have/has + past participle) — used for completed actions with a present result, or experiences up to now.

Usado para ações concluídas com resultado no presente, ou experiências até agora.

+I have handled hundreds of cases.
−She has not testified in this type of case before.
?Have you ever overturned a ruling?

Present Perfect Continuous (have/has + been + -ing) — used for ongoing or recently finished actions, emphasizing duration or continuity.

Usado para ações em andamento ou recentemente finalizadas, enfatizando duração ou continuidade.

+I have been working on this case for three months.
−They have not been negotiating a plea bargain.
?Has the judge been considering the jurisdiction issue?

Key difference: Use Simple for results and completed quantity (how many/how much). Use Continuous for process and duration (how long/since when).
Diferença-chave: Use Simple para resultados e quantidade. Use Continuous para processo e duração.

At the Washington symposium, Luiz joins a comparative law panel with Professor James Crawford, who teaches constitutional law at Georgetown University. The panel, which focuses on differences between legal traditions, attracts scholars from around the world. Common law, which originated in England, relies on judicial precedent and case decisions. Grammar Tip

Grammar

Relative Clauses — Defining vs Non-defining

Defining Relative Clauses (who/which/that) — restrict meaning; they identify which person or thing we mean. No commas.

Orações restritivas — identificam qual pessoa ou coisa queremos dizer. Sem vírgulas.

PeopleJudges who serve on the Supreme Court interpret the constitution.
ThingsCountries that follow common law rely on judicial precedent.
ThingsThe statute which governs extradition was amended last year.

Non-defining Relative Clauses (who/which) — add extra information that can be removed without changing the meaning. With commas. Cannot use that.

Orações explicativas — adicionam informação extra que pode ser removida. Com vírgulas. Não se usa "that".

PeopleProfessor Crawford, who teaches at Georgetown, is an expert on comparative law.
ThingsArbitration, which is growing in popularity, resolves disputes outside court.
ThingsHabeas corpus, which protects individual freedom, exists in both traditions.

Key difference: Defining = essential (no commas, can use that). Non-defining = extra info (commas, NEVER that).
Diferença-chave: Restritiva = essencial (sem vírgulas, pode usar "that"). Explicativa = informação extra (com vírgulas, NUNCA "that").

Grammar Tip

Grammar
Second Conditional — Hypothetical / Unreal Present or Future

Use: To talk about imaginary, hypothetical, or unlikely situations in the present or future.
Uso: Para falar sobre situações hipotéticas, imaginárias ou improváveis no presente ou futuro.

FormStructure
AffirmativeIf + subject + past simple, subject + would + infinitive
NegativeIf + subject + did not + base form, subject + would not + infinitive
QuestionWould + subject + infinitive + if + subject + past simple?

Examples / Exemplos:

  • If the judge granted clemency, the inmate would be released. (Se o juiz concedesse clemência, o preso seria solto.)
  • If we invested in rehabilitation, recidivism would decrease. (Se investíssemos em reabilitação, a reincidência diminuiria.)
  • The company would not face sanctions if it ensured compliance. (A empresa não enfrentaria sanções se garantisse conformidade.)
  • Would you recommend probation if the offense were a misdemeanor? (Você recomendaria provação se a infração fosse uma contravenção?)

Common Mistake: Using "would" in the IF clause.
✗ If I would have more evidence, I would win the case.
✓ If I had more evidence, I would win the case.
NUNCA use "would" na cláusula IF do second conditional.

Grammar Tip

Grammar
Third Conditional — Hypothetical Past Situations

Use: To talk about imaginary situations in the past and their hypothetical results. The event did NOT happen — we are imagining a different past.
Uso: Para falar sobre situações imaginárias no passado e seus resultados hipotéticos. O evento NÃO aconteceu — estamos imaginando um passado diferente.

FormStructure
AffirmativeIf + subject + had + past participle, subject + would have + past participle
NegativeIf + subject + had not + past participle, subject + would not have + past participle
QuestionWould + subject + have + past participle + if + subject + had + past participle?

Examples / Exemplos:

  • If the jury had reviewed the evidence, they would have acquitted the defendant. (Se o júri tivesse revisado as evidências, teria absolvido o réu.)
  • If the witness had not committed perjury, the conviction would not have happened. (Se a testemunha não tivesse cometido perjúrio, a condenação não teria acontecido.)
  • The inmate would have been exonerated sooner if the governor had commuted the sentence. (O preso teria sido inocentado antes se o governador tivesse comutado a sentença.)
  • Would the outcome have been different if the attorney had issued a subpoena? (O resultado teria sido diferente se o advogado tivesse emitido uma intimação?)

Time Expressions / Expressões temporais:

  • back then, at that time, in those days, years ago, before the trial, prior to the verdict

Common Mistake: Using "would of" instead of "would have".
✗ If I had known, I would of helped.
✓ If I had known, I would have helped.
"Would of" NÃO existe em inglês. O som é parecido com "would've" (contração de "would have"), mas a forma escrita correta é SEMPRE "would have".
