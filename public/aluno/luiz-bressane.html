Grammar

Relative Clauses — Defining vs Non-defining

Defining Relative Clauses (who/which/that) — restrict meaning; they identify which person or thing we mean. No commas.

Orações restritivas — identificam qual pessoa ou coisa queremos dizer. Sem vírgulas.

PeopleJudges who serve on the Supreme Court interpret the constitution.
ThingsCountries that follow common law rely on judicial precedent.
ThingsThe statute which governs extradition was amended last year.

Non-defining Relative Clauses (who/which) — add extra information that can be removed without changing the meaning. With commas. Cannot use that.

Orações explicativas — adicionam informação extra que pode ser removida. Com vírgulas. Não se usa "that".

PeopleProfessor Crawford, who teaches at Georgetown, is an expert on comparative law.
ThingsArbitration, which is growing in popularity, resolves disputes outside court.
ThingsHabeas corpus, which protects individual freedom, exists in both traditions.

Key difference: Defining = essential (no commas, can use that). Non-defining = extra info (commas, NEVER that).
Diferença-chave: Restritiva = essencial (sem vírgulas, pode usar "that"). Explicativa = informação extra (com vírgulas, NUNCA "that").

Use: To talk about imaginary, hypothetical, or unlikely situations in the present or future.
Uso: Para falar sobre situações hipotéticas, imaginárias ou improváveis no presente ou futuro.

FormStructure
AffirmativeIf + subject + past simple, subject + would + infinitive
NegativeIf + subject + did not + base form, subject + would not + infinitive

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip — Block 1 Review

Grammar

Block 1 Grammar Summary

1. Present Perfect Simple (have/has + past participle) — completed actions with present relevance.

Ações concluídas com relevância no presente.

I have handled hundreds of cases.

2. Present Perfect Continuous (have/has + been + -ing) — ongoing actions emphasizing duration.

Ações em andamento com ênfase na duração.

I have been working on this case for three months.

3. Relative Clauses — defining (who/which/that, no commas) vs non-defining (who/which, with commas).

Restritivas (sem vírgula) vs explicativas (com vírgula).

Defining: Judges who serve on the Supreme Court interpret the constitution.
Non-defining: Arbitration, which is growing in popularity, resolves disputes outside court.

4. Second Conditional (If + past simple, would + infinitive) — hypothetical present/future.

Situações hipotéticas no presente/futuro.

If the court imposed harsher penalties, witnesses would think twice.

5. Third Conditional (If + had + past participle, would have + past participle) — imaginary past.

Passado imaginário — o evento NÃO aconteceu.

If the prosecution had secured the deposition, the case would not have been dismissed.

6. Mixed Conditionals — combine different time frames (past → present or present → past).

Combinam tempos diferentes (passado → presente ou presente → passado).

If accountability were built into every institution, fewer wrongful convictions would have occurred.

7. Narrative Tenses (Past Simple + Past Continuous + Past Perfect) — telling stories and recounting events.

Narrando histórias e relatando eventos.

The witness was giving her deposition when the attorney interrupted. She had already signed the statement.
