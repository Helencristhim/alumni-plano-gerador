Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Perfect vs Simple Past

GRAMMAR

When to use each tense in professional contexts.

TenseUseExample
Simple PastCompleted action at a specific time in the pastI joined Brink's in 2022.
Signal words (Past)yesterday, last week/month/year, in 2022, agoWe exceeded the target last quarter.
Present PerfectExperience connected to the present, ongoing resultI have overseen multiple projects.
Signal words (Perfect)since, for, already, recently, so far, this yearWe have made headway since January.
Key differenceSimple past = WHEN it happened matters. Present perfect = the RESULT or EXPERIENCE matters, not when.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Third Conditional

GRAMMAR

How to talk about imaginary past situations and their imaginary results.

PartStructureExample
IF clauseIf + subject + had + past participleIf we had launched earlier...
Result clausesubject + would have + past participle...we would have hit the target.
Negative IFIf + subject + had not + past participleIf we had not lost headcount...
Negative resultsubject + would not have + past participle...we would not have missed the target.
Key pointThe third conditional talks about the PAST. The situation is IMAGINARY. It did NOT happen. WOULD goes in the result, NEVER in the IF clause.

Vocabulary

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Modal Perfects

GRAMMAR

How to express judgments, certainty, and speculation about past events.

ModalStructureMeaningExample
should haveshould + have + past participleCriticism or regret: it was the right thing but did NOT happen"They should have aligned the team."
must havemust + have + past participleHigh certainty (90%+): I am almost sure this happened"The delay must have affected Q4."
might havemight + have + past participleLow certainty (40-60%): it is possible but uncertain"The client might have misunderstood."
could havecould + have + past participleMissed opportunity: it was possible but did not happen"We could have avoided the friction."
Key rule: After modal + HAVE, always use the PAST PARTICIPLE. Never add "to" (should have to align) and never use the base form (must have affect).
