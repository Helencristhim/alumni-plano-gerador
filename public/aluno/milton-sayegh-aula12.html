Chapter 3

What You're Supposed To Do

Modals of advice & expectation

Grammar Discovery

Four Ways to Advise

All four guide behavior — but how strong, and whose rule?

You should arrive five minutes early. (good advice)

You ought to state your value clearly. (advice, a little more formal)

You are supposed to be seated when the meeting starts. (the culture's expectation)

You had better not arrive late. (advice + a warning)

should / ought to = advice → supposed to = expected rule → had better = advice with a warning.

Grammar Rule

Modals of Advice & Expectation

FormStrengthUse it for
should / ought to + baseAdvice"You should confirm the time." / "You ought to be early."
be supposed to + baseExpected rule"You're supposed to RSVP before Friday." (the norm, not your opinion)
had better (not) + baseAdvice + warning"You'd better not be late — they will notice." (a consequence is implied)
Watch the formNo "to" after should / had better"You should go" (NOT "should to go"); "ought TO go"; "supposed TO go".
Watch Out

Common Mistake

You should to arrive five minutes early.
You should arrive five minutes early.

After should and had better, use the base verb with no to. Only ought to and be supposed to keep the to.

Practice

Complete the Advice

"You _____ arrive a few minutes early for a New York meeting." → should (advice, base verb, no to)
"You are _____ to confirm your attendance by Friday." → supposed (be supposed to = an expected rule)
"You had _____ not undersell your work in the room." → better (had better = advice + warning)
"You ought _____ keep the small talk short with American buyers." → to (ought TO + base verb)

Claire briefs Milton before his New York trip

Dialogue

Milton & Claire

C
Milton, before your trip, a little advice on our etiquette here. Can I be honest?
M
Please. I'd rather know. What should I do differently?
C
Our punctuality is strict. The small talk is short, then we go straight to numbers.
M
Understood. So I'm supposed to be in my seat at nine, and I ought to state my price early.
C
Exactly. And directness is fine here — we read it as courtesy, not rudeness.
M
Good to know. Then I'd better not undersell our work — I'll state our value with confidence and build rapport through results.

Practice

Your Turn to Advise

From quick tips to full cultural advice

Quick Fire

Advise on the Spot

1 / 5

1. A colleague plans to arrive "fashionably late" to a New York meeting. Advise them.

"You should arrive five minutes early — you're supposed to be seated when it starts."

2. Your colleague wants to make twenty minutes of small talk before business. Warn them.

"You'd better keep the small talk short — they'll want to get to the numbers."

3. They feel rude saying the price is too low. Reassure them about directness.

"You ought to say it clearly — here directness reads as courtesy, not rudeness."

4. After a trade fair, they forget to follow up. Remind them of the expectation.

"You're supposed to follow up within a day — that's how networking becomes rapport."

5. They tend to undersell the brand to seem humble. Give a firm warning.

"You'd better not undersell our work — you should state what it's worth with confidence."

Spot the Error

Fix the Modal

"You should to arrive five minutes early."
"You should arrive five minutes early." (no "to" after should)
"You ought state your price clearly."
"You ought to state your price clearly." (ought TO + base verb)
"You had better to keep the small talk short."
"You had better keep the small talk short." (no "to" after had better)
"You are supposed be seated at nine."
"You are supposed to be seated at nine." (supposed TO + base verb)
