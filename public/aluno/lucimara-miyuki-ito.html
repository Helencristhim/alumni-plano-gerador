Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Simple vs Present Continuous vs Present Perfect (for / since)

GRAMMAR

The three structures behind any personal introduction in English.

FormUseExample
Present Simple
I / you / we + verb (he/she + s)		Your routine, your job title, what is always true.I work in the chemical industry.
Present Continuous
am / is / are + verb-ing		What is happening NOW, this month — not the routine.Right now, I am preparing a registration.
Present Perfect
have / has + past participle		It started in the past and it CONTINUES today. Duration up to now (for / since).I have worked here for twenty years.
Negativehave / has + not + past participle. In speech, contracted: haven't.I haven't been to Lyon this year.
QuestionHow long + have + subject + past participle?How long have you been in the industry?
for vs sincefor + a period of time (for twenty years) · since + a starting point (since 2015)

Watch out -- the classic mistake: in your first language, duration up to now uses the PRESENT tense, so "I work here for twenty years" feels right. In English it is wrong: duration up to now never uses the present simple. The correct form is "I have worked here for twenty years" (in speech, I've worked). And remember: responsible FOR, never "responsible of".

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Perfect with just / already / yet (and the contrast with the Past Simple)

GRAMMAR

Three small words that change everything.

WordUseExample
just
have + just + past participle		It happened a few minutes ago. It goes BEFORE the past participle.I have just landed. (I’ve just landed.)
already
have + already + past participle		It is done — often earlier than expected. Affirmative sentences. It goes BEFORE the past participle.I have already completed the online check-in.
yet
haven’t + past participle + yet		Not up to now (negative) or "so far?" (question). It always goes AT THE END of the sentence.I haven’t checked in yet. / Have you checked in yet?
Negativehave / has + not + past participle. In speech: haven’t / hasn’t.My bag hasn’t arrived yet.
QuestionHave + subject + past participle ... yet?Have you picked up your bags yet?
Past Simple
verb in the past		When you SAY THE TIME or tell the sequence (a story), English needs the past simple: "I landed at nine and took a cab." NEVER "I have landed at nine."

Watch out -- the two classic mistakes: (1) yet goes AT THE END: "I haven’t checked in yet", never "I haven’t yet checked in" in everyday speech. (2) With an explicit time or date, the present perfect is FORBIDDEN: "I landed at nine" (right) and not "I have landed at nine" (wrong). The practical rule: when WHEN matters → past simple; when THE RESULT NOW matters → present perfect.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Past Simple vs. Present Perfect (the travel story)

GRAMMAR

Two trips, two tenses. One question tells you which one to use.

TenseWhen you use itExample
Past Simple
went · took · paid		A CLOSED moment. You say WHEN it happened (in 2023, last night, yesterday, ten minutes ago), or you tell the sequence of a story."When I went to New York in 2023, I took a cab everywhere."
Present Perfect
have / has + past participle		The trip is still OPEN, or the experience covers your whole life. The moment does NOT matter. Signal words: already, never, ever, so far, this time, twice."This time I've already used the subway twice."
Negativehave / has + not + past participle. In speech: haven't / hasn't."I haven't figured out the subway map yet."
QuestionHave + subject + ever + past participle? (= at any time in your life)"Have you ever tried a New York diner?"
THE PAST PARTICIPLE
(the piece that trips you up)		After have / has, the verb CHANGES FORM: take → taken (never "have took"), go → gone, eat → eaten, pay → paid, buy → bought, do → done.
The one testAsk yourself ONE question: can this number still change today? If it can → present perfect. If it is closed and it has a date → past simple. 2023 is closed. September is still open.

Watch out -- the classic mistake: (1) An explicit date with the present perfect is IMPOSSIBLE: the moment you say in 2023, the verb has to go back to the past simple — "I went", never "I have gone... in 2023". (2) The wrong participle after have: "I have never took" is the mistake that gives your level away. The correct form is "I have never taken".
