What Changed in Access

What Changed in Access

Two years ago, a patient with a serious skin disease in the public sector in Brazil used to wait a long time. There used to be very little access to modern treatment, almost no public funding, and not much evidence in Portuguese for the doctors. Many patients used to pay for everything alone, and many simply stopped.

Today the picture is different, and the numbers tell the story. The patient population is large, and it is growing. There is a lot of new evidence for the higher dose, and several new programs give better coverage in the private sector. There are still a few problems: in the public sector there is not much funding yet, and the reimbursement decision is slow. But a manager who reports data well does not hide the difficult part.

So how do you talk about all of this in a global meeting? You separate two families of words. For the things you can count one by one -- patients, doctors, programs, centers -- you ask how many, and you say a few or several. For the things you cannot count -- data, evidence, access, funding, coverage -- you ask how much, and you say a little. And when you compare the past with today, one small chunk does the work: it used to be like this; now it is different.

Chapter 4: The Code

Can You Count It?

How many patients? · How much evidence? · It used to be different.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"How many patients can take this treatment?" — 1, 2, 3...

"How much evidence do we have for the dose?" — you cannot count it

"There used to be very little access in the public sector." — before, not now

"Now there are several new programs." — more than a few

All four are correct. Look at the purple words — what makes English choose each one?

The list you must knowYou cannot count these, ever: data, evidence, access, funding, coverage, information. They have no plural and no "a": never "an evidence", never "two datas".

In Portuguese, "muito" is one word for everything, and "antigamente" is a whole word. In English: one question splits much from many — can I count it? — and one small chunk carries the past: used to.

Language Focus

Can You Count It, or Not?

AnalyseCan you count it, or not?
AHow many patients can take this treatment?
BHow much evidence do we have for the new dose?
CThere are several new programs in the private sector.
DThere is not much funding in the public sector yet.
A and C count things one by one: one patient, two patients; one program, two programs. They take many and several. B and D cannot be counted one by one: you never say "two evidences" or "three fundings". They take much and a little. In Portuguese, "muito" is one word for both -- and that single difference is worth five minutes of your life.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"We have many evidence for the new dose."
"We have a lot of evidence for the new dose."
"How much patients can take this treatment?"
"How many patients can take this treatment?"
"There used to been almost no access."
"There used to be almost no access."

One question decides the quantity: can I count it? And after used to, the verb stays in its base form: used to be, never "used to been".
