The Meeting, Relayed

The Meeting, Relayed

On Thursday, AVRT's board met to review the year. As CEO, Izabel set the agenda, chaired the meeting, and later circulated the minutes to the whole leadership team. The chair opened by thanking everyone. He said that the results had been strong, and he explained that the sensor business had grown faster than the board had expected. He added that the North American launch was now the top priority for the following year.

The CFO then briefed the board and gave a short recap of the numbers. She reported that the margin had improved, but she warned that overhead would rise if the company opened a second plant too soon. A client who had joined by video was next on the agenda. Izabel told the board that the client had asked whether AVRT could shorten its lead time, and whether the team would commit to a firm delivery date. The board agreed to clarify those two points before signing anything.

Not everything was decided. One director asked if the company should defer the second plant until the volume was certain. After some discussion, the board reached a consensus: they would endorse the launch, but defer the new plant for six months. Before closing, Izabel assigned two action items. She told the operations lead to review the delivery schedule, and she asked the finance team to circulate an updated forecast. She also promised to debrief the sales team the next morning. The key takeaway, she wrote in the minutes, was simple: the board backed the plan, but wanted the numbers confirmed first.

Reported speech: when we relay what someone said, the verb usually shifts back one tense (backshift). Present -> past ('is' -> 'was', 'has grown' -> 'had grown'); will -> would; can -> could. Use 'tell + someone' but 'say' with no person ('She told the board...', 'He said that...'). For reported questions, use 'ask if / whether' and keep normal word order: 'She asked whether we could deliver' (NOT 'asked can we deliver').

Chapter 4: The Code

Reported Speech

backshift · say vs tell · reported questions and commands

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"The CFO said that revenue had grown."

"Andy explained that they would sign."

"She asked whether we could deliver on time."

"The chair said, 'The budget is approved.'"

Three sentences share a structure. One is different. Which one, and why?

OriginalReported (backshift)Example
present -> pastis/are -> was/were; has grown -> had grownShe said the margin had improved.
will / can -> would / couldthe modal shifts back one stepHe said they would sign.
say vs telltell + someone; say (to) with no personShe told me; he said that...
reported question / commandask if/whether (no inversion); told + sb + toShe asked whether we could deliver.
Language Focus

Which Form?

AnalyseReported speech: which form?
AThe chair said that the results had been strong.
BThe CFO reported that the margin had improved.
CThe client asked whether AVRT could shorten its lead time.
DIzabel told the finance team to circulate an updated forecast.
Backshift: present -> past, will -> would, can -> could, is/are -> was/were, has/have -> had. Use 'told + someone' (told the board) but 'said' without a person (said that...). Reported questions use 'if / whether' with normal word order and no 'do' support. Reported commands use 'told + someone + to + base verb': She told us to review the budget.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"She said me that the margin improved."

✓ RIGHT

"She told me that the margin had improved."

✗ WRONG

"The client asked can we deliver on time."

✓ RIGHT

"The client asked whether we could deliver on time."

✗ WRONG

"He said the plan is ready and we will start."

✓ RIGHT

"He said the plan was ready and we would start."

Use "tell + someone" but "say" with no person. In reported questions, use "if / whether" with normal word order. And shift the verb back one tense: is -> was, will -> would, can -> could.

Dialogue

Relaying the Board Meeting to Andy

A
Izabel, I missed the board meeting. Can you relay what was said?
I
Of course. The chair said that the year had been strong, and he explained that the sensor business had grown faster than the board expected. I circulated the minutes this morning.
A
Good. And what did the CFO report about the numbers?
I
She briefed the board and gave a short recap. She reported that the margin had improved, but she warned that overhead would rise if we opened a second plant too soon.
A
And the client on the call — what did they ask?
I
They asked whether we could shorten the lead time, and whether we would commit to a firm date. The board agreed to clarify both points before signing.
A
So what did the board finally decide?
I
They reached a consensus: they would endorse the launch, but defer the new plant for six months. I told the operations lead to review the schedule, and the key takeaway was to confirm the numbers first.
