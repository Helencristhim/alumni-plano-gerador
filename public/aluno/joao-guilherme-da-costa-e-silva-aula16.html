Impact

Three Ways to Answer the Same Claim

Rank them, say why, then click to compare

A. "Well… maybe we were a little late on the drawings, I'm not sure, we can look into it…"
Conceding. It sounds reasonable and humble, but the moment you agree to a point you have not checked, you pick up the burden of proof and carry it for them. The back-charge conversation is now lost — you conceded before you read the record.
B. "That's absurd. It's your equipment that failed, not our approval. Don't put this on us."
Attacking. You may be right on the facts, but you have made it personal, and next year you still have to work with this supplier. You win the argument and lose the relationship — and a defensive tone reads as someone who is not sure of the record.
C. "I understand the concern, and I've taken it seriously. But the dated sign-off log doesn't support it — and I'd rather review it together than trade blame."
Holding ground. You acknowledge the concern once, you hold firm on the facts with the record, and you keep the door open. Firm on the facts, soft on the person. This is the whole skill of tonight.

The Tool

The Defender's Move

1 — Acknowledge

Once, genuinely. "I understand the concern, and I've taken it seriously." It costs nothing and it lowers the temperature.

2 — Hold on the Facts

The burden of proof is on them. Put the record on the table. "The drawings were approved on the fourth; the units arrived six weeks later."

3 — Stay Diplomatic

Firm on the point, soft on the person. "To some extent I understand the pressure — but I can't concede what the record doesn't support."

4 — A Next Step

Not a verdict. "Let's review the sign-off log together before anyone talks about a back-charge." The door stays open.

Most engineers either concede too early — and pick up the burden of proof — or turn defensive and lose the relationship. Acknowledge once, hold on the facts, stay warm, offer a next step. That is what holding your ground actually looks like.

Chapter 3: The Code

The Verb That Sets the Terms

The mandative subjunctive: "It is essential that the supplier submit" — base form, no -s.

Grammar Discovery

Why Does the Verb Look Wrong?

"It is essential that the supplier submit the revised test plan."

"Motiva requires that Ibersign account for the two failed units."

"We recommend that the team be notified in writing."

"We request that the units not be shipped until the fix is confirmed."

Every green verb is in the base form — submit, not submits; be, not is; not be shipped, not does not ship. What word comes just before "that" each time — and why does the ordinary -s and do/does disappear?

TriggerSubjunctive formWhy
it is essential / critical / important that…the supplier submit (base form, no -s)The verb loses its ending for every subject → it states a requirement, not an opinion.
require / recommend / insist / request / propose that…the team be notified (be, not is/are)Verbs of requiring take the same base form → be is the subjunctive of be for all persons.
the negativethe units not be shipped (not + base)No do/does. Just not + base verb → "not ship", "not be filed".
compare: ordinary present"the supplier submits" → my opinion"the supplier submit" → a stated term. Same words, very different force.
Why it mattersIn a dispute, the subjunctive lets you be firm and impersonal at once. "It is essential that the schedule remain unchanged" is not an attack and not a plea — it is a condition you are setting. It is the grammar of a person who sets the terms, not one who reacts to them.
Meaning

One Form Sets the Terms

The mandative subjunctive -- how a formal requirement is built
it is essential / critical / important that + subject + BASE verb
A requirement, not a request

After expressions of urgency or importance, the verb stays in the BASE form for every subject -- no -s on he/she/it. "It is essential that the supplier submit the revised plan" (not submits). "It is critical that the schedule remain unchanged" (not remains). The base form is what makes it sound like a norm you are stating, not a favor you are asking.

recommend / require / insist / demand / propose / request that + subject + BASE verb
A formal instruction

The same base form follows verbs of requesting and requiring. "We recommend that the team be notified in writing." "The client requires that every unit be inspected before shipment." "I insist that the log be reviewed by both sides." Notice BE, not is/are -- the subjunctive of the verb be is simply be for all persons.

the negative: subject + NOT + BASE verb
How you forbid, formally

The subjunctive negative is just not + base -- no do/does. "It is essential that the supplier not ship the units before sign-off" (not "does not ship"). "We ask that the claim not be filed until the record is reviewed." The missing do/does is exactly what a Portuguese speaker adds by reflex.

why it holds ground
It states terms instead of pleading

Compare "The supplier needs to submit the plan" with "It is essential that the supplier submit the plan." The first is your opinion about what should happen; the second is a condition you are setting. In a dispute, the subjunctive lets you be firm and impersonal at once -- you are not attacking the person, you are stating the requirement the contract already implies.

One rule runs through all of it: after these expressions the verb loses its ending and its do/does. It is essential that he submit -- not submits, not does not submit but not submit. We recommend that it be inspected -- not is inspected. The moment you hear yourself add an -s or an is, you have slipped back into the ordinary present and lost the formal, requirement-setting force. In a claim meeting, that force is the whole point: it is the difference between asking a supplier for a favor and reminding them of a term.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"It is essential that the supplier submits the revised plan."
"It is essential that the supplier submit the revised plan."
"We recommend that the team is notified in writing."

After it is essential / we require / we recommend that, the verb stays in the base form for every subject: submit, not submits; be notified, not is notified. The reflex to add an -s or an is comes straight from Portuguese agreement — and it turns a stated requirement back into a casual opinion.

Reading

The Corrective-Action Letter

Corrective Action Request & Response to Extension-of-Time Claim -- Motiva Signaling to Ibersign S.A., Line 4 (adapted, written for João's package)

Position. Motiva has received Ibersign's notice of a four-week schedule overrun on the Line 4 interlocking, together with an extension-of-time claim attributing the delay to Motiva's late approval of the interface drawings. Motiva acknowledges the concern and has reviewed it in full. We dispute the claim. The dated record does not support it, and the burden of proof for an extension of time rests with the party requesting it. This letter sets out our position and the corrective action we require, without prejudice to Motiva's rights under the penalty clause.

Findings. The interface drawings were approved on 4 March, a date recorded in the shared sign-off log and countersigned by Ibersign's own project engineer. The axle-counter units left the factory in Shenzhen six weeks after that approval, and two of the units delivered failed the incoming inspection carried out at our depot on 19 April. The timeline therefore attributes the overrun to the late delivery and the failed inspection, not to the drawing approval; there is no concurrent delay on Motiva's side. The claim that Motiva approved the drawings late is, on the record, unfounded. We are ready to substantiate every date in this paragraph from the log.

Corrective action and next step. Motiva requires that Ibersign submit a revised test plan within ten working days, and it is essential that the plan account for the two failed units and their firmware revision. We recommend that both parties review the sign-off log together before any discussion of a back-charge, and we propose, without prejudice, that the cost of the retest be shared pending that review. It is critical that the commissioning date remain unchanged; to that end, we request that the replacement units not be shipped until the firmware fix is confirmed in writing. Motiva remains committed to a constructive resolution and reserves its rights in full while that resolution is pursued.

Motiva Signaling -- formal correspondence, Line 4 (fictional, written for João's package)
Count the subjunctives holding this letter up: "Motiva requires that Ibersign submit", "it is essential that the plan account for", "we recommend that both parties review", "that the cost be shared", "it is critical that the date remain unchanged", "we request that the units not be shipped". Every one is a base-form verb after a word of requirement -- submit, account, review, be, remain, not be shipped. That is what lets the letter dispute a claim and stay coldly professional at the same time: it states terms, it does not plead. It is the exact move you produce tonight.

Three Rules for Holding the Line

A claims advisor. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What is the first rule — separating the claim from the person — and why does it help?
When the supplier says the delay is your fault, that is a negotiating position, not an attack. The engineer who takes it personally defends his character instead of the facts — and the facts are the only thing that wins. Hear the claim as information about what she wants, and your shoulders come down.
2. What is the difference between acknowledging and conceding, and why does it matter?
Acknowledging costs nothing ("I understand why you see it that way, let me look at the record") and keeps the burden of proof on the party making the claim. Conceding ("yes, that was probably our fault") picks the burden up and carries it for them. Never concede what you have not verified; if a claim is unfounded, say so plainly but without heat.
3. What is the third rule about "the grammar of authority", and what example does he give?
The subjunctive. "It is essential that the supplier submit the revised test plan" — the base form (submit, not submits) makes it sound like a norm, not a favor. "It is critical that the schedule remain unchanged." "We recommend that the team be notified." It is the language of a person who sets the terms, not one who reacts to them.
