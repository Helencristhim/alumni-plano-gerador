Sound

The Word That Disappears

In real speech, would is not a word. It is one sound, glued to the pronoun.

I'd take the ferry. — "I would" becomes ONE syllable: ayd
She'd probably say no. — sheed, not "she would"
We'd need three more days. — weed need
If I were you, I'd stay in Brooklyn. — the whole opening runs together: fyuh-wur-yoo-ayd

Chapter 3: The Code

The World That Doesn't Exist

One if. Two distances. English hears which one you mean.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"If my flight is canceled, I will rebook at the desk."

"If I had a free week in New York, I would walk a different neighborhood every day."

"If I took the red-eye, I would gain a whole day."

"If I were you, I would stay in Brooklyn."

One sentence is blue — it can happen tomorrow. Three are purple — they cannot. What happened to the verbs in the purple ones?

ConditionalThe if halfThe result halfMeaning
First
(lesson 4)		if + present simple
if my flight is canceled		will + verb
I will rebook		REAL. It can happen tomorrow.
Second
(tonight)		if + past simple
if I had a free week		would + verb
I would walk		UNREAL. Imagined, or unlikely. It is not true — yet.
The past is not the pastIn the second conditional, the past tense is not TIME — it is distance from reality. And to be becomes were for everybody: If I were you, never "if I was you". Would never enters the if half.

One question decides everything: can this really happen? Yes → will. No → would.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"If I would have more time, I would visit the MoMA every day."
"If I had more time, I would visit the MoMA every day."
"If I was you, I would take the red-eye."
"If I were you, I would take the red-eye."

Would lives in the result half, and only there. And with to be, the second conditional says were — for I, for he, for she, for everybody.

The Wish

I Wish I Had...

Same trick, different door: the past tense means it is not true right now.

"I wish I had more time in this city."
"I wish I understood every word a New Yorker said."
"I wish I were braver when I speak English."
"I wish I could take the whole week off."

"I wish I have" does not exist. If it were true, you would not be wishing for it.

Chapter 4: The Perfect Week

The Proposal and the Money

A dream only becomes a trip when somebody puts a number next to it.

The Artifact

Your Proposal

HUDSON TRAVEL · NEW YORK
Prepared for: Lucimara Ito · September 12–17, 2026
OPTION A — Midtown$3,180
Sunday morning flight · 5 nights, hotel on 45th Street · everything within 10 minutes · 2 museums included
OPTION B — Brooklyn + Manhattan$3,240
Saturday red-eye (lands 7:10 a.m.) · 2 nights Brooklyn, 3 nights Manhattan · ferry pass · nothing booked after 6 p.m.
Difference$60

Note from Nathan: the price is almost the same. The week is not. — N.

1. If you chose Option B, what would you gain — and what would you lose?
"If I chose Option B, I would gain a whole day, because the red-eye lands at seven. I would lose one night of sleep."
2. The two options cost almost the same. So what is the real difference?
"Option A is safe and central. Option B is off the beaten path, and it leaves every evening open."
3. Which one would you take, and why?
"I'd take Option B. If I stayed in Brooklyn, I would see the city that most visitors never see — and I would still be twenty minutes from Manhattan."

The Podcast

No information, no numbers — only what she would do, and what she wishes. Harder than it sounds.

0:000:00

Back to New York — nowhere new. She would just do it completely differently.

Listening Strategy

Catch the Invisible Word

Stop listening for would. Start listening for a d that arrives too early.

If I were you, I'd take the ferry.
I'll take the ferry. — real, and it is happening today
She'd probably say no, but I'd ask anyway.
Sorry — would you, or will you? I lost that one.

And when you truly cannot tell: ask. "Would you, or will you?" is a question a native speaker asks too.

Quick Fire

Answer at Full Speed

1 / 6

The travel agent asks you the big question: if you had one free week in New York, what would you do with it?

  • If I had one free week, I would walk a different neighborhood every day.
  • Answer with a full second conditional -- If + past simple, would + verb.

Two flights, same price. One leaves at midnight and lands at seven. Say what you prefer, and why.

  • I would rather take the red-eye -- if I landed at seven, I would gain a whole day.
  • Would rather + base verb: I would rather arrive early than lose a Sunday.

Your budget is generous, but not unlimited. Say where you would spend more, and where you would spend less.

  • I would splurge on two great dinners, and I would cut back on the hotel.
  • If we cut back on taxis, we would stretch the budget into one more night.

A colleague at the Lyon fair offers you a spontaneous side trip on Saturday. React, and say what you would do with the day.

  • If I had Saturday free, I would take the detour with you -- I love an unplanned day.
  • In an ideal world, I would go somewhere off the beaten path, not to another museum.

Someone asks you what you would change about your English. Answer with a wish.

  • I wish I understood every word a New Yorker said.
  • I wish I had more time to listen -- but I do have eighty minutes in the car every day.

Now the real one: your flight tonight is canceled. Careful -- this one can actually happen.

  • If my flight is canceled, I will rebook at the desk and ask for a voucher.
  • Real and possible = first conditional (will). Imagined = second conditional (would).

1. The red-eye and a full day in the city  |  The morning flight and a real night of sleep

2. Five nights in Midtown  |  Two nights in Brooklyn, three in Manhattan
"I'd rather split the week than stay in Midtown for five nights. If I stayed in Brooklyn, I'd see a different city."
3. One great dinner  |  Five ordinary ones
"I'd rather splurge on one great dinner than eat badly five times. I'd cut back on the hotel to pay for it."

"I'd rather spend the day alone in English. If I took the tour in Portuguese, I would understand everything — and learn nothing."

Hudson Travel, Six in the Evening

N
Hudson Travel, this is Nathan. So — September. Tell me the trip you actually want, not the one you think you should book.
L
Five days, and they are mine. If I had one perfect week in New York, I would spend it walking. Not one museum a day — one neighborhood a day.
N
Good. Then I'd put you in Brooklyn for two nights. It's off the beaten path for most of my clients, and you'd still be twenty minutes from Manhattan.
L
The line is bad — let me read that back to you: two nights in Brooklyn, twenty minutes from Manhattan. Is that right?
N
That's right. Now, the flight. If you took the red-eye on Saturday, you'd land at seven in the morning and you'd gain a whole day.
L
I would rather arrive early and be tired than lose a Sunday. Let's take the red-eye. What would that do to the budget?
N
It saves you about two hundred dollars. If I were you, I'd put that money into the food, not into the room. Most people splurge on the hotel, and then they eat badly for five days.
L
I like that. If we cut back on the hotel, we'd stretch the budget into two great dinners — and I'd keep one evening completely open.
N
Open for what? Most of my clients want every hour on the calendar.
L
Open for the thing I haven't planned. The best night of my last trip was completely spontaneous — a detour, really. I wish I planned less and looked more. So this time, one evening stays empty.
Comprehension

What Did Nathan Say?

1. Where would he put her for two nights, and why?
Brooklyn — it is off the beaten path for most of his clients, and she would still be twenty minutes from Manhattan.
2. What does he say she would gain if she took the red-eye?
She would land at seven in the morning, and she would gain a whole day.
3. How much does the red-eye save, and what would he do with that money?
About two hundred dollars. If he were her, he would put it into the food, not into the room.
4. What does he say most people get wrong?
They splurge on the hotel, and then they eat badly for five days.
