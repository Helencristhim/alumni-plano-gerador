Register

Three Ways to Report the Same Failure

Rank them, say why, then click to compare

A. "Your technician calibrated the relay badly and you did not check it."
A personal accusation, in the active voice, with a finger pointing at a named person. Everything after it is a defense, not a solution — and the meeting is over before it starts.
B. "There seem to be some issues with the relay, more or less."
Too vague to survive. Nothing is recorded, nothing is measurable, and nothing has to be corrected. A statement with no consequence is a statement the supplier can ignore.
C. "The relay was found to be out of tolerance during the functional test, and the calibration certificate had not been issued."
Impersonal passive. The fact sits in the center; the person disappears. Nobody is attacked, and yet nothing can be denied — which is precisely why inspection reports are written this way.

The Distinction

Deviation vs Non-conformance

Deviation

A departure from the specification that can be accepted — if it is documented, justified and approved before the work is done.

A decision. It is signed off.

Non-conformance

A departure that was found, not agreed. It has to be corrected, or formally waived, and the root cause has to be identified.

A failure. It is corrected.

The supplier will call it a deviation because a deviation costs nothing. Your job is to ask one question: was it approved before the work, or found after it?

Chapter 3: The Code

The Grammar That Removes the Person

"You calibrated it wrong."  →  "The relay was found to be out of tolerance."

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"The relay was found to be out of tolerance during the functional test."

"The calibration certificate had not been issued at the time of the test."

"A corrective action must be implemented before the retest."

"We need to have the report resubmitted by Friday."

In the first three, who did it? The person is gone — and the fact stayed. In the fourth, who resubmits the report: you, or them?

FormStructureExample
Passive, simplebe + past participle. The object becomes the subject; the agent is dropped when it is obvious, unknown — or when naming it would start a fight."The submittal was returned with comments."
Passive + reporting verb
found / reported / believed / said		be + participle + to be / to have been. The register of every inspection report ever written."The relay was found to be out of tolerance."
Perfect passivehad been + participle. Something that had NOT happened by the time of something else — the strongest way to date a missing document."The certificate had not been issued at the time of the test."
Modal passivemodal + be + participle. The obligation belongs to the process, not to a person — which is why nobody can take it personally."A corrective action must be implemented before the retest."
Causative
have something done		have + OBJECT + past participle. You require the action; somebody else performs it. The order is fixed: the thing comes BEFORE the participle."We need to have the report resubmitted by Friday."
Causative
get something done / have somebody do		get is the same structure, less formal. have somebody do names the person and uses the BASE verb."I will get the relay retested." · "I will have your team resubmit it."
Why it matters"You calibrated it badly" is an accusation, and everything after it is a defense. "The relay was found to be out of tolerance" is the same fact with the person removed — nobody is attacked, and nothing can be denied. Then the causative puts the work back where it belongs: "we need to have it retested" — not by us, by them, and by Friday.
Meaning

Four Structures, Four Jobs

Four structures that carry an approval meeting
... was found to be ...
You state a finding, not a person

The register of every inspection report. Nobody is accused, and nothing can be denied. "The relay was found to be out of tolerance during the functional test."

... had not been ...
You date what is missing

It fixes the gap in time: at the moment of the test, the document did not exist. "The calibration certificate had not been issued at the time of the test."

... must be ...
The obligation belongs to the process

The requirement comes from the procedure, not from you, so it cannot be taken personally. "A corrective action must be implemented before the retest."

have something done
You require the work; they perform it

The object comes first, the participle second. Your demand, their hands, their cost. "We need to have the report resubmitted by Friday."

The passive is not decoration and it is not politeness. It is what lets you say something serious about a company without saying anything about a person. Use it for the finding; use the causative for the demand.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"The report must be resubmit by Friday."
"The report must be resubmitted by Friday."
"We need to have resubmitted the report by Friday."
"We need to have the report resubmitted by Friday."

After a modal, the passive needs be + past participle: "must be resubmitted". And in the causative, the object comes first: "have the report resubmitted" — "have resubmitted the report" is a different tense and a different meaning.

Should this not be accepted, the rework will be carried out at the supplier's cost, the unit will be retested, and a revised report will be resubmitted through the approval workflow. Sign-off would then be requested at the earliest opportunity.

The Approval Call

C
Yes, we saw that. But honestly, João, it is a minor deviation. The unit works. I would not hold the shipment for this.
J

C
The certificate was issued the following Monday. And the calibration itself was performed by our subcontractor, not by us.
J

Comprehension

About Camille

1. How does Camille describe the failure at the start?
As a minor deviation. She says the unit works and that she would not hold the shipment for it — she is downplaying a failed functional test on a safety relay.
2. What are the two moves she makes to avoid the non-conformance?
First she downplays it ("a minor deviation"). Then she passes the buck: the calibration was performed by their subcontractor, not by them.
3. What does she say about the calibration certificate?
That it was issued the following Monday — which means it had not been issued at the time of the test, and therefore does not cover it.
4. What does she finally commit to?
She will have the unit retested on Thursday and send the certificate herself. And she checks, in the last line, that João is not going to move on this.

Listening 2

Before You Raise That NCR

Your quality manager. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Why does he say the passive is not "cold" or "weak" English?
Because it moves the person out and leaves the fact in. An accusation can be denied and defended; a finding stated in the passive has to be closed. It is the register of every standard he works with.
2. What is the difference between a symptom and a root cause?
The relay reading eleven milliseconds is the symptom. The root cause is the reason behind it — a reference instrument that had drifted and had not been recalibrated. A corrective action agreed before the root cause is identified is a corrective action that fails twice.
3. What should you never do with the word "deviation"?
Never accept the supplier's use of it. A deviation is approved before the work; anything found after it is a non-conformance, and calling it a deviation is how the rework quietly becomes yours.
