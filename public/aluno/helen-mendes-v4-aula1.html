Brainstorming -- Reading for Gist

Read: Making Connections

Read the article once. What is the writer's main message?

Making Connections at a Marketing Summit

Every year, hundreds of marketing professionals meet at the São Paulo Marketing Summit. For many people, the talks are not the most important part. The real value is in the breaks, the coffee, and the conversations between sessions.

Marina works for a small design agency. At her first summit, she felt nervous. She did not know anyone, and everyone looked busy. So she made a simple plan: every break, she introduced herself to one new person.

Marina learned something important. A good first impression is not about a perfect speech. It is about being approachable. She smiled, she asked easy questions, and she listened. “What brings you here?” and “What do you work on?” are enough to start.

Small talk is not a waste of time. It builds trust. After a short, friendly chat, people remember you. Many of Marina's best clients today started as a two-minute conversation near the coffee machine.

Her advice is simple: you do not need to be the loudest person in the room. You only need to be present, curious, and easy to talk to.

Which sentence best summarizes the writer's main message?

A. The talks are the most important part of a marketing summit.
B. To make good connections, be approachable and curious — not the loudest.
C. You need a perfect speech to impress people at a summit.
Answer key — click to reveal
B — The text says you do not need a perfect speech or to be the loudest; you need to be approachable, present and curious.

Diving Deep -- Language Focus

How to Introduce Yourself

Break the ice“Hi, I don't think we've met. What brings you here?”
Introduce yourself“Let me introduce myself. I'm Laura. I work in marketing.”
Ask about the other person“What do you do?” / “Is this your first summit?”
Close & stay in touch“It was great to meet you. Here's my card. Let's stay in touch.”
1. To talk about your job, which is correct: “I'm work in marketing” or “I work in marketing”?
I work in marketing. We use the present simple — no “am” before the main verb “work”.
2. What's the difference between “I'm a brand manager” and “I work in marketing”?
I'm a + job title (brand manager); I work in + area / industry (marketing). Both are present simple.
3. Which questions invite the other person to talk about themselves?
“What do you do?” and “What brings you here?” — open questions keep the conversation going.
4. Why do we say “Here's my card. Let's stay in touch.” at the end?
It is a polite, friendly way to close the conversation and keep the connection — the goal of networking.
