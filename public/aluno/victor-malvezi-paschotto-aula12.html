The right structure at the right moment.

Grammar Map

The Meeting Toolkit

Meeting moveStructureExample
Pitch (imagined)2nd conditional: if + past, would + base"If we funded it, we would triple the reach."
Negotiate (real offer)1st conditional: if + present, will + base"If you approve it, we will share milestones."
Read the roomModals of deduction: must / might / can't + base"She must be weighing the risk."
Handle tensionTentative: I was hoping / I was wondering / could"I was hoping we could explore one option."
Tell the storyNarrative tenses + dramatic present"It had been sliding, then we launched, and so we go live."

The key contrast to keep clean:

Use the second conditional ("if we funded… we would…") to pitch something imagined, and the first conditional ("if you approve… we will…") for a real offer on the table. Then read the room with must / might / can't, soften tension with I was hoping… could, and tell the story with narrative tenses — switching to the dramatic present for one vivid moment.

Common Mistake

Mixed vs Clean

✗ MIXED CONDITIONAL

"If you approve the pilot, we would start in March."

✓ REAL OFFER → WILL

"If you approve the pilot, we will start in March."

✗ TOO CERTAIN

"She didn't reply yet, so she must hate the plan."

✓ WEAK SIGNAL → MIGHT

"She didn't reply yet, so she might still be thinking."

Real offer on the table = first conditional (will). Imagined idea = second conditional (would). A weak signal = might, not must.

Chapter 4

In the Meeting

All five moves, one conversation

Dialogue

The Quarterly Review

D
Victor, you have fifteen minutes. Give me the quarter, and then the ask.
V
Let me headline it: revenue is up nineteen percent. The market had been sliding while we rebuilt the pipeline, then in March we launched the new route, and so we go live, and within a week the orders double.
D
Strong story. So what is the value proposition for next year?
V
If we funded two more regions, we would triple the reach within a year. I'd frame it as an investment, not a cost, and I think it would win quick buy-in from operations.
D
Two regions is a big number. The CFO will push back on the budget.
V
That's why I'd propose a pilot. If you approve one region first, we will share milestones in ninety days, and unless the numbers hold, we pause. That keeps the trade-off small.
D
Hm. (pauses) Let me think about the timing.
V
You've gone quiet, so you might be weighing the risk. I don't want to push — I was hoping we could explore the pilot timing together before you decide.
D
I appreciate that. Run the pilot on one region. If the milestones hold at ninety days, we will talk about the second.
Comprehension

Did You Catch It?

1. What does Diana warn the CFO will do?
Push back on the budget, because two regions is a big number.
2. What does Diana finally agree to?
Running the pilot on one region, and revisiting the second if the milestones hold at ninety days.
3. How does Victor read Diana's silence?
As her weighing the risk — so he invites her to explore the timing instead of pushing.

The Stakeholder Voice Note

Listen first. No text. Sound only.

0:000:00
1. What is Diana's first point about structure?
Lead with the story and the headline, signpost as you go, and close on one clear takeaway.
2. What does she advise about the ask?
Frame it as an investment and be ready to negotiate conditions — come with milestones: if the numbers hold, scale; if not, pause.

Chapter 5

Sharpen the Skills

All five moves, at speed

Quick Fire

Run the Meeting

1 / 5

1. Pitch an imagined idea with the second conditional.

"If we added a second route, we would cut delivery times in half."

2. Put a real offer on the table with the first conditional.

"If you approve the pilot, we will report milestones every thirty days."

3. Read a weak signal with a modal of deduction.

"He keeps glancing at the clock, so he might be short on time."

4. Soften a tense moment with tentative language.

"I was hoping we could look at the numbers together before deciding."

5. Tell the story in two layers, then go vivid with the dramatic present.

"We had rebuilt the pipeline, then we launched, and so we go live, and the orders double."

Detective

Spot the Slip

0 / 4 fixes found

"If you will approve the budget, we will start in March."
"If you approve the budget, we will start in March." (first conditional: no 'will' in the if-clause)
"If we funded it, we will triple the reach."
"If we funded it, we would triple the reach." (second conditional pairs 'funded' with 'would', not 'will')
"She paused once, so she must be rejecting the whole plan."
"She paused once, so she might be weighing it." (a weak signal takes 'might', not the certain 'must')
"I am hoping we can revisit this, so don't decide yet."
"I was hoping we could revisit this." (the past form is softer and more tentative)
