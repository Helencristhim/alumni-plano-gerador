Who Is Rosângela? -- Diagnostic and Personal Introduction at the Airport Gate

Introduce yourself at the departure gate and understand an airport announcement. Key words: passport, boarding pass, gate, flight, destination, carry-on, check-in, delay, departure, terminal. Structures: the verb to be (I am / it is) for who you are, present simple (I live / I travel) for what you do, and questions with are / do. Expression: once in a while.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to every word and read the example. These are the ten words you already see on every screen of every airport.

Passport -- the small book that shows who you are when you travel to another country
"My passport is in my bag."
Boarding pass -- the paper that gives you your seat and lets you enter the plane
"Here is my boarding pass."
Gate -- the door in the airport where you enter the plane
"My gate is B14."
Flight -- one trip in a plane, from one city to another
"My flight is at seven o'clock."
Destination -- the city or the country where you are going
"My destination is Paris."
Carry-on -- a small bag that goes with you inside the plane, not under it
"My carry-on is very small."
Check-in -- the moment when you show your passport and give your bag to the airline
"The check-in is on the second floor."
Delay -- when the plane leaves later than the time on your ticket
"There is a delay of one hour."
Departure -- the moment when the plane leaves the airport
"The departure is at nine in the morning."
Terminal -- one of the big buildings of an airport, with a number or a letter
"My flight leaves from Terminal 3."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

Rosângela is at the airport. She is from São Paulo, and she lives in Vila Leopoldina. She is 68 years old, and she travels to Europe once in a while, with her husband. Today her destination is Paris. Her flight is at seven o'clock, and the departure is from Terminal 3. Her passport and her boarding pass are in her carry-on. The check-in is quick, and she goes to the gate. But there is a delay of two hours, and the gate is B14 now. A traveler sits next to her and asks: "Where are you from?" Rosângela answers alone, in English, for the first time.

1. Why do we say "I am from São Paulo" and not "I live from São Paulo"?
A With a country or a city after "from", English uses the verb TO BE: I am from...
B Because "from" is always followed by the verb "to live".
C Because it is a question, and questions need "to be".
2. "I live in Vila Leopoldina." Why not "I am live in Vila Leopoldina"?
A Because "live" is already the verb. It does not need "am" in front of it.
B Because "live" is a noun here.
C Because the sentence is in the past.
3. "I travel to Europe once in a while." What does "once in a while" mean?
A Every single week, without exception.
B Sometimes, but not often.
C Only one time in my whole life.
4. "Where do you live?" Why does this question need DO, but "Where are you from?" does not?
A Because "live" is an irregular verb.
B Because the question is about the future.
C Because "to be" makes its own question (are you...?), and every other verb needs DO.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Verb To Be vs Present Simple

GRAMMAR

Two structures tell your whole story at the gate: one for WHO you are, one for WHAT you do.

FormUseExample
To be
I am · you are · it is		WHO you are, WHERE you are from, WHAT time something happens.I am Rosângela. I am from São Paulo. My flight is at seven.
Present simple
I live · I travel · I go		WHAT you do: your routine and the facts of your life. No "am" in front of it.I live in Vila Leopoldina. I travel with my husband.
Question with to be
Are you...? Is it...?		The verb to be makes its own question: it jumps in front of you.Where are you from? Is my flight on time?
Question with do
Do you...?		Every other verb needs DO to make a question.Where do you live? Do you travel a lot?
Age
I am 68 years old		In English you never HAVE an age. You ARE your age.I am 68 years old.

The one rule to remember: use am / is / are for who you are, and the verb alone (live, travel, go) for what you do. Never both together: "I am live" does not exist.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer for these real moments at the gate.

A traveler at the gate asks: "Where are you from?" You answer:
A "I have from São Paulo."
B "I am from São Paulo, in Brazil."
C "I am live in São Paulo."
He asks: "How old are you?" The correct answer is:
A "I have 68 years."
B "I am 68 years old."
C "I am 68 years."
You want to ask him where he lives. You say:
A "Where do you live?"
B "Where you live?"
C "Where are you live?"
The screen says your plane leaves two hours late. You tell your husband:
A "There is a delay of two hours."
B "There is a gate of two hours."
C "There is a terminal of two hours."
You do not understand what the agent said. The best thing to say is:
A Say nothing and hope it was not important.
B "Sorry, I do not understand. Can you repeat, please?"
C Ask your son to speak for you.

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt. There is no right or wrong version. Speak for 60 seconds, with no script. This recording is your BASELINE: we listen to it again in Lesson 20.

You are at the departure gate. A traveler sits next to you and says: "Hello, is this seat free?" Introduce yourself. Say your name, where you are from (to be), where you live (present simple), how old you are, how often you travel (once in a while), and where you are going today. Finish with one question for him. Take your time and do not read from a script.
