Reading

Two Universities, One Report

Two Universities, One Report

Last month, Maria Clara visited two universities for the council. The university in Seoul is much larger than her university in Brazil. It is also more modern and more efficient: the rooms are bigger, and the technology is newer. Her own university is older and more traditional. In her opinion, it is warmer and more comfortable. "The Seoul campus is the most impressive place on my trip," she wrote in her report.

This year's conference was more formal than the previous one. It was also more significant, because more delegations came from distant countries. The accommodation was better than last time. The hotel was more comfortable, but it was also more expensive and more distant from the campus. Maria Clara would rather stay in a smaller, closer hotel. In her opinion, a local hotel is more practical than a large one far away.

The best moment was the final round table. It was the most important meeting of the week, and Maria Clara spoke in English, with no interpreter. Her current English is better than her English last year, and every month it is stronger. "The more I practice, the better I speak," she thought. That night, she called her granddaughter Clarinha. "Grandma spoke English today -- better than yesterday!" They both smiled.

Chapter 4: The Code

Comparatives & Superlatives

larger than · more formal than · the most important · the best

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"The university in Seoul is larger than ours. It is also bigger and newer."

"This conference is more formal than the last one. It is also more significant."

"This is the most important meeting of the year, and the best round table of the week."

"My current English is better than last year. Seoul is farther than Paris."

AdjectiveComparative (two things)Superlative (the top one)
Short: largelarger thanthe largest
Short: bigbigger thanthe biggest
Long: formalmore formal thanthe most formal
Long: importantmore important thanthe most important
Irregular: goodbetter thanthe best
Irregular: badworse thanthe worst

Short adjective (1 syllable): add -er / -est (larger, the largest). Long adjective (2+ syllables): use more / the most (more formal, the most formal). Always than after a comparative, and the before a superlative. Irregular: good → better → the best; bad → worse → the worst; far → farther → the farthest.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Seoul is more big than Paris."
"Seoul is bigger than Paris."
"This is the most best meeting of the year."
"This is the best meeting of the year."

Short adjective → -er / -est (bigger, the biggest), never "more big". And good is irregular: better, the best — never "more best".

Quick Fire

Answer Like Someone Just Asked You

1 / 6

A colleague asks: "How was the conference this year?"

  • It was more formal and more significant than last year.
  • Long adjective = more + adjective + than.

Someone asks: "Which university is bigger -- Seoul or yours?"

  • The university in Seoul is larger than mine.
  • Short adjective = adjective + -er + than.

You compare two hotels for the next trip.

  • This hotel is more comfortable, but the other one is cheaper.
  • Use more + adjective for long words.

A colleague asks: "What was the best part of the week?"

  • The final round table was the best moment.
  • Superlative of good = the best (irregular).

Someone asks about your English now.

  • My current English is better than last year.
  • good becomes better than (irregular).

You choose between a large hotel far away and a small local one.

  • I would rather stay in a local hotel.
  • I would rather + base verb = your preference.
