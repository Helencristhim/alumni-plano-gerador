Lesson 12 · The First 90 Days

Listening Before You Lead

Anyone can arrive with the answer. A senior leader arrives with the questions — runs a listening tour, reports back what the room actually said, and earns the right to change something only once she understands it.

Chapter 1: The Handover

Two Ways to Open Day One

A. “I have reviewed the numbers, and here are the three things we are changing this month.”
B. “For the next ninety days I am deciding nothing. I want to hear what is working before I touch anything.”

Which one sounds like a leader who will still be trusted in month six — and why?

Reading

Read for the Main Idea

The Field Note She Wrote on Day 89

Every senior leader who inherits a team feels the same pull in the first week: to arrive with the answer. You were hired because you are good at fixing things, so the fastest way to prove you belong is to fix something visible, immediately. Danielle's predecessor, Gordon, admitted that he had done exactly that -- he told her he had restructured the academic team in his first month and had been wrong about almost all of it, because he had changed the answer before he understood the question. His advice was the opposite of his instinct: decide nothing for ninety days, and spend the ramp-up period on a listening tour instead.

So she did. Over eleven weeks she sat with the frontline staff, not just the directors -- the people who actually run the classrooms -- and she asked the same two questions in every conversation: what is genuinely broken, and what must I not touch? The answers surprised her. One coordinator explained that the last three reorganizations had each been announced as urgent and then quietly reversed, so the team had learned to wait out any new plan rather than commit to it. Another admitted that morale was low, but insisted the cause was not the workload; it was that nobody in head office had ever asked them anything before deciding. A regional director warned her not to mistake their politeness for agreement, and pointed out that a Canadian team will rarely tell you no to your face.

None of this was in the handover document, and none of it could have been. It came from a particular vantage point -- the classroom floor -- that a new leader only reaches by asking early and listening long. The temptation the whole time was to act, because a mandate feels like something you are supposed to spend, and every week she did not restructure anything felt like a week of wasted authority. But credibility, she was learning, is not spent with the title. It is earned in the first quarter, one honest conversation at a time, and the surest way to lose it on day one is to change something before the people living with it believe you understand it.

By day eighty-nine she had a plan, and it was smaller and sharper than the one she would have written on day one. She had learned to lean on Gordon, whose early mistakes were now her shortcuts, and she had learned to size up a culture before trying to move it. The entry plan she finally presented had three phases -- learn, decide, act -- and she was still, deliberately, in the first. When a stakeholder asked why she had not fixed anything yet, she no longer heard a threat. She heard her cue to say, plainly and without apology, that she was still listening, on purpose, and that the listening was the work.

Field note, People & Culture -- Danielle's first quarter, Maple Bear Canada

Chapter 4: The Code

Carry Their Words, Not Your Verdict

He admitted that he had been wrong · She warned me not to restructure · They explained that it had been reversed

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

Gordon admitted that he had been wrong about the restructure.

A coordinator explained that the reorganizations had all been reversed.

He warned me not to mistake their politeness for agreement.

Ruth recommended waiting, and urged me to build a simple entry plan.

In every sentence the tense steps one step back — is becomes was, was becomes had been — and the reporting verb (admitted, explained, warned, recommended, urged) carries how it was said. That precision is what proves you actually listened.

StructureFormWhat it does
Backshiftreporting verb + that + clause, one tense back“Morale is low” → She said morale was low. “It was reversed” → He explained it had been reversed.
Say vs telltell + person · say (no person) + thatHe told me that... · He said that... — never “he said me”.
Verb + object + tourge/advise/warn/ask + sb + to + base verbHe urged me to slow down. Negative: he warned me not to restructure.
Verb + -ing / thatrecommend / suggest + gerund or that-clauseShe recommended waiting. · She suggested that I wait. Never “recommended to wait”.
Why it sounds senior“They all agree” is your assumption — and usually wrong. “A director warned me not to mistake politeness for agreement” relays what was actually said, precisely and attributed. Choosing the reporting verb (admit, insist, point out, warn) IS choosing the meaning.

Put them together and you relay a whole listening tour without inventing a verdict: “She explained that the plans had been reversed, and he insisted that the real problem was never being asked.”

Register

Assume It, or Report It

Your verdict — you flatten every voice into one

Fast, but it erases what people actually said and hands the room your assumption as if it were theirs.

“Everyone agrees the place needs a shake-up.”

Reported speech — you carry each voice precisely

Slower, but it proves you heard them — and the right reporting verb keeps the nuance you would otherwise lose.

“One coordinator explained that past plans had been reversed; another insisted the real issue was never being asked.”

Same tour, two reports. The first invites the team to say “that is not what I meant”. The second is the sentence the frontline staff repeat to each other after you leave the room — because you got them right.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Gordon said me that he had restructured too fast."
"Gordon told me that he had restructured too fast."
"Ruth recommended to wait until the listening tour was finished."
"Ruth recommended waiting until the listening tour was finished."

The first slip is Portuguese arriving on time: say takes no person, so “said me” must become “told me” (or “said that”). The second is the pattern trap: recommend and suggest take the -ing form or a that-clause — never “to”.
