The Challenge

Describe the problem you faced

Brazilian football has a long and passionate history. But for many years, governance was not a priority. Decisions were made behind closed doors, and accountability was limited.

When I joined Corinthians as Chief Compliance Officer, I inherited a department with limited resources and even more limited authority. My first challenge was not technical. It was cultural. I had to convince the leadership that compliance was not a threat to their autonomy, but a tool to protect the institution.

The question I faced every day was simple: How do you enforce rules in an organization that is not used to following them?

Our Approach

Present the three-step strategy

We took a three-step approach. First, we built a team. I recruited five dedicated compliance professionals with backgrounds in law, finance, and investigation. Each one reports directly to me, and I report to the General Director, not to any department head. This independence is critical.

Second, we established clear procedures. We created a compliance manual, a whistleblower hotline, and a code of conduct. Every employee, from the players to the administrative staff, signed the code.

Third, we coordinated with external partners. We work closely with the Brazilian Football Confederation, FIFA, and independent auditors. Transparency is our strongest tool.

Full Keynote Run-Through

Opening

Good afternoon, everyone. My name is Nilo Patussi, and I am the Chief Compliance Officer at Sport Club Corinthians Paulista, one of the largest football clubs in South America.

I have been working in sports law and governance for over fifteen years. Today, I would like to share our experience in building a compliance culture at Corinthians, and the lessons we have learned along the way.

The Challenge

Brazilian football has a long and passionate history. But for many years, governance was not a priority. Decisions were made behind closed doors, and accountability was limited.

When I joined Corinthians as Chief Compliance Officer, I inherited a department with limited resources and even more limited authority. My first challenge was not technical. It was cultural. I had to convince the leadership that compliance was not a threat to their autonomy, but a tool to protect the institution.

The question I faced every day was simple: How do you enforce rules in an organization that is not used to following them?

Our Approach

We took a three-step approach. First, we built a team. I recruited five dedicated compliance professionals with backgrounds in law, finance, and investigation. Each one reports directly to me, and I report to the General Director, not to any department head. This independence is critical.

Second, we established clear procedures. We created a compliance manual, a whistleblower hotline, and a code of conduct. Every employee, from the players to the administrative staff, signed the code.

Third, we coordinated with external partners. We work closely with the Brazilian Football Confederation, FIFA, and independent auditors. Transparency is our strongest tool.

Results

In the past three years, we have investigated over forty potential violations. We have implemented fifteen corrective actions. And most importantly, we have changed the culture. Today, employees come to us before problems happen, not after.

We have also established a quarterly review process with the board, where I present our findings and recommendations. This direct access to the board was not easy to achieve, but it is the foundation of our independence.

Our work has been recognized by FIFA and by the Brazilian Football Confederation as a model for other clubs in South America.

Closing

I want to close with a personal reflection. When I was selected for this FIFA program, I was honored, but also aware of the responsibility. I am the only representative from Brazil. That means I carry not just my own experience, but the story of an entire football nation.

Compliance is not about punishment. It is about protection. We protect the players, we protect the fans, and we protect the future of the game we love.

I am currently working on expanding our program to include youth academies and amateur clubs. Because governance does not start at the top. It starts at the grassroots.

Thank you. I am happy to answer any questions.
