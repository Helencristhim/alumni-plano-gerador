Chapter 3: The Code

Have You Ever...?

have / has · the third form · and the one rule about dates

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I have been to Italy three times."

"Have you ever been to a vineyard?"

"I have never taken a cruise."

"She has seen twenty-three cities in this country."

Every green part has two pieces: a small word, and a verb. Only one of the two ever changes. And now the real question: where is the date? Look again. Is there a single date in these four sentences?

FormUseExample
I / you / we / they have + 3rd formIt happened at some point in your life. It does not matter when — it matters that it is in you.I have been to Italy. · They have seen the coast.
he / she / it has + 3rd formSame thing. Only the small word changes — the verb never does.She has seen twenty-three cities. (never "she have")
Question
Have you ever...?		Ever = at any time in your whole life. It is the question every traveler asks you.Have you ever been to a vineyard? · Has he ever tried it?
Negative
I have never...		Never = zero times, in my whole life. Never is already the no: do not put a second one.I have never taken a cruise. (never "I have not never")
THE RULE
no date		This tense and a date cannot sit at the same table. The second a date arrives, you must switch to past simple (Lesson 2).I have been to Paris. · I went to Paris in 2022. (never "I have been in 2022")

This is the tense of a life, not of a day. That is why it has no date: it is not telling a story, it is telling who you are.

The Anatomy

Two Words, One Life

Two words carry your whole life in them
have / has
the little word -- it changes for he and she

"I have been to Italy." "She has been to Italy." I, you, we, they take HAVE. He, she, it take HAS. That is the only change there is.

past participle
the third form of the verb -- it never changes

"been", "seen", "eaten", "taken". Not the past simple: it is "I have SEEN", never "I have saw". Ten of them tonight, and you already know most.

ever / never
the two keys of the question and the no

"Have you EVER been to a vineyard?" = in your whole life, one time, any time. "I have NEVER been." = zero times, in my whole life. Never already means no -- do not add a second one.

The Contrast

The Passport vs the Diary

Both of them are the past — but they answer different questions. One says it is in me. The other says it happened on a day.

Present perfect — the passport

The stamp is there. Nobody asks the day. No date allowed.

"I have been to Paris."
"Have you ever eaten in a square at midnight?"

Past simple — the diary

The day is written on the page. A date is there.

"I went to Paris in 2022."
"We ate in that square last Tuesday."

In Portuguese, one form does both jobs, and that is exactly why this is hard for you and easy for nobody. In English there is a switch, and the switch is the date. No date → have been. A date → went. That is the whole rule.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I have been to Positano last year."
"I went to Positano last year." — or, with no date: "I have been to Positano."
"Have you ever go to a vineyard?"
"Have you ever been to a vineyard?"
"My husband have never taken a cruise."
"My husband has never taken a cruise."

Three questions, in this order, every time: is there a date in my sentence? Is my verb the third form? Is it he or she? Answer those three and this tense cannot beat you.

Reading

The List Is Yours

The List Is Yours

It is five in the afternoon in the countryside, an hour south of Florence, and Rosângela is sitting at a long wooden table with eleven people she has never met. Behind the table there is a vineyard, and the lines of grapes go all the way down the hill and stop where the road begins. Her husband is next to her, quiet, with a glass in his hand. He does not speak English, and this afternoon he does not need to. On her other side there is a man from Ireland, and he asks her the question that travelers always ask each other -- the easiest question in the world, and the one she used to be afraid of: "So, have you ever been to Italy before?"

Rosângela puts down her glass. Twenty-three cities, she thinks. "Yes, I have," she says. "I have been to Italy three times. I have walked the coast at Positano, and I have seen Capri -- it is an island, and there are cliffs on it that go straight down into the water. I have eaten in a square in Rome at midnight. I have visited a castle in Sintra. And I have never taken a cruise -- my husband says a ship is a hotel that follows you, and we prefer the trains." The man laughs. "You have been everywhere," he says. "I have only been to Rome, and I have never seen the south. Which one is the best?" And Rosângela does something she has never done in English before: she answers a question about her own life, for two minutes, without stopping, and nobody at that table helps her.

Here is what nobody at the table knows. The list is forty years old. The landmarks, the churches, the twenty-three cities, the little train down to the coast -- all of it happened, all of it is hers, and for forty years she had every single one of those places inside her head and no way to say them abroad. In Paris, four years ago, her son said them for her. In London, at passport control, her son said them for her. He said them well -- he is fluent, and that was never the problem. The problem is that a life is not a list of places: it is the sentences you can say about them. This afternoon, on a hill in Tuscany, with a glass of red wine and a stranger from Ireland, Rosângela said her own list out loud, herself. It took her two minutes, and two words: have been.

Listening

The Man Who Has Never Left the Hill

Three minutes of his voice, and then the wine. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does Matteo say about his grandfather and this hill?
His grandfather planted the first line of grapes in 1953 and never left the hill. He never went abroad, not one time in his life. He has been to Florence twice — fifty kilometers away — and he called that a long trip.
2. Matteo says he has never been on a plane. How old is he, and what does he ask the table?
He is fifty-eight, and he has never been on a plane. He has never taken a cruise and he has never seen the coast of Ireland. Everybody at the table has been on a plane — and today they are all on his hill. So he asks: who has traveled?
3. He has shown the vineyard to people from more than forty countries. What is the first question everybody asks him, and what happens after he answers?
Everybody asks the same first question: "Have you ever been to my country?" He always says no. Then they tell him about it — the square in their city, the castle, the island, the food their mother makes — and for three minutes he has been there. He calls that his traveling.
Listening 2

Nineteen Countries and One Word

Margaret is sixty-nine, and she wants to talk about one question. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. For about thirty years Margaret answered that question with one word. Was it because she had no stories?
No — she had them all. She has been to nineteen countries, she has seen the northern lights, she has eaten things she cannot pronounce, and she has slept in a castle in Scotland with no heating. The stories were all in her head. She just said "yes", or "no".
2. What happened at the table after she said "yes"? What is the part that hurts?
Silence. Then that person turned to somebody else, and the conversation went away from her. She sat there with nineteen countries in her head and a cup of tea in her hand — and nobody knew. That is the part that hurts.
3. What does she say a person is really doing when they ask "have you ever...?", and what does she tell you to answer?
They are not asking for yes or no — they are opening a door. So answer with a sentence, not a word: "Yes, I have. I went in 2019, and it was the coldest week of my life." Or: "No, I have never been, but I have always wanted to." She says that is the difference between yes, and a friend — and you do not need perfect English for it.
