Impact

Three Ways to Answer the Same Question

Rank them, say why, then click to compare

A. "I only helped a bit with some international suppliers, nothing major."
You just shrank the best thing about you. The word "only" is doing terrible work: the interviewer believes you, marks you down, and moves on. Selling yourself short is not modesty in an interview — it is misinformation.
B. "I'm very experienced, communicative, and good with international teams."
Adjectives with no evidence. Everyone in the queue says exactly this, and nobody remembers who said it. A claim without a story is a claim the panel has to take on faith — and they will not.
C. "I used to work only in Portuguese. Over three years I got used to running technical calls with German, French and Chinese suppliers in English, under real schedule pressure."
One concrete arc, and the grammar carries it: "used to" says where you came from, "got used to" says you adapted. The panel sees range and evolution — and it is impossible to forget because it is specific.

The Tool

The STAR Method

S — Situation

Set the scene in one sentence. "In 2024, a German supplier delivered forty interlocking units, and six failed the EMC test."

T — Task

What was YOUR responsibility. "My task was to keep the design question open without stopping the line."

A — Action

What YOU did — verbs, decisions. "I accepted the six in writing and put the shared grounding design on record."

R — Result

The outcome, with a number if you can. "As a result, they re-verified the configuration on all forty at their cost."

Most candidates tell the Situation and stop. The Result is the sentence that buys the job. When a behavioral question lands, name the story first, then run S → T → A → R.

Chapter 3: The Code

"I used to" is not "I'm used to"

One depends on the verb form after it. Get it wrong and you sound confused. Get it right and you sound like evolution.

Grammar Discovery

Three "Used To", One Difference

"I used to work on electrification projects."

"I am used to managing suppliers in three time zones."

"It took time, but I got used to reading CENELEC standards in English."

"I would rather give you one real result than a list of skills."

Look at the word right after "used to". In sentence 1 it is a base verb; in 2 and 3 it is an -ing. What changes in the meaning?

StructureMeaningExample
used to + base verbA past habit or state that is now finished. Where you came FROM. Negative/question drop the -d: "I didn't use to...", "Did you use to...?""I used to work on electrification." — I did, and I stopped.
be used to + -ing / nounA state: it is already normal for you. NOT the base verb — the -ing."I'm used to managing suppliers." — it is normal for me now.
get used to + -ing / nounThe process of becoming accustomed — from strange to normal, over time."I got used to reading CENELEC in English." — it became normal.
would rather / had better / may as well
+ base verb		would rather = preference. had better = strong advice, almost a warning. may as well = there is no better option, so let's."I would rather give you a result." · "I had better be specific." · "We may as well start with the hardest one."
Why it mattersIn an interview these three structures narrate your evolution in one breath: "I used to work only in Portuguese; I got used to running calls in English; now I'm used to managing the interface across three countries." That arc — from, through, to — is exactly what a panel wants to hear, and the verb form is the only thing that makes it come out right.
Meaning

Four Structures, Four Jobs

Three ways to say "used to" -- and the modals that position you
I used to work on electrification
PAST habit -- not true now

used to + base verb. A state or habit that was true before and is finished. It is how you narrate where you came FROM. "I used to work on electrification projects before I specialized in signaling." Negative and question drop the -d: "I didn't use to...", "Did you use to...?"

I am used to managing suppliers
A STATE -- already comfortable

be used to + noun / -ing. You are accustomed to it; it is normal for you now. NOT the base verb -- the -ing. "I'm used to managing suppliers in three time zones." "I'm not used to being interviewed in English, but I've prepared."

I got used to reading CENELEC in English
The PROCESS -- becoming comfortable

get used to + noun / -ing. The change from strange to normal, over time. "It took a while, but I got used to reading CENELEC standards in English." "You will get used to the pace."

I would rather / had better / may as well
Positioning in the room

would rather + base verb = preference ("I would rather be judged on the result"). had better + base verb = strong advice, almost a warning ("I had better give you a concrete example"). may as well + base verb = there is no better option, so let's ("We may as well start with the hardest project").

The trap is the verb form. used to + BASE VERB (past habit). be / get used to + -ING (accustomed). "I used to manage" = I did, and I stopped. "I am used to managing" = it is normal for me. Same three words, opposite meanings -- and an interviewer hears the difference.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I am used to manage suppliers in three time zones."
"I am used to managing suppliers in three time zones."
"I used to working only in Portuguese."
"I used to work only in Portuguese."

After be/get used to, the verb takes -ing: "used to managing". After used to (past habit), the verb is the base form: "used to work". Your first language uses one form for both, so English forces a choice it does not otherwise make — and the panel hears the choice you make.

Three Things Before the Panel

Your interview coach. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What is the first mistake he warns about, and what word gives it away?
Selling yourself short. The giveaway is the word "only" — "I only did this", "I just helped" — when in fact João led the interface between three suppliers on a live commissioning.

Answer Like the Panel Is Live

"Tell me about yourself." You have ninety seconds. Do not narrate your whole CV.

  • I'm a signaling engineer at Motiva. I used to work on electrification, and over the last three years I got used to owning the interface between design, installation and commissioning.
  • The part I'm proudest of is the international side: I'm used to running technical calls with German, French and Chinese suppliers, in English, under schedule pressure.

"Why are you leaving Motiva?" Stay positive; do not criticize your employer.

  • I've learned a great deal at Motiva, and I would rather leave for something than away from something.
  • I'm ready for a role with more international scope and a bigger interface to own, and that is exactly what this position offers.

"Tell me about a time you managed a conflict with an international supplier." Use STAR -- name the story first.

  • In 2024, a German supplier argued that six failed units were an installation problem, not a design one. My task was to keep the design question open without stopping the line.
  • What I did was accept the six in writing and put the shared grounding design on record, and as a result they re-scanned the units that passed and re-verified the configuration at their cost.

"What is your biggest weakness in an international environment?" Be honest, then show the fix.

  • I used to sell myself short in English -- I would say I only helped, when I had actually led. I'm getting used to describing my work as it really was.
  • So now I prepare concrete examples in advance, and I would rather give you one real result than a list of adjectives.

They ask something you did not prepare. Buy yourself a second without looking lost.

  • That is a good question -- let me think for a second.
  • I want to give you a real example rather than a general answer, so give me a moment to pick the right one.

"Where do you see yourself in five years?" Ambition, but grounded in the role.

  • I'd like to be leading signaling delivery on a full project, not just an interface -- and I'd rather build that here than start again somewhere else.
  • In the short term, I may as well be honest: my first year is about getting used to your standards and earning that scope.
