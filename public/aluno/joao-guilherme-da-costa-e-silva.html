Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Simple vs Present Perfect vs Present Perfect Continuous vs Past Simple

GRAMMAR

How to tell your career story with the right tense — the very thing every interviewer and every supplier tests in the first two minutes of a call.

FormUseExample
Present Simple
I / you / we + verb (he/she + s)		Current role and routine.I oversee the commissioning.
Present Perfect
have / has + past participle		Experience and duration up to now (for / since).I have worked in signaling for eight years.
Present Perfect Continuous
have been + verb-ing		An activity that started in the past and is still going on right now.I have been working with German suppliers since 2019.
Past Simple
verb + -ed / irregular		Finished action, finished time frame (in 2023).I spent two months in San Francisco in 2023.
Negative (present perfect)have / has + not + past participle. In speech, contracted: haven't.We haven't received the specification.
QuestionHow long + have + subject + past participle?How long have you been in signaling?
for vs sincefor + a period of time (for eight years) · since + a starting point (since 2019)

Watch out — the classic Brazilian trap: your first language expresses duration up to now with a PRESENT tense verb, so "I am an engineer for eight years" and "I work with Germans since 2019" feel natural. Both are wrong. In English, duration up to now never takes the present simple: say "I have been an engineer for eight years" (in speech, I've been) and "I have been working with German suppliers since 2019".

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Third Conditional

GRAMMAR

How to tell the story of what SHOULD have happened on a project — without blaming anyone.

FormUseExample
Affirmative
If + had + past participle, would have + past participle		An imagined past: the condition did not happen, so the result did not happen either.If the supplier had delivered on time, we would have completed the handover in March.
Negativewouldn't have + past participle. In speech, always contracted.If we had involved procurement earlier, we wouldn't have missed the milestone.
QuestionWhat would have happened if + had + past participle?What would have happened if the order had been placed in January?
In speech (contraction)Both halves contract — this is what you actually hear on a call.If they'd sent the drawings, we'd have caught the error.
Second conditional (contrast)
If + past simple, would + verb		An unreal present or future — not the past.If we had more time, we would run another test tomorrow.
Why it mattersThe third conditional lets you review a missed milestone as a process failure instead of one person's fault. It is the language of a professional retrospective — and what separates a senior engineer from an angry one.

Watch out (classic mistake): the temptation is to put would have in BOTH halves — "if the supplier would have delivered". In English that is WRONG: the condition ALWAYS takes had + past participle ("if the supplier had delivered"). Would have belongs only in the RESULT.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Modal Perfects

GRAMMAR

How to say what SHOULD have been done, deduce WHY it was not, and disagree without breaking the relationship.

FormFunctionExample
Affirmative
should have + past participle		An obligation that was NOT met. The criticism lands on the action, never on the person."The ITP should have been submitted on June 3rd."
must have + past participleA logical DEDUCTION about the past: from the evidence, you are almost certain. It sounds analytical, not accusatory."There must have been a bottleneck on your side."
might / may have + past participleAn uncertain POSSIBILITY. It opens a door and lets the other side save face."The drawings might have been sent to the wrong contact."
Negative / impossible
could not have + past participle		IMPOSSIBILITY: the evidence rules the hypothesis out."They could not have run the FAT without the cabinet."
QuestionWhat should have happened? / Could it have been lost?"What should have happened after the design review?"
In speech (contraction)The have reduces to /əv/ — that is what you actually hear on a call."It should've been flagged up." · "They must've missed it."
Why it matters"You didn't deliver" attacks a person and the meeting stalls. "This should have been delivered" states a fact about the process — and the supplier can agree without losing face. That is how you walk out of the meeting with a corrective action instead of an enemy.

Watch out (two classic mistakes): (1) after a modal, it is ALWAYS have, never had — "should have submitted", never "should had submitted"; (2) to deduce something about the PAST, the present tense is not enough: "it must be a miscommunication" talks about right now. About the past, the correct form is "there must have been a miscommunication".

Hedging — the language of diplomatic disagreement: "It would appear that..." (instead of "you are wrong"), "One could argue that..." (instead of "that makes no sense"), "To some extent I understand your position, however..." (instead of a flat "no"). These are the three formulas that carry a disagreement without breaking the relationship — and that read well in the minutes your director will see.
