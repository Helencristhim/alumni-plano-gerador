Lesson 01 -- Pre-class

Back in the Room -- Who Is Sergio?

Reactivating your spoken English at the risk desk. Key words: risk, exposure, regulator, compliance, stakeholder, to assess, to mitigate, deadline, on track, report, partner bank, limit. Structure: present simple (your role and routine) vs present continuous (what you are working on right now).

Risk -- the chance that something bad or uncertain will happen
"My job is to measure financial risk at XP."
Exposure -- the amount of money a company could lose if things go wrong
"We are reviewing our exposure to the market this week."
Regulator -- an official body that controls an industry, like the Central Bank
"The regulator asks for a report every quarter."

GRAMMAR

Sergio is a risk manager at XP. He manages financial risk and works with the regulator and international partner banks. "I assess and mitigate our exposure every week," he says. 1. Why do we say "I assess and mitigate our exposure every week"?

B It is happening only right now, so we use the present continuous.
C It finished in the past, so we use the past simple.
2. "This week, Sergio is reviewing the new exposure limits" uses the present continuous because:
A It is his permanent job forever.
B It is in progress now, during this period.
C It is a habit he does every single day.
3. Which sentence about a state of mind is correct?
A "He is knowing the compliance rules very well."
B "He knows the compliance rules very well."
C "He know the compliance rules very well."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Simple vs Present Continuous

GRAMMAR

How to separate your role and routine from what you are doing right now.

FormUseExample
Present Simple
I / we + verb (he/she + s)		Your permanent role, facts, and routine. Signal words: usually, always, every week.I manage financial risk.
Present Continuous
am / is / are + verb-ing		What is in progress right now or this period. Signal words: now, right now, this week, at the moment.I am reviewing the report.
Third person -sWith he / she / it / the team, the simple verb takes -s.The committee meets on Monday.
Stative verbsknow, need, want, understand, mean stay in the simple form, even for now: I know the rules (not "am knowing").

Lesson 02 -- Pre-class

The Quarterly Report -- What Happened Last Quarter

Reading a risk report and reporting what happened. Key words: quarter, figures, breach, loss, spike, volatility, default, provision, to downgrade, findings. Structure: the past simple (regular and irregular verbs) to narrate what happened last quarter.

▼

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Quarter -- a three-month period in a company's financial year
"The report covers the first quarter of the year."
Figures -- the numbers and amounts in a report
"The figures came back to normal in March."
Breach -- a break of a rule or an agreed limit
"We reported the breach to the regulator."
Loss -- money that a company lost during a period
"The committee recorded a small loss."
Spike -- a sudden, sharp rise in a number
"There was a spike in volatility in January."
Volatility -- how much a price moves up and down
"Market volatility rose sharply last quarter."
Default -- a failure to pay back money that is owed
"A partner bank went into default on a loan."
Provision -- money set aside to cover a possible loss
"We set aside a provision for the loss."
To downgrade -- to lower an official rating or score
"The agency did not downgrade the desk."
Findings -- the main results and conclusions of a review
"The findings of the report were clear."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Past Simple

GRAMMAR

How to say what happened and finished in the past.

FormUseExample
Regular verbs
verb + -ed		A finished action in the past. Signal words: last quarter, in January, two days ago.We reported the breach.
Irregular verbs
new form		Same use, but the verb changes shape.rise → rose, fall → fell, set → set
Was / WereThe past of "to be" (was for I/he/she/it, were for you/we/they).The quarter was hard; the figures were high.
Did / Did notNegatives and questions use the base verb after did: we did not breach the limit; did the bank default?

Lesson 03 -- Pre-class

The Committee Decides -- Recommending Action

Reporting what the team has done and defending a recommendation in a risk committee. Key words: committee, recommend, hedge, threshold, escalate, counterparty, approve, outstanding, buffer, flag. Structure: the present perfect (have/has + past participle) for a result up to now, against the past simple for a finished time.
0%
▼

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Committee -- a group of people who meet to make decisions together
"The committee has approved the new exposure limits."
To recommend -- to suggest an action as the best thing to do
"I recommend that we raise the capital buffer."
To hedge -- to protect a position against a possible loss
"We have hedged the exposure this week."
Threshold -- a level that triggers an action when it is crossed
"The exposure has fallen below the threshold."
To escalate -- to raise an issue to a higher level for a decision
"We have not escalated it to the board yet."
Counterparty -- the other bank or company on the other side of a deal
"The counterparty has breached the internal limit."
To approve -- to officially accept a plan or an action
"The board has approved the action plan."
Outstanding -- not yet resolved or dealt with
"The counterparty issue is still outstanding."
Buffer -- an extra amount kept aside to absorb a shock
"We have raised the capital buffer this month."
To flag -- to point out something that needs attention
"I have flagged the risk to the committee."

2. Which sentence is correct?
A "We have added a hedge this week."

3. How do we make the negative present perfect?
A "We have not escalated it yet."
B "We have not escalate it yet."
C "We not have escalated it yet."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Perfect

GRAMMAR

How to say what the team has done, with a result that matters now.

FormUseExample
Positive
have / has + past participle		A past action with a result up to now. No finished time. Signals: already, just, so far, since, for.We have reviewed the exposure.
Negative
have / has not + participle		Something not done up to now. Signal: yet.We have not escalated it yet.
Question
Have / Has + subject + participle		Ask about a result up to now.Have you reviewed the report?
Past simple, not present perfectA finished time (yesterday, last quarter, two weeks ago) always takes the past simple: we reviewed it last quarter.
