Chapter 3: The Causative

Have / Get It Done

I cut my hair · I had my hair cut · She had her bag stolen

Discover the Code

Listen and Find the Pattern

"I cut my hair." (I did it myself)

"I had my hair cut." (have + object + past participle)

"I need to get my documents translated." (get + object + past participle)

"She had her bag stolen." (experience -- it happened to her)

MeaningFormExample
You do it yourselfsubject + verb + objectI cut my hair.
You arrange a servicehave + object + past participleI had my hair cut.
You arrange a service (informal)get + object + past participleI got the car fixed.
Something happens TO youhave + object + past participleShe had her bag stolen.

The have/get part changes for the tense (I had / I'm having / I'll have / I'm going to get), but the second verb is always the past participle: cut, translated, painted, fixed, set up.

Common Mistake

Wrong vs Right

✗ WRONG

"I had my hair to cut."

✓ RIGHT

"I had my hair cut."

✗ WRONG

"I had translated my documents." (= I translated them)

✓ RIGHT

"I had my documents translated."

Keep the order have/get + OBJECT + past participle. The object comes BEFORE the past participle. "I had my hair cut" -- not "I had cut my hair" (that means you did it earlier).

Read for the Main Idea

When a Place Becomes Home

When a Place Becomes Home

There is a moment, somewhere in the first weeks abroad, when the excitement of arriving gives way to a long and unglamorous list. The flights are booked, the photos are taken -- and now the taps need fixing, the documents need translating, and the internet stubbornly refuses to work. This is the quiet, real business of settling in. Nobody writes postcards about having the locks changed or getting a SIM card set up, yet this is exactly the work that turns a strange address into a home. You stop being a tourist the day you first have something fixed in your own name.

The language of all this is wonderfully practical. We rarely do these jobs ourselves; we arrange for someone else to do them. So we say we are having the apartment painted, getting the car serviced, or having a few papers translated. The grammar is small but it changes everything: instead of "I painted the wall", which means you held the brush, we say "I had the wall painted", which means a professional did it for you. Once you can hear that difference, a whole world of errands, quotes, deposits and appointments opens up to you in English -- the world of actually living somewhere, not just visiting it.

And there is, of course, the red tape. Every country has its forms, its stamps, its offices that close for lunch exactly when you arrive. The paperwork can be maddening, and the temptation is to feel defeated by it. But there is a strange satisfaction in getting it all sorted -- in dropping the coat off, picking up the quote, signing up for the service, and finally crossing the last item off the list. The reward is not the clean apartment or the working internet. The reward is the feeling, on some ordinary evening, of looking around a place that now works, in a language you now speak, and realising that you have made yourself at home in the world.

The Pattern Map

Three Uses

ARRANGE A SERVICE -- have/get + object + past participle

"I had my hair cut." · "We got the car fixed." · "I'll have the documents translated."

AN EXPERIENCE -- something happens TO you

"She had her bag stolen." · "He had his flight cancelled."

NOT THE PLAIN PASSIVE -- you still ARRANGED it

Passive: "The car was fixed." · Causative: "I got the car fixed." (I arranged it)

The second verb is always the past participle: cut, fixed, translated, painted, set up, stolen, cancelled.

Check Your Answers

Causative Answer Key

+Reveal the causative answer key
The causative = have / get + OBJECT + past participle. We use it when we ARRANGE for someone else to do a job, instead of doing it ourselves. Compare: "I cut my hair" (I did it) vs "I had my hair cut" (the hairdresser did it for me). Form in any tense: have/get changes for the tense (had / am having / will have / am going to get), but the second verb is ALWAYS the past participle (cut, translated, painted, fixed, set up). 'Have' and 'get' mean the same here, but 'get' is a little more informal. There is also an EXPERIENCE causative for bad things that happen TO you: "She had her bag stolen", "He had his flight cancelled" -- here you did not arrange it; it happened to you. Do not confuse this with the plain passive ("The car was fixed"): the causative keeps YOU as the person who arranged the service.

  2. I'm going to get my documents translated.
  3. We're having our apartment painted this week.
  4. I need to get the car fixed.
  5. She had her bag stolen at the station.
  6. I'm going to have the internet set up tomorrow.
