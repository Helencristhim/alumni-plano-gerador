Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Present Simple (I work / she works)

GRAMMAR

Use the present simple for what you do every day. The secret is the small -s: with she, he, or it, the verb takes an -s. In questions and negatives, the -s moves to does.

SubjectYes (+)No (-)Question (?)
I / you / we / theyI work at a university.I do not work on Sunday.Do you work here?
he / she / itShe works at a school.She does not travel alone.Does she study English?

Watch out: do not forget the -s with she / he (she works, not "she work"). And use doesn't, never "don't", with she / he: "He doesn't speak Portuguese". Place: at an institution (at a university), in a city (in Maceio).

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Past Simple (I worked / I went)

GRAMMAR

Use the past simple for finished actions in the past. There are two families of verbs: regular verbs add -ed (work → worked), and irregular verbs have a new form to learn by heart (go → went).

TypePresentPast (+)Negative (-)
Regular (+ ed)work / study / startworked / studied / startedI did not work
Irregular (new form)go / lead / be / havewent / led / was / hadI did not go

Watch out: after did not (didn't), always use the base verb: "I didn't study", never "I didn't studied". For a past habit that is now finished, use used to + base verb: "I used to work in administration". Time words: in 2005, at nineteen, for many years, last year, ago.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Present Continuous (I am working / she is arriving)

GRAMMAR

Use the present continuous for something happening right now or around now (this week, these days). It always has two parts: the verb be (am / is / are) + verb-ing.

Subject+ Yes- No? Question
II am workingI am not workingAm I working?
he / she / itShe is workingShe is not workingIs she working?
you / we / theyWe are workingWe are not workingAre we working?

Spelling of verb-ing: make → making (drop the e), sit → sitting (double the consonant), travel → traveling. Compare: the present simple is for routines ("I travel once a month"), the present continuous is for now ("I am traveling right now"). Useful phrase: I am looking forward to + noun or verb-ing ("I am looking forward to the conference").

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Habit or Now?

GRAMMAR

English has two presents. Use the present simple for a habit, a routine, or a permanent fact (I travel). Use the present continuous (am/is/are + verb-ing) for something happening now or a temporary situation (I am traveling). One question decides: habit or now?

MeaningTenseFormSignal words
Habit / routine / factPresent simpleI travel / she travelsalways, usually, every day
Now / temporaryPresent continuousI am traveling / she is travelingnow, at the moment, this week, currently

Watch out: in the present simple, add -s for she/he/it: "she travels". In the present continuous, don't forget am / is / are: "I am preparing", never "I preparing". Two useful phrases for a dinner: It depends on... (a careful answer) and I would rather... (a polite preference).

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Will or Going To?

GRAMMAR

English has two ways to talk about the future. Use going to for a plan you already have (I am going to present tomorrow). Use will for an offer, a promise, or a decision at the moment of speaking (I'll help you). After will, always use the base verb, with no "to" and no "-s".

UseFormExample
Offer / decision nowwill + base verbI will help → I'll help
Promise (negative)won't + base verbI won't be late
Request / questionWill + you + base verb?Will you join us?
A plan (decided before)going to + base verbI am going to present tomorrow

Watch out: after will, never use "to" and never add "-s": "she will help", not "she will helps" and not "she will to help". The short forms are I'll, she'll, we'll and the negative won't. Two useful phrases for a round table: I agree and I disagree.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Comparatives and Superlatives (larger than / more formal than / the best)

GRAMMAR

Use a comparative to compare two things (with than), and a superlative to point to the top one (with the). Short adjective (1 syllable): add -er / -est. Long adjective (2+ syllables): use more / the most.

AdjectiveComparative (two things)Superlative (the top one)
Short: largelarger thanthe largest
Short: bigbigger thanthe biggest
Long: formalmore formal thanthe most formal
Long: importantmore important thanthe most important
Irregular: goodbetter thanthe best
Irregular: badworse thanthe worst

Never mix the two: say bigger (not "more big"), and the best (not "the most best"). Always than after a comparative, and the before a superlative. Irregular: good → better → the best; bad → worse → the worst; far → farther → the farthest.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- How Much? How Many?

GRAMMAR

One question decides everything: can you count it? This is how English says how much and how many.

QuantifierUse it withExample
many · a few · too manyCOUNTABLE things — you can count them: 1 seat, 2 seats, 3 seats.too many meetings · a few documents · not many seats
much · a little · too muchUNCOUNTABLE things — you cannot count them: time, money, space, preparation.not much time · a little money · too much preparation
a lot of · enoughBOTH — countable and uncountable. Safe and easy when in doubt.a lot of documents · a lot of time · enough seats · enough money
Questions: How many? / How much?How many for countable, How much for uncountable.How many documents? · How much time?
Ready phrase: Time flies.Nothing to build — one idiom. It means time passes very fast. Perfect for the countdown to November."November is close already. Time flies!"

Common mistake: many people say "much meetings" or "many time". Swap them: it is many meetings (you can count them) and much time (you cannot). When in doubt, a lot of and enough work for both, every time.
