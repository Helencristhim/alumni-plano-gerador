Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

Ziláudio is a farmer and a lawyer. He lives in Sorriso, in the state of Mato Grosso. He grows soybeans, and it is his main crop. His fields are very big: the farm is two thousand hectares. The harvest starts in January. He buys seeds from a supplier in Cuiaba.

Ziláudio works at a law firm too. He is a lawyer for small companies. "I am a farmer and a lawyer," he says. "I work in two worlds." He travels to the United States two times a year, and he wants to speak English at work.

1. Why do we say "Ziláudio is a farmer" but "he grows soybeans"?
A Be says WHAT he is. A normal verb says what he DOES.
B Because "farmer" is a long word and "soybeans" is short.
C Because one is the past and the other is the present.
2. The text says "he grows", "he lives", "he works". Where does the -s come from?
A It is a plural: he grows many things.
B In the present simple, he / she / it always takes -s.
C It only happens with the verb "to grow".
3. "He lives in Sorriso." "He works at a law firm." Why not the same word?
A They are the same. You can use in or at anywhere.
B At is for big places and in is for small places.
C In is for a city or a country. At is for a place where you do something, like a firm.
4. Which sentence about Ziláudio is correct?
A "He is grow soybeans in Mato Grosso."
B "He is a farmer, and he grows soybeans in Mato Grosso."
C "He a farmer and grow soybeans in Mato Grosso."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- To Be and the Present Simple

GRAMMAR

Two forms. Be says what you ARE. The present simple says what you DO.

FormUseExample
To be
I am · he / she / it is · we / they are		WHAT you are: your job, your place.I am a farmer.
Present Simple
I / you / we + verb		What you DO: your routine, what is always true.I grow soybeans.
Third person
he / she / it + verb-s		With he, she or it, the verb always takes -s.He grows soybeans.
NegativeWith be: am / is / are + not. With other verbs: do not / does not + verb.I am not a doctor. I do not grow corn.
QuestionWith be: Are you...? With other verbs: Do you + verb?Are you a farmer? Do you grow soybeans?
in · at · forin + a city or a country (in Sorriso, in Brazil) · at + a place where you do something (at a law firm) · for + who you work for, or how long (for small companies, for fifteen years)

Watch out -- the classic mistake: never use be and a normal verb together. "I am work in Sorriso" is wrong — it is either "I am a farmer" (be) or "I work in Sorriso" (present simple), never both. And in English you always need the subject: "I am a farmer", never "Am a farmer".

A "How much rain do you get in the season?"

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- How Much, How Many, and the Third Person

GRAMMAR

Two number questions, one small verb ending. Ask yourself: can I count it?

FormUseExample
How manyThings you can COUNT: trucks, silos, acres, tons.How many trucks do you use?
How muchThings you do NOT count: rain, soybean, storage, machinery.How much rain do you get?
Third person
he / she / it / the farm + verb-s		With he, she, it or the farm, the verb takes -s.The farm has. It produces.
Number + unitAfter a number, the unit stays as it is — no extra word.two thousand hectares · fifty tons
on · from · toon + a surface (on the farm, on the land) · from ... to + one point to another, in time or in space (from January to March, from the farm to the port)

Watch out -- the classic mistake: never say "how much trucks" — you can count trucks, so it is "how many trucks". And with the farm or it, the verb is never bare: "The farm has", not "The farm have".

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

Ziláudio is at a big booth at the fair. He walks up to an exhibitor and starts a conversation. "Hi, I'm Ziláudio," he says. "What does your company do?" The exhibitor smiles. They talk with each other about soybeans and seeds.

Ziláudio wants more information. "Do you have a brochure?" he asks. "Of course," she says, and gives him one. Then he asks politely: "Could I have your business card?" She gives him her card, and he gives her his — a good contact in the same industry. Her booth is next to the entrance, so it is easy to find again.

1. Ziláudio says "Could I have your business card?" instead of "I want your card." Why?
A Could makes the request polite. "I want" sounds like an order.
B Because "could" is the past of "can".
C Because you only use "could" with business cards.
2. "Do you have a brochure?" Why does the question start with do?
A Because the answer is yes.
B In English, a question about an action needs a starter word: Do you + verb.
C Because "brochure" is a French word.
3. "He is at the booth." "Her booth is next to the entrance." Why not the same word?
A They are the same. You can use at or next to anywhere.
B At is an exact point (at the booth). Next to means right beside something.
C At is for people and next to is for places.
4. Which question is correct?
A "Where you are from?"
B "Where are you from?"
C "From where you are?"

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Asking Questions & Being Polite

GRAMMAR

A question starts with a special word. To ask for something, could is the polite key.

FormUseExample
be + you
Are you...? / Where are you...?		Ask about a fact: place, name, job.Where are you from?
Do + you + verb
Do you...? / What do you...?		Ask about an action or a habit.Do you have a brochure?
Could + I / you + verbAsk for something the polite way (a request).Could I have your card?
Short answerAnswer with the same helper word.Yes, I do. / Of course, here you are.
at · with · next toat + an exact point (at the booth, at the entrance) · with + who you talk or work with (with an exhibitor) · next to + right beside something (next to hall two)

Watch out -- the classic mistake: a question in English needs a starter word. "You have a card?" is wrong — say "Do you have a card?" And never ask for something with "I want" — it sounds like an order. Use "Could I have...?", the polite way. In business, polite always wins.
