Reading the Signs -- Guessing What Is Going On When You Are Abroad

Reading a situation you cannot fully understand and guessing out loud with modals of deduction. Key words: to make sense of something, to figure something out, a hunch, to jump to conclusions, to read between the lines, a dead giveaway, to take a wild guess, to be bound to happen, odds are, to strike someone as, come to think of it, for all I know. Focus: modals of deduction (must be / might be / cannot be for now; must have / might have / cannot have + past participle for the past).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Ouça cada palavra e leia o exemplo. Toque em Listen para escutar.

To make sense of something -- to understand something that is confusing (entender algo confuso)
"I could not make sense of the timetable at all."
To figure something out -- to work something out, to find the answer (descobrir, sacar como algo funciona)
"It took me a while to figure out the ticket machine."
A hunch -- a feeling that something is true, based on intuition (um palpite, uma intuição)
"I had a hunch the ferry would be late, and it was."
To jump to conclusions -- to decide something too quickly, without enough information (tirar conclusões precipitadas)
"Do not jump to conclusions; there may be a simple reason."
To read between the lines -- to understand a meaning that is not said directly (ler nas entrelinhas)
"She did not complain, but reading between the lines, she was upset."
A dead giveaway -- a clear sign that reveals the truth about something (um sinal claro que entrega a verdade)
"His sunburn was a dead giveaway that he was a tourist."
To take a wild guess -- to guess when you have very little information (dar um chute, arriscar um palpite)
"If I had to take a wild guess, I would say it is a wedding."
To be bound to happen -- to be very likely or almost certain to happen (ser bem provável, quase certo de acontecer)
"In a place this beautiful, you are bound to meet interesting people."
Odds are -- it is probable that; most likely (as chances são de que; provavelmente)
"Odds are the shop is shut for lunch, not for the day."
To strike someone as -- to give someone a particular impression (dar a impressão de; parecer a alguém)
"She strikes me as someone who has travelled a lot."
Come to think of it -- a phrase used when you suddenly realize something (agora que penso nisso, pensando bem)
"Come to think of it, the museum is closed on Mondays."
For all I know -- used to say you have no real information about something (que eu saiba; pelo que sei (ou seja, não sei ao certo))
"For all I know, the festival could start tomorrow."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Leia o texto e responda às perguntas.

At a harbor in Norway, Marcelo could not make sense of the crowd by the water. He had a hunch that something rare was happening, but he did not want to jump to conclusions. "The boats have not gone out," he thought, "so it must be serious, or it must be something wonderful." An old man had been standing there since dawn. "He cannot have wandered down by chance," Marcelo said. "He must have known this was coming." Odds are he had seen it many times. When we guess in English, we use must for what is almost certain, might or could for what is possible, and cannot for what is impossible. For the past, we add have and a past participle: must have known, might have missed, cannot have driven.

1. O velho estava lá desde o amanhecer. Por que Marcelo diz "he cannot have wandered down", e não "he must not have"?
A Para uma dedução negativa sobre o passado, o inglês usa cannot have (algo impossível), não "must not have".
B Porque "wandered" está escrito errado.
C Porque "cannot have" e "must not have" significam a mesma coisa.
2. Which sentence guesses correctly about the past?
A "She must has left."
B "She must have left."
C "She must to leave."
3. Every shop on the street is shut on a sunny Wednesday. Which guess is strongest?
A "It must be a public holiday."
B "It could be a holiday, maybe."
C "It cannot be a working day."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Modals of Deduction

GRAMMAR

Como adivinhar em inglês (explicação em inglês e português).

How sure? / CertezaNow (modal + be)Past (modal + have + participle)
almost certain / quase certoIt must be closed.It must have closed.
possible / possívelIt might / could be closed.It might / could have closed.
impossible / impossívelIt cannot be closed.It cannot have closed.

Atenção: depois de um modal, use o verbo puro: "must be", nunca "must to be". No passado, sempre have + partícipio: "must have gone", nunca "must have went". Para um palpite negativo sobre o passado, o inglês diz cannot have (can't have), não "must not have".

Lesson 13 — Reading the Signs -- Guessing What Is Going On When You Are Abroad

Film
Rear Window (1954) -- directed by Alfred Hitchcock

A photographer with a broken leg spends his days watching the neighbors across the courtyard, and slowly becomes convinced that one of them must have committed a crime. He has no proof, only signs: a suitcase, a missing wife, a late-night departure. It is the whole grammar of tonight's lesson turned into a film -- reading a situation from a distance and deducing what must have happened. Connection to Lesson 13: notice how every guess he makes is a modal of deduction ("he must have...", "she cannot have just left...").

Tip: pause on a scene and say your own deduction out loud before he does -- "He must have...", "She might have...", "That cannot be...".
