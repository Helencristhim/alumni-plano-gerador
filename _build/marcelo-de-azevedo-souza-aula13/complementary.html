Aula 13 -- Reading the Signs

Film
Rear Window (1954) -- directed by Alfred Hitchcock

A photographer with a broken leg spends his days watching the neighbors across the courtyard, and slowly becomes convinced that one of them must have committed a crime. He has no proof, only signs: a suitcase, a missing wife, a late-night departure. It is the whole grammar of tonight's lesson turned into a film -- reading a situation from a distance and deducing what must have happened. Connection to Lesson 13: notice how every guess he makes is a modal of deduction ("he must have...", "she cannot have just left...").

Tip: pause on a scene and say your own deduction out loud before he does -- "He must have...", "She might have...", "That cannot be...".

Podcast
Cautionary Tales -- Tim Harford -- any episode

Tim Harford tells true stories about mistakes and what we can learn from them -- and almost every one turns on someone reading the signs wrongly and jumping to conclusions. It is the perfect companion to tonight's lesson: a whole podcast about the moment a hunch turns out to be right, or wonderfully wrong. Great ear training too, full of natural deduction ("they must have known...", "it could not have been an accident").

Tip: after one episode, sum up the key mistake in three sentences of deduction -- "They must have thought...", "They cannot have realised...", "Odds are they...".

How to use modal verbs of deduction -- English In A Minute (BBC Learning English)

A short, clear BBC lesson on exactly tonight's grammar: how must, can't, may, might and could are used to guess how sure we are about something. It puts the rules from your Grammar Tip into a quick, memorable minute -- the perfect warm-up before you make your own deductions out loud.

Tip: pause and repeat each example aloud, then invent one of your own with must, might and cannot before moving on.
