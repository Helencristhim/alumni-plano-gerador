Chapter 3: The Structures

Three Structures, Reviewed

Conditionals, reported speech and diplomatic modals — the grammar of the first half

Grammar Review

Notice the Pattern

"If we had not tested that edge case, the outage would have been worse."

"The engineer said the root cause was a bad config."

"It might be worth rolling back, rather than shipping tonight."

"If I were you, I would escalate it now."

Which structure is each one? What is the pattern behind each?

StructurePatternUse it for
Third conditionalIf + past perfect, would have + past participleAn unreal past: a postmortem, a "what if"
Reported speechsaid (that) + backshift: is → was, will → wouldRelaying what someone said on a call
Diplomatic modalsmight, could, would, "it might be worth..."Softening a recommendation or a disagreement
Second conditionalIf + past, would + verb ("if I were you...")Giving advice about an unreal present
Key ideaYou are not learning these tonight — you already own them. The review is about using them fluently, without stopping to build each sentence.

These four structures carry most of what you say at work: a postmortem, a debrief, a recommendation, a piece of advice.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"If we would have tested it, the outage would not have happened."
"If we had tested it, the outage would not have happened."
"She said the root cause is a bad config."
"She said the root cause was a bad config."
"You should to escalate it now."
"You should escalate it now."

In the third conditional, the if clause takes had + past participle, never "would have". Reported speech needs the backshift: is becomes was. Modals like should take the bare verb: "should escalate", never "should to escalate".

Chapter 4: The Benchmark

The Review Conversation

Ana's midpoint benchmark with her mentor, Marcus

Dialogue

The Midpoint Review

M
Ana, welcome to your midpoint review. No pressure -- just tell me, how do you feel about your English now?
A
Honestly, much more confident. Six months ago, I would have frozen on a call like this.
M
Give me one real example. Where did your English make a difference?
A
Last week, during an outage, I reported what the on-call engineer had said and flagged a discrepancy in the logs.
M
Nice. And if you had not caught that discrepancy?
A
If we had not caught it, the root cause would have stayed hidden, and the outage would have been worse.
M
Good. Now, a hard one -- would you ever push back on a manager?
A
I would. I would signpost my answer, then say it might be worth rolling back, rather than shipping, if I were unsure.
M
That is exactly the benchmark I wanted. Let me reiterate your goal for the second half: keep speaking, and be a little bolder.
