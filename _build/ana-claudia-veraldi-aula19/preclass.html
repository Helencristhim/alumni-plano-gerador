Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

A quick review of twelve words from the first half. Listen to each one and read the example.

Requirement -- something the system must do, agreed with the client
"Each requirement is a small promise to the client."
To escalate -- to pass a serious issue to a higher level or team
"If it blocks the release, we escalate it right away."
Root cause -- the real, underlying reason for a problem
"The root cause was a bad config, not the network."
Blocker -- something that stops you from making progress on a task
"My only blocker is that the new build is not ready."
To follow up -- to check on something or continue it after a first contact
"I will follow up with the dev team after the review."
Edge case -- an unusual situation at the limit of normal use
"As an edge case, I test a password with 200 characters."
To deploy -- to release software so that users can use it
"We deploy the new version every two weeks."
Outage -- a period when a system is down and unavailable
"The outage lasted two hours, and users could not log in."
Trade-off -- a balance between two good things you cannot both fully have
"It is a trade-off between speed and safety."
To mitigate -- to make a risk or a problem less serious
"To mitigate the risk, she kept the old version ready."
To reiterate -- to say something again to make it clear or to stress it
"Let me reiterate the one decision we made on the call."
To signpost -- to use small phrases that guide the listener through your answer
"She signposts her answer: first this, then that."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Halfway through the program, Ana prepared for her midpoint review by looking back at the structures she now owned. She thought about a recent outage. On the call, the on-call engineer had said, "the fix is live", so Ana could report that he said the fix was live, shifting the tense one step back. She could also describe what almost happened: "if we had not caught the discrepancy, the root cause would have stayed hidden, and the outage would have been worse." That is a third conditional -- an unreal past, with had + past participle in the if clause and would have + past participle in the result. And when she disagreed with a plan, she no longer sounded blunt: instead of "we must not deploy on Friday", she softened it with a modal -- "it might be worth waiting, and if I were you, I would wait until Monday." None of this was new tonight; it was a review. The point of the midpoint is not to learn more words, but to use the words and structures you already have -- fluently, without stopping to build each sentence.

1. The engineer said, "the fix is live." How does Ana report it?
A He said the fix is live.
B He said the fix was live.
C He says the fix will be live.
2. Which sentence is a correct third conditional?
A If we would have caught it, the outage would not happen.
B If we had caught it, the outage would not have happened.
C If we catch it, the outage would not have happened.
3. What is the point of the midpoint review, according to the text?
A To use the words and structures she already has, fluently.
B To learn as many brand-new words as possible.
C To stop using conditionals and reported speech.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Structures Reviewed

GRAMMAR

The three structures from the first half, side by side. You already own them -- the goal is to use them fluently.

StructurePatternExample
Third conditionalIf + had + past participle, would have + past participleIf we had tested it, the outage would not have happened.
Reported speechsaid (that) + backshift: is → was, will → wouldHe said the root cause was a bad config.
Diplomatic modalsmight / could / would + verb; "it might be worth..."It might be worth rolling back tonight.
Key ruleIn the third conditional, never use "would have" in the if clause. Reported speech needs the backshift. Modals take the bare verb: "should escalate", not "should to escalate".
