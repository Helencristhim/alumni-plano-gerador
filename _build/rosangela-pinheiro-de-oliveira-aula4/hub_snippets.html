Lesson 04 -- Pre-class

What Is Happening Right Now? -- Boarding, the Flight and the Hotel Arrival

Say what is happening at this moment: the flight is boarding, the receptionist is looking for your reservation, we are staying for six nights. Key words: to board, overhead bin, flight attendant, seat belt, to land, hotel lobby, front desk, reservation, room key, elevator. Structure: the present continuous (am / is / are + verb-ing) for now and for this trip, against the present simple for what you always do. Expression: I am looking forward to...

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to every word and read the example. Five words live inside the plane, and five live inside the hotel. Together they are your first hour in Europe.

To board -- to get on the plane when they call your row
"We are boarding at gate twenty-two."
Overhead bin -- the closed box above your seat, where your carry-on goes
"My carry-on is in the overhead bin."
Flight attendant -- the person who works inside the plane and helps the passengers
"The flight attendant is standing at the door."
Seat belt -- the belt you close for take-off and landing
"Please close your seat belt."
To land -- when the plane touches the ground at the end of the flight
"We are landing in Lisbon at eight o'clock."
Hotel lobby -- the first big room when you enter a hotel, with sofas
"My husband is waiting in the hotel lobby."
Front desk -- the counter in the hotel where they give you your key
"I am going to the front desk alone."
Reservation -- the room you booked before you arrived, with your name on it
"Our reservation is under the name Pinheiro de Oliveira."
Room key -- the small card that opens the door of your room
"Here is your room key. Room four eleven."
Elevator -- the small room that takes you up to your floor
"The elevator is on your left."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

It is four o'clock, and Rosângela is standing at gate twenty-two. The flight is boarding right now. A flight attendant is standing at the door of the plane, and she is smiling. Rosângela is putting her carry-on in the overhead bin, and she is closing her seat belt. She usually sleeps on a night flight, but tonight she is not sleeping: she is looking out of the window. Nine hours later, the plane is landing in Lisbon. Now she is walking into the hotel lobby, and she is going to the front desk alone. Her husband is sitting on a sofa, and he is waiting with the bags. "We have a reservation, under the name Oliveira." The receptionist is looking at his computer, and he is not finding it. Rosângela is not panicking. "Our reservation is under the name Pinheiro de Oliveira. We are staying for six nights." "Here it is. Room four eleven. Here is your room key, and the elevator is on your left." She is looking forward to walking around Lisbon, and she is doing it all alone.

1. "The flight is boarding right now." Why is it "is boarding" and not "boards"?
A Because "board" is an irregular verb.
B Because it is happening at this moment, while she speaks. For now, English uses am / is / are + verb-ing.
C Because it happened yesterday.
2. "She usually sleeps on a night flight, but tonight she is not sleeping." Why are there two different tenses?
A "Usually sleeps" is her habit, every trip. "Is not sleeping" is only tonight, right now.
B Because the first one is a question and the second one is an answer.
C Because "sleep" has no -ing form.
3. The receptionist is not finding the reservation. Where does "not" go?
A At the end: "He is finding not it."
B In the middle, between the two pieces: is + not + finding.
C Before the subject: "Not he is finding it."
4. The receptionist is not finding the name, and her husband is sitting on a sofa. What does Rosângela do?
A She calls her husband and he talks to the receptionist for her.
B She stays calm and gives her full name: Pinheiro de Oliveira.
C She looks for another hotel.
5. "She is looking forward to walking around Lisbon." Why "walking" and not "walk"?
A Because "to walk" does not exist in English.
B Because the sentence is in the past.
C Because after "looking forward to", the verb always takes -ing. That "to" is not the "to" of "to walk".

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Present Continuous

GRAMMAR

In Lessons 2 and 3 you learned to talk about the past. Now you talk about this exact moment.

FormUseExample
Yes
am / is / are + verb-ing		Two pieces, always. Something happening at this moment.I am waiting. She is boarding. We are staying.
No
am / is / are + not + verb-ing		"not" goes in the middle, between the two pieces.He is not sleeping. I am not finding it.
Question
Am / Is / Are + you + verb-ing?		The first piece jumps to the front. The -ing never moves.Are you checking in? What is he doing?
Now vs alwaysFor a habit, use the present simple, with no -ing. For this moment or this trip, use the present continuous.I usually travel in September. / Right now I am traveling in July.
Spelling of -ingMost verbs just add -ing. Short verbs double the last letter; verbs in -e lose the e.board → boarding · sit → sitting · come → coming
looking forward to + verb-ingThis "to" is not the "to" of "to walk". After it, the verb takes -ing.I am looking forward to walking around Lisbon. (never "to walk")

The one rule to remember: the present continuous has two pieces — am / is / are AND the verb with -ing. One piece alone is never enough. "I am going", never "I am go". "She is waiting", never "She is wait".

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt. There is no right or wrong version. Speak for 60 seconds, with no script.

Close your eyes and put yourself in the hotel lobby in Lisbon, right now, with your bags. Tell me what is happening around you, as if it is happening at this moment: What are you carrying? What is your husband doing? What is the receptionist doing? Then say what you usually do on a trip, and what you are doing differently today. Use at least four sentences with "am / is / are + verb-ing", and finish with one sentence starting "I am looking forward to..." Take your time and do not read from a script.
