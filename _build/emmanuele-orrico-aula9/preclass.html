Lesson 09 -- Pre-class

Networking in English -- Starting and Keeping a Conversation

Open, keep and close a conversation at a global event: the welcome reception, the conference dinner and the moment to exchange contacts. Key words: conference, panel, speaker, session, to catch up, to mention, to break the ice, once in a while. This lesson has no new grammar: it is a review that puts together the present simple (I work), the present continuous (right now we are launching) and going to (I am going to present) inside one real conversation. The flow of the lesson: Open → Share → Ask → Bridge → Close. Opener of the lesson: "Have you been to this conference before?"

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. These are the eight words of the room.

Conference -- a big professional event with talks and meetings
"The immunology conference is in São Paulo this year."
Panel -- a group of experts who discuss a topic on a stage
"She is going to speak on the panel about market access."
Speaker -- the person who gives a talk at an event
"The main speaker is a doctor from Paris."
Session -- one part of an event, with one topic
"The morning session is about new treatments."
To catch up -- to talk with someone you have not seen for a while
"Let's catch up at the dinner tonight."
To mention -- to say something quickly, not as the main point
"She mentioned the new study during the coffee break."
To break the ice -- to start a conversation and make people relax
"A simple question is a good way to break the ice."
Once in a while -- sometimes, but not often
"We meet the Global team once in a while."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

The conference dinner starts at seven. Emmanuele does not know many people, so she decides to break the ice. She goes to a man near the window. "Hi, I'm Emmanuele. Have you been to this conference before?" she says. The man is from the Global team. They start to talk. "What do you do?" he asks. "I work at Sanofi Brazil. I am the national demand manager," she answers. "Right now, we are launching a new product in dermatology." She also mentions the immunology panel from the afternoon session. "And in August, I am going to present the results to the Global team," she adds. "What about you?" They only see each other once in a while, so it is good to catch up. At the end, she says: "It was great to catch up. Let's stay in touch."

1. "I work at Sanofi Brazil." Why is it "work" and not "am working"?
A Because it is the past.
B Because it is her JOB — a fact that is always true. For your job, use the present simple.
C Because "work" is an irregular verb.
2. "Right now, we are launching a new product." Why the -ing form here?
A Because it is happening NOW, in this period. "Right now" is the signal.
B Because it is a plan for next year.
C Because "we" is plural.
3. "What do you do?" Why is there a "do" in the question?
A Because every English question needs "what".
B Because a present simple question needs "do": "What do you do?", never "What you do?".
C Because the verb "do" is repeated for style.
4. Which line is the best way to CLOSE the conversation?
A "Ok, bye."
B "It was great to catch up. Let's stay in touch."
C "I go now."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Three Times, One Conversation

GRAMMAR

No new grammar today. This is a review: in one real conversation, the three times you already know live together.

TimeUse it forExample
Present simple
(I work / I am)		Your job and facts that are always true. In a question, add do: "What do you do?"I work in immunology. I am the national demand manager.
Present continuous
(we are launching)		What is happening NOW, in this period. Signal words: right now, at the moment.Right now, we are launching a new product.
Going to
(I am going to present)		A plan for the future. You already decided it.In August, I am going to present the results.
Ready opener: Have you been to this conference before?One block to break the ice. Do not build it — use it whole, every time."Have you been to this conference before?"

The flow: Open → Share → Ask → Bridge → Close. You do not improvise the whole conversation — you just take the next step. The line that helps you most is the bridge: "What about you?" — it gives the ball back and takes the weight off you.

Have the whole conversation, out loud, in five steps: OPEN (break the ice with a question), SHARE (your name, your company, your job), ASK (a question to the other person), BRIDGE (say your current project and then "What about you?"), and CLOSE (end warmly: "It was great to catch up.
