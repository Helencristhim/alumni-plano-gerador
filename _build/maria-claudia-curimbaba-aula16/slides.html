Chapter 3 -- Shall, Must & May

Language Discovery

Obligation, requirement, permission -- and the passive for the term

Discovery

Listen and Notice

What does each modal tell you -- a duty, a requirement, a permission, or a prohibition?

"Each party shall deliver the goods within thirty days."

"The warranty must cover twelve months."

"Either party may terminate the agreement with sixty days notice."

"Confidential information must not be disclosed to any third party."

The Rule

Shall, Must & May

ModalMeaningExample
shall + base verbobligation / firm duty"each party shall deliver the goods"
must + base verbrequirement / hard condition"the warranty must cover twelve months"
may + base verbpermission / right (optional)"either party may terminate the agreement"
shall not / must not / may notprohibition"the information must not be disclosed"

Key idea: always use modal + base verb, never "shall to deliver". Shall and must bind you; may only gives you a right. And to report the term itself, use the passive: modal + be + past participle ("the goods shall be delivered", "the data must not be disclosed").

Language Focus

Writing the Term

Contracts state most terms in the passive: modal + be + past participle. The doer is often obvious, so you can leave it out.

"The goods shall be delivered within thirty days."

"The payment must be made on time."

"Confidential information must not be disclosed."

Pattern: subject + shall / must / may (+ not) + be + past participle (delivered, made, disclosed). Add "by + doer" only if it matters ("the report shall be approved by the board"). The modal carries the rule; the passive keeps the focus on the term.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"The goods shall delivered within thirty days."
"The goods shall be delivered within thirty days."

The passive always needs be: say "the goods shall be delivered", never "the goods shall delivered". And a modal always takes the base verb, never "to": say "each party shall deliver", never "shall to deliver".

Artefact

The Supply Agreement

SUPPLY AGREEMENT · CLAUSE SUMMARY

Curimbaba Holding & Houston Partners

Counterparty: Maria Claudia Curimbaba · Governing law: New York

Clause 1 -- Obligations (shall)

Each party shall act in good faith. The counterparty shall deliver the goods within thirty days; the goods shall be delivered in full and on time.

Clause 2 -- Requirements (must)

The agreement stipulates that the warranty must cover twelve months and the payment must be made on time. Under the confidentiality covenant, all data must not be disclosed to any third party.

Clause 3 -- Permissions (may)

Either party may terminate the agreement with sixty days notice and may request an audit once a year. The contract may be renewed by either party.

Clause 4 -- Force majeure, default & jurisdiction

If delivery is prevented by events beyond control, neither party shall be in default, and the affected party has a clear remedy. Any dispute shall be settled in the courts of the New York jurisdiction.

The parties hereby agree to the terms above. Signed: Maria Claudia Curimbaba, on behalf of the holding.

Comprehension

About the Agreement

1. Why do the obligation clauses use "shall"?

Because they are firm duties, not choices: each party shall act in good faith, and the counterparty shall deliver the goods within thirty days. "Shall" makes the duty binding.

2. Which clause uses "may", and what does it allow?

Clause 3, the permissions: either party may terminate with sixty days notice, may request an audit once a year, and the contract may be renewed. "May" gives a right, not a duty.

3. Where is the passive used, and why?

In the terms themselves -- "the goods shall be delivered", "the payment must be made", "data must not be disclosed", "any dispute shall be settled" -- because the focus is on the term, not on who does it.
