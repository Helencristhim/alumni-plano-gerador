The World's Most Amazing Vacation Rentals — Season 1, Episode 1

Three friends visit the same city and each one chooses a different place to stay: one expensive, one middle, one cheap. Then they sit down together and compare. Listen to how they do it: "How much is it a night?", "How many people can sleep here?", "It is small, but it is worth it." That is your whole lesson, in three voices, in a real house. Every episode is a budget conversation — the same one you had at the desk in Amsterdam.

Tip: watch with English subtitles, and do not try to catch every word. Stop each time somebody says a price or a number, and say the question out loud yourself before they do: "How much is it?" or "How many rooms are there?". Ask yourself the one question of this lesson every time: can I count it?

Podcast
Speak English Now — Episode 66: Route 66, How to Plan a Road Trip (A2-B1)

Georgiana plans a whole trip out loud, and she uses the words of this lesson while she does it: plan your trip, schedule your trip between March and June, go in the spring or the fall and not in the summer — which is exactly what off-peak means, without the word. She is American, she speaks slowly, and she never explains grammar: she just plans, and you listen. At the end there is a short story to practice the structures.

Tip: listen once with your eyes closed, and do not read — your ear before your eyes. Then listen again with the free transcript open, and mark every sentence that says WHEN to go and WHY. Then close everything and plan one day of your own trip out loud, the same way she does.

YouTube
engVid — Learn English Tenses: FUTURE, "will" or "going to"? (free, no subscription)

This is the exact question of your lesson, and nothing else: when do you say "will", and when do you say "going to"? Rebecca is a teacher with forty years in the classroom, she writes every sentence on the board while she says it, and she uses the same rule we used in class — the plan you made before is "going to", the decision you make in the second is "will". She is North American, like the voices in your material.

Tip: watch it twice. The first time, only listen. The second time, pause before every example and decide yourself: plan, or decision made right now? Then say your own sentence out loud. Listen for one thing especially: she says "I'll", not "I will" — that is what your ear will hear at a real ticket desk.
