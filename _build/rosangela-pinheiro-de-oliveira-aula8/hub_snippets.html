Lesson 08 -- Pre-class

Planning the Perfect Day -- Using "Will" for Spontaneous Decisions and Talking About Quantities

Plan one day in Amsterdam alone, and fix it when it breaks: read the opening hours, book a time slot, find the museum sold out at nine in the morning, and decide what to do about it on the spot. Key words: itinerary, schedule, time slot, free time, guided tour, opening hours, sold out, budget, crowded, off-peak. Structure: will for the decisions you make in the moment (against going to for the plans you made before), and much / many / a lot of / a little / a few. Expression: It is worth it.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to every word and read the example. These are the ten words of one day as a tourist. Watch number two: schedule is said SKED-jool in American English -- the "ch" is a K. And watch number four: free time is free of plans, not free of money.

Itinerary -- the list of the places you visit on a trip, one after another, in order
"My itinerary has only three lines on it today."
Schedule -- the list of the times when things happen: the trains, the tours, the museum
"We walked along the canal with no schedule."
Time slot -- the half hour on your ticket, when you are allowed to come in
"My time slot is nine to nine thirty."
Free time -- the hours in your day with nothing planned in them
"Three hours of free time, and nothing in the itinerary."
Guided tour -- a visit with a person who walks with the group and explains everything
"The guided tour is one hour, and it is nine euros."
Opening hours -- the time a place opens and the time it closes
"The opening hours are nine in the morning until six in the evening."
Sold out -- when there are no tickets left, because everybody bought them
"There is a sign on the door: today is sold out."
Budget -- the money you decide to spend on something, and not one euro more
"Our budget for the day is sixty euros for the two of us."
Crowded -- full of people, with no space to walk
"At two in the afternoon the museum is very crowded."
Off-peak -- the quiet hours, when a place is not busy and it often costs less
"Nine in the morning is off-peak: there are not many people."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

It is eight in the morning in Amsterdam, and Rosângela is at the breakfast table with her itinerary next to the coffee. It has three lines on it: the museum, a canal boat, and dinner near the water. Her budget is sixty euros for two people. "We are going to take the tram at nine," she says to her husband. She decided that last night, at the hotel, with the opening hours open on her phone.

At nine there is a sign on the door: SOLD OUT. The entrance is crowded, and the line is long. She reads the sign twice. Then she says it out loud, in English: "All right. I will ask." Nobody decided that last night. She decided it there, on the street, in one second. At the desk: "Is it sold out for the whole day?" -- "I am afraid so. Saturday is our busiest day. But tomorrow at nine we have a lot of tickets. Nine is off-peak: there are not many people, and the rooms are quiet." -- "Then I will take two for tomorrow at nine. What is the time slot?" -- "Nine to nine thirty. And there is a guided tour at the same time. There are only a few places left. Nine euros." -- "Is it worth it?" -- "In my opinion, yes." -- "Then two, please."

Outside, the morning is empty: three hours of free time and no schedule. "How much time do we have?" her husband asks, in Portuguese. "A lot of time," she says, "and not many plans. I will find something." And she does.

1. "We are going to take the tram at nine." Why "going to" and not "will" here?
A Because it is a plan she made before -- last night, at the hotel. It was already decided.
B Because "will" does not exist with the verb "take".
C Because she is talking to her husband and not to a stranger.
2. "All right. I will ask." Why "will" and not "going to"?
A Because "going to" is only for tomorrow, never for today.
B Because she decided it right there, in one second, when she saw the sign. It was not in any plan.
C Because "will" is the polite form, and she is speaking to a stranger.
3. "There are not many people" but "How much time do we have?" Why two different words?
A Because "much" is for questions and "many" is for normal sentences.
B Because "many" is more polite than "much".
C Because you can count people, but you cannot count time. Countable takes "many", uncountable takes "much".
4. The man says there are "only a few places left". What does that tell Rosângela?
A There are some places, but a small number -- so if she wants the tour, she should take it now.
B There are no places left at all -- the tour is sold out too.
C The places are cheap, because "a few" is about the price.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Will, and How Much of It

GRAMMAR

In Lesson 5 you learned going to: the plan you made before. Today you get the other half — the decision you make in the moment, when the plan breaks.

FormUseExample
will + plain verbYou decide it NOW, because something just happened. Same word for I, you, he, she, we — it never changes.I will ask. · I will take two. · She will find something.
Never
will to		No "to" after will, and no "-ing" either. The verb stays plain."I will ask" (never "I will to ask", never "I will asking")
going to
vs will		The difference is WHEN you decided, not when it happens. Decided before = going to. Decided now = will. In Portuguese one word does both jobs, and that is why this one is hard."We are going to take the tram at nine" (planned last night) · "Sold out? I will ask" (decided this second)
many / a few
+ things you count		Museums, people, tickets, places, euros: you can count them, one by one.How many museums? · not many people · only a few places left
much / a little
+ things you cannot count		Time, money, traffic, rain, art: you cannot count them one by one.How much time? · not much money · a little time before it opens
a lot of
+ both		The safe one. It works with both, and it is what people really say.a lot of tickets (you count) · a lot of traffic (you do not)

The one rule to remember: before you choose between much and many, ask one question — can I count it? People yes, time no. And the trap: money you cannot count ("how much money?"), but euros you can ("how many euros?"). The same thing in your hand, two different words.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer for these real moments in a day as a tourist.

Last night you decided: the tram, at nine, to the museum. This morning you tell your husband. You say:
A "We will take the tram at nine." (But you did not decide it now.)
B "We are going to take the tram at nine." (It has been the plan since last night.)
C "We will to take the tram at nine."
You arrive and the sign says SOLD OUT. You did not plan this. You say:
A "All right. I will ask at the desk."
B "All right. I am going to ask at the desk." (Did you plan this last night?)
C "All right. I will to asking at the desk."
You want to know the time you have before the boat leaves. You ask:
A "How many time do we have before the boat leaves?"
B "How much time do we have before the boat leaves?"
C "How much times do we have before the boat leaves?"
The tour has three places left. Three is a small number, and you can count them. The man says:
A "There are only a little places left."
B "There is only a few place left."
C "There are only a few places left."
Your budget was sixty euros and you spent fifty-four. Your husband asks what is left. You say:
A "We have a little money left -- six euros."
B "We have a few money left -- six euros."
C "We have a little euros left -- six money."
The guided tour is nine euros each, and you think it is a good idea. You say:
A "It is nine euros, but it is worth the pain."

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt. There is no right or wrong version. Speak for 60 seconds, with no script.

Choose a city in Europe you want to visit, and plan one day in it out loud. What are you going to do in the morning, in the afternoon, and in the evening? How much time does each thing take, and how many places can you really see in one day? What is your budget? Then tell me about a day on a trip when the plan went wrong -- Italy, Paris, London, anywhere. What happened, and who fixed it? And finish with this: next time it happens, and you are alone, what will you do? Use "going to" for the plan, "will" for the moment it breaks, and at least one of "a few", "a little" and "a lot of". Take your time and do not read from a script.
