Chapter 3: The Code

Decided Right Now

I will ask · how much time · how many people

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"The sign says sold out. All right, I will ask."

"Tomorrow at nine? Then I will take two, please."

"We have a lot of time. I will find something."

"I do not know the times. Let me check — I will look it up."

In all four, something just happened, and the answer comes in one second. The green word never changes: not "he wills", never. And after it comes... what, exactly?

FormUseExample
I will + plain verbYou decide it NOW, in the moment, because something just happened. Same word for everybody: I, you, he, she, we.I will ask. · I will take two. · She will find something.
Never
will to		No "to" after will. No "-ing" either. The verb stays plain and naked."I will ask" (never "I will to ask", never "I will asking")
Offers and
help		"Will" is also how you offer to do something for somebody, right now."I will look it up." · "I will help you." · "I will wait here."
Short form
I'll		In real speech, "I will" almost always sounds like "I'll". Your ear needs this one."I'll ask." · "I'll take two." · "I'll have the coffee."
Negative
will not / won't		Say no to something, on the spot."I will not pay eighty euros for that." · "I won't be long."

"Will" is the language of the moment the plan breaks. It costs one word, and it is the difference between freezing and answering.

The Contrast

Going To vs Will

Both of them are the future — but they were born at different moments. One was decided last night. The other was decided one second ago.

Going to — the plan

Decided before, at the hotel. It is on the paper.

"We are going to take the tram at nine."
"We are going to eat near the water tonight."

Will — the surprise

Decided now, on the street, in one second.

"Sold out? All right — I will ask."
"There is music behind that door. I will go in!"

In Portuguese, one little word does both jobs. In English there are two, and the ear hears the difference: the plan is "going to", the surprise is "will".

The Second Code

How Much, How Many

One question decides everything: can I count it? Museums, tickets, people, euros — I can count them. Time, money, traffic, rain — I cannot.

How many + things you count (museums, people)
How much + things you cannot count (time, money)
A lot of + both (the safe one — it never fails)
A few + things you count (a small number, but some)
A little + things you cannot count (a small amount, but some)
The trap: money you cannot count, euros you can
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"All right. I will to ask the man at the desk."
"All right. I will ask the man at the desk."
"How much museums do you want to visit today?"
"How many museums do you want to visit today?"
"We have a few time before the boat leaves."
"We have a little time before the boat leaves."

After will, the verb is plain: no "to", no "-ing". And before you choose between much and many, ask yourself one question: can I count it? Museums yes, time no.

Listening

The Museum Information Line

You call before you go. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Today is sold out. What does the message say about tomorrow morning at nine?
Tomorrow at nine they still have a lot of tickets. Nine in the morning is the off-peak time slot: there are not many people, the rooms are quiet, and the sunflowers are almost yours alone.
2. What is a time slot, and what happens if you arrive at the wrong hour?
The time slot is the half hour on your ticket, when you come in. If you arrive at eleven and your slot is two, they cannot let you in.
3. What does the message say about the guided tour: how long, how much, and is it worth it?
One hour, nine euros, and there are only a few places on each tour, so you book it with your ticket. Many visitors say it is worth it: with the tour you understand what you are looking at.
Listening 2

Two Lines, and a Lot of Space

Saskia is at the next table, with her coffee. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. For many years Saskia had an itinerary with a schedule for every hour. What always happened to it?
It never worked. Something is always sold out, or it rains, or there is a lot of traffic. By eleven o'clock the whole day was broken, she was angry, and her husband was very quiet.
2. How does she plan a day now? How many things are on her itinerary?
Only two: they are going to see one museum in the morning, and eat somewhere near the water in the evening. All the hours in the middle are free time -- and that is where the day happens.
3. What happened last October, and how did she decide it?
They had a few hours with no plan, they walked past a little door with music behind it, and she said "I will go in" -- she decided it on the street, in one second. It was a small concert in a church, six euros, and it is the most beautiful thing she has ever done in the city.

Comprehension

About the Man at the Desk

1. Before Dirk recommends anything, what is the first thing he wants to know?
How much time she has. He says: "Let me check. First: how much time do you have?"
2. What does he say about the canal boat -- when it leaves, how long it is, and how much?
It leaves in twenty minutes, it is one hour, and it is eighteen euros each. There are only a few places left on that one.
3. Which thing is sold out today, and what does he say about the same thing tomorrow?
The guided tour of the old city at eleven is sold out today. Tomorrow there are a lot of places at eleven, and it is off-peak, so the streets are quiet. Ten euros — and in his opinion it is worth it.

Behind the station, at the water.
