Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. These are the words of a business plan.

Forecast -- a statement about future numbers, based on today's data
"The forecast for new patients is strong this quarter."
Initiative -- a new plan or project to reach a goal
"The team is going to start a new atopic dermatitis initiative."
Timeline -- a plan that shows when each step is going to happen
"The timeline is on the wall for everyone to see."
Phase -- one stage of a bigger plan
"The rollout is going to happen in three phases."
Roadmap -- a simple plan of the steps to reach a goal
"The Rain Plan is a simple roadmap for the third quarter."
Kickoff -- the official start of a project or a plan
"In July, the team is going to start the kickoff of the new initiative."
Budget -- the money a team plans to spend on a project
"The budget is ready, and the plan is approved."
To expand -- to make something bigger, or grow into new areas
"The team is going to expand the program to two new regions."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the short update, then answer the questions. Notice the words in bold.

Here is the plan for the third quarter. In July, we are going to start the kickoff of the new initiative. In August, we are going to launch the higher dose — that is the biggest milestone. The rollout is going to happen in three phases, and the team is going to expand the program to two new regions. The forecast for new patients is strong, and I am going to present all of this to Paris in ninety days.
1. Why does the text say "we are going to launch" and not "we is going to launch"?
A Because the subject is "we" (many), so it takes are. The am/is/are follows the subject.
B Because "launch" is a long word.
C Because every plan sentence uses "are".
2. The rollout "is going to happen". Why is the verb "happen" and not "happens"?
A Because "the rollout" is plural.
B Because the verb after "going to" always stays in its base form — happen, launch, start — never with -s.
C Because "happen" is in the past.
3. What does "going to" tell us about the plan in this update?
A That the plan already happened last quarter.
B That it is a plan already decided for the future — the kickoff, the launch and the rollout are coming.
C That the team is doing all of it right now, at this moment.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- One pattern for the whole plan: going to + base verb

GRAMMAR

Use going to for a plan that is already decided. The pattern is always the same: subject + am / is / are + going to + base verb. Only the am/is/are changes, following the subject. The verb after "going to" never changes.

Subjectam / is / are + going toExample
Iam going to + verbI am going to present the plan.
He / she / it / the rollout (one)is going to + verbThe rollout is going to happen in phases.
We / you / they (many)are going to + verbWe are going to launch the higher dose.
The rule to keepThe verb after going to is always the base form: going to launch, going to happen, going to expand. Never "going to launches", never "going to launched", and never drop the "to".

Common mistake: "we is going to launch" and "going to launches" — both are wrong. Quick fix: first choose am/is/are for the subject (we are, the rollout is), then keep the verb in its base form (launch, not launches).

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the situation below. There is no right or wrong answer — speak for two minutes, with no script.

Marc Dubois asks you in the pre-presentation call: "Can you walk me through your team plan for the next quarter?" Present the plan for a REAL product of yours (atopic dermatitis, asthma or COPD), in this order: (1) open it -- "Here is the plan for..."; (2) the kickoff -- what you are going to start, and when; (3) the launch and the milestone -- what you are going to launch in which month; (4) the rollout -- how it is going to happen (in phases); (5) the reach and the forecast -- where you are going to expand, and the forecast for new patients; and (6) the hard question -- what you are going to do if a target is not met. Use at least four sentences with "going to". Speak for two minutes. Do not read from a script.
