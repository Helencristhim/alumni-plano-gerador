Problem Solving -- When Things Go Wrong

Turn a client complaint into a kept client: name the problem, apologize once with control, and commit to a fix. New words: complaint, defective, faulty, delay, apologize, resolve, inconvenience, refund, replacement, compensation. New structure: the first conditional (if + present, ... will + base) for real, likely fixes, and the second conditional (if + past, ... would + base) for softer advice and offers. PT: transformar uma reclamacao em um cliente mantido -- nomear o problema, pedir desculpas com controle e comprometer-se com uma solucao, usando a primeira e a segunda condicional.

"I will resolve this today so the collection still opens on time."
Inconvenience -- transtorno / incomodo
trouble or extra effort a problem causes someone
"I understand this is a real inconvenience before your opening."
Refund -- reembolso
money that is paid back to a buyer for a problem

a new item sent to take the place of a faulty one
"We will ship the replacements on Wednesday."
Compensation -- compensacao
something given to make up for trouble or loss

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the call summary and answer the questions. / Leia o resumo da ligacao e responda.

A buyer in New York called with a complaint: three pendants arrived defective, the clasps were faulty, and the collection opened that weekend, so any delay was a serious inconvenience. Milton stayed calm. "Let me apologize for the trouble," he said. Then he committed to a real fix: "If you send the faulty pieces back today, we will ship the replacements on Wednesday." When the buyer hesitated, he advised gently: "If I were you, I would wait for the replacements rather than take a refund." And to keep the relationship, he offered compensation: "To make up for the trouble, we would cover the express freight." He promised to resolve it that day.

1. Why does Milton say "If you send the faulty pieces back today, we will ship..." (not "we would ship")?
A It is a real, likely fix — the first conditional uses present + will.
B "Will" and "would" mean exactly the same thing here.
C The first conditional always uses "would".
2. Why does he switch to "If I were you, I would wait..."?
A Because he is really the buyer.
B It is soft, hypothetical advice — the second conditional uses past + would.
C He made a grammar mistake under pressure.
3. Which sentence is correct after "if" in a real condition?
A "If you will return them, we will refund you."
B "If you return them, we will refund you."
C "If you returning them, we will refund you."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- First & Second Conditional

GRAMMAR

Use the first conditional for real fixes, the second for softer offers and advice.

TypeStructureExample
First (real)If + present simple, ... will + base verb"If you confirm now, we will start tonight."
Second (hypothetical)If + past simple, ... would + base verb"If I were you, I would keep the order."
Word orderResult first → no comma"We will resolve this if you confirm now."
Watch the traps: after "if" in a real condition, use the present simple, NOT "will" ("if you send", not "if you will send"); in the second conditional use the fixed form "were" for all subjects ("if I were you"); "will" = a real promise, "would" = a softer, more polite offer. PT: use a PRIMEIRA condicional (if + presente, ... will + verbo) para solucoes reais e provaveis -- "Se voce confirmar agora, comecaremos hoje a noite." Use a SEGUNDA condicional (if + passado, ... would + verbo) para conselhos e ofertas mais suaves -- "Se eu fosse voce, ficaria com o pedido." Nunca use "will" depois de "if" numa condicao real; e use sempre "were" (nao "was") na segunda condicional.
