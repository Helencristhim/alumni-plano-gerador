What Could Have Happened — Modal Perfects

Look back at the past with modal perfects: should have (criticism), could have (a missed possibility), might/may have (a guess) and would have (an imagined result), plus 12 look-back words: aftermath, setback, overlook, foresee, reckless, cautious, backfire, bound to, spare, pull off, worthwhile, inevitable.

Vocabulary

Aftermath — the situation that results from a bad event
"The aftermath of the storm was hard." (consequências)
Setback — a problem that delays or reverses progress
"The bad grade was a real setback." (revés)
Overlook — to miss or fail to notice something
"I overlooked one small detail." (deixar passar)
Foresee — to know or expect something before it happens
"No one could foresee the result." (prever)
Reckless — acting without thinking about the danger
"That was a reckless decision." (imprudente)
Cautious — careful, trying to avoid problems or danger
"She is cautious with money." (cauteloso)
Backfire — to have the opposite effect to the one you wanted
"The plan backfired badly." (sair pela culatra)
Bound to — almost certain to happen or be true
"Hard work is bound to pay off." (fadado a)
Spare — to make time or something available to give or use
"Can you spare an hour today?" (dispor de)
Pull off — to succeed at something difficult or unexpected
"He pulled off a great result." (conseguir)
Worthwhile — worth the time, money or effort you put in
"It was a worthwhile lesson." (que vale a pena)
Inevitable — certain to happen and impossible to avoid
"A change was inevitable." (inevitável)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Mark looks back at a math test with a bad aftermath. To criticize a choice he did not make, he uses should have plus the past participle: I should have started earlier. To talk about a chance that existed, he uses could have: I could have asked my teacher for help, but it was a setback. To guess about something he is not sure of, he uses might have: my friend never replied, so he might have been busy. And to imagine a different result, he uses would have: I would have passed if I had spared an hour a day. Studying at midnight was reckless and backfired, but it was a worthwhile lesson.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip — modal perfects

GRAMMAR

Para olhar para o passado e julgar ou supor o que era possível, o inglês usa um modal + "have" + particípio passado. / To look back at the past and judge or guess what was possible, English uses a modal + "have" + the past participle.

StructureUse / UsoExample
should have + p.p.criticism / a coisa certa que você não fezI should have studied more.
could have + p.p.possibility / uma chance que existiaI could have taken the earlier train.
might / may have + p.p.guess / um palpite quando não tem certezaShe might have missed the bus.
would have + p.p.imagined result / o resultado imaginadoI would have passed if I had tried.

Key point / Ponto-chave: a forma é sempre modal + have + particípio passado, e todas olham para o PASSADO. Na fala, "should have" soa como should've e "would have" soa como would've — mas escreve-se sempre "have", nunca "of".

Stage 2: Put the Steps in Order

Order

Listen first, then put the steps of using a modal perfect in the correct order.

?Next, for a chance that existed but did not happen, you use could have.
?First, you decide what you want to say about the past: a criticism, a missed chance, a guess, or an imagined result.
?Finally, for the imagined result of a different choice, you use would have, often with an if clause.
?Then, for the right thing you did not do, you use should have plus the past participle.
?After that, for a guess when you are not sure, you use might have or may have.
