Chapter 4: The Code

Mixed Conditionals & The Verb After the Verb

"If I had taken it, I would be in London now."

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"If I had taken that job, I would be in London now." (past → now)

"If I were more risk-averse, I would have turned it down." (now → past)

"I am considering relocating, and I have decided to negotiate."

"I stopped negotiating" vs "I stopped to negotiate" — opposite meanings.

In the first two, one half is about the past and the other about now. Is that allowed? And in the last one — what on earth changed?

FormUseExample
Mixed: past → presentA past condition with a result you are living TODAY. The most useful conditional in a career conversation.If I had taken that job, I would be in London now.
Mixed: present → pastWho you ARE explains what you DID. Rarer, and it sounds very senior.If I were more risk-averse, I would have turned it down.
Verb + GERUNDconsider, avoid, risk, involve, keep, delay, suggest, mind.I am considering relocating.
Verb + INFINITIVEdecide, agree, offer, refuse, manage, hope, intend, plan, afford.I have decided to negotiate.
Preposition + GERUND (always)After ANY preposition, the verb takes -ing. No exceptions.committed to relocating / interested in moving
The meaning trapstop doing = you quit it. stop to do = you paused IN ORDER to do it.I stopped negotiating. ≠ I stopped to negotiate.

The mixed conditional is how adults talk about the roads they did not take. And the gerund/infinitive choice is how you say what you are willing to do — which, in a negotiation, is the whole game.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"If I had taken that job, I would have been in London now."
"If I had taken that job, I would be in London now."
"I am considering to relocate to London next year."
"I am considering relocating to London next year."
"I am committed to negotiate the equity package."
"I am committed to negotiating the equity package."

If the result is happening NOW, use would + verb, not would have. After consider use -ing. And after ANY preposition — including the to in "committed to" — the verb ALWAYS takes -ing.

Chapter 5: The Negotiation

Sarah Calls Back

Nine lessons later. Same voice, different man.

Dialogue

The Offer on the Table

S
Felipe, they want you. Base is competitive, the equity package vests over four years, and they would like you in London in six weeks.
F
Thank you — that means a lot. I am seriously considering relocating, and I have decided to negotiate two things before I say yes. Six weeks is not possible: my notice period is three months.
S
Notice can usually be shortened. What is the second thing?
F
If I leave in March, I forfeit my bonus. So I am asking for a buyout of what I give up, or a sign-on bonus that covers it. And I should say this plainly: the twelve-month non-compete is my deal breaker.
S
Most candidates do not say that out loud. Why is it a deal breaker?
F
Because if the role did not work out, twelve months would leave me unemployable in my own sector. If I had understood that clause five years ago, I would be in a very different position today. I am committed to negotiating everything else in good faith — but not that.
Comprehension

About Sarah

1. What three things does Sarah put on the table?
A competitive base, an equity package vesting over four years, and a start in London in six weeks.
2. What does she say about the notice period?
That notice can usually be shortened.
3. What surprises her about Felipe?
That he names his deal breaker out loud — she says most candidates do not.

Five Years Later

0:000:00
1. What does she say she would be doing if she had stayed?
She would still be running the same team in the same building — and she would be comfortable.
2. What does she say she regrets about the negotiation?
Not negotiating the equity package — if she had pushed, she would own twice as much today.
3. What was her deal breaker, and did she hold it?
The non-compete. She held it, and they removed it — because she asked.
Listening 2

How Executives Negotiate Badly

A headhunter, being honest. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does he say is the most expensive emotion in a negotiation?
Gratitude — candidates say yes too fast because they feel grateful to have been chosen.
2. What does he say the company expects?
That you will negotiate. They have budgeted for it, and saying yes immediately costs you money.
3. What does he say about naming your deal breaker?
Say it out loud and early — it makes you easier to deal with, not harder.
