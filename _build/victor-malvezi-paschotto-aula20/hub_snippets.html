Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read how a coach builds a growth plan, and answer the questions.

A good coaching conversation runs on two tools. The first is the open question. Instead of asking "Are you stuck?", which only gets a yes or a no and ends the conversation, the coach asks "What's the one roadblock in the way?" — an open question that starts with what or how and makes the person think. The second tool is the zero conditional, which maps the growth plan as reliable cause and effect: "If you block two hours each morning, the deep work gets done." Notice the shape — if + present simple, then present simple; there is no will, because this is a general truth about how things work, not a single future prediction. Compare the first conditional, which you already know: "If you block tomorrow morning, you will finish the deck" describes one specific future result. The zero conditional ("if you block your mornings, the work gets done") describes a system that always works. A coach asks open questions, then maps the plan with zero conditionals — and lets the person own it.

1. Which is the open coaching question?
A "Are you stuck?" (yes/no — closed)
B "What's the one roadblock in the way?" (open)
C "Do you need help?" (yes/no — closed)
2. The zero conditional for a growth plan is:
A "If you will block your mornings, the work gets done." (will after if)
B "If you block your mornings, the work gets done." (if + present, present)
C "If you blocked your mornings, the work would get done."
3. What makes the zero conditional different from the first conditional?
A Nothing — they are the same.
B Zero (if + present, present) = a general truth/system; first (if + present, will) = one future result.
C The zero conditional always uses "would".

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Open Questions & the Zero Conditional

GRAMMAR

Two tools carry a coaching conversation.

ToolShapeExample
Open questionWhat / How + clause"What would success look like?"
Closed questionDo / Are / Can + ... (yes/no)"Are you on track?" — avoid in coaching
Zero conditionalIf + present, present"If you block mornings, the work gets done."
vs 1st conditionalIf + present, will"If you block tomorrow, you will finish it."
The two tools: (1) ask open questions (what/how/why) that can't be answered yes or no — a closed question (do/are/can) ends the conversation; (2) map the plan with the zero conditional: if + present simple, present simple, for reliable cause and effect, never will after if. PT: duas ferramentas conduzem uma conversa de coaching. Faça perguntas ABERTAS (what/how/why), que não podem ser respondidas com sim/não — uma pergunta fechada (do/are/can) encerra a conversa. E mapeie o plano com o ZERO CONDITIONAL: if + presente, presente, para causa e efeito que sempre vale ("se você bloqueia as manhãs, o trabalho sai"). Nunca use will logo depois de if. Compare com o first conditional (if + presente, will), que é para um resultado futuro específico.

Quiz

A "Do you want to grow?" (yes/no — closed)
B "What would success look like for you in a year?" (open)
C "Here's exactly what you should do." (telling, not coaching)

A "If you will protect your mornings, the work gets done." (will after if)
B "If you protect your mornings, the deep work gets done." (zero conditional)
C "If you protected your mornings, the work would get done." (not a general truth)
The person pauses and looks unsure. What does a good coach do?
A Jump in immediately with the answer. (rescuing, not coaching)
B Hold the silence and let them think — "Take your time. What comes to mind?"
C Change the subject to avoid the awkward pause.
You want the person to own the next step. What's best?
A "I'll set up the plan and send it to you." (you own it, not them)
B "So what's one step you could take this week to own it?" (hands over ownership)
C "Just do whatever you think." (vague, no commitment)
