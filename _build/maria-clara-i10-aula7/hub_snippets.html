I'll Handle It -- Instant Decisions and Offers with "Will"

Decide in the moment, in English: offers (I'll help you), quick decisions (I'll have the water), promises (I won't be late) and requests (Will you...?) with "will". See the difference between a plan (going to) and a decision now (will). Key words: handle, offer, promise, decision, reply, request, immediately, available. And two phrases for a round table: "I agree" and "I disagree".

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Take your time — listen twice, then say it out loud. These are the words you need to say yes, to promise, and to answer at an official dinner.

To handle -- to take care of a problem or a task yourself
"Don't worry, I'll handle the schedule."
An offer -- to say you will give or do something for someone
"That is a very kind offer, thank you."
To promise -- to say you will certainly do something
"I promise I'll reply today."
A decision -- a choice you make after thinking
"It is an important decision for the delegation."
To reply -- to answer a message, an email, or a question
"I always reply to my emails in the morning."
A request -- something you ask for in a polite way
"I have a small request for you."
Immediately -- right now, and not later
"I'll send the document immediately."
Available -- free and ready to help or to meet
"I am available after lunch."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

Tomorrow, Maria Clara is going to present at the conference — that is her plan, and it is on the agenda. But tonight, at the official dinner, she decides in the moment. The waiter comes, and she says, "I'll have some water, please." The rector has a request: the speaker for Friday is not available, so he asks, "Will you say a few words?" It is a big decision, but Maria Clara replies, "It depends on the schedule, but I'll do my best. I won't take much of your time. I promise." The rector smiles. "I think it will be a great session." Maria Clara agrees. She will handle it, and she will reply to his email immediately.

1. What is Maria Clara's plan for tomorrow? (decided before)
A She is going to present at the conference.
B She is going to serve the water.
C She is going to travel to China.
2. Why does she say "I'll have some water"? (not "I am going to have")
A Because it is an old plan.
B Because she decides at the moment, when the waiter comes.
C Because she is going to travel.
3. What is the rector's request?
A To present tomorrow morning.
B To say a few words on Friday, because the speaker is not available.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Will or Going To?

GRAMMAR

English has two ways to talk about the future. Use going to for a plan you already have (I am going to present tomorrow). Use will for an offer, a promise, or a decision at the moment of speaking (I'll help you). After will, always use the base verb, with no "to" and no "-s".

UseFormExample
Offer / decision nowwill + base verbI will help → I'll help
Promise (negative)won't + base verbI won't be late
Request / questionWill + you + base verb?Will you join us?
A plan (decided before)going to + base verbI am going to present tomorrow

Watch out: after will, never use "to" and never add "-s": "she will help", not "she will helps" and not "she will to help". The short forms are I'll, she'll, we'll and the negative won't. Two useful phrases for a round table: I agree and I disagree.

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection
