The Distinction

Experience vs Track Record

Experience

"I have seven years of experience in railway signaling."

It is time. Everybody on the shortlist has it. It proves nothing.

Track record

"In 2023 I ran the commissioning of a signaling system in San Francisco with the client on site, and we released it on the contractual date."

It is evidence: a date, a decision, an outcome. Only you have it.

A panel never remembers the years. It remembers the one project you described so clearly that they could see it.

Chapter 3: The Code

Same Facts. Different Weight.

"I have experience in signaling."  →  "What I bring to this role is..."

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

What I bring to this role is seven years in railway signaling.

It was in San Francisco that I first ran a full commissioning cycle.

What sets me apart is that I read the standard and I run the site.

The reason I moved into signaling is that safety is where engineering becomes non-negotiable.

Each one could be said in five words. Instead, the sentence is split in two — and the important half is kept for last. Why would anyone do that on purpose?

FormFunctionExample
What + subject + verb + is + the pointYou announce that something important is coming, and then you deliver it. The panel leans in."What I bring to this role is seven years in signaling."
It was + the fact + that + the restYou put one single fact under the spotlight: a place, a time, a person, a decision."It was in San Francisco that I first ran a commissioning cycle."
The reason + clause + is that ...You take control of the story behind a decision, before anybody misreads it."The reason I moved into signaling is that safety is non-negotiable."
The verb is always singular"What" behaves like "the thing that" — so the verb is is / was, even before a plural."What I bring is seven years." (not are)
In speechThe pause falls just before the "is". That pause is thinking time you are allowed to take."What I learned from that experience … was that fluency is a habit."
Why it mattersUnder pressure, a nervous candidate empties everything he knows into one long sentence, and the panel stops listening. A cleft sentence does the opposite: it tells the panel where to look. Same information. Completely different candidate.
Meaning

Four Patterns, Four Jobs

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"What I bring to this role, it is deep expertise in signaling."
"What I bring to this role is deep expertise in signaling."
"It was in San Francisco where I ran my first commissioning cycle."
"It was in San Francisco that I ran my first commissioning cycle."

Portuguese repeats the pronoun and loves onde. English does neither: after the "what" clause the verb comes straight away, and the it-cleft always takes that — never where, never when.

Dialogue

The Panel

S
Thank you for making the time, João. Twenty minutes, and I would rather use them well. So — tell me about yourself.
J
Let me set out my background in three steps. What I do at Motiva is own the signaling design for metro line extensions, from the specification to commissioning. What I bring to this role is seven years of managing German, French and Chinese suppliers under contractual pressure.
S
Good. Everyone on this shortlist has seven years. What makes you the right person, and not the other four?
J
What sets me apart is that I read the standard and I run the site. Most engineers do one or the other. It was in San Francisco, in 2023, that I did both on the same project: two months of field commissioning, with the client watching every test.
S
Tell me about a conflict with an international supplier. Not the theory. One project, and what you decided.
J
One example that comes to mind is a supplier who missed three consecutive deadlines on a signaling package. What I did was put forward a recovery plan before anyone mentioned the penalty clause. What I learned from that experience was that a corrective action with a date is worth more than an apology. That is stakeholder management, and it is most of the job.
S
One more, and it is the uncomfortable one. You told us there was a period when you were not using English intensively. This role is in English, every day, with a regulator on the call. How do I know that will not be a problem?
J
I can account for it. For two years I was on site, in Portuguese, every day, and the language went quiet. What I learned from that was that fluency is a habit, not a memory. It was an interview, in March, that made me rebuild the habit deliberately — weekly training, technical reading, and this conversation. I did not throw in the towel. I would say my greatest strength in this area is exactly that: resilience.
Comprehension

About Sofie

1. Why does Sofie say the seven years are not enough?
Because everyone on the shortlist has seven years. She is asking for the difference, not for the time.
2. What kind of answer does she ask for on the supplier conflict?
Not the theory: one project, and the decision he took.
3. She calls her last question "the uncomfortable one". Is it a trap?
No. She says the role is in English every day, with a regulator on the call, so the risk is real and she has to ask. It is the question the candidate should have prepared first.
4. What does she want to hear about the quiet years — an apology or something else?
Not an apology: evidence. She wants the period accounted for, and proof that the habit was rebuilt.
Listening

Sofie Calls Back

Two days after the panel. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What convinced the panel — and what did not?
Not the seven years. The way he set out the San Francisco commissioning: the problem, the decision, and what it cost. She calls that a track record.
2. What does Sofie say he does under pressure?
He retreats into detail. Asked about competing priorities, he gave three minutes of relay logic. She wanted one sentence about how he decides what to drop.
3. What does she tell him to prepare for the final round?
Not the specification. The four sentences that make his value proposition, and the answer about the gap in his English — because she will ask it in front of the board.
Listening 2

Three Rules Before You Sit Down

A headhunter who places signaling engineers. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What is the panel really testing, according to him?
Not your English. Whether the engineering survives the English. A wrong preposition is forgivable; burying the decision inside the description is not.
2. Why does he call cleft sentences "the cheapest confidence you can buy"?
The information is identical, but the weight is not. It buys you half a second to think, and it tells the panel where to look.
3. What should you do when you do not know the answer?
Say so, and then say what you would do to find out. A subject matter expert is not somebody who knows everything: it is somebody the team trusts when the answer is not in the manual.
