Performing Under Pressure -- The International Job Interview

The international interview: how to turn seven years of experience into EVIDENCE, control what the panel hears first, and answer the uncomfortable question without apologizing. Key words: track record, cross-functional, high-stakes, resilience, stakeholder management, value proposition, technical lead, subject matter expert, performance-driven, results-oriented, to set out, to account for, to burn the midnight oil, to throw in the towel. Structure: cleft sentences ("What I bring to this role is...", "It was in San Francisco that...", "The reason I... is that...") + the four interview answer patterns.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Lessons 2 to 4 gave a name to the work: the actions, the inspection, the contract. This one gives a name to YOU — the words an interview panel is waiting to hear. You have already DONE all of this at Motiva; what is missing is the English label. Listen to each term and read the example.

Track record -- a documented history of results you can point to, with dates and numbers
"I can demonstrate a track record of delivering signaling packages on time."
Cross-functional -- involving people from different departments and disciplines at the same time
"I lead cross-functional teams: signaling, civil works and the client."
High-stakes -- where the consequences of getting it wrong are serious and public
"Signaling is a high-stakes discipline: the failure mode is a collision."
Resilience -- the ability to keep performing when the pressure does not stop
"What that year taught me was resilience, not patience."
Stakeholder management -- keeping everyone with an interest in the project informed, aligned and calm
"Stakeholder management is half the job: the client, the supplier and the regulator."
Value proposition -- the one concrete reason to choose you instead of the other candidate
"My value proposition is simple: I read the standard and I run the site."
Technical lead -- the engineer who owns the technical decisions on a project, and answers for them
"I was the technical lead on the Line 5 signaling package."
Subject matter expert -- the person the team goes to when the answer is not in the manual
"On interlocking logic, I am the subject matter expert for my team."
Performance-driven -- working against a number: a metric, a KPI, a measurable target
"It is a performance-driven environment: every milestone has an indicator."
Results-oriented -- asking to be judged by what you delivered, not by how busy you looked
"I am results-oriented: judge me by the commissioning date, not by my hours."
To set out -- to explain your background clearly and in order, so the listener can follow it
"Let me set out my experience in three steps."
To account for -- to explain a decision or a period that somebody is entitled to ask about
"I can account for the two years when I stopped using English."
To burn the midnight oil -- to work late into the night to finish something that matters (informal)
"We burned the midnight oil for a week before the factory acceptance test."
To throw in the towel -- to give up when it gets hard -- the thing you say you did NOT do (informal)
"That interview went badly, but I did not throw in the towel."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

It is four o'clock on a Tuesday and João Guilherme is sitting in front of a panel of two. Sofie Claes, Head of Signaling Engineering, has twenty minutes and a shortlist of five engineers, all of them with seven years of experience. Experience is time, and everybody on that list has it. What wins the room is a track record: a project, a decision, an outcome.

He opens carefully. "Let me set out my background in three steps. What I do at Motiva is own the signaling design for metro line extensions, from the specification to commissioning. What I bring to this role is seven years of managing German, French and Chinese suppliers under contractual pressure." Sofie is not impressed yet. She asks the question that decides everything: what makes him the right person, and not the other four? "What sets me apart is that I read the standard and I run the site. Most engineers do one or the other. It was in San Francisco, in 2023, that I did both on the same project." That is a value proposition, and it takes one sentence.

Then comes the uncomfortable question, the one he did not prepare in March: there was a period when he was not using English. He does not apologize. He accounts for it. "What I learned from that experience was that fluency is a habit, not a memory. It was an interview, in March, that made me rebuild the habit." He did not throw in the towel, and the word for that, in an interview, is resilience. Stakeholder management, a cross-functional team, a high-stakes discipline where the failure mode is a collision: the engineering was always there. Tonight, so was the English.

1. "What I bring to this role is seven years in signaling." Why is the verb is, and not are, if "seven years" is plural?
A Because What... works like "the thing that" — a SINGULAR subject. The verb agrees with it, never with what follows.
B Because English drops subject-verb agreement after what.
C Because "seven years" is treated like an amount of money, so it turns singular.
2. "It was in San Francisco that I ran my first commissioning cycle." What does this structure DO to the sentence?
A It adds a new piece of information about San Francisco.
B It puts ONE fact under the spotlight — the place — and tells the panel where to look. The information is the same; the weight is not.
C It makes the sentence more formal, but the effect is purely decorative.
3. Why does "What I bring to this role is seven years..." work better in an interview than "I have seven years of experience"?
A Because it is longer, and long sentences sound more professional.
B Because it announces that something important is coming, saves the decisive information for the END — and buys you half a second to think.
C Because it avoids the verb have, which is far too informal.
4. Which sentence is correct?
A "It was in San Francisco where I ran my first commissioning cycle."
B "What I bring to this role, it is deep expertise in signaling."
C "It was in San Francisco that I ran my first commissioning cycle."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Cleft Sentences

GRAMMAR

How to split a sentence in two so that you control what the panel hears first — and buy yourself half a second to think.

FormFunctionExample
wh-cleft
What + subject + verb + is + the point		You ANNOUNCE that something important is coming, and only then deliver it. The panel leans forward. The listener knows a headline is on its way."What I bring to this role is seven years in signaling."
it-cleft
It was + the fact + that + the rest		You put ONE fact under the spotlight: a place, a date, a person, a decision. The linking word is always that — never where, never when."It was in San Francisco that I ran my first commissioning cycle."
The reason + clause + is thatYou take control of the story behind a decision before anybody reads it the wrong way."The reason I moved into signaling is that safety is non-negotiable."
AgreementAfter What..., the verb is ALWAYS is / was, even in front of a plural — because What means "the thing that", a singular subject."What I bring is seven years." (never are)
Question / negativeIt behaves the same way: What he wants is a date. / What I do not do is promise dates I cannot defend."What I do not do is promise a date I cannot defend."
In speechThe pause falls right BEFORE the is. That pause is thinking time — and it belongs to you."What I learned from that experience … was that fluency is a habit."
Why it mattersUnder pressure, a nervous candidate dumps everything he knows into one long sentence — and the panel stops listening. A cleft sentence does the opposite: it tells the panel where to look. Same information, completely different candidate.

Watch out (two classic mistakes): (1) the repeated pronoun — the subject pronoun does NOT come back after the cleft: "What I bring to this role it is..." is wrong; the correct form is "What I bring to this role is..."; (2) the where — the English it-cleft demands that, even when you are pointing at a place: "It was in San Francisco that I ran my first commissioning cycle."

The four interview patterns: "One example that comes to mind is..." (the behavioral question), "What I learned from that experience was..." (the closing line of any story), "The way I approach this kind of challenge is..." (the method question) and "I would say my greatest strength in this area is..." (the direct question). Four skeletons: all you change is the filling.

Stage 2: Put the Interview in Order

Order

Put the stages of an international interview answer in the right order.

?State your value proposition in one cleft sentence: what sets you apart from the other candidates.
?Close by asking the panel a question only an engineer would ask.
?Set out your background in three steps, starting from the value and not from the biography.

?Account for the uncomfortable question with evidence, not with an apology.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the strongest answer for each real moment of an international interview — including the uncomfortable question about your English.

The panel opens with "Tell me about yourself." You have ninety seconds. You start with:
A "I was born in São Paulo and I studied electrical engineering..."
B "Let me set out my background in three steps. What I do at Motiva is own the signaling design for metro line extensions."
C "Well, it is difficult to summarize seven years, but I will try."
"Everyone on this shortlist has seven years. What makes you the right person?" You answer:
A "What sets me apart is that I read the standard and I run the site. Most engineers do one or the other."
B "I am very dedicated and I always give my best."
C "I think my experience speaks for itself."
She asks for a conflict with an international supplier. The strongest opening is:
A "Suppliers are always late, it happens on every project."
B "One example that comes to mind is a supplier who missed three consecutive deadlines. What I did was put forward a recovery plan before anyone mentioned the penalty clause."
C "I would have to think about that one for a moment."
"You were not using English intensively for a while. How do I know that will not be a problem?" You answer:
A "I am sorry about my English, I know it is not perfect."
B "My English is fine, it was only a bad day in that interview."
C "I can account for it. What I learned from that period was that fluency is a habit, not a memory — and I have been in weekly training since March."
The technical director asks how you handle competing priorities. He wants ONE sentence, not three minutes. You say:
A "The way I approach this kind of challenge is to ask which item is on the critical path for safety approval."
B "It depends. Let me explain the relay logic first, and then the interlocking table, and then..."
C "I work hard and I burn the midnight oil until everything is done."
