Lesson 18 -- Pre-class

Speaking Without a Script -- Holding the Floor and Staying Fluent

A live client call goes off-script. Ana cannot freeze, so she uses fluency strategies to hold the floor and keep talking. New words: to hold the floor, to stall, train of thought, to backtrack, to digress, tangent, to elaborate, to reword, to improvise, to signpost, to trail off, to ramble. Focus: fluency strategies -- stalling to buy time, holding the floor, signposting, and paraphrasing when the exact word will not come.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Learn the strategies for speaking without a script. Tap Listen to hear each one.

To hold the floor -- to keep control of the conversation and your turn to speak
"When the questions came fast, Ana held the floor and finished her point."
To stall -- to slow down on purpose to buy time before you answer
"She stalled for a second: ‘That’s a great question, let me think.’"
Train of thought -- the line of thinking you are following as you speak
"The interruption made her lose her train of thought."
To backtrack -- to go back and correct or restate what you just said
"Let me backtrack: I said Tuesday, but the fix ships on Thursday."
To digress -- to move away from the main topic while you are speaking
"I don’t want to digress, so let me get back to the release."
Tangent -- a related but off-topic point you drift onto
"He went off on a tangent about the old system."
To elaborate -- to add more detail to what you have said
"Could you elaborate on the root cause a little more?"
To reword -- to say the same thing using different words
"If they don’t understand, I reword it in plainer English."
To improvise -- to speak without preparation, thinking on your feet
"The demo broke, so Ana had to improvise."
To signpost -- to use small phrases that guide the listener through your answer
"She signposts her answer: ‘First…, and second…’"
To trail off -- to gradually stop speaking without finishing your sentence
"Under pressure, her voice trailed off mid-sentence."
To ramble -- to talk on and on in an aimless, unfocused way
"When she is nervous, she rambles instead of getting to the point."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

When the client asked something off-script, Ana did not freeze. To buy a second, she used a stalling phrase: "That’s a great question, let me think for a moment." To keep her turn when someone jumped in, she held the floor: "If I can just finish this point, then I’ll come to that." To help the client follow her, she signposted her answer: "There are two parts to this. First…, and second…" And when the exact word would not come, she reworded the idea instead of stopping: "In other words, what I mean is the fix is low-risk." None of it was scripted — the phrases simply kept her talking and in control.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Fluency Strategies

GRAMMAR

Fluency is not about knowing every word. It is a set of ready-made phrases that keep you talking and in control.

StrategyWhen to use itPhrase
Stall (buy time)a surprise question, your mind goes blank"That’s a great question, let me think for a moment."
Hold the floorsomeone interrupts before you finish"If I can just finish this point, then I’ll come to that."
Signpostyour answer has more than one part"There are two parts to this. First…, and second…"
Rewordthe exact word will not come"In other words, what I mean is…"
Common mistakeDo not fill the pause by rambling or letting your voice trail off. Use a purposeful phrase to stall or signpost, then continue.
