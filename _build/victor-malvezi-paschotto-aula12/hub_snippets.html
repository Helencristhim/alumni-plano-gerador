Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

A recap of the go-to words from Blocks 7–11 — the language that carries a stakeholder meeting. Tap Listen to hear each word.

Value proposition -- the clear reason your idea or offer is worth choosing
"Our value proposition is simple: one route, three new regions."
Buy-in -- agreement and support from the people who matter
"I need the CFO's buy-in before I take this to the board."
Trade-off -- giving up one thing in order to gain something else
"The trade-off is a slower launch for a safer one."
Common ground -- an area where two sides already agree and can build
"We found common ground on the timeline, then negotiated the budget."
Read the room -- to sense the mood and unspoken signals of the people present
"I read the room and softened the ask when I saw them tense up."
Rapport -- a friendly connection that makes people trust and listen to you
"A minute of rapport at the start makes a hard ask much easier."
Defuse -- to calm down a tense situation before it escalates
"A little empathy defused the tension before it became a fight."
Acknowledge -- to openly recognize a point, a feeling, or a valid concern
"I acknowledged her concern first, then explained the plan."
Signpost -- to tell your audience clearly where the talk is going next
"Let me signpost the plan: headline first, then the detail."
Takeaway -- the single key point you want the audience to remember
"If there's one takeaway today, it's that the model scales."
Push back -- to politely resist or question a proposal
"The CFO will push back on the budget, so I came prepared."
Milestone -- a checkpoint that marks progress toward a bigger goal
"We'll set ninety-day milestones so the board can track the pilot."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the meeting prep and answer the questions.

The Growth Manager opened the stakeholder meeting with the story: the market had been sliding while the team rebuilt the pipeline, then in March they launched the new route. To pitch the next step, he used an imagined case: "If we funded two more regions, we would triple the reach." He signposted his value proposition and aimed for early buy-in. When the CFO began to push back on the budget, he moved to a real offer: "If you approve the pilot, we will share milestones in ninety days." She went quiet, so he read the signal: "You might be weighing the risk." He stayed tentative to defuse the tension: "I was hoping we could explore the timing together." He acknowledged her concern, found common ground, and closed on one takeaway: start with one region.

1. Why does he say "If we funded… we would triple" for the pitch but "If you approve… we will share" for the offer?
A The second conditional pitches an imagined idea; the first conditional makes a real offer on the table.
B They mean exactly the same thing; he just varies the words.
C "Would" is always more polite than "will."
2. Why does he choose "you might be weighing the risk" instead of "you must be rejecting it"?
A "Might" and "must" mean the same level of certainty.
B The signal is weak, so "might" deduces possibility without overstating.
C "Must" is grammatically wrong after a comma.
3. Which line is the most tentative way to handle the tension?
A "You need to decide on the budget now."
B "I was hoping we could explore the timing together."
C "If you don't approve this, the plan fails."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Five Meeting Moves

GRAMMAR

Each move in a stakeholder meeting has its own structure.

MoveStructureExample
Pitch (imagined)2nd conditional: if + past, would + base"If we funded it, we would triple the reach."
Negotiate (real offer)1st conditional: if + present, will + base"If you approve it, we will share milestones."
Read the roomModals: must / might / can't + base"She might be weighing the risk."
Handle tensionTentative: I was hoping / I was wondering / could"I was hoping we could explore one option."
Tell the storyNarrative tenses + dramatic present"It had been sliding, then we launched, and so we go live."
Watch the trap: keep the two conditionals clean. A real offer on the table takes the first conditional ("if you approve, we WILL"); an imagined idea takes the second ("if we funded, we WOULD"). Use "might" for a weak signal and "must" only when the evidence is strong. PT: cada movimento da reuniao tem sua estrutura -- second conditional ("if + passado, would") para o que e imaginado (pitch), first conditional ("if + presente, will") para uma oferta REAL na mesa; modais (must/might/can't) para deduzir sinais; formas tentativas ("I was hoping... could") para suavizar; e tempos narrativos para contar a historia. Nao troque will por would entre a oferta e o pitch.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best response for each meeting situation.

You want to pitch an imagined growth idea. Which is correct?
A "If we added a region, we will double the reach."
B "If we added a region, we would double the reach."
C "If we would add a region, we double the reach."
You are making a real offer on the table. Which is correct?
A "If you approved the pilot, we would start Monday."
B "If you approve the pilot, we will start Monday."
C "If you will approve the pilot, we start Monday."
A stakeholder goes silent after your ask. You see only a weak signal. What do you say?
A "You must hate the idea."
B "You might be weighing the risk — shall we talk it through?"
C "You can't be serious about this."
The room is getting tense. How do you soften your ask?
A "You have to decide right now."
B "I was hoping we could look at the options together."
C "There's no other option, so let's move on."
