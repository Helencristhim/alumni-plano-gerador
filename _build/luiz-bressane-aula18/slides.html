Chapter 2: Words of Advice

The Language of the Conference

Vocabulary

Words 1-4

Candid
Honest and direct, even when the truth is hard to hear
"I have to be candid with you: the evidence against you is strong."
Caveat
A warning or condition attached to advice or a deal
"Take the deal, but with one caveat: you give up your right to a trial."
Outlook
The likely future result of a situation; the prospects
"With this evidence, the outlook at trial is not in our favour."
Reassure
To calm someone's fears by giving them comfort or confidence
"I can reassure you that I will be with you at every stage."
Vocabulary

Drawback
A disadvantage or negative side of an option
"The main drawback of the deal is that you cannot reopen the case."
Trade-off
What you give up in order to gain something else
"The trade-off is clear: certainty now in exchange for the chance of acquittal."
Implication
A consequence that follows from a decision, often not obvious
"Think about the implications for your record before you accept."
Prospect
The chance or likelihood that something will happen
"The prospect of an acquittal is low, but not impossible."

Chapter 3: The Language of Advising

Advise, Warn & Recommend

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

I advise you to take the deal.

I recommend accepting the suspended sentence.

I suggest you take the night to think.

I warn you against rushing this decision.

VerbPatternExample
advise / urge+ person + to + verbI advise you to wait.
recommend / suggest+ -ing (no person)I recommend accepting.
recommend / suggest+ (that) + person + base verbI suggest you take time.
warn+ person + against + -ingI warn you against rushing.

Each verb takes its own pattern. Advise and urge take a person + to + verb. Recommend and suggest NEVER take "to + verb": use the -ing form, or a that-clause with the base verb (I suggest you take, not "to take"). Warn takes a person + against + -ing. Choosing the right pattern is what makes advice sound professional.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"I recommend to accept the deal."

✓ RIGHT

"I recommend accepting the deal."

✗ WRONG

"I suggest you to take the night."

✓ RIGHT

"I suggest you take the night."

"Recommend" and "suggest" never take "to + verb": use the -ing form ("recommend accepting") or a that-clause with the base verb ("suggest you take"). "Advise" and "urge" need a person + to: "I advise you to wait", not "I advise waiting" when you mean a specific person.

Chapter 4: In the Conference Room

Advising the Client

Dialogue

The Conference

C
So you got me a deal. Does that mean I walk free? I just want this over.
L
Let me brief you, and I have to be candid. It is a good deal, but it is not freedom. The outlook at trial is poor, so I advise you to think carefully before refusing.
C
But I am innocent. What is the drawback if I take it?
L
The main drawback is the record, with one caveat: the sentence is suspended, so you serve no time. I recommend weighing that against the prospect of conviction at trial, which is real.
C
You are scaring me. Just tell me what to do.
L
I will not decide for you, but I can reassure you: whatever you choose, I am with you. I urge you to be realistic, and I warn you against rushing. I suggest you take the night to weigh every implication.
C
Thank you for being upfront with me. The trade-off is hard, but I understand it now.
Comprehension

The Conference

1. What did the client expect when they heard there was a deal?
That they would walk free — they believed the deal meant immediate freedom.
2. How did counsel describe the outlook at trial?
Poor — which is why he advised the client to think carefully before refusing the deal.
3. What was the main drawback, and what caveat softened it?
The drawback was the criminal record; the caveat was that the sentence was suspended, so no time would be served.
4. Did counsel decide for the client?
No — he refused to decide, urged the client to be realistic, warned against rushing, and suggested taking the night.

Real Document

Client Advice Memo

CONFIDENTIAL — CLIENT ADVICE

RE: STATE v. R. ANDRADE — PLEA OFFER

From: Luiz Bressane, Public Defender · To: The Client

The offer: Plea to a lesser charge; suspended sentence; full restitution. No custodial time.

My candid assessment:

1. Outlook at trial: poor. Prospect of conviction is real.

2. Drawback of the deal: a criminal record.

3. Caveat: the sentence is suspended — no time served if conditions are met.

4. Trade-off: certainty now in exchange for the chance of acquittal.

The decision is yours:

→ Take the night. The right to choose stays with you.

Counsel's note: frame every section as advice, never as an order ("I advise you to weigh the record", "I recommend taking the night", "I warn you against rushing"). Be upfront; manage expectations; never decide for the client.

Give an advice line for point 1 (the outlook).
I advise you to be realistic: the outlook at trial is poor, so refusing the deal is a serious risk.
Give a recommendation line for the final point (take the night).
I recommend taking the night to think, and I suggest you weigh every implication before you sign.
Chapter 5: Drill

Sharpen the Advice

Detective · Transformation · Building

Spot the Error

Fix the Advice

"I recommend to accept the deal."
I recommend accepting the deal. (recommend + -ing, never "to")
"I suggest you to take the night."
I suggest you take the night. (suggest + that-clause, base verb)
"I advise to wait before signing."
I advise you to wait before signing. (advise + person + to + verb)
"I warn you against to rush this."
I warn you against rushing this. (warn + against + -ing)
Transformation

Make It Advice

"Take the deal." → advise
I advise you to take the deal.
"Accept the suspended sentence." → recommend
I recommend accepting the suspended sentence.
"Think it over tonight." → suggest (that-clause)
I suggest you think it over tonight.
"Do not rush the decision." → warn against
I warn you against rushing the decision.
