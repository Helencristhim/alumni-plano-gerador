Chapter 3 -- The Code

Grammar Discovery

Softeners: agreeing and disagreeing diplomatically

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

What do the orange words do before "but"?

"I see your point, but I have to push back." (soften, then disagree)

"That is valid, but I am not sure about the cost." (concede, then disagree)

"I agree with the price, however the quality worries me." (agree, then push back)

"Fair enough, but let us find a compromise." (accept, then resolve)

Grammar Rule

The Diplomatic Structure

StepFunctionExample
1acknowledge / soften"I see your point..."
2push back"..., but I have to push back."
3give a reason"...because quality matters more."
4resolve"Let us find a compromise."

Key idea: never disagree without a softener. Acknowledge first ("I see your point", "That is valid"), then push back with but or however, give a clear reason, and look for a compromise. This keeps the debate respectful.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"No, you are wrong about the supplier."
"I see your point, but I have to push back on the supplier."

Acknowledge with a softener first, then push back with a clear reason. Never just say "you are wrong".

Grammar Practice

Choose the Softener

"_____I see your point, but I have to push back." (acknowledge)
"That is a _____valid concern, however I disagree." (reasonable)
"_____Fair enough, let us find a compromise." (polite acceptance)
"I agree with the price, but I want to _____raise a concern about quality." (mention a worry)
