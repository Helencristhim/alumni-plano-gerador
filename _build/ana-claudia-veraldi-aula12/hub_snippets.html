Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Learn the words for making smart decisions under pressure. Tap Listen to hear each one.

Trade-off -- a balance between two good things you cannot both fully have
"It is a trade-off between speed and safety."
Hypothetical -- imagined, not real; used to think about a possible situation
"She asked herself a hypothetical question: what if I had more time?"
Contingency -- a plan for something that might go wrong
"Her contingency plan was to roll back to the old version."
To mitigate -- to make a risk or a problem less serious
"To mitigate the risk, she kept the old version ready."
Downside -- the negative part or disadvantage of a choice
"The downside was that a small visual bug might slip through."
To postpone -- to move something to a later time
"She decided to postpone the low-risk tests to the next sprint."
To justify -- to give a good reason for a decision
"She could justify that choice to her team in one line."
To assess -- to judge how good, bad or risky something is
"First she had to assess the risk of each part."
Alternative -- another option you can choose instead
"The alternative was to test everything by hand, but that was too slow."
Reasonable -- fair and sensible
"It was a reasonable thing to leave for later."
To reconsider -- to think again about a decision
"If the risk grew, she would reconsider the plan."
Cost-effective -- giving good value for the time or money spent
"Automated tests were more cost-effective, so she chose them."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

With only two days to test, Ana planned in hypotheticals. She asked herself, "If I had more time, what would I automate first?" — an imagined situation, not a real one. She made a trade-off: "If we tested everything by hand, we would miss the deadline," so she chose the cost-effective option. She assessed the low-risk screen and said, "If it failed, nobody would lose money," so postponing it was reasonable. And she built a contingency: "If the feature failed badly, we would roll back." The past forms here do not mean the past — they mark the situation as imagined.

2. "If it failed, nobody would lose money" is formed with:
A will + base verb in both parts.

C the present continuous.

A To report a bug that was already found.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Second Conditional

GRAMMAR

Second conditional = If + past simple, would + base verb. Use it for imagined or unlikely situations, and to weigh options before you decide.

TypeFormExample
AffirmativeIf + past simple, would + base verbIf I had more time, I would automate the checkout.
Negativewould not (wouldn't) + base verbIf it failed, nobody would lose money and we would not panic.
QuestionWhat would + subject + base verb?What would you do if the tests failed on Friday?
Common mistakeDo not use "will" in the if-part: not "If I will have time" — say "If I had time, I would..." The past form marks the idea as imagined.
