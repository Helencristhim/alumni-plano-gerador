Chapter 1: First vs Second

From Real to Imagined

Last time: "If we enforce this framework, we will mitigate the risk." That is real and likely. Tonight: "If I were leading this team, I would start differently." That is a hypothetical — and it is interview gold.

The Big Idea

One Word Changes Everything

First = real & likely

"If I get this role, I will build the team."

Second = hypothetical

"If I were leading it, I would start with trust."

The second conditional is not about doubt. It is how you step into a scenario that does not exist yet and reason out loud — the exact skill a senior leader shows every day.

Chapter 3: The Hypothetical Code

If I Were... I Would...

The grammar of imagined situations

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"If I found a breach, I would evaluate the scope first."

"If I had more time, I would test every system."

"If the trade-off were that sharp, I would choose safety."

"If I were building the team, I would start with trust."

The blue verbs look like the past — but the situation is imagined. The green result always uses would. Why?

PartFormExample
The condition (if)if + past simple
(were for all subjects: I / he / it were)		If I were in that position...
If I had more time...
The resultwould + base verb
(would / wouldn't / I'd)		...I would evaluate the risks.
...I wouldn't rush to hire.
MeaningA hypothetical or diplomatic situation — imagined, not real yet. It shows your thinking, not your uncertainty. (First conditional = if + will = real and likely.)

In an interview: the second conditional lets you show how you would think your way through a problem you have never seen.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"If I would lead the team, I would restructure it."
"If I led the team, I would restructure it."
"If I was in that position, I would act fast."
"If I were in that position, I would act fast."
"If I had the budget, I will hire two experts."
"If I had the budget, I would hire two experts."

The if part takes the past (never "would"). The result takes would (never "will"). In formal English, use were for everyone: "If I were you..."

Chapter 4: The Interview

Meet Mark

A senior recruiter for a VP of AI Safety role

Dialogue

The Final Round

M
Thanks for coming back for the final round, Lucia. I want to run a few hypotheticals. First one: if you discovered a major security breach in your first week, what would your first move be?
L
That is a great question. If I found a breach that early, I would not panic. I would first evaluate the scope, then I would prioritize the systems most at risk before I told anyone outside the team.
M
Good instinct. Here is a harder one. If you had to choose between shipping fast and shipping safely, which would you prioritize?
L
Honestly, if the trade-off were that sharp, I would choose safety. My approach would be to delay the launch by a week rather than expose users to a risk I could not measure.
M
Interesting. And what criteria would you use to make that call?
L
I would weigh up three things: the size of the exposure, the number of users affected, and whether we could reverse the damage. If all three were high, I would hold the release.
M
Last one. If you were building an AI safety team from scratch, where would you start?
L
I would leverage the people I trust first. If I had a strong core of three, I would build the framework around them, because in my experience, judgment travels through people, not documents.
