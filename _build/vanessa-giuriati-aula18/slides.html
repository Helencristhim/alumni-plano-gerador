Chapter 3

If I Were You

The second conditional: if + past, would + verb

Grammar Discovery

Imagining the Result

"If I were you, I would make a plan first."

"If I had more time, I would test it again."

"I would talk to her if I knew the answer."

"Suppose you tried it — what would happen?"

PartFormExample
If-part (the condition)if + past simpleIf I had more time...
Result (imagined)would + base verb...I would test it again.
"were" for everyoneIn this structure, use "were" with every subject: "If I were you...", "If he were here...". The order can flip: "I'd wait if I were you" = "If I were you, I'd wait" (comma when the if-part comes first).
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"If I was you, I'd wait."

✓ RIGHT

"If I were you, I'd wait."

✗ WRONG

"If I would have time, I would help."

✓ RIGHT

"If I had time, I would help."

✗ WRONG

"If I am in your situation, I help."

✓ RIGHT

"If I were in your situation, I would help."

After "if", use the past simple (had, knew, were) — never "would". Use "were" with every subject ("if I were you"). The result part takes "would + verb".

Dialogue

Asking for Advice

D
Vanessa, I got a job offer, but I'm not sure I should take it.
V
That's a big decision. If I were in your situation, I'd write down the pros and cons first.
D
I'm worried I'd make a mistake either way.
V
I'd recommend imagining you're already there. Suppose you took it — would you feel excited or anxious?
D
Honestly? Excited. But what if it doesn't work out?
V
If I were you, I'd take the chance. Otherwise, you might regret not trying.
D
You're right. I think I knew that already.
V
It's your call, of course. But I really wish someone had pushed me like this years ago.
Comprehension

Did You Catch It?

1. What was the first thing she recommended he do?
If she were in his situation, she'd write down the pros and cons first, and imagine he had already taken the job.
2. What was her final advice, and the reason?
"If I were you, I'd take the chance." Otherwise, he might regret not trying.
3. How did she keep it gentle, not bossy?
She framed it as her own view ("if I were you"), said "it's your call," and shared her own wish, so the decision stayed his.
Listening

The Advice That Helped

Listen first. No text. Sound only.

0:000:00
1. What situation was she stuck in, and why didn't she act?
She was in a job she didn't enjoy but was afraid to leave, so she kept complaining instead of doing anything.
2. What did her wise friend say?
"If I were you, I would stop waiting for the perfect moment." She asked her to imagine being sixty: would she regret staying or regret trying?
3. What did she learn about giving advice?
Telling someone what to do rarely works, but "if I were in your situation, I would..." feels gentle and honest, because you imagine the world from their side.
Listening

A Mentor at Work

A different voice, a different message. Listen carefully.

0:000:00
1. What mistake did he make as a new manager?
He just told people the answer ("do this, do that"). He thought he was helping, but people stopped coming to him because they felt he wasn't listening.
2. What advice did his older colleague give?
"If I were you, I would ask more and order less." So he started saying "if I were in your situation, I would... but what do you think?"
3. What is his key rule for giving advice?
Imagine the problem from their side first, frame it as your opinion (recommend, don't force), offer it gently, and otherwise let them choose. That builds trust.

Detective

Spot the Error

0 / 4 errors found

"If I was you, I would wait."
"If I were you, I would wait." (use "were" with every subject)
"If I would have time, I would help."
"If I had time, I would help." (no "would" after "if" — use the past simple)
"If I am in your situation, I take the job."
"If I were in your situation, I would take the job." (if + past, would + verb)
"I recommend to wait a week."
"I recommend waiting a week." (recommend + -ing, not "to")
