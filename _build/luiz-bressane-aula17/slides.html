The Language of the Table

Vocabulary

Words 1-4

Leniency
Mildness in punishment; a softer or reduced penalty
"We asked the prosecution for leniency in exchange for a guilty plea."
Concession
Something you give up to reach an agreement
"A guilty plea on a lesser count was the only concession we would make."
Leverage
An advantage that gives you power to push for better terms
"Weak evidence gave us the leverage to demand a suspended sentence."
Waive
To give up a right voluntarily, on purpose
"My client will not waive his right to appeal under any deal."

Chapter 3: The Language of Bargaining

Prefer & Concede

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

My client would rather go to trial than accept those terms.

We would prefer probation to any custodial sentence.

We will plead guilty, provided the charge is downgraded.

We will not settle unless the sentence is suspended.

StructurePatternUse
would rather ... thanwould rather + base verb + than + base verbstate a preference between two options
would prefer ... towould prefer + noun + to + nounprefer one thing over another
provided / as long asmain clause + provided (that) + presentagree ONLY on this condition
unlessnegative main clause + unless + present"if not" — the deal is off without it

Use would rather / would prefer to say what your client wants; use provided / as long as / unless / even if to name the condition. Note: provided and as long as mean "only if"; unless means "if not"; even if means "whether or not it changes anything". The condition clause stays in the present, never "will".

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"We would rather to go to trial."

✓ RIGHT

"We would rather go to trial."

✗ WRONG

"We will settle unless the sentence will be suspended."

✓ RIGHT

"We will settle unless the sentence is suspended."

After "would rather", use the base verb with no "to": "would rather go", "would rather wait." After "provided / as long as / unless / even if", keep the condition in the present, never "will": "provided the charge is downgraded", "unless probation is offered."

Chapter 4: At the Table

Working the Offer

Dialogue

Across the Table

P
Counsel, here is our offer: a guilty plea to the full charge, four years in custody. Take it, or we go to trial.
L
Your evidence is thin, and we both know it. My client would rather go to trial than accept a custodial sentence on that count. That is my leverage.
P
Then make me a concession. What is your client willing to give?
L
He will plead to the lesser charge and make full restitution, provided you downgrade the principal count. We would prefer probation to any time in custody.
P
Probation is a stretch. I could offer a suspended sentence, but only if he agrees to cooperate and name his counterpart.
L
We will accept a suspended sentence as long as he keeps his right to appeal. He will not waive that, even if it costs us the deal.
P
Then we have a deal: lesser charge, suspended sentence, full restitution as a stipulation. I will forgo the second count.
Comprehension

The Negotiation

1. What was the prosecution's opening offer?
A guilty plea to the full charge and four years in custody — take it or go to trial.
2. What leverage did counsel use to push back?
The evidence was thin, so the client would rather go to trial than accept a custodial sentence.
3. What was the one term counsel would not give up?
The client's right to appeal — he would not waive it, even if it cost the deal.
4. What was the final deal?
A plea to the lesser charge, a suspended sentence, and full restitution as a stipulation; the second count was forgone.

Chapter 5: Drill

Sharpen the Deal

Detective · Transformation · Building

Spot the Error

Fix the Bargain

"My client would rather to go to trial."
My client would rather go to trial. (base verb, no "to")
"We will settle unless the sentence will be suspended."
We will settle unless the sentence is suspended. (present after "unless")
"We would rather probation than custody."
We would prefer probation to custody. (prefer + noun + to + noun)
"He will plead guilty, provided the charge will be downgraded."
He will plead guilty, provided the charge is downgraded. (present after "provided")
Transformation

Make It a Bargain

"Trial is better for us than this offer." → would rather ... than
We would rather go to trial than accept this offer.
"Probation is better than custody." → would prefer ... to
We would prefer probation to custody.
"We agree only if the charge is downgraded." → provided
We agree, provided the charge is downgraded.
"If the sentence is not suspended, no deal." → unless
There is no deal unless the sentence is suspended.

Build the Position
