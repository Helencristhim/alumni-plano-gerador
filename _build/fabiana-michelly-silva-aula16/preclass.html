Aula 16 -- Pre-class

Leading a Project Kick-off -- Future Continuous (will be + -ing)

Future continuous (will be + verb-ing) for describing actions in progress across a project timeline, contrasted with future perfect (will have + past participle). New vocabulary: kick-off, deliverable, work stream, dependency, onboard, ramp up, cadence, scope creep, charter, stakeholder map, ownership, success criteria.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Kick-off -- the first meeting that officially launches a project
"Welcome to the kick-off for the supplier-integration project." (Reunião de abertura)
Deliverable -- a concrete result the project must produce and hand over
"The deliverables for phase one are the charter and the map." (Entregável)
Work stream -- a self-contained part of the project with its own owner and goal
"Each work stream has a single owner."
Dependency -- when one task or stream relies on another before it can start
"The data stream depends on contracts, so watch that dependency." (Dependência)
Onboard -- to bring a new supplier, partner, or member into the system
"We will be onboarding four new suppliers." (Integrar)
Ramp up -- to increase activity or output gradually, step by step
"We will be ramping up the pilot gradually, not all at once." (Acelerar gradualmente)
Cadence -- the regular, predictable rhythm of meetings or check-ins
"I will be running the kick-off cadence personally." (Ritmo / cafência)
Scope creep -- uncontrolled growth in what a project is expected to cover
"Guard against scope creep: new requests go through me." (Expansão descontrolada do escopo)
Charter -- the founding document that defines a project's goal, scope, and owners
"The project charter and the stakeholder map are due by week two."
Stakeholder map -- a view of everyone affected by the project and how much each one cares
"The stakeholder map shows who we will be keeping informed."
Ownership -- being the single person clearly responsible for a result
"Ownership is clear from day one: one owner per stream." (Titularidade / responsabilidade clara)
Success criteria -- the clear, agreed measures that define whether a project succeeded
"The success criteria are on-time onboarding, clean data, zero disruption." (Critérios de sucesso)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

A strong kick-off is mostly about painting the timeline. Instead of vague promises, a good leader describes what the team will be doing at each point. "Over the next three months, we will be onboarding four suppliers," she says, "and I will be running the cadence for the first weeks." The future continuous -- will be + verb-ing -- shows an action that is in progress at a future moment, not finished. She uses it for the whole rollout: "While we ramp up, I will be tracking the success criteria." For things that are complete by a deadline, she switches to the future perfect: "By the end of week two, we will have delivered the charter." Notice the difference: will be delivering means still in progress, while will have delivered means already done.

1. Why does the leader say "we will be onboarding four suppliers" instead of "we will onboard four suppliers"?
A The future continuous shows the action in progress across the whole period, not as a single event.
B "Will be onboarding" means she is not sure it will happen.
C The two forms mean exactly the same thing in every case.
2. What is the difference between "will be delivering" and "will have delivered"?
A They are interchangeable.
B "Will be delivering" is still in progress; "will have delivered" is already finished by that point.
C "Will have delivered" is a question form.
3. How is the future continuous formed?
A will + be + verb-ing (e.g., will be tracking).
B will + have + past participle.
C will + base verb only.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best response for each professional situation.

You are opening a kick-off and want to describe what the team will be doing across the next quarter. You say:
A "Over the next quarter, we will be onboarding the new suppliers one at a time." (future continuous, in progress)
B "We will onboarded the suppliers." (wrong form)
C "We are going to do many things." (vague, no timeline)
A deliverable must be finished by a specific deadline. The best line is:
A "By week two, we will be delivering the charter." (still in progress -- wrong for a deadline)
B "By week two, we will have delivered the charter." (future perfect -- finished by then)
C "By week two, we will deliver maybe the charter." (vague and ungrammatical)
A new request lands mid-project and could expand the scope. As the leader, you say:
A "To avoid scope creep, that request comes to me first before it enters a work stream." (protects the scope)
B "Sure, add it straight into the data stream." (invites scope creep)
C "Ignore the request completely." (avoids the stakeholder)
