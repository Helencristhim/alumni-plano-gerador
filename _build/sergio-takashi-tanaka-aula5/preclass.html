Lesson 05 -- Pre-class

What If -- Negotiating the Hypothetical

Debating an imagined risk scenario and negotiating with a partner bank. Key words: collateral, credit line, waiver, concession, counteroffer, freeze, downside, bailout, insolvent, negotiate. Structure: the second conditional (if + past simple, ... would + verb) for an imagined situation, against the first conditional for a real plan.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Collateral -- an asset you promise to a lender as security for a deal
"If they asked for more collateral, we would offer bonds."
Credit line -- the most a bank agrees to lend you at any time
"If the bank cut our credit line, the buffer would be tight."
Waiver -- an agreement to drop a rule or requirement for a time
"If we needed a waiver on the limit, we would ask early."
Concession -- something you agree to give up to reach a deal
"If they pushed, we would offer a small concession, not a big one."
Counteroffer -- a new offer you make in reply to another offer
"If we did not like their terms, we would make a counteroffer."
To freeze -- to stop something from being used or changed
"If the bank froze our credit line, we would post more collateral."
Downside -- the possible loss or negative side of a situation
"If the downside stayed limited, we would sign the deal."
Bailout -- emergency money to rescue a bank or company
"If a partner bank asked for a bailout, the news would spread fast."
Insolvent -- unable to pay what it owes
"If a partner bank became insolvent, we would cut the exposure at once."
To negotiate -- to discuss terms in order to reach an agreement
"If the offer were unfair, we would negotiate a longer deadline."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Before every big negotiation, the desk runs a hypothetical. What would happen if a partner bank became insolvent overnight? If that bank froze our credit line, we would post more collateral the same day. If it asked for a concession, we would offer a small one, not a fire sale. We would never panic; instead, we would negotiate a waiver and protect the buffer. If I were the head of the committee, I would want one clear answer: if the downside grew, would we still be safe? A good counteroffer is the calm reply to a hard what-if.

1. Why do we say "If a partner bank became insolvent, we would cut the exposure"?
A It is an imagined, unlikely situation, so we use the second conditional (if + past, would).
B It is a real plan for next week, so we use the first conditional.
C It already happened, so we use the past simple.
2. Which sentence is correct?
A "If they asked for a concession, we would offer a small one."
B "If they would ask for a concession, we would offer a small one."
C "If they asked for a concession, we will offer a small one."
3. In formal English, how do we use "to be" in the imagined if-part?
A "If I were the regulator, I would ask the same."
B "If I was the regulator, I would ask the same."
C "If I am the regulator, I would ask the same."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Second Conditional

GRAMMAR

How to talk about a situation that is only imagined, and its imagined result.

FormUseExample
Positive
If + past simple, ... would + verb		An imagined or unlikely situation and its result. Not a plan -- a "what if".If the market crashed, we would unwind.
Negative
... would not + verb		An imagined result that does not happen.We would not panic.
Question
Would + subject + verb		Ask about an imagined result.Would your committee accept it?
The verb "to be"In the imagined if-part, formal English uses were for every subject: if I were you. A real, likely plan uses the first conditional instead: if liquidity falls, we will unwind.
