Aula 11 -- Pre-class

Reading the Signals -- Interpreting What Must Have Happened

Interpreting ambiguous business signals: a client who goes quiet, a late shipment, a rival that cuts its price. Key words: infer, assumption, speculate, red flag, gut feeling, plausible, rule out, jump to conclusions, read between the lines, far-fetched, stall, hold back. Structure: modals of deduction -- must / might / could / can't + be (now) and + have + past participle (past) -- to say how sure you are (They must be busy; they cannot have signed yet).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Infer -- to conclude something from evidence, not from a direct statement (inferir / deduzir)
"From the warm tone of the email, I infer that the client is still interested."
Assumption -- something you accept as true without proof (suposição / premissa)
"My assumption is that they lost a major client last quarter."
Speculate -- to form a theory without firm evidence (especular)
"I do not want to speculate too far, but they might be comparing prices."
Red flag -- a clear warning sign of a problem (sinal de alerta)
"Two weeks of silence after a strong meeting is a red flag."
Gut feeling -- an instinct or hunch, not based on hard facts (intuição / palpite)
"My gut feeling is that they are stalling to gain leverage."
Plausible -- reasonable and likely to be true (plausível)
"A scheduling delay is the most plausible explanation for the silence."
Rule out -- to eliminate something as a possibility (descartar)
"I would rule out the worst case: they cannot have signed yet."
Jump to conclusions -- to judge before you have the facts (tirar conclusões precipitadas)
"Let us not jump to conclusions after only two weeks of silence."
Read between the lines -- to understand a meaning that is not stated openly (ler nas entrelinhas)
"If I read between the lines, the tone was warmer than the silence suggests."
Far-fetched -- unlikely; hard to believe (improvável / forçado)
"The idea that this price cut is a real long-term threat is far-fetched."
Stall -- to delay deliberately to gain time (enrolar / ganhar tempo)
"They might be stalling to gain a little leverage before they answer."
Hold back -- to keep information or a decision to yourself (segurar / não revelar)
"Do not hold back your own story: your quality and lead time still win."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Andy is worried. The client has gone quiet for two weeks. "Let us not jump to conclusions," Izabel says. "They replied warmly last week, so they must still be interested. Their procurement lead was traveling, so the delay could just be a scheduling thing." Andy raises a red flag: a colleague saw the client at a supplier fair. "Then they might be comparing prices," Izabel admits, "but they cannot have signed with anyone yet, because our quote is still open." On the rival that suddenly cut its price, she is just as careful. "That price is below cost, so they cannot be making money on it. They must have lost a big account, and this might be a defensive move." Her read: "They must be stalling for leverage. I would rule out the worst case."

1. "They must still be interested." How sure is Izabel?
A Almost certain, based on the evidence (must = strong deduction).
B Just one weak possibility among many.
C She thinks it is impossible.
2. "They cannot have signed with anyone yet." What does this mean?
A They probably signed with a rival.
B It is impossible that they signed (deduction about the past: can't have + participle).
C They are not allowed to sign.
3. "It could just be a scheduling thing." Why "could be" and not "must be"?
A It is only one possibility, not a certainty (could / might = possible).
B Because "must be" is always wrong.
C Because the sentence is about the past.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Modals of Deduction

GRAMMAR

Saying how sure you are (explicação em inglês e português).

Certainty / CertezaNow / AgoraPast / Passado
Certo (~95%)must + be/base
They must be busy.		must have + particípio
They must have left.
Possível (~50%)might / could / may + be/base
It might be a delay.		might / could / may have + particípio
She could have missed it.
Impossível (~0%)cannot (can't) + be/base
They cannot be serious.		cannot (can't) have + particípio
They cannot have signed.
Cuidado / Watch outNunca use "to" depois do modal: errado "must to be" → certo "must be". Use o particípio depois de "have": "must have gone" (não "go"). Para o passado impossível use can't have, não "mustn't have".
