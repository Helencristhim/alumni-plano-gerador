Chapter 1: Reading the Signals

From What Was Said to What It Means

Last class you relayed what people said at the board meeting: she reported that the margin had improved, he explained that the launch was the priority. That was reported speech. But sometimes no one says the thing directly. The client goes quiet for two weeks. A rival suddenly drops its price. You have to read the signals and decide what they mean. Welcome to the language of deduction.

Chapter 3

The Code

Modals of deduction: must / might / could / can't + be and + have + participle

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"They must be busy — they replied last night."

"They must have found a cheaper supplier."

"It might be a delay, or it could be internal approvals."

"They cannot have signed yet — our quote is still open."

How sure?NowIn the past
Certainmust + be / base
They must be busy.		must have + participle
They must have left.
Possiblemight / could / may + be / base
It might be a delay.		might / could / may have + participle
She could have missed it.
Impossiblecannot (can't) + be / base
They cannot be serious.		cannot (can't) have + participle
They cannot have signed.

We use modals of deduction to say how SURE we are about a situation, based on evidence. Never put "to" after the modal, and use a past participle after "have".

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"They must to be negotiating with a rival."

✓ RIGHT

"They must be negotiating with a rival."

✗ WRONG

"She must have forgot the email."

✓ RIGHT

"She must have forgotten the email."

✗ WRONG

"They mustn't have signed yet."

✓ RIGHT

"They can't have signed yet."

Never put "to" after a modal. After "have", use the past participle (gone, forgotten, found). For something impossible in the past, use "can't have", not "mustn't have".

Chapter 4

In the Room

Making sense of a quiet client and a surprise price cut

Dialogue

The Quiet Client

A
Izabel, the client has gone quiet. Two weeks of silence after that strong meeting. What do you make of it?
I
Let us not jump to conclusions. They replied warmly before, so they must still be interested. Their procurement lead was traveling — the silence could just be a scheduling thing. That is the most plausible read.
A
There is one red flag, though. A colleague saw their team at a supplier fair last week.
I
Then they might be comparing prices. But they cannot have signed with anyone yet — our quote is still open and they asked us to hold it. So I would rule out the worst case.
A
Fair. And the rival that suddenly cut its price yesterday — how do you read that?
I
That price is below cost, so they cannot be making money on it. My assumption is that they must have lost a big account and this is a defensive move. I would not speculate beyond that.
A
So what is your read overall? Should we worry?
I
My gut feeling is that they are stalling for leverage. If I read between the lines of that last email, the tone was warm. The idea that we are losing them is far-fetched. We should not hold back — I will call them Thursday.
