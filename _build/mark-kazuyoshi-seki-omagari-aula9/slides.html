Chapter 3 -- Signpost It

Grammar Discovery

Signpost phrases: guide your audience through a talk

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

What does each orange phrase do in a talk?

"Today, I'm going to talk about my project." (opening)

"Let me start with some background." (first point)

"Moving on to the results, the numbers were clear." (transition)

"To sum up, the project saved us time." (conclusion)

Grammar Rule

Signpost Your Talk

FunctionSignpost phraseExample
OpenToday, I'm going to talk about...Today, I'm going to talk about my city.
Move onLet's move on to... / Moving on to...Let's move on to the next point.
HighlightI'd like to highlight... / The key point is...I'd like to highlight one idea.
ConcludeTo sum up... / Let me recap...To sum up, practice every day.

Key idea: signpost phrases do not add new facts; they GUIDE your audience from one part of the talk to the next. Open, move on, highlight, conclude -- four signposts and your talk has a clear shape.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"In resume, English is important."
"To sum up, English is important."

"In resume" is a false friend from Portuguese. To close a talk, say To sum up or In summary.
