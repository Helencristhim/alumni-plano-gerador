Lesson 08 -- Pre-class

Eating Out with Confidence -- Restaurants, Orders, and Recommendations

In Lesson 7 you learned to speak up at the hotel. Now you sit down to eat — and this time nothing is wrong: you are the one hosting. From the first appetizer to the last thing you sign. Key words: appetizer, entree, side dish, dietary restriction, gluten-free, medium-rare, complimentary, tab, sommelier, tipping etiquette. Structure: recommendations and suggestions — the grammar of offering an idea ("I’d recommend the sea bass." / "You might want to try the tasting menu." / "Why don’t you start with the soup?" / "Should I mention the dietary restriction now?"). The rule that catches everybody: after recommend comes a noun or -ing ("recommend the salmon" / "recommend trying it"), never "to try".

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Take your time — listen twice, and say it out loud. These are the ten words that live between the first course and the last thing you sign at an American restaurant.

Appetizer -- the small dish you eat first, before the main course
"Why don’t we share an appetizer? The soup here is very good."
Entree -- the main course of the meal -- the big plate, in American English
"For my entree, I’ll go with the steak, medium-rare."
Side dish -- a smaller dish that comes next to the main one, like vegetables or fries
"Could I get a side dish of grilled vegetables with the steak?"
Dietary restriction -- a food someone cannot or must not eat, for health, allergy or belief
"My guest has a dietary restriction — he can’t eat gluten."
Gluten-free -- made without wheat or gluten -- safe for someone who can’t eat it
"Do you have any gluten-free options for the main course?"
Medium-rare -- cooked so the meat is still pink and warm in the middle
"I’ll have the steak, medium-rare, please."
Complimentary -- given free by the restaurant, as a gift, with nothing to pay
"The bread and olive oil are complimentary — on the house."
Tab -- the bill -- the paper that shows how much you owe at the end
"Could we have the tab, please? It’s on me tonight."
Sommelier -- the restaurant expert who helps you choose the wine
"The sommelier recommended a light red with the fish."
Tipping etiquette -- the polite rules about how much extra money you leave after a meal
"American tipping etiquette is eighteen to twenty percent, added by you."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

Lucimara arrives first, on purpose, to read the menu before her guest sits down. When the server brings a small plate of warm bread, he says it is complimentary — on the house. She sees the appetizers on the left, the entrees in the middle, and a short list of side dishes at the bottom.

When Mr. Chen sits down, she does not hand him the menu in silence. "Why don’t you start with the soup?" she says. "And you might want to try the sea bass — I’d recommend it. Marco says it is the best thing tonight."

There is one thing she must get right. Mr. Chen has a dietary restriction: he is gluten-free. So before they order, she turns to the server. "Should I mention it to the kitchen now?" Yes, always now, he tells her — better asked early than sent back later. For herself, she orders the steak, medium-rare, and a side of vegetables.

At the end, the tab arrives in a small folder. In the United States, the tip is not printed on the bill; you add eighteen to twenty percent yourself. She knows the tipping etiquette now. "It’s on me," she tells Mr. Chen, before he can reach for his wallet.

1. "I’d recommend it." After recommend, which is correct?
A "I’d recommend to try the sea bass." (never "to" after recommend)
B "I’d recommend the sea bass." or "I’d recommend trying the sea bass." (a noun, or the -ing form)
C "I’d recommend that you trying it."
2. "Why don’t you start with the soup?" What is Lucimara doing here?
A Ordering her guest to eat the soup — it is a command.
B Making a friendly SUGGESTION as a question — her guest is still free to choose something else. Note: after why don’t you, the base verb (start), no "to".
C Asking a real question about the reason she does not eat soup.
3. The bread is complimentary. What does that mean for the bill?
A It is free — the restaurant gives it as a gift, and it does not appear on the tab.
B It is a compliment from the chef, and you pay extra for it.
C It is the most expensive thing on the menu.
4. "Should I mention it to the kitchen now?" Why does Lucimara ask BEFORE ordering?
A Because you are not allowed to eat gluten in the United States.
B Because a dietary restriction is easier to plan early than to fix on the plate — and Should I...? is how you ask for advice.
C Because the kitchen closes before the meal starts.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Recommendations and Suggestions (the grammar of offering an idea)

GRAMMAR

A host does not give orders. A host offers ideas. These are the four ways English does it.

OpenerFormExample
I’d recommend...I’d recommend + a NOUN, or + verb-ing. Never "to"."I’d recommend the salmon." / "I’d recommend trying it."
You might want to...You might want to + base verb. The "to" belongs to want to and stays."You might want to try the tasting menu."
Why don’t you...?Why don’t you + base verb. A suggestion in the shape of a question. No "to"."Why don’t you start with the soup?"
Should I...? / Shall I...?Should I + base verb. Use it to ASK for a recommendation, or to offer to do something."Should I mention the dietary restriction now?"
Asking for oneWhat do you recommend? — your best defense against a fast menu."What would you recommend? It’s my first time here."
The one ruleAfter recommend, a noun or -ing (never "to try"). After why don’t you and should I, the base verb, no "to". The word "to" only survives inside want to. Each opener OFFERS an idea — the other person can always say no.

Watch out -- the classic mistake: the mind translates "recomendo experimentar" and puts an infinitive: "I’d recommend to try". In English that is wrong. It is "I’d recommend trying" or "I’d recommend the salmon". Remember: recommend takes a noun or an -ing, and it never takes "to".

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer. Question 1 is the trap: after "recommend", the word is never "to".

You loved the fish here last time. You want to recommend it to your guest. You say:
A "I’d recommend to try the sea bass."
B "I’d recommend the sea bass — I had it last time, and it was excellent."
C "I recommend you for try the sea bass."
The menu is a wall of words you half understand. The best move is:
A "What would you recommend? It’s my first time here." (short answer, easy to catch)
B Point at something and hope it is what you wanted.
C Read the whole menu out loud until you find a word you know.
Your guest cannot eat gluten. Before you order, you turn to the server and say:
A "He is not allowed to eat here." (that is not what a dietary restriction means)
B "He’s gluten-free — should I mention it to the kitchen now?"
C Say nothing, and hope the main course has no gluten.
The warm bread arrives before you order. You are not sure if you pay for it. It is:
A Complimentary — free, on the house. It will not be on the tab.
B An appetizer you have to pay for separately.
C The most expensive item, added to the tab automatically.
The tab arrives, and your Chinese supplier reaches for his wallet. As the host, you:
A Let him pay — and forget that in the U.S. you add the tip yourself.
B "Please, it’s on me." — said early, and then you add eighteen to twenty percent for the tip.
C Wait, because the tip is already printed on the bill in America.
