Series
The Diplomat — Season 1, Episode 1 (Netflix)

Kate Wyler spends the whole series doing exactly what you will be doing in August: disagreeing, in English, with people who hold more power, more history and more information than she does — without ever capitulating and without ever fighting. Watch the pattern: she concedes out loud the part where the other person is right (“you are right about that”), and only then holds the part that is hers. Connection to Lesson 9: listen for the concessive structure throughout — While / Granted / That said — and notice that HER point always lands in the main clause, at the end of the sentence, which is the only one the room remembers.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Write down THREE moments where Kate concedes a point and immediately holds her own. Then rewrite each one for your academic council: same structure, your content (the sample, the focus groups, the six weeks).

Podcast
Hidden Brain — “Why Conversations Go Wrong” (with Deborah Tannen)

The linguist Deborah Tannen shows how two people stating the SAME facts can walk out of a room with opposite verdicts — and how a reasonable disagreement turns into a confrontation without anyone telling a single lie. It is the theory behind the most important slide of Lesson 9: “While the sample is small, the pattern is consistent” and “While the pattern is consistent, the sample is small” hold the same two facts and deliver contrary judgments. Connection to Lesson 9: what Tannen calls conversational style, grammar calls the main clause.

Tip: listen once, with no pauses. Then summarize it out loud in ninety seconds, using THREE different concessive structures (“While she argues that...” / “Granted... That said...” / “Even if we accept that... it still...”). Record it and listen back: did your concession come BEFORE your thesis, or after it?

YouTube
Julia Dhar — “How to disagree productively and find common ground” (TED, 12 min)

A three-time world debate champion explains why the point of a well-run disagreement is NOT to win, and how you separate a criticism of the ARGUMENT from a criticism of the PERSON — which is literally the difference between taking issue with his premise and attacking Dr. Prescott. Connection to Lesson 9: she names the move this whole lesson trains, starting from the common ground, and shows why conceding the other side's true point STRENGTHENS your position instead of weakening it.

Tip: watch it twice. On the second pass, write your own thirty-second answer to Dr. Prescott’s line (“that is not a finding, that is a rumor”) using her three moves — concede, counter, propose — and the expressions to concede, to defer to and to stand your ground. Bring it in writing to Lesson 10.
