Lesson 09 -- Pre-class

Diplomatic Pushback -- Disagreeing With People Who Know More Than You

Disagreeing with an academic council of senior researchers without capitulating and without fighting: concede out loud what is true, hold what is yours, and turn the objection into a concrete ask. Key words: counterpoint, reservation, premise, assumption, merit, anecdotal, rigor, peer-reviewed, common ground, to concede, to take issue with, to play devil's advocate, to stand your ground, to defer to, to hold water. Structure: concessive clauses (While / Although the sample is small, the pattern is consistent · Granted... That said... · Much as I would like to... · Even if we accept that... still) — his point goes in the subordinate clause, yours goes in the main clause, and the room walks out remembering the main clause.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the vocabulary of someone who disagrees with a specialist and is still taken seriously.

Counterpoint -- an argument you place against the one on the table, without rejecting the person who made it
"My counterpoint is not that he is wrong about the sample. It is that the sample is not what the number is measuring."
Reservation -- a specific doubt you name out loud, rather than a general feeling of discomfort
"I have one reservation about the plan, and I would rather say it now than nod and take it to the corridor afterwards."
Premise -- the thing an argument stands on before it starts, and usually never proves
"I take issue with the premise, not with the conclusion. If the premise holds, everything he said after it is correct."
Assumption -- something taken as true without evidence, and invisible to the person holding it
"The assumption in this room is that silence means agreement. The whole assessment exists to test that one sentence."
Merit -- the part of an argument that is genuinely good, whether or not you accept where it ends up
"There is real merit in what he is saying about the sample. There is none at all in what he concludes from it."
Anecdotal -- based on individual stories rather than on systematic evidence, and it can still be true
"He called the focus groups anecdotal, and he is right. Anecdotal is not the same as wrong, and that is the sentence I have to be able to say."
Rigor -- the discipline that makes a finding survive somebody actively trying to break it
"They are not attacking me. They are applying the only rigor they have, and if my finding cannot survive it, I would rather know on Thursday."
Peer-reviewed -- checked by other specialists in the field before anybody is asked to believe it
"It is not peer-reviewed, and it was never meant to be. A diagnostic and a study are two different objects with two different jobs."
Common ground -- the part both sides already agree on, and the only place a disagreement can safely start
"Let me start from the common ground: none of us believes that a faculty which says nothing is a faculty with nothing to say."
To concede -- to give a point away because it is true, not because you are losing
"I will concede the sample before he takes it from me. What I will not concede is what the sample means."
To take issue with -- to disagree with one specific claim, deliberately and out loud, and never with the person
"I take issue with the premise, and not with a single thing you concluded from it."
To play devil's advocate -- to argue a position you do not actually hold, in order to test whether the other one survives
"Let me play devil's advocate against my own finding for a moment, because you are going to do it anyway."
To stand your ground -- to hold your position under pressure, without raising your voice and without moving
"She conceded the sample without hesitating, and then she stood her ground on what it meant."
To defer to -- to accept somebody's authority on a point where they genuinely know more, as a choice and never a surrender
"I defer to this council entirely on the research question. I would not defer to anyone on what a sixty-two percent silence means."
To hold water -- (of an argument) to survive examination, used almost always in the negative
"That is a fair premise to test, and it does not hold water on exactly one point."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Teresa Vidal lost the room in eleven seconds, and she lost it by agreeing. She had presented a culture assessment to an academic council once before, in Lisbon, and she had come out of it certain that the mistake had been arrogance. So this time she arrived humble. When the chair took off his glasses and said that a response rate of thirty-four percent was, in his field, anecdotal rather than evidence, she said: you are absolutely right, and I will redo the whole thing. Every word of that sentence was polite, and every word of it was true. It also told nine people that the culture work could wait a year, and they believed her, because she had said it herself.

There is a third answer, and it is not a personality — it is a structure. While the sample was small, the pattern inside it was consistent. Granted, four focus groups are not systematic. That said, they were the only occasion on which anybody had asked that faculty a question in four years. Even if we accept that the instrument is not peer-reviewed, a healthy faculty that is asked how it feels does not still decline to answer at a rate of two thirds. And much as she would have liked to defend the thirty-four percent, the senior move was to say, out loud and before anyone else could, that she would rather replace it — with an instrument the council itself helped her build.

Notice what those sentences do, because they all do the same thing twice. Each one concedes the part that is genuinely true, and each one puts that concession in the clause that depends. And then each one puts the finding — her finding, the one piece of ground that is hers — in the clause that stands. It is not decoration. A room walks out remembering the main clause. “While the sample is small, the pattern is consistent” and “While the pattern is consistent, the sample is small” contain exactly the same two facts and hand down opposite verdicts. Teresa never said a false word in Lisbon. She simply put her own weakness in the clause that survives, and the council heard her argue against herself in her own voice.

The chair told her something afterwards that she has repeated ever since. He said that every consultant who had ever stood in that room had arrived certain, and that he had never believed a single one of them; that rigor is not hostility, it is the only professional courtesy a researcher has to offer a stranger; and that what he had actually been trying to find out was not whether her data was perfect, but whether she knew where it was thin. To defer to him on the research question would have cost her nothing at all. To take issue with him on what a two-thirds silence means would have cost her nothing either — and it was the only thing in the building that was hers.

1. “While the sample is small, the pattern is consistent.” — why does the ORDER of the two clauses CHANGE the argument?
A It does not change it: the two clauses hold the same facts, so the meaning is identical.
B Because the concessive clause (While...) is the subordinate one — it is grammatically DEMOTED. The room walks out remembering the main clause. Invert the order and the same two facts hand down OPPOSITE verdicts.
C Because in English the concessive clause must always come at the end of the sentence.
2. The number one mistake of this lesson: why is “Although the sample is small, but the pattern is consistent” wrong?
A Because although and but do the SAME job, and English uses one of them, never both. It is the Portuguese double marker crossing over whole.
B Because “although” can only be used at the end of a sentence, never at the start.
C Because after “although” the verb has to move into the past.
3. And mistake number two: why is “Despite the response rate is low, the finding holds” wrong?
A Because “despite” only exists in British English.
B Because the correct form would be “Despite that the response rate is low”.
C Because despite / in spite of are prepositions: they take a noun or an -ing form, never a clause. The correct options are “Despite the low response rate”, “despite the fact that the response rate is low”, or simply “although”.
4. According to the text, what did Teresa actually do wrong in Lisbon?
A She was arrogant with the chair, and she defended a number that could not be defended.
B She agreed with everything. She conceded the whole assessment instead of only the part that was true — and by saying it herself, she persuaded nine people that the culture work could wait a year.
C She used the wrong vocabulary, and the council could not follow her technical argument.
5. What does the chair mean when he says that rigor is not hostility?
A That researchers enjoy attacking consultants, and the only defense is to never show them weak data.
B That breaking her argument is the only professional courtesy he has to offer a stranger — and that what he is really testing is not whether her data is perfect, but whether she knows where it is thin.
C That he did not actually disagree with her, and was only being polite in front of the council.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Concession & Counter-Argument

GRAMMAR

The concessive clause is the structure that does TWO things in a single sentence: it hands the other person the point where he is right, and it holds yours. And it decides, silently, which of the two the room will remember.

StructureHIS point (subordinate clause)YOUR point (main clause)
1. While / Although / Even though + clause, + main clauseWhile the sample is small,the pattern is consistent.
2. Granted, ... That said, ...Granted, the groups are anecdotal.That said, they are the only question anyone asked.
3. Much as I + verb, + main clauseMuch as I would like to defend it,I would rather replace it.
4. Even if + clause, + main clause with stillEven if we accept that it is anecdotal,the silence still requires an explanation.
Pivots (they work with any of them)That said · Having said that · Nonetheless · Even so · And yet — they all say the same thing: I heard you, and I have not moved.

Watch out (mistake number 1): “Although the sample is small, but the pattern is consistent” is WRONG. Although and but do the same job, and English uses ONE of them, never both. The Portuguese double marker does not survive the crossing. Choose: “Although the sample is small, the pattern is consistent” OR “The sample is small, but the pattern is consistent”.

Watch out (mistake number 2): despite and in spite of are PREPOSITIONS — they take a noun or an -ing form, NEVER a clause. “Despite the response rate is low” → “Despite the low response rate” or “Despite the fact that the response rate is low”. If you want the full clause, the easy route is to switch to although.

Why this matters to you: the concessive clause is NOT politeness — it is hierarchy. The subordinate clause is grammatically demoted, and the room walks out remembering the MAIN one. “While the sample is small, the pattern is consistent” and “While the pattern is consistent, the sample is small” hold the SAME TWO FACTS and hand down OPPOSITE verdicts. Under pressure, the instinct is to put the concession last, because it sounds humbler — and that inverts your own argument without you saying one false word. His point comes first. Always.

The three-move answer: CONCEDE (give what is true, before anyone takes it) → COUNTER (hold what is yours, in the main clause) → PROPOSE (turn the objection into a concrete ask, with a date on it). In that order, and nothing else. A researcher who helps you build the instrument does not spend March attacking the instrument.

Stage 2: Put the Diplomatic Pushback in Order

Order

Put the five moves in the order in which you will face the Canadian academic council.

?Hold your finding in the main clause, where the room will remember it: that said, the sixty-two percent who said nothing are the finding.
?Turn the objection into an ask: two members of the council, in March, building the next instrument with you.
?Start from the common ground, and hand them the weakest part of your own work before anybody has to find it.
?Defer to them on the ground where they genuinely know more, and stand your ground on the one that is yours.
?Concede the part of the objection that is genuinely true, out loud and without flinching: granted, the sample is small.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each real moment of your academic council in Canada.

Dr. Prescott says: “Thirty-eight percent. In my field, that is not a finding. That is a rumor.” The most senior answer is:
A “You are absolutely right. I will redo the whole assessment and come back when I have a proper number.”
B “Granted, and I concede it without any reservation. That said, the sixty-two percent who chose not to answer are not missing data. They are the finding.”
C “With respect, thirty-eight percent is a perfectly normal response rate and the methodology is sound. I stand by the data.”
Which sentence uses the concessive structure CORRECTLY?
A “Although the instrument is not peer-reviewed, but it was never designed to be.”
B “Despite the sample is small, the pattern inside it is consistent.”
C “While the instrument is not peer-reviewed, it was never designed to be: a diagnostic and a study answer two different questions.”
He says: “You have been here six weeks. What could you possibly know about this faculty?” You say:
A “Very little, and that is precisely why I ran an assessment rather than arriving with a plan. I defer to this council on what the faculty is. I would not defer to anyone on what the response rate says about how it feels to be asked.”
B “Six weeks is enough. I have twelve years of experience in organizational culture.”
C “You are right, I am too new. Perhaps the council should lead this work instead.”
You want to disagree with his reasoning without attacking him. Which sentence does that?
A “I think you have misunderstood what this assessment is for.”
B “I take issue with the premise, and not with a single thing you concluded from it. If the premise held, everything you said after it would be correct.”
C “That argument is simply wrong, and I can prove it.”
He asks what exactly the council is being asked to approve. The strongest close is:
A “The culture strategy, in full, as it appears in the deck.”
B “Nothing today. I would rather come back when the data is stronger.”
C “Not the strategy. One thing: a second round of the instrument, designed with two members of this council, in March. Much as I would like to defend the thirty-eight percent, I would rather replace it.”
