Chapter 3: The Code

Three Words in Portuguese. One in English.

Sei fazer · posso · você pode? — três ideias, um can.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

O que vem logo depois do can em cada frase?

I can explain the numbers.

Can I join the meeting?

Can you send me the report?

Sofia can explain it too.

Depois do can vem sempre um verbo — e ele está sempre pelado. Sem to. E na frase 4: por que Sofia can explain, e não “Sofia cans explains”?

O empregoInglêsEm português
1. HabilidadeI can explain the numbers.Eu sei explicar os números.
2. PermissãoCan I join the meeting?Posso entrar na reunião?
3. PedidoCan you send me the report?Você pode me enviar o relatório?
NegativoI can't sign it today.Eu não posso assinar hoje.
O can nunca mudaI can · you can · Sofia can · we canSem -s na terceira pessoa. Nunca.
A regra em uma linha: can + verbo pelado. Sem to, sem -s, sem nada no meio. E na pergunta o can pula na frente do sujeito — igualzinho ao is e ao does da aula 8. É o mesmo ajudante, com outro nome.
Register

Could Is Just Can in a Suit

CAN — UM COLEGA

"Can you send me the report?"

"Can you explain that?"

"Can I ask you something?"

O David e o Ricardo. Todo dia.

COULD — UM CLIENTE

"Could you send me the report?"

"Could you explain that, please?"

"Could you repeat that?"

O cliente de Miami. A Sofia.

Na dúvida, use can. O can não é grosseiro — é o que todo mundo neste escritório fala o dia inteiro. O could só põe uma distância gentil, e serve pra cliente e pra gestor. Ninguém na história desta empresa se ofendeu com um can. Não trave escolhendo a palavra — as duas funcionam.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Can you to explain that?"
"Can you explain that?"
"I don't can explain in English."
"I can't explain in English."

O primeiro erro é o português empurrando: “pode me explicar” tem o rabinho do infinitivo, e a mão traz o to junto. Depois do can o verbo vem pelado. O segundo é o contrário — é a aula 3 funcionando bem demais: ela aprendeu que negativa pede don't. Truque: o can já é o ajudante da frase. Ele não aceita sócio, e não carrega mala: vira can't sozinho.

1. What can Paula do on Friday? And what can't she do?
She can explain the numbers to the client. She can't sign the document — not without Ricardo's report.
2. Paula asks Ricardo for three things. What are they?
Send the report, print two copies for Thursday, and let her join the meeting on Friday.
3. How many times does Paula say sorry in this email?
Zero. And the email is still polite. The sorry was never doing that job.
Listening

David's One Word

Sound first — no text. Leia as perguntas antes de ouvir.

0:000:00
1. David says "can" has three jobs. What are they?
Saying what you are able to do ("I can explain the numbers"), asking for permission ("Can I join your call?"), and asking for things ("Can you send me the report?").
2. David says the verb after "can" is "naked". What does he mean?
There is no "to". You say "can help", never "can to help". He says your language puts a little tail on the verb — and the tail stays at home.
3. What is the difference between "can" and "could"?
"Could is just can in a suit." Can is for a colleague, could is for a client. Both are correct — and David says nobody in the history of the company has ever been offended by either one.

Comprehension

About David

1. When can David explain the numbers to Paula?
Now — not on Thursday. He asks her if she has ten minutes.
2. Can Paula join the call with Miami on Friday? What does David say?
Yes. He says she can, and she should — because the account is hers.
3. What does David ask Paula to do?
Print the report for the call. She says she can print it on Thursday.
4. At the end, what does David say Paula does not need?
The sorry. She asks for things perfectly well — she just apologizes first, and she doesn't need to.
The Bridge

Same Sentence, Different Room

Você acabou de pedir três coisas ao David, e foi fácil. Por que com o cliente é diferente?

TO A COLLEAGUE

"Can you explain the numbers?"

"Can you repeat that?"

"Can you send me the report?"

TO THE MIAMI CLIENT

"Could you explain the numbers?"

"Could you repeat that, please?"

"Could you send me the report?"

Uma palavra de diferença. Só uma. Então o que muda de verdade quando o cliente entra na sala não é o inglês — é o medo. E medo não se resolve com vocabulário: se resolve falando assim mesmo. Ouça o que a sua gestora tem a dizer sobre isso.

The Miami Client — No Screen

Scenario: Friday, two o'clock. The client from Miami is on the call, and he speaks fast — too fast. You catch one word in five. Stop him. Ask him to repeat, ask him to slow down, and then do your job: tell him what you can do with his account. No screen, no notes. And no sorry in front of anything.
