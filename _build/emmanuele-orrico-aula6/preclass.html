Lesson 06 -- Pre-class

How Is It Going? -- Reporting Progress to the Global Team

The check-in in the middle of the road: Marc already has the PLAN, now he wants the PROGRESS. How many centers are on track, how much real progress there is, what the delays are — and how to say all of that in three lines, without saying sorry. Key words: campaign, milestone, update, progress, to roll out, to track, on track, ongoing, to focus on, to discuss. Structures: how many x how much, and the quantifiers a lot of, a few, a little, some and any — one question decides all of them: can you count it? Expression of the lesson: "So far, so good."

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. These are the ten words of a progress report — the words the Global team wants to hear on a Friday call.

Campaign -- an organized set of actions to bring a medicine to doctors
"The dermatology campaign is on track in eleven centers."
Milestone -- an important point in a project, where you check if you are on time
"The first milestone is complete."
Update -- new information about the situation of a project
"Here is a short update on the campaign."
Progress -- the movement forward in a project
"We have a lot of progress in the private market."
To roll out -- to start something step by step, in more and more places
"We are rolling it out in four more centers this month."
To track -- to follow the numbers of a project, to see where it is
"My team is tracking the delays in the public market."
On track -- going well -- at the right speed, at the right time
"The campaign is on track in eleven centers."
Ongoing -- happening now, and not finished yet
"The roll-out is ongoing in four centers."
To focus on -- to give your attention and your time to one thing
"This month we are focusing on the private market."
To discuss -- to talk about something with other people, to make a decision
"On Friday we are going to discuss the delays."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

It is Friday, and Marc is on the call. He already has the plan — today he wants the progress. "So, how is it going?" he asks. Emmanuele opens her update. "The first milestone is complete, and the dermatology campaign is on track."

"How many centers are active?" — "Eleven. And we are rolling it out in four more this month." Marc writes the number down. "And how much progress is there in the private market?" — "A lot of progress," she says. "This month we are focusing on the big hospitals."

Then the difficult part, and she does not apologize for it. "We have a few delays in the public market. There is not much information about the reimbursement yet, so my team is tracking it every week. The roll-out there is still ongoing." Marc nods. "Are there any problems you cannot solve alone?" — "Not yet. So far, so good. But on Friday I would like to discuss the delays with you."

1. Why does Marc ask "How many centers" and not "How much centers"?
A Because you can COUNT them: one center, two centers, three centers. Things you can count take how many.
B Because "centers" is a short word.
C Because it is a question, and every question uses "how many".
2. And why does he ask "How much progress" in the next line?
A Because "progress" is already a plural word.
B Because you CANNOT count it: nobody says "one progress, two progresses". Things you cannot count take how much.
C Because the question is about money.
3. She says "We have a few delays" and "there is not much information". Why are the two words different?
A Because "delays" is a bad thing and "information" is a good thing.
B Because the first sentence is affirmative and the second one is negative — only for that.
C Because you can count delays (one delay, two delays) → a few. And you cannot count information → much.
4. Which update is correct?
A "We have many progress, and there is not many information about the reimbursement."
B "We have a lot of progress, and there is not much information about the reimbursement."
C "We have much progresses, and there are not much informations about the reimbursement."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- One question decides everything: can you count it?

GRAMMAR

Before you choose the word, count in your head: one center, two centers — yes, you can count it. One progress, two progresses — no, that does not exist. This one question gives you the answer every time.

StructureUseExample
how many · a fewFor things you CAN count: 1 center, 2 centers, 3 centers.How many centers are on track? · We have a few delays.
how much · a littleFor things you CANNOT count. You never say "two progresses".How much progress is there? · We have a little time.
a lot ofIt works for BOTH — your safety net. When you are not sure, use this one.a lot of centers · a lot of progress
some / anysome = you HAVE it. any = you ASK, or you do NOT have it.We have some good news. · Are there any updates? · There is not any news yet.
The list to learn by heartYou never count these words in English: progress, information, news, time, work, feedback. They have no plural, and they never take "a": never "an information", never "two progresses", never "many feedbacks".

Common mistake: "many progress" and "how much centers" — both are wrong, and both for the same reason: the wrong word for the wrong side of the question. The quick fix: count first. One center, two centers → you can count it → many / a few. One progress, two progresses? → that does not exist → much / a little. And the most expensive mistake of all: "an information". In English it does NOT exist. It is always some information — and the same goes for news ("The news is good", never "the news are").

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each real moment of the call with the Global team.

Marc opens the call: "So, how is it going?" The best way to start is:
A "Sorry, I have a lot of problems and my English is not good."
B "Here is a short update on the dermatology campaign. The first milestone is complete, and we are on track."
C "I am going to send you forty slides tomorrow."
He asks how many centers are active. You answer:
A "There is much centers active."
B "Eleven centers are active, and we are rolling it out in four more this month."
C "We have a little centers active."
You want to say there is a LOT of progress in the private market. The safe way is:
A "We have a lot of progress in the private market."
B "We have many progress in the private market."
C "We have many progresses in the private market."
Now the difficult part: there are two or three delays, and almost no information about the reimbursement. You report:
A "We have a little delays and there is not many information."
B "We have a few delays in the public market, and there is not much information about the reimbursement yet."
C "I am very sorry, we have many delays and I do not have an information."
Before you close the call, you want to know if he has news. You ask:
A "Do you have some updates from your side?"
B "Are there any updates from your side?"

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the situation below. There is no right or wrong answer — speak for two minutes, with no script.

It is Friday, and Marc Dubois opens the call with one question: "So, how is it going?" Give him the update on a REAL project of yours (atopic dermatitis, asthma or COPD), in this order: (1) open it -- "Here is a short update on..."; (2) the good news, and the things you can COUNT (how many centers are active, how many doctors your team is visiting, how many milestones are complete); (3) the things you CANNOT count (how much progress there is in the market, how much time you still have, how much information is still missing); (4) the problems -- say them honestly, and do not apologize for them; (5) ask him one question back ("Are there any updates from your side?"); and (6) close with the expression of the lesson: "So far, so good." Speak for two minutes. Do not read from a script.
