Lesson 02 -- Pre-class

Growing Up in Butantan -- From São Paulo to the Countryside

Telling the story of where you come from and why you left. Key words: to grow up, childhood, memory, to miss, crowded, rush hour, to commute, downtown, noisy, traffic jam, belongings, back then. Structures: past simple (the events) vs past continuous (the scene around them), joined by while and when.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

To grow up -- to spend the years of your childhood in a place
"I grew up in Butantan, in the west of the city."
Childhood -- the time in your life when you were a child
"My childhood was noisy, but it was happy."
Memory -- something from the past that you still remember
"My clearest memory is the smell of bread in the morning."
To miss -- to feel sad because someone or something is no longer with you
"I miss the bakery, but I do not miss the traffic."
Crowded -- full of people, with very little free space
"The bus was so crowded that I could not breathe."
Rush hour -- the part of the day when the traffic is heaviest
"I left home at six to avoid rush hour."
To commute -- to travel regularly between your home and your work
"I used to commute two hours a day, each way."
Downtown -- the central business area of a city
"Everything happened downtown, so everyone went there."
Noisy -- full of loud sound that makes it hard to relax
"The street was noisy from six in the morning."
Traffic jam -- a long line of cars that can barely move
"I was sitting in a traffic jam when I decided to leave."
Belongings -- the things that you own and take with you
"We sold half of our belongings before the move."
Back then -- at that time in the past
"Back then, nobody locked their front door."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Ana grew up in Butantan, a busy neighborhood in the west of Sao Paulo. Her whole childhood happened on one street, and back then it was full of children. It was noisy and it was crowded, but she loved it. Her clearest memory is her grandmother, who made bread every single morning. Later, as an adult, she commuted almost two hours a day. She hated rush hour. One afternoon she was sitting in a traffic jam downtown, and it was raining, and the radio was playing something she did not even like. She looked at the clock and she realized she had been in that car for almost two hours. That was the moment. Two months later they sold most of their belongings and left. She misses a few things about the city. She does not miss the noise.

1. Why does the text say "she was sitting in a traffic jam" and not "she sat"?
A It was already in progress, and it is the scene around the decision (past continuous).
B It happened only once and it is completely finished.
C It is something she still does every day.
2. "Two months later they sold most of their belongings" is in the past simple because:
A It describes the background of the story.
B It is a finished event, one step of the story.
C It is a routine she repeated every month.
3. The text says "she hated rush hour", never "she was hating". Why?
A Because the sentence is about the present.
B Because "hate" is a state verb, and state verbs do not take -ing.
C Because "hate" has no past continuous form in English.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Past Simple vs Past Continuous

GRAMMAR

How to tell a story: the events, and the scene around them.

FormUseExample
Past Simple
verb + -ed (or irregular)		The events of the story, one after another. Finished.I decided to leave. We sold the flat.
Past Continuous
was / were + verb-ing		The scene: something already in progress around the event.I was sitting in traffic. It was raining.
Negativedid not + verb · was not / were not + verb-ingI did not know. They were not listening.
QuestionDid + subject + verb · Was / Were + subject + verb-ingDid you grow up here? / Were you living alone?
while vs whenwhile + the long scene · when + the short event: "While I was driving, the phone rang."
State verbshate, love, know, want, believe stay simple in the past too: "I knew everyone" (not "I was knowing").
