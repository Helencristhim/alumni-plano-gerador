Chapter 3 -- Set & Move

Set a Time, Then Move It

Ask if they are free, then reschedule politely

Grammar Notice

Notice the Patterns

Which phrase asks for a time, and which one changes it?

"Are you free on Wednesday morning?"

"Would Thursday at three be convenient for you?"

"I am afraid we have to reschedule our call."

"Could we move it to Thursday instead?"

Grammar Rule

Ask for a Time & Move It

Use it forPatternExample
Ask for a timeAre you free on + day?Are you free on Wednesday morning?
Confirm it worksWould + day + be convenient?Would Thursday at three be convenient?
Announce a changeI am afraid we have to rescheduleI am afraid we have to reschedule our call.
Propose a new timeCould we move it to + new day?Could we move it to Thursday instead?

Key idea: to book a time, ask Are you free on + a day; to change it, say I am afraid we have to reschedule, then propose a new time with Could we move it to + a new day.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"Are you free in Wednesday? Could we move it for Thursday?"
"Are you free on Wednesday? Could we move it to Thursday?"

We say free on + a day (not "free in"), and we move it to a new day (not "move it for").

Dialogue

Moving the Appointment

P

"Hi Karen, the supplier wants to make an appointment. He has a slot on Wednesday morning. Are you free then?"

K

"Oh, there is a problem. Wednesday morning clashes with the board meeting. I am afraid we have to reschedule."

P

"No problem. Let's not cancel -- let's just postpone it. Could we move it to Thursday at three instead?"

K

"Thursday at three is convenient for me. Write to him politely. Say we are sorry for the change, and ask if that day works."

P

"I will write: I am afraid we have to reschedule. Could we move it to Thursday at three? Would that day be convenient for you?"

K

"That is clear and kind. You did not cancel, you just moved it, and you asked politely. Send the new invite. Great work, Percival."
