Lesson 17 -- Pre-class

The Reassurance Call -- Winning Back the Client

Making the reassurance call to David Chen, the key distributor left hesitant after the failed Halo launch. Key words: reassure, on track, deliverable, restore, win back, retain, reliability, rapport, follow through, interim, downtime, uptime. Structure: future perfect (By the March review, we will have retested every unit) and future continuous (This time next month, we will be running the pilot).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Reassure -- to remove someone's doubts and restore their confidence
"I will reassure David with dates, not adjectives."
On track -- progressing as planned and likely to hit the target
"The recovery is on track for the March review."
Deliverable -- a specific result you promise to produce by a set date
"Every line is a deliverable with a date."
Restore -- to bring something back to its former good condition
"By quarter-end, we will have restored full reliability."
Win back -- to recover a customer or their business after losing it
"I would rather win David's business back than start over."
Retain -- to keep a customer or employee rather than lose them
"If we deliver, we will retain the Meridian account."
Reliability -- the quality of working consistently without failure
"Reliability is the whole point for a sensor line."
Rapport -- a close, trusting working relationship
"We have good rapport, so he will hear me out."
Follow through -- to complete what you have promised to do
"He needs to see that we will follow through this time."
Interim -- temporary, covering the time until something is final
"The interim firmware is live while we finish the rebuild."
Downtime -- a period when a system is not working or available
"Downtime will be measured in minutes, not hours."
Uptime -- the time a system stays working and available
"We will be holding uptime above ninety-nine percent."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Izabel opens the reassurance call with David Chen, the distributor whose team lost money on the Halo failure. "Here are the dates," she says. "By the March review, we will have retested every unit, and we will have shipped the first certified batch to your warehouse." David asks about reliability. "The interim firmware is already live," she answers. "By the end of the quarter, we will have restored full reliability, and we will be holding uptime above ninety-nine percent." Then she explains the pilot. "During April, while your team evaluates the samples, we will be monitoring the sensors in real time and sending you a weekly report. Every line is a deliverable with a date." David has good rapport with her, and he would rather retain AVRT than start over.

1. "By the March review, we will have retested every unit." When is the retest finished?
A Before the March review — the future perfect means completed by that point.
B During the review itself.
C Some time after the review.
2. "During April, we will be monitoring the sensors." What does this tense show?
A An action finished before April.
B An action in progress at that future time — the future continuous.
C A habit she has now.
3. Why "will have restored" and not "will restore" for reliability by quarter-end?
A "By quarter-end" needs the future perfect: the action is complete by that deadline.
B Because "restore" is irregular.
C Because the sentence is a question.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Future Perfect & Future Continuous

GRAMMAR

Committing to what will be done by a deadline, and what will be happening at a point.

TypeStructureExample
Future perfect
finished by a deadline		will have + past participleBy March, we will have retested every unit.
Future continuous
in progress at a point		will be + verb-ingThis time next month, we will be running the pilot.
Time markersFuture perfect loves by + a deadline (by March, by then, by the end of the quarter). Future continuous loves a point in time (this time next month, during April, at 3 p.m.).
Watch outNEVER drop the participle: not "will have retest" but "will have retested". NEVER drop "be" in the continuous: not "we will running" but "we will be running". Do not use the future simple ("we will retest by March") when you mean "finished by".
