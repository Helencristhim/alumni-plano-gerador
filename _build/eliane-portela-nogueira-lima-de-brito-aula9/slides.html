Lesson 9

Passing It On

Reporting back what was said, after a meeting or a call

Chapter 1: The Debrief

From Telling Your Story to Passing It On

Last class you told your whole professional story in English. Today the skill is different: you were in the meeting, and a colleague was not. Your job now is to relay what everyone said — accurately, clearly, and without changing the meaning.

Chapter 1: The Debrief

Four Quick Prompts

Speak for about a minute on each. Imagine you are reporting back to someone who missed the meeting.

What did the supplier tell you on your last call? What did your team decide in the last meeting? What did a colleague ask you recently? What did someone recommend that you do?

Chapter 3

Reported Speech

How to report what people said — the backshift, say vs tell, and reporting verbs

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"The chair said that the proposal had been approved."

"He told me that the price was fixed."

"She explained that the checks were complete."

"They asked whether we could get back to them."

Direct wordsReported (one step back)
"It is fixed."He said (that) it was fixed.
"It has been approved."She said it had been approved.
"We will sign."They said they would sign.
"Can you help?"He asked whether I could help.
say vs tellShe said (no object) / She told me (+ object). Never "said me".

Reporting verb + (that) + the verb one step into the past. Use "tell" with a person, "say" without one. For questions, use "asked if / whether" and drop the inversion.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

She said me the news.
She told me the news.
He said the deal is signed.
He said the deal was signed.
They asked was the meeting long.
They asked whether the meeting was long.

Three rules in one slide: use "tell" with a person and "say" without; shift the verb one step back when you report a past statement; and drop the question inversion after "asked whether".

Chapter 4

The Handover Call

Two colleagues relay what was said in a meeting and a call

Dialogue

The Handover Call

D
Hi Eliane. Since I missed the board meeting, could you relay what was decided?
E
Of course. Let me recap. The chair said that the proposal had been approved, and she asked me to reiterate that it is confidential until Friday.
D
Good to know. Did anyone flag a concern?
E
Yes. The finance director pointed out that the delivery window was too long, and he recommended that I get back to the supplier this week.
D
Have you spoken to them yet?
E
I did, yesterday. They told me that the price had been fixed for a year, and the manager promised that the first shipment would arrive in March.
D
Excellent. Did you run the new timeline by the clinical team?
E
Not yet. He also disclosed that the factory was moving in the summer, so I want to loop them in before I give them a heads-up.
D
Perfect recap. Send me the notes, and I will get back to you with the full minutes.

He promised that the first shipment would arrive in March, and he disclosed that the factory was moving in the summer.

Self-Assessment

What I Learned

I can report what someone said with the backshift: "is" becomes "was", "has" becomes "had", "will" becomes "would".
I use "tell" with a person ("he told me") and "say" without one ("he said that").
I can report questions with "asked if / whether" and no inversion.
I know 12 relay words: relay, recap, paraphrase, reiterate, loop in, flag, heads-up, disclose, confidential, verbatim, get back to, run by.
I can hand over a meeting and debrief my team, relaying accurately what everyone said.
