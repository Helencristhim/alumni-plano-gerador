Synopsis -- a short summary of a paper: aim, methods, results and conclusion

Prediction -- what you predict and then test in your study
"Our prediction was that a higher dose would improve recovery." (Previsão)
Results -- what your study discovered and reports
"Our results have shown a strong effect." (Resultados)
Peer review -- when other experts read and check your work before it is published
"The paper passed peer review after two rounds of comments." (Revisão por pares)
Citation -- a reference to another study you build on
"Each claim needs a citation to support it." (Citação / referência)
Manuscript -- the written paper you prepare before it is published
"We finished the manuscript and sent it to the journal." (Manuscrito / artigo)
Submission -- the act of sending your work to a journal for review
"The submission is due at the end of the month." (Submissão / envio)
Journal -- a publication where research papers appear after review
"The paper was accepted by a leading medical journal." (Revista científica / periódico)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Patricia is preparing the abstract of a new manuscript. An abstract is short, but every sentence does one job. The first sentence gives the background. The second states the hypothesis: she predicted that a higher dose would lead to a better outcome. Then come the methods, written in the past simple, because they are finished: the team recruited ninety patients and measured their recovery over six months. After the methods come the findings, and here the tense changes. She uses the present perfect, because the findings are new and still matter today: the study has shown a strong association, and the team have found that the effect is significant. Every claim is backed by a citation. The register is careful -- there is no "I think" and no "maybe". Once the abstract is ready, Patricia will send the submission to the journal, and peer review will begin.

1. Which tense is used for the methods, and why?
A The past simple, because the methods are finished.
B The present perfect, because they still matter.
C The future, because they will be repeated.
2. Why are the findings in the present perfect?
A Because they happened a long time ago.
B Because they are new and still matter today.
C Because they are only a prediction.
3. What happens after the abstract is ready?
A The study is repeated from the start.
C The findings are deleted.
B Patricia sends the submission to the journal and peer review begins.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Past simple vs present perfect

GRAMMAR

Em um abstract, usamos o past simple para métodos terminados e o present perfect para achados que ainda importam. / In an abstract, we use the past simple for finished methods and the present perfect for findings that still matter.

Function / FunçãoStructure / EstruturaExample / Exemplo
Method / Métodopast simple"We measured recovery over six months."
Finding / Achadohas/have + past participle"The study has shown a strong effect."
Finding (we) / Achado (nós)have + past participle"We have found a clear association."
Negative / Negativohave not + past participle"We have not found any side effects."

Key point / Ponto-chave: Use o past simple para métodos terminados (we recruited, we measured) e o present perfect (has/have + particípio passado) para achados que ainda valem (the study has shown, we have found). Cuidado com o particípio: é has shown, nunca has showed. No registro acadêmico, evite "I think" e "maybe": prefira "the results suggest".
