Film
Under the Tuscan Sun (2003) — Diane Lane, in Tuscany

A woman arrives alone in Tuscany. Her life has fallen apart, she does not speak the language, and she buys an old house on a Wednesday because a bird did something on her head. Then everything goes wrong: the roof, the water, the workers, the words she does not have. You walked those roads — Amalfi, Sorrento, Positano, Capri, Tuscany, twenty-three cities. This is the same country, from the other side: not the tourist who visits it, the woman who has to live it, alone, without the language. And she manages. That is the whole film.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Frances tells her own story out loud all the time — that is your grammar, working in the wild. Every time you hear "I was..." plus an -ing verb, stop the film and say the sentence out loud after her. And watch her face in the scenes where she does not understand the Italian: that face is Lesson 11. She does not need perfect Italian to survive the day, and you do not need perfect English.

Podcast
Speak English Now — Episode 350: False Friends That Confuse Romance Language Speakers (A2-B1)

Georgiana made this episode for us: for people whose first language is Portuguese, Spanish, Italian or French. False friends are the words that look like ours and mean something completely different — and they are dangerous exactly because they look easy. She takes four of them apart: eventually, assist, sympathetic and constipated. Watch "eventually" closely: it does not mean "from time to time", which is what your ear wants it to mean. It means "in the end, finally" — and it is a storyteller's word. "Eventually, the plane took off." That is your lesson, in one word.

Tip: listen once with your eyes closed, no text — your ear before your eyes. Then read the free transcript while you listen again. And then think about the false friend from today's lesson, the one she does not mention: to realize. It does not mean "to do" or "to make something happen", which is what it looks like. It is the second when your head understands something. Say it out loud five times in a real sentence: "I was lining up when I realized..."

YouTube
BBC Learning English — 6 Minute Grammar: Past Simple and Past Continuous (free, no subscription)

Six minutes, one presenter, and exactly the two tenses you learned tonight — the long line and the scissors that cut it. She gives you the same rule from a different mouth, with different examples, and she says the sentences at normal speed so your ear can hear where "was" and "were" disappear. Six minutes is nothing: you can do this while the coffee is on the stove.

Tip: watch it twice. The first time, only listen — do not read anything. The second time, pause after every example and build your own sentence out loud, about a real trip: "I was walking through Positano when..." One thing to notice: she is British, and your material is American. The grammar is identical — only the music of the voice changes, and in Europe you are going to hear both. This is good practice for the ear.
