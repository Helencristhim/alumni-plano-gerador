What Were You Doing When It Happened? -- Telling Travel Stories

The alarm that did not go off, the taxi that broke down in a traffic jam, and the plane that took off without you. For ten lessons you learned to survive the day; now you learn to tell it. Key words: to realize, to rush, to take off, to break down, to line up, to go off, stuck, traffic jam, relieved, on time. Structure: past continuous for the long action that was already happening, plus past simple for the short event that cuts it -- "I was waiting at the gate when they announced the delay." Expressions: All of a sudden... / At that point... / I could not believe it.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to every word and read the example. These are the ten words of a travel day that goes wrong. Watch number one very carefully: to realize looks like a word you already know, and it is a trap. It is not something you DO with your hands -- it is the second when you UNDERSTAND something. And watch number six: when an alarm goes off, it does not go anywhere. It rings.

To realize -- the second when you suddenly understand or notice something
"I was lining up when I realized my passport was in the other bag."
To rush -- to move or do something very fast, because there is no time left
"We were rushing to the gate when they closed the door."
To take off -- when the plane leaves the ground and goes up into the air
"We were watching our plane take off. Without us."
To break down -- when a car, a bus or a machine stops working and does not go any more
"We were sitting in a traffic jam when the taxi broke down."
To line up -- to stand one behind the other and wait for your turn
"I lined up at the information desk and I waited twenty minutes."
To go off -- when an alarm suddenly makes a loud noise (it rings -- it does not leave)
"I was sleeping, and my alarm did not go off."
Stuck -- you cannot move and you cannot leave: both doors are closed
"We were stuck in that taxi for fifty minutes."
Traffic jam -- a long line of cars on the road, and nobody is moving
"There was a traffic jam on the highway, and we missed the flight."
Relieved -- happy and calm again, because the problem is finished
"They gave me a seat on the next flight, and I was so relieved."
On time -- not early and not late: at exactly the right minute
"The next flight took off on time, and I slept for three hours."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

Frank and his wife had a flight from Rome at eleven in the morning, so they had a whole quiet morning in front of them. At seven o'clock Frank was sleeping like a baby, and his alarm did not go off. It did not go off because he set it for seven in the evening. At half past eight his wife was shaking his shoulder and shouting his name, and that is how the day started.

They were laughing in the taxi at a quarter to nine, because two hours is a lot of time. Then the taxi stopped on the highway. A traffic jam. They were sitting in that taxi for fifty minutes, and they were not moving one meter. They were stuck. At that point Frank was still calm. Then, all of a sudden, the engine made a noise like a sick animal, and the taxi broke down -- right there, in the middle of the traffic jam. The driver was looking at the engine and Frank was looking at his watch, and it was twenty past ten. He could not believe it.

They walked on the side of the highway with two suitcases, and a bus stopped for them. At the airport, Frank was lining up at the desk when he realized his passport was in the other suitcase -- the one his wife was carrying. They were rushing to the gate when the woman there closed the door. And through the window they were watching their plane take off. Without them. The next morning they finally left Rome, and that flight took off on time. Frank says he was never so relieved in his life. His wife still tells this story at every dinner party, and she tells it better than he does.

1. "Frank was sleeping when his wife started shouting his name." Why "was sleeping" and not "slept"?
A Because sleeping is something you do every night, and past simple is only for one time.
B Because the sleeping was already happening. It is the long action, and the shouting is the short event that cuts it in the middle.
C Because it happened in the morning, and past continuous is the tense for mornings.
2. "They were sitting in that taxi for fifty minutes." What does the past continuous tell you here?
A That they sat down one time, at one exact second.
B That they are still sitting there today.
C That it was long, and it went on and on. It is the background of the story, not one quick fact.
3. "Frank was lining up when he realized his passport was in the other bag." Which action started first?
A The lining up. He was already in the line, and then, in one second, the understanding arrived.
B The realizing. He understood first, and then he went to the line.
C Both started at exactly the same second.
4. The text says "he realized", never "he was realizing". Why not?
A Because "realize" is an irregular verb and irregular verbs never take -ing.
B Because realizing is one second, not a line. You cannot be realizing something for twenty minutes -- your head understands, and it is done.
C Because it happened at the airport, and at the airport everything is past simple.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Past Continuous

GRAMMAR

In Lesson 4 you learned present continuous: "the flight is boarding" -- now. Today it is the same -ing, on an older day: "the flight was boarding" -- yesterday. You already know most of this.

FormUseExample
I / he / she was + verb-ingThe long action. It was already happening -- you did not start it in that second.I was waiting at the gate. · The agent was checking my passport.
we / you / they were + verb-ingSame thing, other people. We and they never take "was".We were having dinner. · They were rushing to the gate.
long + when + shortThe story. The long line, and the short event that cuts it in half.I was waiting at the gate when they announced the delay.
while + long, shortSame story, other door. After when comes the short one; after while comes the long one.While I was waiting, they announced the delay.
NegativePut not after was / were. The -ing does not change.We were not moving one meter. · I was not listening.
QuestionWas / were goes first. No "did" -- the was does that job itself.What were you doing when it happened? · Were you sleeping?
Verbs of the headrealize, know, understand, want: these are one second, not a line. They almost never take -ing.I realized my mistake. (never "I was realizing")

The one rule to remember: ask yourself which action was already going on. That one is the line: was / were + -ing. The one that arrives and cuts it is the scissors: past simple, from Lesson 2. And when is the hand that holds the scissors. Two traps: was alone is nothing (the -ing is not optional), and we and they take were, never "was".

Stage 3: Pronunciation

Speaking

Listen to the model, then record yourself. Take your time: listen twice before you speak. Two things to watch: in real speech "was" and "were" are tiny and weak -- say "I wuz waiting", not "I WAS waiting". The big word is the -ing one. And "could not" in a story is almost always "couldn't" -- one word, fast.

I was waiting at the gate when they announced the delay.
We were sitting in a traffic jam when the taxi broke down.
All of a sudden, my alarm did not go off and I was still sleeping.
At that point I realized my passport was in the other bag.
We were rushing to the gate, and I could not believe it.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer for these real moments in a travel story.

Somebody asks what happened on your trip. You want to start the story with the background. You say:
A "The flight was late. We waited. We arrived."
B "I was having a coffee at the gate when my phone rang."
C "I have a story about a flight and a coffee and a phone."
You and your husband were both at dinner when the alarm rang. You say:
A "We was having dinner when the alarm went off."
B "We were having dinner when the alarm went off."
C "We were have dinner when the alarm went off."
In one second, at the desk, your head understood that the passport was not with you. You say:
A "I was lining up when I realized my passport was in the other bag."
B "I was lining up when I was realizing my passport was in the other bag."
C "I lined up when I realized my passport in the other bag." (Where is the background?)
The taxi stopped working on the way to the airport, and you could not move for fifty minutes. You say:
A "The taxi took off and we were relieved."
B "The taxi broke down, and we were stuck in a traffic jam for fifty minutes."
C "The taxi went off, and we were lining up for fifty minutes."
Your plane left at exactly the hour on your ticket, not one minute late. You say:
A "The flight took off with a delay."
B "The flight was taking off on time." (It is one second, not a line.)
C "The flight took off on time."
You are telling the story of yesterday, and you want to say that your head could not accept it. You say:
A "The door was already closed. I could not believe it."
B "The door was already closed. I cannot believe it."
C "The door was already closed. I was not believing it."

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt. There is no right or wrong version. Speak for 60 seconds, with no script.

Twenty-three cities in Italy, and Paris, and London. Somewhere in all of that, something went wrong: a train, a bag, a taxi, a hotel, or a door that was already closed. Tell me that day. Start with the background -- where were you, and what were you doing when it happened? Then tell me what happened, what you did, and how it ended. Use "was" or "were" plus -ing at least three times, and use the spine: "All of a sudden...", "At that point...", "I could not believe it." And finish with this: on that day, who did the talking -- you, or somebody else? Next time, alone, what are you going to say? Take your time and do not read from a script.

Under the Tuscan Sun (2003) — Diane Lane, in Tuscany

A woman arrives alone in Tuscany. Her life has fallen apart, she does not speak the language, and she buys an old house on a Wednesday because a bird did something on her head. Then everything goes wrong: the roof, the water, the workers, the words she does not have. You walked those roads — Amalfi, Sorrento, Positano, Capri, Tuscany, twenty-three cities. This is the same country, from the other side: not the tourist who visits it, the woman who has to live it, alone, without the language. And she manages. That is the whole film.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Frances tells her own story out loud all the time — that is your grammar, working in the wild. Every time you hear "I was..." plus an -ing verb, stop the film and say the sentence out loud after her. And watch her face in the scenes where she does not understand the Italian: that face is Lesson 11. She does not need perfect Italian to survive the day, and you do not need perfect English.

Podcast
Speak English Now — Episode 350: False Friends That Confuse Romance Language Speakers (A2-B1)

Georgiana made this episode for us: for people whose first language is Portuguese, Spanish, Italian or French. False friends are the words that look like ours and mean something completely different — and they are dangerous exactly because they look easy. She takes four of them apart: eventually, assist, sympathetic and constipated. Watch "eventually" closely: it does not mean "from time to time", which is what your ear wants it to mean. It means "in the end, finally" — and it is a storyteller's word. "Eventually, the plane took off." That is your lesson, in one word.

Tip: listen once with your eyes closed, no text — your ear before your eyes. Then read the free transcript while you listen again. And then think about the false friend from today's lesson, the one she does not mention: to realize. It does not mean "to do" or "to make something happen", which is what it looks like. It is the second when your head understands something. Say it out loud five times in a real sentence: "I was lining up when I realized..."
