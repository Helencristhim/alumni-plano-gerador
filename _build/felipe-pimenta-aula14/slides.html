Chapter 4: The Code

STAR & Result Language

"I rebuilt the model, which took margin from minus three to plus six."

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I rebuilt the pricing model, which took margin from minus three to plus six per cent." (action → number)

"I cut the close from twelve days to five, which meant we reported a week earlier." (action → consequence)

"I renegotiated the covenant so that we kept eight per cent of headroom." (action → purpose)

"I disclosed the error early, resulting in zero audit adjustments." (action → outcome)

Every line above ends on a result, not a duty. What are the small linking words doing the work?

STAR stepWhat it doesLanguage
Situationset the scene in one line"There was a division losing money every month."
Taskname your job in it"My job was to find the root cause."
Actionone clean thing YOU did"So I rebuilt the pricing model over one quarter."
Result (the number)tie the action to a measurable outcomewhich took / which meant / so that / resulting in → "...margin from −3% to +6%."

The whole trick is the last row: use which took / which meant / so that / resulting in to link the action to a number. Situation and Task stay short; the Result is where the answer lands. A story with no number at the end is still just a job description.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I was responsible for the monthly reporting cycle."
"I cut the monthly close from twelve days to five, which meant we reported a week earlier."
"I managed a budget of two hundred million."
"I found two hundred thousand in overspend and stopped it in one quarter."
"I'm very good under pressure."
"The night we found the hole, I had a costed fix ready before the board met at nine."

A responsibility is invisible; a result is not. Turn every duty into one action you took, and land it on a number. Never let an adjective do the work a story should do.

Chapter 5: The Panel

In the Interview Room

One story. One curveball. No dodging.

Dialogue

A Story, Then the Curveball

N
Let's start with a competency question, Felipe. Tell me about a time you turned a struggling function around.
F
There was a division losing money every month. My job was to find out why, and the turning point was realising the pricing model was the root cause. I rebuilt it over one quarter, which took margin from minus three per cent to plus six.
N
Let me probe that. Plenty of people claim a turnaround. How do I know it was you, and not the market?
F
Fair question. Let me back it up with something tangible: the market was flat that quarter, and only the two products I repriced moved. I can quantify it — those two lines went from a nine per cent loss to breakeven, while the rest of the division was unchanged.
N
Good. Now a curveball: tell me about a real failure — and please don't give me "I work too hard."
F
I won't dodge it. Early on, I under-hedged an FX exposure and it cost us four hundred thousand. It was my call and my mistake. What I did next is the part I'm proud of: I built the hedging policy that has caught every exposure since — zero losses in three years.
Comprehension

About Nina

1. What kind of question does Nina open with?
A competency question — asking for a time he turned a struggling function around.
2. Why does she probe his first answer?
Because plenty of people claim a turnaround; she wants to know it was him, not the market.
3. What does she specifically ask him NOT to give as a failure?
A fake one — "I work too hard" — a strength dressed up as a weakness.
Listening

What the Head-Hunter Hears

A CFO head-hunter, on what wins the room. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does she say is the fastest way to lose her?
Reading your CV back to her — she already has it.
2. What does the weaker candidate do wrong?
He tells her what he was responsible for — oversaw a team, managed a budget — which tells her nothing about what actually happened.
3. What does she mean by "give me the film, not the job description"?
Tell the story of what changed, backed up by a tangible result, and quantify the ending.
Listening 2

The Question Everyone Dreads

An interview coach, on the failure question. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Why does he call the failure question "generous", not a trap?
Because it is the one place you get to prove you are honest, self-aware, and still standing.
2. What is the mistake most people make with it?
They dodge it — giving a fake failure, a strength dressed up as a weakness — and the interviewer stops trusting everything else.
3. What three moves does he teach?
Name a real failure in one sentence, quantify what it cost, and close on what changed afterwards — own it, size it, land on the lesson.
