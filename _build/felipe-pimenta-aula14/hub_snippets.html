Lesson 14 -- Pre-class

The CFO Interview -- Owning the Room

An interview does not test your CV; it tests whether you can make one year of it land, out loud, in ninety seconds, with a number at the end. Key words: to quantify, tangible, to back up, a turning point, a curveball, to think on your feet, to probe, to dodge, to rehearse, rapport, a competency question, a hypothetical. Structure: the STAR competency answer (Situation, Task, Action, Result) and result language -- linking an action to a measurable outcome with which took, which meant, so that, resulting in.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. Every example is a real line from a CFO interview -- this is how an answer sounds out loud, not how a CV reads on paper.

To quantify -- to put a hard number on a result, so it can be measured
"Don't just say you cut costs -- quantify it: by how much, over what period?"
Tangible -- real and measurable; something you can point to
"Give me one tangible result, not a list of responsibilities."
To back up -- to support a claim with evidence or a real example
"Any candidate can claim leadership; the good ones back it up with a story."
A turning point -- the moment a situation changed direction, in a story
"The refinancing was the turning point that saved the company."
A curveball -- an unexpected, difficult question meant to test you
"Then she threw me a curveball: 'Tell me about your biggest failure.'"
To think on your feet -- to respond well and quickly, without preparation
"A good CFO has to think on their feet when the board pushes hard."
To probe -- to ask deeper follow-up questions to test an answer
"A sharp interviewer will probe until the story either holds or falls apart."
To dodge -- to avoid answering a question directly
"If you dodge the hard question, the interviewer stops trusting the easy answers."
To rehearse -- to practise an answer out loud before the interview
"Rehearse your three best stories until you can tell each in ninety seconds."
Rapport -- an easy, warm connection between two people
"The best interviews feel less like a test and more like rapport."
A competency question -- a question that asks for a real past example of a skill
"Almost every CFO interview opens with a competency question."
A hypothetical -- a what-if question about an imagined situation
"She gave me a hypothetical: 'What if you found a hole in the accounts on day one?'"

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

The panel opened with a competency question: tell us about a turnaround. The weaker candidate listed his duties -- he was responsible for reporting, he managed a budget -- and said nothing about what he had changed. The stronger candidate told a story. There was a division losing money; her job was to find the cause; so she rebuilt the pricing model, which took margin from minus three per cent to plus six. Every claim she made, she could back up with something tangible she could quantify. When the panel began to probe and then threw her a curveball -- tell us about a failure -- she did not dodge it. She named a real one, said what it cost, and closed on what she built afterwards, resulting in a control that never failed again. She had rehearsed the story until she could tell it in ninety seconds, and she could still think on her feet when they pushed. Notice the shape of every strong answer: a situation in one line, one action that was hers, and a number at the end.

1. Why does the weaker candidate's answer fail, even though every sentence is true?
A He lists his responsibilities instead of telling one story with an action and a measurable result.
B He talks about a turnaround, which the panel did not ask for.
C He speaks for too long and runs out of time.
2. What does "which took margin from minus three to plus six" add to her answer?
A Nothing -- it just repeats the action in different words.
B It links her action to a measurable result, which is where the answer lands.
C It hedges the claim so she does not over-commit.
3. When the panel throws the failure curveball, what does she do?
A She dodges it with a fake weakness dressed up as a strength.
B She names a real failure, quantifies the cost, and closes on what she built afterwards.
C She refuses to answer and moves on to another question.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- STAR Competency Answers and Result Language

GRAMMAR

An interview answer is a story with a shape. STAR gives you the shape -- Situation, Task, Action, Result -- and result language is what links the action to a number, so the answer lands on something the panel can measure.

STAR stepWhat it doesLanguage
Situationset the scene in one line"There was a division losing money every month."
Taskname your job in it"My job was to find the root cause."
Actionone clean thing YOU did"So I rebuilt the pricing model over one quarter."
Result (the number)tie the action to a measurable outcomewhich took / which meant / so that / resulting in → "...margin from −3% to +6%."
The curveballown a failure, do not dodgename it · quantify the cost · land on the lesson

The test that never fails: read your answer back and ask -- where is the action, and where is the number? Situation and Task stay short; the Action is one thing you did; the Result is where you land, tied on with which took, which meant, so that, or resulting in. A story with no number at the end is still just a job description -- and the panel already has your CV.

Stage 2: Put the STAR Answer in Order

Order

Put the steps of a strong competency answer in the right order. Opening with a list of your duties -- and no action and no number -- is the mistake that loses the room.

?Describe the one action that was yours.
?Set the situation in one line.
?Land it on a measurable result -- the number.
?Name your task -- what you had to do.
?Link the action to the outcome (which took, so that, resulting in).

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the strongest interview answer in each situation.

"Tell me about a time you improved a process." The strongest opening is:
A "I was responsible for the monthly reporting cycle."
B "There was a close taking twelve days; I redesigned it, which took it to five."
C "I've always been good at improving processes."
The panel asks: "How do we know that result was you, not the market?" You:
A Back it up with something tangible: "the market was flat; only the products I repriced moved."
B "You'll have to trust me on that one."
C "It was a team effort, so it's hard to say."
"Tell me about your biggest failure." The strongest answer:
A "Honestly, I work too hard and care too much."
B Names a real failure, quantifies what it cost, and closes on what changed afterwards.
C "I can't really think of one right now."
A hypothetical: "You find a hole in the accounts on day one." You:
A "First I'd quantify it, then disclose it early, then fix the control -- in that order."
B "I'd wait and see if anyone else noticed."
C "That would never happen on my watch."
"Where do you see yourself in five years?" The answer that builds rapport:
A "In your job, ideally."
B "Leading finance for a business operating internationally -- which is exactly why this role matters to me."
C "I haven't really thought about it."

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt below. There is no right or wrong answer -- speak for 2 to 3 minutes, without a script.

An interviewer asks you to describe your biggest professional achievement. Answer it in STAR: set the situation in one line, name your task, describe the one action that was yours, and land it on a measurable result -- tie the action to the number with which took, which meant, so that, or resulting in. Then take a curveball: name a real failure, quantify what it cost, and close on what you built afterwards. Do not list your responsibilities, and do not dodge the failure.
