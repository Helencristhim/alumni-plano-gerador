Said and Done — Reported Questions

Retell the questions people ask with reported questions: ask + if/whether for yes/no questions, ask + a wh-word for wh-questions, statement order, no do/does/did, the verb shifts one step back and there is no question mark (she asked where I lived, he asked if I was ready), plus 12 words for asking and answering: inquire, query, wonder, raise, probe, look into, respond, reply, mention, point out, request, refer to.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Inquire — to ask for information in a formal or polite way
"She inquired whether the room was free." (indagar)
Query — a question, especially one asking for clarification
"He raised a query about the price." (dúvida)
Wonder — to ask yourself something because you are curious
"She wondered what the answer was." (perguntar-se)
Raise — to bring up a question, point or topic to discuss
"A fan raised a question about a sequel." (levantar)
Probe — to ask searching questions to learn more
"She probed why they made that choice." (sondar)
Look into — to investigate or examine something closely
"They promised to look into the issue." (investigar)
Respond — to say or do something in answer to a question
"He responded that he was not sure." (responder)
Reply — to give an answer back to someone
"She replied that she would think about it." (replicar)
Mention — to say something briefly, without full detail
"He mentioned that the launch was delayed." (mencionar)
Point out — to make someone notice a fact or detail
"She pointed out that testing took months." (apontar)
Request — to ask for something in a polite, formal way
"The host requested that he reveal a secret." (solicitar)
Refer to — to mention or speak about something
"She referred to his earlier answer." (referir-se a)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Mark retells an interview with a game designer. First, the host asked how the studio had started, and the designer explained it. Then she inquired whether the team had expected the success, and he replied that they were stunned. She wondered what the hardest part was, and he pointed out that testing had taken months. A fan asked if there would be a sequel, but the designer responded that nothing was confirmed. Notice that each reported question uses statement order (subject before verb), drops do, does or did, shifts the verb one step back, and has no question mark.

1. What word do we use to report a yes/no question?
A that
B if or whether
C what
2. What happens to do/does/did in a reported question?
A it is dropped
B it is kept at the start
C it moves to the end
3. Does a reported question take a question mark?
A yes, always
B no, it is really a statement
C only for wh-questions

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip — reported questions

GRAMMAR

Para recontar uma pergunta, o inglês usa a reported question: o verbo de relato (ask, wonder, inquire) + if/whether (para sim/não) ou a palavra interrogativa (para wh-), em ordem de afirmação. / To retell a question, English uses a reported question: the reporting verb (ask, wonder, inquire) + if/whether (for yes/no) or the wh-word (for wh-questions), in statement order.

Type / TipoDirectReported
Yes/No"Are you ready?"She asked if I was ready.
Yes/No (formal)"Did they win?"She asked whether they had won.
Wh-"Where do you live?"She asked where I lived.
Wh-"What does he want?"She asked what he wanted.

Key point / Ponto-chave: a reported question usa ordem de afirmação (sujeito antes do verbo), não usa do/does/did, o verbo costuma recuar um tempo (live → lived) e não leva ponto de interrogação. Use if/whether para perguntas de sim/não e a palavra interrogativa (where, what, why) para wh-questions.

Stage 2: Put the Steps in Order

Order

Listen first, then put the steps of building a reported question in the correct order.

?After that, you put the subject and the verb in normal statement order, with no inversion.
?First, you start with a reporting verb, like she asked, he wondered or they inquired.
?Finally, you shift the verb one step back and never add a question mark.
?Then, for a yes/no question you add if or whether, and for a wh-question you keep the question word.
?Next, you remove the auxiliary do, does or did, because it is not a real question.
