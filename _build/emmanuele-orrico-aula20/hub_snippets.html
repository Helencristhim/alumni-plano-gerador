Handling Questions in Real Time -- Responding Confidently When Global Asks the Unexpected

The slides are the easy part — the questions are the real test. This is the lesson of three small words: should (a recommendation), could (an option), and might (uncertainty). You use them to answer live when Global asks something you did not expect — and you learn the phrase that buys you a second: "That's a great question — let me think about that for a second." Key words: risk, recommendation, alternative, obstacle, approach, uncertainty, possibility, suggestion. New grammar: the modal verbs should, could, might (modal + the base verb).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. These are the eight words of a good answer — the risks, the options, and the recommendations.

Risk -- the chance that something bad could happen
"The biggest risk is a late approval."
Recommendation -- advice about the best thing to do
"My recommendation is to focus on the private market."
Alternative -- a different option you can choose
"One alternative could be to start in the Northeast."
Obstacle -- something that makes progress difficult
"The main obstacle is access in the private market."
Approach -- a way of doing or dealing with something
"They want a calm, clear approach."
Uncertainty -- the feeling of not being sure what will happen
"There is some uncertainty about the timeline."
Possibility -- something that might happen or might be true
"A late approval is a real possibility."
Suggestion -- an idea you offer for someone to think about
"I would suggest we start small — that is my suggestion."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Emmanuele presents Brazil's results to the Global team. The slides are ready and she knows the numbers. But then David asks a question she did not expect: "What is the biggest risk right now?" For a moment, her mind goes blank.

She remembers the plan: do not rush, buy one second. "That's a great question — let me think about that for a second." Then she answers with three small words. "We should focus on the private market" — that is her recommendation. "We could adjust the HCP targeting" — that is one alternative. "The rollout might take a little longer" — there is some uncertainty. Notice the shape: after should, could and might, the simple verb — should focus, could adjust, might take. No "to", no "s".

David is happy. Emmanuele did not need a perfect answer; she needed a calm approach. A short pause is not a weakness — it shows you are thinking. "I don't know" becomes "One option could be this, and another might be that." And that, in any language, is what confidence sounds like.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- should, could, might

GRAMMAR

Use the modal verbs should, could and might to answer questions with confidence. They all work the same way: the modal + the base verb (no "to", no "s"). Only the meaning changes.

WordUseExample
shoulda recommendation (the best idea)"We should focus on the private market."
couldan alternative / a polite option"We could adjust the HCP targeting."
mightuncertainty (possible, not sure)"The rollout might take a little longer."
The one shapeModal + the base verb: should focus, could adjust, might take. No "to", no "s".

Common mistake: do not put "to" after the modal, and do not add "s". Not "We should to focus", but "We should focus". Not "It might takes longer", but "It might take longer". To buy time before a hard answer: "That's a great question — let me think about that for a second."
