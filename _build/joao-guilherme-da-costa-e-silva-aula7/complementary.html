Lesson 7 -- Never Back Down: The Quarterly Project Review

Documentary
Challenger: The Final Flight (Netflix, 4 episodes · watch EP. 3 and 4)

This is your lesson pushed to the limit: schedule pressure against an engineer who takes a stance. On the teleconference the night before, the Thiokol engineers say do not launch; management asks them to reconsider; the next morning Challenger breaks apart. What decides the investigation afterwards is nobody's memory — it is the RECORD: who said, who claimed, who assured NASA that it was safe, who acknowledged the risk and who denied ever hearing it. Listen to the Rogers Commission reading the minutes back: it is pure reported speech, and every reporting verb is worth a career.

Tip: watch with English subtitles and write down 8 reported-speech sentences from the investigation ("they told us that...", "he claimed that...", "management assured them that..."). For each one, decide: does the chosen verb protect the speaker, or expose him? Then rewrite THREE of them with a different reporting verb and feel the weight change.

Podcast
The Big Dig (GBH) — Part 6: "The Up Down Charts" (~45 min, free)

The story of the largest urban infrastructure project in the United States — tunnels under Boston — and this episode is exactly your topic: for years the up-down charts shown in the meetings hid the real cost and schedule variance, until one disclosure pulls the curtain down. It is your whole lesson at the scale of billions: schedule slippage that nobody escalates, a steering committee kept in the dark, and the gap between what was said in the meetings and what the schedule actually showed. Native American English, real pace, journalistic storytelling.

Tip: listen ONCE without pausing. Then record 90 seconds, out loud, reporting the episode as if it were a set of minutes: what the managers claimed, what they never acknowledged, what the public was told and what the audit found. Use at least FOUR different reporting verbs.

YouTube
ALL the REPORTING VERBS for B2 First (FCE) — SMASH English (13 min)

The exact structure of the lesson, at the exact level, from the same channel as the video in Lesson 6. Every reporting verb with its PATTERN: assure somebody THAT + clause · undertake / promise / offer TO + infinitive · deny / acknowledge + -ing · accuse somebody OF + -ing. It is the pattern, not the meaning, that trips up Brazilian speakers: "they assured us to send the schedule" is wrong; "they undertook to send it" and "they assured us that they would send it" are right.

Tip: after the video, take the last real meeting you had with a supplier and write 6 sentences reporting what each side said — one with assured us that, one with undertook to, one with claimed that, one with acknowledged, one with denied and one with told us that. Put the DATE in front of every one of them. That is the summary email you are actually going to send.
