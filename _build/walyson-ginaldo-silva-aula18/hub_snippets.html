Small Talk Before Business -- Building Rapport

Making small talk to build rapport before a meeting starts. Learning open questions (How / What...?) instead of yes/no questions, and follow-ups (What about you?) to keep the conversation going. New vocabulary: small talk, rapport, icebreaker, common ground, weather, weekend, hobby, traffic, jet lag, host, guest, get along.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Small talk -- light, friendly conversation about easy topics, not business
"We made some small talk about the weather before the meeting." (Conversa informal)
Rapport -- a good, friendly connection and trust between people
"A little small talk helps you build rapport with a new client." (Sintonia / Boa relação)
Icebreaker -- a question or comment that starts a friendly conversation
"The flight is a good icebreaker: How was your trip?" (Quebra-gelo)
Common ground -- an interest or experience that two people share
"We both like hiking, so we found common ground quickly." (Interesse em comum)
Weather -- if it is sunny, rainy, hot or cold -- a safe small-talk topic
"The weather is beautiful today, isn't it?" (Tempo / Clima)
Weekend -- Saturday and Sunday, when most people do not work
"How was your weekend? Did you do anything nice?" (Fim de semana)
Hobby -- an activity you do for fun in your free time
"Hiking is my favorite hobby on the weekend." (Passatempo / Hobby)
Traffic -- the cars and buses on the road, often slow in a big city
"The traffic from the airport was heavy this morning." (Trânsito)
Jet lag -- the tired feeling you get after a long flight across time zones
"After the flight from Europe, I had a bit of jet lag." (Fuso horário / Jet lag)
Host -- the person who receives and looks after a visitor
"As the host, I welcomed Clara and offered her a coffee." (Anfitrião)
Guest -- the visitor who is received and looked after by the host
"Clara was our guest, so I made her feel welcome." (Convidado / Visitante)
Get along -- to have a good, friendly relationship with someone
"We get along well, so the meeting was relaxed." (Se dar bem)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the short text and answer the questions.

When a client like Clara arrives for a meeting, Walyson does not start with prices. As the host, he makes a little small talk first to build rapport. He breaks the ice with an open question: "How was your flight? Do you have any jet lag?" Then he chooses safe topics -- the weather and the traffic from the airport. To find common ground, he asks an open question about her free time: "What did you do on your weekend?" Clara says she went hiking, her favorite hobby. Walyson loves hiking too, so he follows up: "I love hiking. What about you?" They both relax, and Walyson knows they will get along before they even talk business.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Open questions & follow-ups

GRAMMAR

Para small talk, use perguntas ABERTAS (How, What, Why) que pedem uma resposta longa, e não perguntas fechadas (Do, Did, Was) que só pedem sim ou não. Depois de ouvir, use um follow-up para continuar a conversa. / For small talk, use OPEN questions (How, What, Why) that invite a longer answer, not closed yes/no questions. After you listen, use a follow-up to keep the talk going.

Closed / Fechada (yes-no)Open / Aberta (says more)
"Did you have a good trip?""How was your trip?"
"Do you like Santos?""What do you think of Santos?"
"Was your weekend nice?""What did you do on the weekend?"

Key point / Ponto-chave: perguntas abertas começam com How, What, Why, Where, When e pedem uma resposta longa -- ótimas para criar rapport. Depois de ouvir, continue com um follow-up: "What about you?" ou "Really? Tell me more." / Open questions start with How/What/Why and invite a longer answer; keep the talk going with a follow-up like "What about you?"
