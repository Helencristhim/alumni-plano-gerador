Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Twelve everyday words for natural conversation. Listen to each one and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Small talk -- light, friendly conversation about the small, everyday things in life
"We made small talk about the weather."
To chat -- to talk with someone in a friendly, relaxed way
"We chatted for a few minutes at the door."
Casual -- relaxed, friendly and not formal
"It was just a casual conversation, nothing serious."
Awkward -- uncomfortable or slightly embarrassing
"There was an awkward silence for a moment."
To interrupt -- to stop someone while they are speaking
"Sorry to interrupt, but I have a quick question."
To nod -- to move your head up and down to show you agree
"She nodded to show she understood."
To mention -- to say something briefly, without many details
"He mentioned that he was a little tired."
Brief -- short, using only a little time or a few words
"We had a brief chat before the meeting."
To leave out -- to not include something
"In fast talk, we leave out the obvious words."
To sum up -- to say the main points in a short, clear way
"To sum up, we agreed on almost everything."
Likewise -- in the same way; the same is true for me
"She was glad to help, and likewise, so was I."
To go on -- to continue talking or doing something
"Please go on, I am listening."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

I had a brief chat with my neighbor at the bakery. When she said she loved the coffee, I did not repeat the whole sentence; I just said, "So do I." She asked if the shop was open on Sunday, and I answered, "I think so," instead of the long version. She wanted a small cake, so the server asked, "Which one?" When she mentioned that she does not like big parties, I nodded and said, "Neither do I." We left the obvious words out, and the conversation felt light and natural.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Ellipsis & Substitution

GRAMMAR

To sound natural, English often leaves out words you can guess and replaces them with one short word. Learn the four short words below. After "so" and "neither" at the start, the verb comes before the subject (So do I, Neither can I).

Short wordReplacesExample
soa whole idea"Is it open?" -- "I think so."
one / onesa noun"Which cake?" -- "The small one."
do / does / dida verb phrase"I love it." -- "So do I."
neither / eithera negative idea"I cannot go." -- "Neither can I."

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the most natural short answer for each real situation.

A friend says, "I really enjoy quiet mornings." You feel the same. You say:
A "So do I."
B "So I enjoy."
C "I do so enjoy it."
A colleague says, "I have not tried that new cafe yet." You have not either. You say:
A "Neither have I."
B "So have I."
C "Me too have I."
At a shop, the server asks, "Which bag would you like?" You want the blue bag. You say:
A "The blue one, please."
B "The blue, please."
C "The one blue bag please one."
Someone asks, "Do you think the train is on time?" You are fairly sure it is. You say:
A "I think so."
B "I think it."
C "I so think."

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer.

Think about a friendly chat you had recently -- with a neighbor, a colleague or someone at a shop. Describe it in English. What small talk did you make? When did you agree? Try to use short answers instead of repeating whole sentences: "So do I," "Neither do I," "I think so," "The small one." Take your time and speak naturally.
