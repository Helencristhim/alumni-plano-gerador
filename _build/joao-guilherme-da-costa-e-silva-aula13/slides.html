The Distinction

Signed vs In Force

The revision you signed

"Revision C was current on the day the purchase order was placed, so we certified the units to it."

It felt safe. It was, for a while.

The revision in force

"An amendment had superseded revision C before the units shipped — so on delivery day, the certificate pointed at a document that no longer applied."

"Current" means current on delivery day.

When a contract stipulates a standard "in its current revision", current is a moving target. The question is never only "which revision?" — it is "current as of when?" That one word has settled — and started — a great many disputes.

Chapter 3: The Code

The Past Before the Past

"When we caught it, they shipped it"  →  "When we caught it, they HAD already shipped it."

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

By the time we caught the discrepancy, the supplier had already shipped the forty units.

The amendment had superseded revision C before the units left the factory.

When the audit began, the team had been following the superseded annex for three months.

We rejected the certificate because the wording had changed and nobody had flagged it.

Every sentence has two past moments. The action that came first takes had + past participle. One of them measures how long it had gone on, with had been + -ing.

StructureWhat it doesIn the report
had + past participleThe EARLIER of two past events — the past before the past. It fixes the ORDER, and in a dispute the order is the liability."By the time we flagged it, they had already shipped the units."
had been + -ingHOW LONG something had been going on up to that past point. It shows the problem was not new."They had been manufacturing to the old revision for three months."
by the time / before / when + PASTThese words set the LATER past moment; the earlier action then goes into the past perfect. Both clauses are past — the perfect just marks which came first."By the time the audit began, revision C had been superseded."
already / just / never / not yetalready = it had happened before then; never / not yet = it had not happened up to then. They live inside the past perfect."We had never received the amendment, and they had already closed the file."
What it sounds likeIn speech, had almost never survives as a full word: it collapses to 'd. we had becomes "we'd", they had been becomes "they'd been", it had becomes "it'd". The tense is carried by the PARTICIPLE at the end (shipped, superseded, changed), not by the little 'd at the front — so listen for the participle.
Meaning

Four Patterns, One Timeline

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"When we caught the discrepancy, the supplier already shipped the units."
"When we caught the discrepancy, the supplier had already shipped the units."
"By the time the amendment appeared, they were manufacturing to revision C for three months."
"By the time the amendment appeared, they had been manufacturing to revision C for three months."

Portuguese lets one simple past do the work of a whole sequence, so the ORDER quietly disappears. English marks it: the earlier event takes had + past participle, and a duration running up to a past point takes had been + -ing — never the plain past continuous.

Sound First

What You Read vs What You Hear

The participle survives. The little "had" does not.

You hear: "they'd-already-shipped'em"
"They had already shipped them by then."

You hear: "they'd-been-buildin' to it for months"
"They had been building to it for months."

You hear: "nobody'd-checked the portal"
"Nobody had checked the portal."

You hear: "by-then it'd-been superseded"
"By then it had been superseded."

The tense survives at the end, in the participle: shipped, building, checked, superseded. Stop hunting for a stressed had — it will not be there. Listen for the participle, and you will hear the order.

Comprehension

About Sabine

1. Which revision had the supplier certified the units to, and what had happened to it by the time they shipped?
Revision C — current when the purchase order arrived. But a December amendment had already superseded it before the first unit shipped, so they had certified to a revision that no longer applied.
2. What does Sabine admit her side had NOT done?
Nobody on the supplier's side had checked the portal, and they had not caught the amendment. She refuses to pretend they had caught it and stayed silent.
3. Where exactly had the amendment made its change, and why did both teams miss it?
It changed one provision in Annex D. The main clause had stayed the same, so both teams read the part they already knew and never opened the annex.
4. What does Sabine say she would rather do than argue?
She would rather fix the annex reference in the contract than spend six months arguing about whose loophole it was. She wants to agree on the timeline first.

Listening 2

Three Rules for the Fine Print

A standards advisor, thirty years of it. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does he say "current revision" really means, and why does it matter?
Current means current on the day of delivery, not the day you signed. A standard is not fixed — an amendment can supersede the revision you certified to without changing the number on the cover, so you must check which revision is in force before you cut metal.
2. Why does he tell you to read the annexes?
Because everybody reads the main clause and nobody reads Annex D, and Annex D is exactly where the committee hides the change. The devil is in the details, and the details live in the fine print at the back.
3. What is his rule for settling a dispute?
Reconstruct the sequence in the past perfect, event by event, on paper: who had done what by when, what had already happened before the inspector opened the file. Once the timeline is laid out honestly, liability stops being an argument and becomes a date.
