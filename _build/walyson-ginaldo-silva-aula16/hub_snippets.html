Negotiating by Email -- Offers and Counteroffers in Writing

Making and responding to written offers and counteroffers to negotiate the coffee price by email. Learning the second conditional (if + past simple, would + base verb) to bargain politely about a deal that is not agreed yet. New vocabulary: offer, counterproposal, threshold, justify, sweeten, firm up, subject to, lock in, match, settle on, final offer, withdraw.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Offer -- a price or set of terms that you propose to the other side
"Here is my offer: three dollars forty per kilo." (Oferta / Proposta)
Counterproposal -- a different offer you send back when you do not accept theirs
"I do not accept the price, so here is my counterproposal." (Contraproposta)
Threshold -- a limit you cannot cross; the lowest price you can accept
"Three dollars twenty is below my threshold." (Limite / Piso)
Justify -- to give a clear reason that explains why your price is fair
"My cost has gone up, so I can justify the new price." (Justificar)
Sweeten -- to add something extra to make an offer more attractive
"If you matched the volume, we would sweeten the deal with free freight." (Adoçar / Tornar mais atraente)
Firm up -- to make an offer or order definite and final
"If she raised the price, I would firm up the order today." (Fechar / Confirmar em definitivo)
Subject to -- depending on a condition; only valid if that condition is met
"This offer is subject to the harvest and to my director's approval." (Sujeito a / Condicionado a)
Lock in -- to fix a price or rate so it cannot change later
"If you accepted today, we would lock in the rate for the season." (Travar / Fixar)
Match -- to offer the same price, volume or terms as the other side
"If you covered the freight, I would match your volume." (Igualar / Equiparar)
Settle on -- to agree, after bargaining, on a final price or figure
"In the end we settled on three dollars thirty-five." (Chegar a um acordo sobre / Fechar em)
Final offer -- the last price or terms you are willing to give in a negotiation
"Three dollars forty is my final offer." (Oferta final / Última oferta)
Withdraw -- to take back an offer that you made before
"I cannot withdraw the offer before Friday." (Retirar / Revogar)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the email and answer the questions.

Dear Clara,

Thank you for your offer. Three dollars twenty is below my threshold, and let me justify it: my cost has gone up. Here is my counterproposal: if you raised the price to three forty, I would firm up the order today. To sweeten it, if you covered the freight, I would match your volume and we would lock in the rate. This offer is subject to the harvest, and I cannot withdraw it before Friday. If we settled on three thirty-five, I would be happy. Three forty is my final offer.

Best regards,
Walyson

1. Why does Walyson not accept the offer of three dollars twenty?
A It is below his threshold, because his cost has gone up.
B He does not want the deal at all.
C The harvest already failed.
2. What would Walyson do if Clara raised the price to three forty?
A He would withdraw the offer.
B He would firm up the order today.
C He would lower his volume.
3. What figure would make Walyson happy?
A Three dollars twenty.
C Two dollars eighty.
B Three dollars thirty-five, if they settled on it.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The second conditional

GRAMMAR

Usamos o second conditional para falar de um acordo imaginário, ainda não fechado, ao negociar. É mais educado que o first conditional. / We use the second conditional for an imaginary, not-yet-agreed deal when we bargain. It is more polite than the first conditional.

Part / ParteForm / FormaExample / Exemplo
If clause / Condiçãoif + past simpleIf you raised the price...
Result / Resultadowould + verbo base...I would firm up the order.
Meaning / Sentidoimaginário, não fechadooferta hipotética e educada

Key point / Ponto-chave: depois de if use o passado simples (raised), e use would só no resultado, com o verbo base (would firm up). Compare com o first conditional (aula 13): "If you order more, I will give a discount" (real) vs. "If you ordered more, I would give a discount" (imaginário, mais educado). / After if use the past simple; use would only in the result, with the base verb.

Order

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best sentence for each situation.

The buyer's price is too low. You want to make a polite, imaginary counterproposal by email. You write:
A "If you raised the price, I would firm up the order today."
B "If you would raise the price, I will firm up the order."
C "Raise the price now."
You want to make your offer more attractive by adding free freight. You write:
A "I withdraw my offer."
B "Let me sweeten the offer: if you matched the volume, I would cover the freight."
C "The freight is your problem."
You want to protect your offer with a condition and a deadline. You write:
A "This price never changes."
B "This offer is subject to the harvest, and I cannot withdraw it after Friday."
C "Take it or leave it whenever."
