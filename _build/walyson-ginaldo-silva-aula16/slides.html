Chapter 3 -- The Code

Grammar Discovery

The second conditional to bargain

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

What tense is in the orange "if" part, and what word follows it?

"If you raised the price, I would firm up the order today."

"If you matched the volume, we would lock in the rate."

"If she covered the freight, I would match her volume."

"If we settled on three thirty-five, I would be happy."

Grammar Rule

The Second Conditional

PartFormExample
If clauseif + past simple"If you raised the price..."
Resultwould + base verb"...I would firm up the order."
Meaningimaginary / not agreed yeta polite, hypothetical offer

Key idea: use the second conditional for a deal you have not agreed yet: if + past simple, then would + base verb. It is softer than the first conditional (aula 13). Compare: "If you order more, I will give a discount" (real, likely) vs. "If you ordered more, I would give a discount" (imaginary, more polite in bargaining).

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"If you would lower the price, I would buy more."
"If you lowered the price, I would buy more."

After if, use the past simple (lowered). Use would only in the result part, with the base verb (would buy).

The Counterproposal Email

NEW MESSAGE

ToClara Mendes, Trading Desk
FromWalyson Silva, Sucafina
SubjectCounterproposal -- Coffee Price and Volume

Dear Clara,

Thank you for your offer. Unfortunately, three dollars twenty is below my threshold. Let me justify it: my cost has gone up this season.

Here is my counterproposal: if you raised the price to three dollars forty, I would firm up the order today. To sweeten it, if you covered the freight, I would match your volume of twenty containers, and we would lock in the rate.

This counterproposal is subject to the harvest, and I cannot withdraw it before Friday. If we settled on three dollars thirty-five, I would be happy. Three dollars forty is my final offer.

Best regards,
Walyson Silva
