Aula 10 -- Pre-class

Difficult Conversations -- Handling Tension with Care

Open a hard talk without triggering defensiveness, stay candid yet constructive, and protect the relationship. New words: candid, acknowledge, empathy, defensive, constructive, morale, own up to, defuse, assertive, clear the air, address, fallout. New structure: tentative / diplomatic language (I was hoping we could..., I was wondering if..., it would help if...) plus I-statements to soften a difficult message.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

The language of handling tension with care. Tap Listen to hear each word.

Candid -- honest and direct, even when the truth is uncomfortable
"I'll be candid: last quarter fell short of what we promised."
Acknowledge -- to accept and recognize a point, an effort, or a feeling
"She acknowledged his frustration before she explained the decision."
Empathy -- the ability to understand and share another person's feelings
"He led with empathy, so the conversation never turned into a fight."
Defensive -- quick to protect yourself when you feel criticized or blamed
"When feedback sounds like an attack, people get defensive and stop listening."
Constructive -- intended to help someone improve, not only to point out a flaw
"Good feedback is constructive: it points to a fix, not just a fault."
Morale -- the overall confidence, energy, and spirit of a person or team
"Handled badly, one hard talk can damage the whole team's morale."
Own up to -- to admit a mistake honestly instead of hiding or blaming
"He owned up to the missed deadline instead of blaming the tools."
Defuse -- to reduce the tension in a difficult or heated situation
"A calm question can defuse a tense moment before it grows."
Assertive -- confident and clear about your point without being aggressive
"Being assertive means stating your point firmly but respectfully."
Clear the air -- to resolve tension by talking about it openly and honestly
"They finally cleared the air and moved forward as a team."
Address -- to deal with a problem or issue directly
"It's better to address a small issue early than to let it grow."
Fallout -- the negative results that follow a conflict or a mistake
"Avoiding the talk only made the fallout worse later."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the prep note and answer the questions.

The Growth Manager had to address a drop in one team member's results, and he was dreading it. He knew that "You missed your targets" would make the man defensive and shut the talk down, so he chose a softer opening. "I was hoping we could look at the last quarter together," he said, and then, "Before I say anything, how do you see it?" Leading with a question and a little empathy helped defuse the tension. The manager stayed candid about the gap — pretending it was fine would help no one — but he kept it constructive, talking about fixes rather than blame. He was careful to acknowledge the effort first. Because the conversation felt safe, the man owned up to two slips instead of defending himself. They agreed on one next step, which protected his morale and helped them clear the air. An honest, assertive approach meant they avoided the fallout a harsher talk would have caused.

B "I was hoping" means the conversation already happened in the past.
C "I was hoping" is the only correct way to start any sentence.
2. What does staying "candid but constructive" mean in the note?
A Avoiding the problem to keep things pleasant.
B Being honest about the gap while focusing on fixes, not blame.
C Telling the person exactly what they did wrong, as harshly as possible.
3. Which is the diplomatic way to raise a problem with someone?
A "You always let the team down."
B "I noticed a couple of slips — I was wondering if we could look at them."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Tentative / Diplomatic Language

GRAMMAR

Use tentative forms and I-statements to soften a difficult message.

GoalPatternExample
Soften a requestI was hoping (that) we could + base"I was hoping we could review this together."
Ask tentativelyI was wondering if you could + base"I was wondering if you could walk me through it."
Suggest gentlyIt would help if + past / Perhaps we could + base"It would help if we set one clear owner."
Own the messageI-statement: I noticed / I felt"I noticed the tone got tense — I'd like to reset."
Watch the traps: after "I was hoping we" / "I was wondering if you", use "could" (not "can"); "it would help" takes the base verb (would help, not would helped). Swap "you always / you never" for an I-statement. PT: linguagem diplomatica -- formas no passado e com "would" criam distancia e soam mais educadas ("I was hoping", "it would help"), mesmo querendo dizer algo no presente; use frases com "I" (I noticed, I felt) em vez de acusar com "you always".
