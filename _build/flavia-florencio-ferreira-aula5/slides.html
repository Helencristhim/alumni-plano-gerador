Chapter 3: At, On, In & the Clock

At, On, In & the Clock

Telling the time and prepositions of time

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"The meeting starts at nine o'clock."

"I have lunch at half past twelve."

"The workshop is on Wednesday."

"I read my emails in the morning."

Why at nine o'clock, but on Wednesday and in the morning?

UseWhenExample
atclock times, noon, nightat nine o'clock · at noon
ondays and dateson Monday · on March 15th
inparts of the day, monthsin the morning · in April

A time on the clock = at. A day = on. A part of the day = in. (But: at night.)

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"The meeting is in nine o'clock."
"The meeting is at nine o'clock."
"I start work in Monday."
"I start work on Monday."
"I have a break at the afternoon."
"I have a break in the afternoon."

Clock time → at. Day → on. Part of the day → in.

Chapter 4: A Call with London

A Partner Wants to Talk

The conversation begins

Dialogue

Scheduling a Call

G
Good morning, Flavia. Thank you for your time. Can you tell me about a normal day at your office?
F
Of course! I arrive at eight o'clock in the morning. I start with emails, and then I have meetings.
G
That is early! When do you have lunch, and when do you finish?
F
I have lunch at half past twelve. I take a short break in the afternoon, and I finish at six o'clock.
G
Perfect. I am in London, so we have a few hours of difference. Can we talk on Thursday, in the morning?
F
Yes! Thursday at ten o'clock is perfect. I write the appointment now — Thursday, ten in the morning.
Comprehension

About Greg

1. Where is Greg?
In London.
2. What does he want to know about?
A normal day at Flavia's office, and when she is free.
3. When do they agree to talk?
On Thursday, at ten o'clock in the morning.
