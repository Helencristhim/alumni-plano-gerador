Acknowledge -- to recognize an objection openly before answering it

Deal-breaker -- a point so important that without it there is no deal
"Our logo on the shirt is a deal-breaker." (Ponto inegociável)
Concession -- something you give up to move the deal forward
"I offered a small concession to keep the talks going." (Concessão)
Budget -- the amount of money available to spend
"The yearly price was too high for his budget." (Orçamento)
Reconsider -- to think about something again and maybe change your mind
"Would you reconsider if we added a category?" (Reconsiderar)
Guarantee -- a firm promise that something will happen
"I could not give a guarantee about exact numbers." (Garantia)
Finalize -- to complete the last details and make a deal official
"Shall we finalize the terms today?" (Finalizar / fechar)
Stick to -- to refuse to change your position on a point
"We stuck to our price and did not move." (Manter-se firme)
Sign off -- to give final approval to a deal or document
"He smiled and signed off on the contract." (Aprovar / assinar)
On board -- agreeing with and supporting the plan
"Once he was on board, the rest was easy." (De acordo / a bordo)
Trade-off -- giving up one thing in order to gain another
"A longer contract for a lower price was a fair trade-off." (Troca / contrapartida)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

When the sponsor raises an objection, Nilo never argues. First, he acknowledges the concern: "I understand your concern. That is a fair point." Then, to persuade, he uses the second conditional -- a soft, hypothetical offer. He says, "If we lowered the fee, would you sign for four seasons?" Notice the pattern: after if, the verb is in the past simple (we lowered), and the result uses would or could + a base verb (you sign) -- never "would" after "if". The past tense here does not mean the past; it makes the offer gentle and only imagined. When he reaches his deal-breaker, he protects it but trades a small concession. And when the sponsor is on board, he closes: "So, do we have a deal? Shall we finalize the terms today?"

1. Which sentence is the correct second conditional?
A If we lowered the fee, would you sign for four seasons?
B If we would lower the fee, would you sign for four seasons?
C If we lowered the fee, would you to sign for four seasons?
2. What should Nilo do first when he hears an objection?
A Argue and defend his price.
B Acknowledge the concern: "I understand your concern. That is a fair point."
C End the meeting immediately.
3. After "if" in the second conditional, which tense do we use?
A The past simple -- "if we lowered", "if you kept".
B "would" -- "if we would lower".
C The future -- "if we will lower".

Key point / Ponto-chave: para uma oferta hipotética, use If + passado simples (we lowered) e would/could + verbo base (you sign) -- nunca would depois de if.
