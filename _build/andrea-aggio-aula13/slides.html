Chapter 4: Relative Clauses

Relative Clauses

who · which / that · where · defining vs non-defining

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"We are an agency that specializes in luxury travel."

"The prospects who visit our stand get a free brochure."

"Patagonia, which is our flagship, attracts thousands of visitors."

Which word connects to a person, which to a thing, and which to a place? And why does one sentence use commas while the others do not?

FormUseExample
who / that (people)essential info, no commasThe team that runs our groups is here.
which / that (things)essential info, no commasA package that includes flights sells fast.
where (places)for locationsThis is the region where we specialize.
, which / , who (extra info)non-defining: commas, never 'that'Our turnover, which grew last year, is strong.

Relative clauses: use who for people, which/that for things, where for places. Defining clauses give essential information (no commas); non-defining clauses add extra information (with commas, and never 'that').

Language Focus

Describe the Company

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"We are a company who specializes in tourism."

✓ RIGHT

We are a company that specializes in tourism.

✗ WRONG

"Patagonia, that is our flagship, is popular."

✓ RIGHT

Patagonia, which is our flagship, is popular.

Use who for people and which/that for things. In a non-defining clause (the one with commas that just adds extra detail), you can never use 'that' -- use which for things and who for people.

Listening 2

How to Build a Pitch

A short business tip on describing your company with relative clauses. Sound first -- no text.

0:000:00
1. Which relative word do we use for people, and which for things?
Who for people; which or that for things.
2. When do we use commas in a relative clause?
In non-defining clauses -- when the information is extra, not essential.
3. What can you never use in a non-defining clause?
The word 'that' -- use which or who instead.
