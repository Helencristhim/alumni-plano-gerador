Chapter 3: The Code

Just · Already · Yet

Three words that tell the other person where you are in the story.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I have just landed in New York."

"I have already completed the online check-in."

"I haven't gone through customs yet."

"My flight landed at nine, and I took a cab to the hotel."

Where does each orange word sit? And why is the last sentence blue — no "have" at all?

WordMeaningPosition
justA few minutes agohave just + past participle
alreadyDone — often sooner than expectedhave already + past participle
yetNot now, but it is still cominghaven't + past participle ... yet (end)
Past SimpleYou say when, or you tell the sequenceI landed at nine and took a cab.

The result now → present perfect. The moment it happened → past simple.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I haven't yet checked in at the hotel."
"I haven't checked in at the hotel yet."
"I have landed at nine and I have taken a cab."
"I landed at nine and took a cab."

yet goes at the END of the sentence. And the moment you say a time — at nine, yesterday, last night — English switches to the past simple.

Detail

Result Now, or Moment in the Past?

If the sentence says WHEN, English uses the past simple

"My flight ___landed at nine fifteen." (the time is there)
"Don't worry, I ___have already landed." (a message to your family — the result)
"Yesterday I ___went through customs in four minutes."
"I'm at the green line. I ___haven't gone through customs yet."

Listening Strategy

When You Miss One Piece

Don't ask for the whole sentence again. Ask for the piece you lost.

Sorry, which belt was it? I didn't catch the number.
Let me check I understood: the room will be ready at three, right?
Could you spell the name of the street for me, please?
I'm sorry, could you say that again a little more slowly? It's been a long flight.
