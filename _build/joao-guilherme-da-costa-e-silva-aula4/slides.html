Three Ways to Open the Same Email

Rank them, say why, then click to compare

A. "As you are no doubt aware, this is the third time we are chasing the same document."
Passive-aggressive. The irony ("as you are no doubt aware") makes the reader defensive, and a defensive supplier gives you nothing. It reads as a complaint, not as a position.
B. "Sorry to bother you again, I know you are extremely busy, but if it is not too much trouble, could we maybe have an update?"
Over-apologetic. You are the client, and the deadline was a contractual obligation, not a favor. Apologizing for asking tells the supplier the date is negotiable.
C. "I am writing to record that the revised baseline schedule has now been outstanding for three weeks, and to put forward a recovery plan."
Diplomatically assertive. A fact, a date, and a proposal. No irony, no apology, nothing personal — and it survives being forwarded to a director.

The Distinction

Mediation vs Arbitration

Mediation

A neutral third party helps you reach your own agreement. Nothing is imposed, and you can walk away.

A conversation. The relationship survives it.

Arbitration

A neutral third party decides for you. The decision is final, and it is binding on both sides.

A judgment. Somebody loses.

This is why you always propose mediation first — and why naming arbitration too early tells the supplier the relationship is already over.

Chapter 3: The Code

The Word Order of Weight

"They have never missed three deadlines."  →  "Never have they missed three deadlines."

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"Never has the supplier missed three consecutive deadlines before."

"Not only did they fail to submit the schedule, but they also sent an incomplete revision."

"Hardly had we begun the factory acceptance test when a critical non-conformance was identified."

"Under no circumstances will we accept a fourth revision of the schedule."

Every sentence opens with a negative or limiting expression. Look at what comes immediately after it. Where did the subject go?

TriggerStructureExample
Never / Never before+ auxiliary + subject + verb (question word order)"Never has this package been three weeks behind the baseline schedule."
Not only ... but ... also+ auxiliary + subject + BASE verb. With no other auxiliary, use did."Not only did they miss the date, but they also sent the wrong revision."
Hardly / Scarcely ... when
No sooner ... than		+ had + subject + past participle. Two past events, almost simultaneous."Hardly had we begun the FAT when the connector failed."
Under no circumstances
At no time / On no account		+ auxiliary + subject. The strongest refusal in professional English."Under no circumstances will we waive the hold points."
Only after / Only whenclause + auxiliary + subject. The inversion lands on the MAIN clause."Only after the third delay did we invoke the penalty clause."
Why it mattersSame information, different weight. "They have never missed three deadlines" is a fact. "Never have they missed three deadlines" is a fact with a case behind it. It signals that you have read the contract — and it survives being forwarded to a director. Use it once or twice in an escalation email. Three times and it sounds theatrical.
Meaning

Four Patterns, Four Jobs

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Never the supplier has missed three consecutive deadlines."
"Never has the supplier missed three consecutive deadlines."
"Not only they failed to submit the schedule, but they also sent the wrong revision."
"Not only did they fail to submit the schedule, but they also sent the wrong revision."

After a negative opener, the sentence takes question word order — auxiliary before subject. And after did, the verb goes back to its base form: "did they fail", never "did they failed".

The Escalation Call

J
Thanks for making the time, Katrin. I will be direct. The revised baseline schedule is three weeks overdue, and it would be remiss of us not to flag that this is the third missed milestone on the package.
K
I understand. As I explained in writing, the strike at the port was outside our control. We consider this a force majeure event.
J
I have read Clause 14 carefully. The strike was announced six weeks in advance, so our legal team does not believe force majeure applies. Not only did the schedule slip, but no new date has been confirmed in writing either.
K
That is a strong position, João. Are you telling me you are going to invoke the penalty clause?
J
Under no circumstances do I want to start there. But I have to be honest with you: if the fourth deadline is missed, I am afraid we are left with no alternative but to invoke it.
K
And what would you like from us instead?
J
Let me put forward a proposal. You give me a recovery plan by Friday, with a date we can both defend, and we treat the minutes of this call as a binding agreement. If we disagree later, we go to mediation before anyone mentions arbitration.
K
I would rather bite the bullet now than end up in arbitration next year. You will have the plan on Friday, and I will confirm the date in writing.
Comprehension

About Katrin

1. How does Katrin explain the delay?
The strike at the port was outside their control. She calls it a force majeure event, and says she explained it in writing.
2. What does she ask João directly — and why is the question dangerous?
Whether he is going to invoke the penalty clause. A "yes" ends the relationship; a "no" tells her the date is negotiable. He has to answer both at once.
3. What does she call "a strong position"?
João's argument that force majeure does not apply, because the strike was announced six weeks in advance.
4. What does she finally commit to, and why?
A recovery plan by Friday and the new date confirmed in writing. She would rather bite the bullet now than end up in arbitration next year.

Listening 2

Before You Send That Email

Your contracts manager. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Why should you invoke a penalty clause late, and only once?
Because it tells the supplier the relationship is over and the lawyers are in charge. On the Line 2 package, the supplier stopped answering the phone.
2. What is the difference between mediation and arbitration?
In mediation, a neutral third party helps the two sides reach their own agreement and either side can walk away. In arbitration, the third party decides, and the decision is binding.
3. Why is a strike announced six weeks in advance not force majeure?
Because force majeure means an event nobody could plan around. An announced strike is a schedule risk, and the liability for it is contractual.
