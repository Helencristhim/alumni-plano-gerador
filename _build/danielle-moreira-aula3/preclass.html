Lesson 03 -- Pre-class

Speaking Like a Senior -- Executive Communication in 1:1s, OKRs and Strategic Alignment

Run a 1:1, read an OKR dashboard and lead a strategic alignment meeting in English: open with the number, put a proposal forward, disagree with a senior leader and close with one owner and one date. Key words: OKR, key performance indicator, cascading goals, deliverable, milestone, accountability, cross-functional, bandwidth, buy-in, to put forward, to push back, to move the needle, to cut to the chase, to bring about, to circle back to. Structure: mixed conditionals (a past cause with a cost today / a present condition with a past result) + executive discourse markers (Building on that... / My take on this is... / What I would like to propose is...).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the vocabulary your Canadian peers already use every day — what separates the person who reports the meeting from the person who runs it.

OKR -- one goal you can say out loud, plus the numbers that prove you reached it
"Our first OKR for Canada is one objective and three numbers. Nothing else."
Key performance indicator -- a number you watch all year to see whether the business is healthy
"Engagement is the key performance indicator I will be judged on in Canada."
Cascading goals -- goals that flow down: the company sets one, and every team writes its own version
"Without cascading goals, nine academic teams will write nine different strategies."
Deliverable -- the concrete thing you promised to hand over, on a date
"The onboarding journey is not an idea. It is a deliverable, and it has a date."
Milestone -- a checkpoint with a date on the way to the goal
"Give me one milestone, one owner and one date. That is the whole meeting."
Accountability -- owning the result in public, whether it worked or not
"A team that agrees with everything I say is not accountability. It is silence with good manners."
Cross-functional -- involving people from different teams who do not report to each other
"The group is cross-functional, which is exactly why nobody owns the date."
Bandwidth -- the time and attention a person or a team actually has left
"They do not lack commitment. They lack bandwidth, and those are different problems."
Buy-in -- the moment people stop obeying a plan and start wanting it
"Their buy-in is not a nice-to-have. It is the deliverable."
To put forward -- to formally offer an idea so that a group can decide on it
"What I would like to put forward is one owner and one date."
To push back -- to disagree with a plan, professionally, and say so out loud
"Please push back if you think the target is wrong. I would rather hear it now."
To move the needle -- to produce a change big enough to show up in the numbers
"Tell me which single milestone would actually move the needle this quarter."
To cut to the chase -- to give the point first and the story afterwards
"Let me cut to the chase: two of the four objectives are off track."
To bring about -- to cause a change, not merely to want one
"This is the one change that would bring about real accountability."
To circle back to -- to return, on purpose, to a point you left open
"Before we close, I would like to circle back to the onboarding deliverable."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

It is the last week of the quarter at Maple Bear Canada, and the OKR dashboard is not kind. Two of the four objectives are off track. The onboarding deliverable slipped by six weeks; the cross-functional group that was supposed to own it has met exactly twice; and the engagement key performance indicator is still marked as not measured, because there is nothing live to measure. If someone had owned that deliverable in the first quarter, the team would not be having this conversation today. If the goals had cascaded across the nine academic teams last year, every one of them would have its own version of the objective right now, instead of nine different interpretations of it.

The directors blame bandwidth. Claire, the VP, does not. Her reading is colder and more useful: nobody owns the date, so nobody carries the accountability for it, and a milestone without an owner is a wish with a calendar. What she wants on Monday is not a status report. She wants the one milestone that would actually move the needle, and a proposal to reach it. And she wants Danielle to push back on her if the target itself is wrong — because half the leadership team agrees with everything she says, and that is not accountability. That is silence with good manners. If Claire were not the kind of leader who asks to be contradicted, she would have lost this team a long time ago.

So Danielle opens with the number, not the story. She cuts to the chase: two of four, six weeks late, no owner. Then she puts forward one owner, one milestone and one date — September fifteenth. The academic directors will resist, and she wants them to resist in the room rather than after it, because their buy-in is not a nice-to-have: it is the deliverable itself. That single change, she argues, is what would bring about real accountability. Before she closes, she circles back to the engagement number, and names the week it will finally be measured.

1. Why does "If someone had owned that deliverable, we would not be having this conversation today" use would not be having and not would not have had?
A Because the cause is in the past, but the result is now — and a present result takes would + verb, never would have.
B Because both halves of the sentence are in the past.
C Because the conversation can still happen in the future.
2. "If Claire were not the kind of leader who asks to be contradicted, she would have lost this team a long time ago." Which direction is this?
A A past cause with a present result.
B A present condition (this is how she is, permanently) with a past result — hence were + would have lost.
C A real condition about the future.
3. According to the text, what is the real reason the onboarding deliverable is late?
A The cross-functional group does not have the bandwidth to do the work.
B Nobody owns the date, so nobody carries the accountability for it.
C The engagement KPI was measured too late in the quarter.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Mixed Conditionals

GRAMMAR

The grammar of executive communication: the two halves of the sentence sit in different tenses, because the decision belongs to one time and the cost belongs to another.

FormUseExample
Direction 1 — condition
if + had + past participle		The decision that was NOT taken, back in the past. The past cause.If we had shipped it in Q1...
Direction 1 — result
would + base verb		The cost you feel today. The present result....we would be measuring engagement right now.
Direction 2 — condition
if + past simple / were		A permanent limit of the present. The present cause.If I were not leading two countries...
Direction 2 — result
would have + past participle		The consequence that is already closed, in the past. The past result....I would have delivered the diagnostic in July.
Negativehad not + past participle / would not be + -ingIf nobody had owned it, we wouldn't be here today.
QuestionWhere + would + subject + be + -ing + if...?Where would we be today if we had shipped it in Q1?
Discourse markersBuilding on that... (add to what was just said) · My take on this is... (give your own reading) · What I would like to propose is... (open a proposal) · To circle back to... (return to an open point)

Watch out (the mixed-conditional slip): if the result is happening now, the result half is would be — never "would have been". "If we had launched in Q1, we would have been hitting our KPI now" is wrong; the correct form is "we would be hitting our KPI now".

Watch out (the classic slip): would NEVER goes into the if half. "If I would be less busy" is wrong — the correct form is "If I were less busy". And in formal register it is always if I were, never "if I was".

Collocation: in English it is align on priorities (never "align in"), reach a milestone (never "make a milestone"), and explain something to someone (never "explain me the OKR").
