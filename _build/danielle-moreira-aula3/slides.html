Chapter 3: The Meeting

Five Moves That Run a Room

Open · Propose · Push back · Summarize · Close

Phrase Bank

Open, Propose, Push Back, Summarize, Close

OPEN

“Let me cut to the chase. I have one number, one risk and one proposal.”

PROPOSE

“What I would like to put forward is one owner, one milestone and one date.”

PUSH BACK

“Building on that, my take on this is different, and here is the number behind it.”

SUMMARIZE

“To circle back to where we started: the deliverable is late, and the owner is now named.”

CLOSE

“So we leave with one milestone and one owner. I will bring the numbers on Monday.”

Chapter 4: The Code

A Past That Is Still Costing You

if we had shipped it then · we would be measuring it now

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

“If we had shipped the deliverable in the first quarter, we would be measuring engagement right now.”

“If you had joined us a year ago, we would already have cascading goals in both countries.”

“If I were not leading two regions, I would have finished the diagnostic last month.”

“If the group had more bandwidth, they would have hit the milestone in June.”

The two halves are in different times. In the first two, the cause is in the past and the cost is right now. In the last two, the cause is permanent and the cost is already behind us.

DirectionFormExample
Past cause → present resultif + had + past participle, would + base verbIf we had shipped it in Q1, we would be measuring it now.
Present cause → past resultif + past simple / were, would have + past participleIf I were not leading two regions, I would have finished it.
Why it sounds seniorIt connects a decision nobody can change to a cost everybody can feel today. That is what an OKR review is for.
RegisterIn formal English it is if I were, never "if I was" — and in a Canadian boardroom that difference is heard.

In speech it contracts hard: If we'd shipped it, we'd be measuring it now. Train the contraction — that is what you will actually hear.

Register

Two Directions, Two Uses

Past cause, present cost

Use it to show what a decision is still costing the business today.

“If we had built the framework last year, we would be hitting our engagement KPI right now.”

Present cause, past cost

Use it to explain a limit you live with, without apologizing for it.

“If I were not running two countries, I would have delivered the diagnostic in July.”

Both sentences are polite. Neither one is soft. The first names a cost; the second names a constraint. A senior leader is the person in the room who can do both without blaming anyone.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"If we had launched the framework in Q1, we would have been hitting our KPI now."
"If we had launched the framework in Q1, we would be hitting our KPI now."
"If I would be less busy, I would have finished the report."
"If I were less busy, I would have finished the report."

If the result is happening now, the result half is would be — never "would have been". And the if half never takes would: in the mixed conditional it takes had + past participle, or were.

Dialogue

The 1:1

N
Let's cut to the chase. Two of the four objectives are off track. Where do you want to start?
D
With the onboarding deliverable, because it blocks the other three. If we had shipped it in the first quarter, we would be measuring engagement right now instead of arguing about it.
N
The cross-functional group tells me they do not have the bandwidth. Six people, two meetings, no progress.
D
Then bandwidth is not the problem. Nobody owns the date, so nobody is accountable for it. What I would like to put forward is one owner and one milestone: a working onboarding journey by September fifteenth.
N
The academic directors will push back. They will tell you September is unrealistic, and two of them have been here longer than I have.
D
Then I want them to push back in the room, not after it. If they were not the people who will run this, I would not have asked for their names on the milestone. Their buy-in is not a nice-to-have. It is the deliverable.
