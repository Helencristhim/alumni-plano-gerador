Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

The language of goals and growth: aims, dreams and the energy to reach them. Tap Listen to hear each word. / O vocabulario de metas e crescimento.

Objective -- objetivo
a clear, specific result you are aiming to reach
"My main objective this year is to enter the European market."
Aspiration -- aspiracao / sonho
a strong hope or dream of achieving something important
"My aspiration is to build a brand people in Paris know."
Achievement -- conquista / realizacao
something difficult that you succeed in doing through effort
"Closing a deal in English is my proudest achievement."
Momentum -- impulso / embalo
the forward energy that keeps something growing once it has started
"We've got real momentum -- let's not lose it."
Sustain -- manter / sustentar
to keep something going steadily over a long period of time
"The challenge now is to sustain this growth."
Vision -- visao
a clear, confident picture of the future you want to create
"My vision is a brand known across Europe."
Breakthrough -- avanco decisivo
a sudden, important advance that solves a problem or moves you forward
"The real breakthrough was speaking without fear."
Ambition -- ambicao
a strong desire to achieve something important, with the drive to pursue it
"My ambition is to lead in three new markets."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read how Milton reflects on his year, then answer. Notice "used to" and "would". / Leia a reflexao do Milton e responda; observe "used to" e "would".

A year ago, Milton used to dread every English call. Before he picked up the phone, he would rehearse each sentence -- and still freeze. He used to apologize for his English every two minutes. Today, none of that is true. His proudest achievement was closing an investment in English, and the real breakthrough was the day English stopped being the wall and became the tool. Now he looks ahead. His objective is to enter Europe; his aspiration is a brand people in Paris know. To get there, he has to sustain the momentum he has built and grow the team. He has the vision now, and for the first time the ambition feels reachable.

A No -- "used to" shows it was a past habit that has changed.
B Yes -- he still dreads every call.
C It describes a future plan.
2. Which sentence correctly uses "would" for a past habit?
A "He would be nervous before every meeting."
B "He would rehearse each sentence before a call."
C "He would a brand in Paris."
3. What is Milton's objective for the year ahead?
A To apologize less on calls.
B To enter the European market.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Used To & Would

GRAMMAR

Use these forms to describe past habits and states that are no longer true -- perfect for reflecting on how far you've come.

FormUseExample
used to + verbpast habit OR past state, now changedI used to freeze on calls. / I used to be nervous.
would + verbrepeated past action only (NOT states)I would rehearse before every call.
didn't use to / Did you use to...?negative and question (drop the "d")I didn't use to speak up. / Did you use to...?
Common mistake: use "used to" -- not "would" -- for past states like be, have or feel (I used to be nervous, not "I would be nervous"); and drop the "d" in the negative and question (I didn't use to, not "didn't used to"). PT: use "used to" e "would" para habitos do passado que mudaram. "Used to" serve para acoes E estados; "would" so para acoes repetidas, nunca para estados. No negativo e na pergunta, tire o "d": "didn't use to", "Did you use to...?".
