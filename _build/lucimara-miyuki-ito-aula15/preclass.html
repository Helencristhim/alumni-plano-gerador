Lesson 15 -- Pre-class

Small Talk That Works -- Starting, Sustaining, and Exiting Casual Conversations

The cocktail reception at the trade fair in Lyon: how to walk up to a stranger, open with an icebreaker, find common ground, keep the conversation alive, and exit gracefully. Key words: small talk, icebreaker, common ground, networking, reception, to mingle, follow-up question, to wrap up. Grammar: relative pronouns (who, which, that, where) -- the small words that connect two ideas into one smooth sentence: a company that makes ingredients, the colleague who introduced me, a city where the food is great.

Vocabulary

Common ground -- a shared interest or experience that connects two people

Networking -- meeting new people to build useful professional connections
"The reception is really about networking, not selling."
Reception -- a social event where guests gather, usually with drinks and food
"The reception starts at seven in the main hall."
To mingle -- to move around a room and talk with many different people
"After a few minutes, I moved on to mingle with other guests."

"Where in Lyon? is a good follow-up question."
To wrap up -- to bring a conversation, meeting, or talk to a polite end
"It was great meeting you is a warm way to wrap up."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

Tonight is the welcome reception at the trade fair in Lyon, and Lucimara knows almost no one. She takes a breath and walks up to a man who is standing alone. Her icebreaker is simple: "Hi, I don't think we've met. What brings you here?"

In a minute they find common ground. He is a buyer who sources ingredients; she runs a company that makes the very ingredients which go into his products. She keeps the small talk going with a follow-up question: "Lyon is a city where the food is amazing -- have you tried it yet?"

Notice the little words that do all the work: who for a person, that or which for a thing or a company, and where for a place. Each one glues a second idea onto a noun, so she says it all in one smooth line instead of two short ones.

When it is time to move on, she wraps up warmly: "It was great meeting you -- let's stay in touch." Then she goes off to mingle. A year ago she would have stayed silent in the corner; tonight she is doing real networking, in English.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Relative Pronouns (who, which, that, where)

GRAMMAR

A relative pronoun glues a second idea onto a noun, so you can say it all in one smooth sentence. Choose the pronoun by what comes right before it: a person, a thing, or a place.

PronounUse it forExample
whopeoplethe colleague who introduced me
which / thatthings, companiesa company that makes ingredients
whereplacesa city where the food is great
The one ideaThe pronoun links a noun to more information about it — one smooth line instead of two short, choppy ones.

Watch out -- the classic mistakes: in Portuguese one word (que) does everything, but in English you choose. For a person, use who, never "which". For a thing or a company, use that or which, never "what". For a place, use where, not "that". And do not repeat the subject: the buyer who he sat next to me should be the buyer who sat next to me.
