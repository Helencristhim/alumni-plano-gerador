Chapter 3: The Code

"The Company That Makes..."

Relative pronouns — the small words that connect two ideas into one natural sentence.

Grammar Discovery

One Word That Joins Two Ideas

That is the colleague who introduced me to our supplier.

I work for a company that makes chemical ingredients.

We make ingredients which go into shampoos and creams.

Lyon is a city where the food is absolutely incredible.

Each orange word connects two ideas. Look at what comes right before it — a person, a thing, or a place?

PronounUse it forExample
whopeoplethe colleague who introduced me
which / thatthings, companiesa company that makes ingredients
whereplacesa city where the food is great
The one ideaA relative pronoun glues a second idea onto a noun, so you say it all in one smooth line instead of two short, choppy ones.

One trap: for a person use who, never "which"; and for a thing use that or which, never "what".

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I work for a company what makes ingredients."
"I work for a company that makes ingredients."
"She's the colleague which introduced me."
"She's the colleague who introduced me."

In Portuguese one word, que, does everything. In English you choose: that or which for a thing, and always who for a person — never what.

The Voice Message

A colleague leaves you a heads-up before the reception. Faster, more natural — harder than it sounds.

0:000:00

At seven, in the main hall, which is the big room where they had the opening ceremony.
2. Who is Sofia, and what does she do?
She's the woman who runs the cosmetics pavilion, and Camille will introduce her.

Because she'll exchange them all night while networking.
4. What topic does Camille suggest if the small talk feels hard?

Listening Strategy

Catch the Connector

In fast speech, the little connector word decides the meaning — catch it and the sentence opens up.

"She's the person who I told you about." — who = a person
"It's the main hall, which is next to the entrance." — which = a thing/place
"A company that makes ingredients for care products." — that = a company
"Lyon is a city where the food is amazing." — where = a place

Catch the connector, and ask yourself: person, thing, or place? The rest of the sentence follows.

Chapter 6: Your Turn

Open, Connect, and Exit

Guided > Semi-free > Free

Dialogue

A Stranger at the Reception

L
Hi, I don't think we've met. I'm Lucimara. What brings you here tonight?
M
Nice to meet you, Lucimara. I'm Mark. I'm a buyer — I source ingredients for a cosmetics brand back in the States. And you?
L
Oh, that's common ground, then. I'm on the other side of that — I run a company that makes the ingredients buyers like you look for.
M
No way — that's perfect timing. What kind of ingredients does your company make?
L
We're a company that produces chemical ingredients which go into shampoos, creams, and lotions.
M
That's exactly what I'm here to find. Is this your first time at the Lyon fair?
L
It is, and I love it already. Lyon is a city where the food is incredible — have you tried it yet?
M
Not yet, but everyone keeps telling me the same thing. Look, I'd really like to continue this — do you have a card?
L
Of course, here you go. It was great meeting you, Mark — let's stay in touch. I'm going to mingle a little, but let's talk soon.
M
Likewise — I'll email you next week. Enjoy the reception, Lucimara!
Comprehension

What Did Mark Say?

1. What is Mark's job, and who does he work for?
He's a buyer who sources ingredients for a cosmetics brand in the United States.
2. Why was meeting Lucimara good timing for him?
Because he's there to find exactly the kind of ingredients her company makes.
3. What did Mark ask for before they said goodbye?
He asked for her business card, so they could continue the conversation.
4. How did Mark say he would follow up?
He said he would email her next week.
