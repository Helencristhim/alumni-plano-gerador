Chapter 2 -- Summary Words

The Anatomy of a Briefing

Eight words every report writer needs

Vocabulary -- Part 1

Why, What and How Far

Why the report exists
Background
The context that explains why the report or project exists
"One line of background tells the reader why this report was written."
What the report set out to answer
Objective
The specific question or goal the report sets out to address
"The objective was to assess the risk of entering a new market."
What the report discovered
Findings
The main facts or results that a report discovered
"The findings show that demand was higher than expected."
How wide the report reaches
Scope
The boundaries of what a report or project covers
"The scope of this review is the last quarter only."

The action the report advises

Chapter 3 -- Impersonal Reporting

The Result, Not the Person

How a briefing stays objective and concise

Discover the Pattern

Where Is the Person?

"Revenue was increased by twelve percent."

"The targets were met ahead of schedule."

"It was agreed that the budget would be approved."

"The decision to enter the market followed the analysis."

The Rule

Passive & Nominalization

ToolPersonal / longImpersonal / short
Passive voice"I increased revenue.""Revenue was increased."
Passive voice"The team met the targets.""The targets were met."
Nominalization"We decided to enter the market.""The decision to enter the market."
Nominalization"The team analyzed the data.""The analysis of the data."

Key idea: the passive voice (be + past participle) reports a result without naming who did it, so the summary sounds objective. Nominalization turns a whole clause into one noun ("we decided" → "the decision"), so the summary gets shorter. Together they make a briefing impersonal and concise.

Watch Out

Common Mistake

Revenue was increase by twelve percent.
Revenue was increased by twelve percent.

The passive needs the verb "to be" plus the past participle: was increased, were met, was approved. A bare base form ("was increase") is the most common slip in formal reporting.

Practice

Report It Impersonally

"The targets _____were met ahead of schedule." (passive)
"The budget _____was approved by the board." (passive)
"We decided to expand → the _____decision to expand." (nominalization)
"The team analyzed the data → the _____analysis of the data." (nominalization)

Did You Catch It?

Listening 1 Questions

1. Why does the board want an executive summary instead of the full report?

They do not have time to read the full forty-page report, so they need it boiled down to half a page.

2. What rule does Sarah give for a briefing's tone?

Keep it impersonal: write "revenue was increased", not "I increased revenue" — focus on the result, not the person.

3. What should the summary close with, and where is the detail?

One clear recommendation and the scope of what happens next; the full report is in the attachment.

Listening 2

How to Condense

0:00 / 0:00

1. What does nominalization do to "We decided to enter a new market"?

It turns the clause into one noun: "the decision to enter a new market" — one noun replaces a whole sentence.

2. Why does Eduardo use the passive voice for reporting?

In a formal summary he does not say who did what; it sounds objective and puts the result in front of the reader.

3. What does the reader know after thirty seconds?

The background, the findings, and the recommendation.
