Lesson 12 -- Pre-class

I Have Always Loved Traveling -- The List Is Yours

Twenty-three cities, Positano, Capri, Paris, London. The places have been yours for forty years -- tonight you get the two words that let you say them. Key words: abroad, cruise, landmark, square, castle, vineyard, countryside, coast, cliff, island. Structure: present perfect for the experiences of a lifetime -- have/has + the third form -- "Have you ever been to Italy?" / "I have never taken a cruise." And the one rule that decides everything: no date, or past simple. Expressions: Have you ever...? / No, I have never... / I have always wanted to...

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to every word and read the example. These are the ten words of the places of a lifetime -- and you have walked on four of them already. Two of them are traps for your ear, and they are worth your time: in castle the T is silent (say KA-sul), and in island the S is silent (say EYE-land). The letter is there. The sound is not. And watch number six: vineyard is not two words -- say VIN-yard, fast, one piece.

Abroad -- in or to another country, not the one you live in
"For forty years she had those places in her head and no way to say them abroad."
Cruise -- a holiday on a big ship that stops in a different city every day
"I have never taken a cruise. We prefer the trains."
Landmark -- the famous thing everybody knows a city by, and everybody photographs
"Go to the small towns too, not only the landmarks."
Square -- the open space in the middle of a town, with buildings all around it
"I have eaten in a square in Rome at midnight."
Castle -- a big stone building from hundreds of years ago, built strong to keep people safe
"I have slept in a castle in Scotland with no heating."
Vineyard -- a farm on a hill where they grow grapes to make wine
"I have never been to a vineyard. This is the first one in my life."
Countryside -- the land outside the cities: fields, hills and small villages
"Go to the small towns in the countryside, not only the landmarks."
Coast -- the part of the land that is next to the sea
"I have walked the coast at Positano."
Cliff -- a high wall of rock that goes straight down, usually into the sea
"Capri is an island, and the cliffs go straight down into the water."
Island -- land with water all the way around it: you arrive by boat or by plane
"I have seen Capri. It is an island."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

Matteo is fifty-eight years old, and his family owns a vineyard on a hill in the countryside, an hour south of Florence. His grandfather planted the first line of grapes in 1953 and never left that hill. He never went abroad, not one time in his life. He has been to Florence twice -- fifty kilometers away -- and he called that a long trip.

People ask Matteo the same thing every week: have you traveled? And he says no. He has never taken a cruise. He has never seen the coast of Ireland. He has never been on a plane -- fifty-eight years old, and not once. Everybody at his table has been on a plane, and today they are all standing on his hill. So, he asks them: who has really traveled?

Here is the part he likes best. Matteo has shown that vineyard to people from more than forty countries, and he has learned one thing from all of them: everybody asks the same first question. "Have you ever been to my country?" He always says no. And then they tell him about it -- the square in their city, the castle, the island, the food their mother makes. And for three minutes, he says, he has been there. Forty countries, and he has never left the hill.

1. The text says Matteo "has never been on a plane" -- it does not say when. Why is there no date?
A Because the writer forgot it. The date would fit perfectly in that sentence.
B Because this tense is about a whole life, not about a day. "Never" means zero times in fifty-eight years -- there is no date to give.
C Because planes are in the future, and the present perfect is the tense of the future.
2. "His grandfather never went abroad" -- but "he has been to Florence twice". Why does the text change tense in the same paragraph?
A Because the grandfather is old, and old people take the past simple.
B Because Florence is close and Ireland is far. Distance decides the tense.
C Both are experiences of a whole life, with no date. "Went" and "has been" can both do that job here -- and neither sentence tells you the day, which is the point.
3. "Everybody at his table has been on a plane." Which word would break this sentence?
A "yesterday" -- a date. "Everybody has been on a plane yesterday" is wrong. With a date you must say "went": "Everybody went on a plane yesterday."
B "everybody" -- it is too many people for this tense.
C "plane" -- you cannot use the present perfect to talk about transport.
4. The text says "he has shown that vineyard", never "he have shown". Why not?
A Because "show" is an irregular verb, and irregular verbs always take "has".
B Because it happened many times, and "has" is the plural one.
C Because Matteo is "he". I, you, we and they take "have"; he, she and it take "has". It is the only word that changes.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Perfect for Experiences

GRAMMAR

This is the one you told us was your gap. Look at how small it is: two words, and one rule about dates. And you already own half of it -- the middle column of every verb list has been yours since Lesson 2.

FormUseExample
I / you / we / they have + 3rd formIt happened at some point in your life. It does not matter when. It matters that it is in you.I have been to Italy. · They have seen the coast.
he / she / it has + 3rd formSame thing, other person. Only the small word changes -- the verb never does.She has seen twenty-three cities. (never "she have seen")
Question
Have you ever...?		Ever = at any time in your whole life. This is the question every traveler on earth asks you.Have you ever been to a vineyard? · Has he ever tried it?
Negative
I have never...		Never = zero times, in my whole life. Never is already the no: do not put a second no next to it.I have never taken a cruise. (never "I have not never taken")
The third formAfter have/has comes the third form, not the past simple. Five of tonight's ten are the same word twice.see → saw → seen. I have seen it. (never "I have saw")
THE RULE
no date		This tense and a date cannot sit at the same table. The moment a date arrives, switch to past simple (Lesson 2).I have been to Paris. · I went to Paris in 2022. (never "I have been to Paris in 2022")

The one rule to remember: the date is the switch. No date and you are talking about your life: have/has + the third form. A date walks in -- last year, in 2019, two days ago, on Monday -- and the present perfect walks out: past simple. In Portuguese one form does both jobs, and that is exactly why this feels hard. It is not you: it is two doors where your language has one. Two traps: he and she take has, never have; and after have comes the third form, never the past simple.

Stage 3: Pronunciation

Speaking

Listen to the model, then record yourself. Take your time: listen twice before you speak. Three things to watch. In real speech "have" almost disappears -- say "I've been", not "I HAVE been"; the big word is the third form. "Have you ever" runs together into one piece: "hav-yoo-EVer". And in number three, remember: KA-sul and EYE-land -- the T and the S are silent.

Yes, I have. I have been to Italy three times.
Have you ever been to a vineyard?
I have slept in a castle, and I have seen an island with cliffs.
No, I have never been, but I have always wanted to.
I went to Paris in 2022, and it was the best trip of my life.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer for these real moments at a table in Europe.

A stranger asks: "Have you ever been to Italy?" You have -- three times. What do you say?
A "Yes." (And then the conversation goes to somebody else.)
B "Yes, I have. I have been to Italy three times, and I have seen twenty-three cities."
C "Yes, I have been to Italy in 2016, 2019 and last month."
He asks: "And when did you go to Paris?" It was 2022. You say:
A "I have been to Paris in 2022."
B "I went to Paris in 2022, with my son."
C "I have gone to Paris when it was 2022."
You want to say that in your whole life you have not done something -- not once. You say:
A "I have never taken a cruise."
B "I have not never taken a cruise."
C "I have never took a cruise."
You are talking about your husband, and about Ireland. Neither of you has been there. You say:
A "My husband have never been to Ireland."
B "My husband has never been to Ireland, and I have never been either."
C "My husband has never been to Ireland last year."
He says he has one week in Italy and asks what he has to see. You have twenty-three cities behind you. You say:
A "I do not know. I am not from here."
B "Go to the south. I have been three times, and the coast is the best part -- not only the landmarks."
C "I have been going to the south in 2019 and it has been good."
The conversation is dying. You want to keep it alive, so you ask him something. You say:
A "Did you ever have been to Brazil?"
B "Have you ever went to Brazil?"
C "Have you ever been to Brazil?"

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt. There is no right or wrong version. Speak for 60 seconds, with no script.

You are at a long table in the countryside, with a glass of wine and eleven people you have never met. The man next to you asks the easiest question in the world: "Have you ever been to Italy?" Answer him -- and do not stop at yes. How many times have you been? Which cities have you seen? Tell him about the coast, the island, the square, the castle. Then tell him two things you have never done, and one thing you have always wanted to do. Somewhere in there he is going to ask you when -- and when he does, remember the switch: a date means "I went", not "I have been". Use "I have been to..." at least four times. Take your time and do not read from a script. Nobody at this table is going to help you, and nobody needs to.
