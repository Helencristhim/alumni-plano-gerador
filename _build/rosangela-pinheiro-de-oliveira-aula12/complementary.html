Film
Letters to Juliet (2010) — Vanessa Redgrave, in Verona and Tuscany

A woman of seventy goes back to Italy to look for a man she has not seen in fifty years. That is the whole film, and that is tonight's grammar with a face on it: everything she says about her life comes out as I have never forgotten, I have always wanted to, I have waited fifty years — because a life does not have one date, it has a list. Watch where they go: the square in Verona, the countryside, the vineyards, the small towns that are not landmarks. You have walked those roads. And watch who does the talking: the older woman, not the young one.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Every time Claire says "I have..." plus a verb, stop the film and say the sentence out loud after her — that is your grammar, alive, in a real mouth. Then do one thing for me: when the film ends, say her sentence with your own life in it. "I have been to twenty-three cities in Italy." Out loud, in your kitchen, to nobody. That is the exercise.

Adept English — Episode 755: Exploring the Amalfi Coast (free audio and transcript)

Twenty minutes on the Amalfi coast — the road, the houses on the cliffs, Positano, Conca dei Marini, and the ruins at Pompeii and Herculaneum. Hilary built this lesson to teach exactly one thing: the vocabulary for describing a place. That is tonight's ten words doing their job in the wild, and she says cliff and coast more times than we did in the whole lesson. And here is why this one, for you: you have been there. You walked Amalfi, Positano, Capri. So you are not learning a place — you are learning the words for a place that is already yours.

Tip: listen once with your eyes closed and no text — your ear before your eyes, the way you learn best. Then read the free transcript while you listen again, and mark every place she describes. Then the real exercise: tell your husband about Positano out loud, in English, using her words and tonight's structure — "I have walked that road. The cliffs go straight down." One note: Hilary is British and your material is American. The grammar is identical; only the music changes, and in Europe you are going to hear both.

YouTube
Oxford Online English — The Present Perfect Verb Tense (free, no subscription)

Two teachers, one whiteboard, and the tense you told us was your gap — explained slowly, from a different mouth, with different examples. This is the same rule you met tonight: have or has, plus the third form, and the date that changes everything. Watch it after the lesson, not before: it will feel like a review instead of a wall, and that is the difference between understanding it and believing you can use it.

Tip: watch it twice. The first time, only listen — do not read, do not write. The second time, pause after every example and build the same sentence with your own life in it: not "I have been to London", but "I have been to Positano". A tense you practice with somebody else's life never sticks. One note: they are British, like the podcast. The present perfect is identical in American English — only the accent moves.
