What Comes Next? -- Forecasting Brazil's Results for the Global Team

After you report the past, the Global team always asks for one more thing: the forecast. This is the lesson of will — the language of prediction. You use it to say what you expect, what you predict, and what you think will happen with the Brazil numbers: "Sales will increase", "It will stay steady", "We will have a small shortfall, but it will recover". Key words: projection, estimate, increase, decrease, predict, steady, peak, shortfall. New grammar: will for predictions and forecasts (with going to, the decided plan, in contrast).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. These are the eight words of a forecast — the numbers going up, going down, and the honest ones in the middle.

Projection -- a number you expect in the future, based on your data
"My projection for Q4 is a strong increase."
Estimate -- an approximate number, not the exact one
"This is only an estimate, not the final number."
Increase -- when a number goes up
"We expect an increase in the second half."
Decrease -- when a number goes down
"There was a small decrease last month."
Predict -- to say what you think will happen
"I predict a strong year for immunology."
Steady -- slow and regular, without big changes
"After the peak, the numbers stay steady."
Peak -- the highest point
"Sales reached their peak in September."
Shortfall -- when you have less than the plan
"We have a small shortfall in the public market."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

The Global team has the Q3 results. Now Marc asks the question they always ask: "What do you predict for Q4?" Emmanuele is ready. "My projection is positive. I think sales will increase by about eight percent." Marc wants more: "Will that be steady?" She answers with the same small word: "Yes, after the peak in September, the numbers will stay steady."

Then the honest part. The public market is below plan. She does not hide it: "We will probably have a small shortfall there — about five percent. But it will recover in Q1." Notice the word in every prediction: will. She uses will for what she expects from the numbers — not for a plan already decided (that is "going to": "We are going to launch in Q3"), but for what she thinks will happen.

Marc is happy. "An honest forecast is more useful than a perfect one." And that is the whole idea: the past results are the story, but the forecast — what will happen next — is the value. A manager who can say "here is where we are, and here is where I think we will be" is a manager the Global team trusts.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- "will" for predictions

GRAMMAR

This is the easiest grammar in English: will never changes, and the verb after it stays simple. You use will to say what you predict and expect about the future. Keep going to for the plan you already decided, and use will for what the numbers point to.

UseStructureExample
A prediction (+)will + base verb (same for I / you / it)"Sales will increase." · "It will stay steady."
Negative (−)will not (won't) + base verb"We won't miss the target by much."
Question (?)Will + subject + base verb?"Will Brazil reach the goal?"
will vs going togoing to = a plan already decided ("We are going to launch in Q3"). will = what you predict from the numbers ("I think it will be a success").

Common mistake: after will, use the simple verb — no to, no -s. Not "Sales will to increase" and not "It will grows", but "Sales will increase" and "It will grow". And for a prediction about the future, use will, never the present: not "Next quarter is better", but "Next quarter will be better".
