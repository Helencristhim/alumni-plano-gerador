Chapter 3: The Code

How You Used to Shop

A habit that was true then, and is not true now.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

I used to shop only in Brazil, but now I buy things abroad.

I didn't use to feel comfortable returning things in English.

There used to be a duty-free counter here, but they closed it.

Did you use to keep every receipt?

Every sentence talks about a habit that was true then, and is not true now. What is the pattern?

FormStructureExample
Affirmativeused to + base verbI used to shop only in Brazil.
Negativedidn't use to + base verb
(the -d disappears)		I didn't use to feel comfortable.
QuestionDid + subject + use to + base verb
(again, no -d)		Did you use to keep every receipt?
The one ideaused to = a habit or state that was true in the past and is not true anymore. It always contrasts then with now.

One trap to remember: the -d is only in the affirmative. After did / didn't, it is always use to.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I use to shop only in Brazil."
"I used to shop only in Brazil."
"Did you used to keep every receipt?"
"Did you use to keep every receipt?"

Two rules, and only two: in the affirmative, keep the -d — used to. After did / didn't, drop it — use to. The did already carries the past.

The Contrast

Used to vs Now

The whole meaning of "used to" lives in the gap between then and now.

"I used to pay full price. Now I wait for a markdown."
"I used to keep things that didn't fit. Now I exchange them."
"I didn't use to check the return policy. Now I read it first."

Your Receipt

Silk Scarf (final sale)$60.00
Subtotal$300.00

1. You want to return the jacket. Are you inside the return window, and what do you need?
Yes — the policy is thirty days with the receipt, and she has the receipt, so she is fine.
2. Which item on this receipt would normally be a problem to return, and why?
The silk scarf — it is marked "final sale," so normally it cannot be returned (unless it is defective).
3. Talk about receipts using "used to." How did you use to handle them?
Free answer — for example: "I used to throw my receipts away, but now I keep every one until the return window closes."
The Artifact

The Return Policy

SituationWhat you get
Item + receipt, within 30 daysFull refund to your card, or an exchange
Item, but no receiptStore credit only, at the current price
"Final sale" item (like the scarf)Cannot be returned by choice
Item is defective (broken)Full refund, no time limit, no restocking fee — even if "final sale"
Electronics returned openedRefund minus a 15% restocking fee
1. You have no receipt. What can you get, and at what price?
Store credit only — and at the current price, which may be lower if the item has been marked down.
2. The scarf is "final sale," but it arrived with a hole. Can you return it?
Yes. A defective item can be returned for a full refund with no time limit — that beats "final sale."
3. The scarf is defective. Ask the cashier to return it — polite, but firm.
"I know this was final sale, but it's defective — there's a hole in it. Your policy covers defective items, so I'd like a full refund, please."

Listening Strategy

Catch the Numbers

At the register, the money is in the numbers — and fast speech hides them.

There's a fifteen percent restocking fee. — fif-TEEN, stress at the end
It was fifty percent off on clearance. — FIF-ty, stress at the front
That comes to a hundred and forty dollars. — "and" almost disappears
You have thirty days to return it. — THIR-ty, not thir-TEEN

The whole difference between -teen and -ty is where the stress lands. Learn to hear the stress, and the price stops hiding.

Dialogue

Bloomingdale's, Returns Counter

N
Hi there, welcome to returns. What can I help you with today?
L
Hi. I'd like to return this jacket. The zipper is broken, so it's defective — and here's the receipt.
N
Oh, I'm sorry about that. Let me take a look. Yes, that zipper's completely stuck. Since it's defective, our return policy gives you a full refund, no time limit.
L
That's good to hear. Actually, I really liked the jacket — could I exchange it for the same one that isn't broken, instead of a refund?
N
Of course. Let me check the stock. We do have your size. And because it was defective, there's no restocking fee — the exchange is completely free.
L
Thank you. Honestly, I used to just keep things when they broke, because I didn't know how to return them in English. I'm learning.
N
Well, you're doing great. One more thing — I see you're in our loyalty program, so I'll keep your points on the exchange. Anything else?
L
Actually, yes. I also bought this bag, but I found the exact same one cheaper on another website. Do you do a price match?
N
We do, if it's the identical item. Show me the page? ... Yes, that's the same bag. I'll match it and put the difference back on your card.
L
That's wonderful — thank you. Let me just check I got it all: the jacket is exchanged, no fee, and the price on the bag is matched. Is that right?
Comprehension

What Did Nathan Say?

1. What did Nathan say the return policy gives her, since the jacket is defective?
A full refund with no time limit, because the item is defective.
2. Why was there no restocking fee on the exchange?
Because the jacket was defective — so the exchange was completely free.
3. What did he do about her loyalty points?
He kept her points on the exchange, since she is in the loyalty program.
4. How did he respond to her price-match request?
He agreed — it was the identical item, so he matched the price and put the difference back on her card.

The "Final Sale" Argument

Scenario: An outlet store. Your scarf was marked "final sale," but it has a hole — it's defective. The clerk first says no returns on final sale. Stay calm, but hold your ground: a defective item can be returned, even on final sale. Don't apologize, and don't give up at the first "no."

I understand it's final sale, but... it's defective — there's a hole your policy covers defective items I'd like a refund or an exchange could I speak to a manager?
