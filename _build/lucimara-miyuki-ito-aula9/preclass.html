Lesson 09 -- Pre-class

Shopping, Returning, and Negotiating Price -- Being Direct and Polite in American Stores

In Lesson 8 you were a guest at the table. Now you are a customer at the register — and the money is yours. Key words: exchange, defective, return policy, warranty, cashier, markdown, clearance, price match, restocking fee, store credit, loyalty program, size conversion. Grammar: used to — a habit that was true then and is not true now ("I used to shop only in Brazil." / "I didn't use to feel comfortable returning things." / "Did you use to keep every receipt?"). Direct, never rude — and no apologizing when the item is defective and you are in the right.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Take your time — listen twice, then say it out loud. These are the twelve words of a store counter: returning, exchanging, and getting the right price.

Exchange -- to give an item back and get a different one instead
"I'd like to exchange this for a larger size, please."
Defective -- broken or not working the way it should
"The zipper is broken -- the jacket is defective."
Return policy -- the store's written rules about giving items back
"Their return policy gives you thirty days with a receipt."
Warranty -- a written promise to repair or replace a product if it breaks
"The blender has a two-year warranty, so they fixed it for free."
Cashier -- the person at the register who takes your payment
"The cashier scanned the tag and told me the sale price."
Markdown -- a reduction in an item's price
"There was a fifty percent markdown on the coats after the holidays."
Clearance -- a section of heavily discounted items the store wants to clear out
"I found these boots on the clearance rack for almost nothing."
Price match -- when a store agrees to match a lower price from a competitor
"They did a price match once I showed them the cheaper website."
Restocking fee -- a charge some stores take when you return certain items
"There was a fifteen percent restocking fee on the returned TV."
Store credit -- money that can only be spent at that same store
"Without a receipt, they only gave me store credit, not cash."
Loyalty program -- a rewards plan that gives regular customers points or discounts
"Their loyalty program gave me ten dollars off after five visits."
Size conversion -- changing a size from one country's system to another
"I needed a size conversion -- a Brazilian 38 is a US small."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

Lucimara used to keep everything she bought abroad, even when it was wrong. If a jacket did not fit, it stayed in the closet with the tags on. She didn't use to know that she could walk back into the store and return it. Notice the shape: used to for the old habit, and the little word -d is there — used to, not use to.

This time is different. The zipper on her new jacket is broken, so it is defective. She reads the return policy first: thirty days with the receipt, but for a defective item, no time limit at all. She takes it to the cashier and says, calmly, "This is defective — I'd like to exchange it." Because it is broken, there is no restocking fee.

Then she notices a bag she bought is cheaper on another website. "Do you do a price match?" she asks. They do. She also has no receipt for a second item, so for that one they can only give her store credit, not cash. On the way out she passes the clearance rack, full of big markdowns, and remembers her loyalty program points.

A year ago, none of this would have happened. She didn't use to ask questions, and she used to pay whatever the tag said. What changed was not the stores — it was her. She learned the words, and she learned that a defective item is a right, not a favor.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- "Used to" for Past Habits

GRAMMAR

Used to talks about a habit or a state that was true in the past and is not true anymore. It always contrasts then with now.

FormStructureExample
Affirmativeused to + base verb (keep the -d)I used to pay full price.
Negativedidn't use to + base verb (no -d)I didn't use to check the return policy.
QuestionDid + subject + use to + base verb (no -d)Did you use to keep every receipt?
The contrastused to (then) + now + present simpleI used to keep things that didn't fit; now I exchange them.
The one ideaused to = a past habit that is not true anymore. The verb after it is always the base form — never -ing, never past.

Watch out -- the two classic mistakes: (1) dropping the -d in the affirmative: "I use to shop..." — it is always "I used to shop..." (2) keeping the -d after did / didn't: "Did you used to...?" — the did already carries the past, so it is "Did you use to...?". Learn those two, and your "used to" sounds native.

Stage 2: Return It, in Order

Order

This is how you return a defective item and get what you are owed — in the order that actually works, from stating the problem to confirming at the end.

?Say what you want: "I'd like a refund, or an exchange for the same one."
?State the problem clearly: "I'd like to return this — it's defective."
?Confirm at the end: "So the exchange is free, with no restocking fee. Is that right?"
?Show the proof: "Here's the receipt — I bought it yesterday."
?Hold your ground if needed: "Your policy covers defective items, even on final sale."

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer — speak for about 90 seconds, with no script.

Talk about how you used to shop, and how you shop now. Use "used to" and "didn't use to" at least twice each. Then imagine you are at a store counter with a defective item and no help on the screen: state the problem, ask for an exchange or a refund, ask about a price match, and confirm at the end. Be direct and polite — and remember, with a defective item you are not asking for a favor. Take your time and do not read from a script.
