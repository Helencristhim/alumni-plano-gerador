Film
Moneyball (2011)

Billy Beane and a young analyst build a data-driven proposal to a skeptical front office: a clear rationale, honest projections, and one value proposition that the room does not want to hear. It is Lesson 12 in action -- persuading with a plan, not a dream. Watch how they streamline the case until it is impossible to ignore, and listen for how often decisions are framed with "decide to", "aim to" and "worth trying".

Tip: after a scene, sum up Beane's plan in two sentences -- one with a verb + gerund ("He recommends signing...") and one with a verb + infinitive ("He decided to trade...").

Podcast
How I Built This with Guy Raz

Guy Raz interviews the founders behind iconic companies, and again and again they describe the plan they wrote to raise money: the executive summary, the value proposition, the projection, the first tight years before break-even, and the contingency that saved them. You will hear the vocabulary of Lesson 12 spoken by the people who lived it. Direct connection: writing a proposal that persuades an investor to say yes.

Tip: choose one episode, then write two sentences about the founder's plan -- one using worth + gerund ("It was worth investing in...") and one using aim + infinitive ("They aimed to reach...").

BBC Learning English -- "Gerund or infinitive? Ask BBC Learning English"

A short BBC Learning English clip that answers exactly this lesson's question: when two verbs come together, do you use the -ing form or to + verb? It explains the common verbs that take each pattern and how to remember them. Direct connection to Lesson 12: the one grammar choice that makes your proposal sound native.

Tip: pause after each example and say one sentence about your own proposal that uses the same pattern, out loud.
