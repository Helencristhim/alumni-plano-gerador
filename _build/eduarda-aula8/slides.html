Chapter 3 — The Code

First Conditional

Look at the purple words. What is the relationship between the two parts?

If we wait, we will lose the deal.
If the market improves, the company will recover.
If we act now, we will lead the deal.
If the deal fails, our capital will stay protected.
PartTenseExample
If-clause (condition)present simple"If we wait,"
Main clause (result)will + base verb"we will lose the deal."
Grammar Rule

First Conditional & Opinion Frames

StructureUseExample
If + present, ... will + basereal future result"If we wait, we will lose the deal."
In my view, ...state an opinion"In my view, we should act now."
The data suggests (that) ...present evidence"The data suggests the market will recover."
The bottom line is ...conclude"The bottom line is we should act."
Common Mistake

Watch Out!

"If we will wait, we will lose the deal."
"If we wait, we will lose the deal."

In the first conditional, the if-clause uses present simple. Will goes only in the result.

Speaking

Oral Drilling

1. State your opinion on the timing.
"In my view, we should act now."
2. Back up your opinion with evidence.
"The data suggests the market will recover next quarter."
3. Argue a consequence with the first conditional.
"If we wait, we will lose the deal."
4. Address the downside.
"The downside is limited: if the market falls, our capital is protected."
5. Push back on an estimate politely.
"I want to push back on that estimate. In my view, it is too low."
6. Give the bottom line.
"The bottom line is: if we act now, we will lead the deal."
