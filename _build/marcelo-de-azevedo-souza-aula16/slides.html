Chapter 4: The Code

Mixed Conditionals

Linking a past cause to a present result, and back again

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"If I had not gone, I would not be who I am now."

"If I were not so curious, I would never have gone."

"If she had studied medicine, she would not be a pilot today."

"If he were not afraid of flying, he would have visited us years ago."

Each sentence mixes time: a past half and a present half, in either order.

TypeIf-clauseResult
past → presentif + had + participlewould + base (would be)
present → pastif + past simple (were)would have + participle

Never use would in the if-clause: "if I had gone", not "if I would have gone". Use were (not "was") for an unreal present state: "if I weren't so cautious".

Language Focus

Which Type?

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"If I would have gone, I would be happier."

✓ RIGHT

"If I had gone, I would be happier."

✗ WRONG

"If I weren't curious, I would never gone."

✓ RIGHT

"If I weren't curious, I would never have gone."

Never put would in the if-clause — use had + participle. And a past result always needs would have + participle, never "would" alone.
