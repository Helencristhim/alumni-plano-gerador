Film
The Terminal (2004) — Tom Hanks, Steven Spielberg

Viktor Navorski lands in New York and, while he is in the air, his country disappears. His passport is worth nothing, he cannot enter and he cannot go home, and he lives in the airport terminal. This is the whole lesson, for two hours: a foreign traveler with almost no English, in a building full of people who say no, and he never shouts once. He asks, he waits, he asks again, and he learns the language at the same time. And that is the only reason he wins.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Do not copy Viktor's English at the beginning — it is broken on purpose. Listen to the officials instead: they speak clear American English and they are full of your three words. Every time you hear "you should...", "you could..." or "it might...", say the sentence out loud after them. And watch what Viktor does when they say no: he never raises his voice, and he never goes away.

Podcast
Speak English Now — Episode 345: Stop Apologizing So Much in English! (A2-B1)

This is the other half of your lesson, and Georgiana says it better than anybody: "Sorry, can I ask a question?", "Sorry, my English is bad", "Sorry for bothering you". She explains why this habit makes your voice small — and she is talking about polite, warm people, not rude ones. Pilar, the duty manager, told you the same thing from behind the desk: you are not bothering anybody. Saying sorry is right when you interrupt or when you are late. It is not right before every sentence you say in English.

Tip: listen once with your eyes closed, no text — your ear before your eyes. Then read the free transcript while you listen again, and mark every "Say this instead". Then do this out loud: make your complaint from the role-play again, from the beginning, and count how many times you say "sorry". The answer should be zero.

YouTube
Oxford Online English — Modal Verbs for Possibility: can, could, may, might (free, no subscription)

Two teachers, a quiet screen, and the half of today's lesson that is hardest for us: the maybe. Should is easy, because we have it in Portuguese. Could and might are the hard ones — the flight might be delayed, she might have a room, I could try the other desk. Nothing is certain, nobody is promising anything, and that is exactly what those two words are for. They speak slowly and they write every sentence on the screen while they say it. Same channel as your hotel and shopping videos.

Tip: watch it twice. The first time, only listen. The second time, pause after every example and say your own sentence out loud, about your own trip: "The flight might...", "I could...". One thing to notice: these teachers are British, and your material is American. The words are identical — only the music of the voice changes, and in Europe you are going to hear both.
