Lesson 09 -- Pre-class

Something Went Wrong -- Handling Problems at the Hotel, the Restaurant and the Airport

Eleven at night in Madrid, and the room you booked in March belongs to another family. Say what is wrong, give the facts, ask for the manager -- and never shout, and never say sorry for complaining. Key words: complaint, to complain, manager, double-booked, overcharged, damaged, missing item, to resolve, compensation, apology. Structure: should for advice, could for an option or a polite question, and might for a maybe. Expressions: There seems to be a problem with... / I was told that... / I would like to speak to the manager, please. Idiom: I agree / I disagree.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to every word and read the example. These are the ten words of a night that goes wrong. Watch number two: complaint is the thing and to complain is the action -- you MAKE a complaint, or you complain, but never "make a complain". And watch number eight: resolve is said re-ZOLV, with a Z.

Complaint -- the thing you say or write when something is wrong and you want it fixed
"I would like to make a complaint about my room."
To complain -- to tell somebody that something is wrong, because you want them to fix it
"I never complain, but tonight I have to."
Manager -- the boss of the hotel or the restaurant: the person who can say yes when nobody else can
"I would like to speak to the manager, please."
Double-booked -- when a hotel sells the same room to two different people
"I am afraid the room is double-booked, madam."
Overcharged -- when they made you pay more money than the real price
"I think we were overcharged: we did not order this wine."
Damaged -- broken: something on it does not work any more
"My suitcase is damaged. The wheel is broken."
Missing item -- a thing that is not there any more: it is not broken, it is gone
"I have a missing item: my camera was in room three one two."
To resolve -- to finish a problem, so that it is not a problem any more
"The manager resolved it in five minutes."
Compensation -- money or something free that a company gives you because they made a mistake
"They gave us a bigger room as compensation."
Apology -- the words 'I am very sorry', said by the person who made the mistake
"Thank you for the apology, and thank you for the room."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

It is eleven at night in Madrid, and Rosângela is at the front desk of her hotel with her suitcase next to her. She booked a double room in March. The receptionist looks at the screen for a long time, and then he says the room was given to another family this afternoon. The hotel is double-booked, and it is full.

She is tired, and for two seconds she says nothing. Then: "There seems to be a problem. I booked the room in March, and I was told that it was confirmed. Could you check it again, please?" She does not shout. The man checks again, and her confirmation is there, in the system. "You are right, madam. But the room is gone, and I am alone tonight. I do not know what I should do." -- "It is not your fault. But I am not going to sleep in the street. I would like to speak to the manager, please."

The manager comes. She might have a room on the fourth floor, she says, and if she does not, she could call the hotel across the street. In four minutes she resolves it: a bigger room, on the fourth floor, and breakfast for two as compensation. "Our apology, madam." The gentleman before Rosângela shouted at the desk for ten minutes. He is still standing there, and he has no room.

1. "Could you check it again, please?" Why "could" here?
A Because it is the past of "can", and she is talking about March.
B Because it is the polite way to ask somebody to do something. It is a request, not an order.
C Because she is not sure if the room exists.
2. The receptionist says: "I do not know what I should do." What does "should" mean there?
A The right next step -- he does not know which action is the correct one.
B A maybe -- he is not sure if something is going to happen.
C A polite request -- he is asking her for help.
3. "She might have a room on the fourth floor." Why "might" and not "has"?
A Because "might" is more polite than "has".
B Because the room is on the fourth floor and not on the first.
C Because she is not sure yet. Maybe the room is free, maybe it is not -- and she is not promising anything.
4. The man who shouted for ten minutes has no room, and Rosângela has a bigger one. What is the text saying?
A That you should never complain in a hotel, because it does not work.
B That complaining works -- but calmly, with the facts, and by asking for the manager. Shouting gets you nothing.
C That the hotel liked her because she was a woman.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Should, Could, Might

GRAMMAR

In Lesson 6 you learned would like, and in Lesson 8 you learned will. These three are from the same family, and they follow the same rule.

FormUseExample
should + plain verbAdvice: the right next step. Stronger than a maybe, softer than an order.You should ask for the manager. · We should keep the boarding pass.
could + plain verbOne option of many. And in a question, the polite way to ask for anything at all.I could try the other desk. · Could you check it again, please?
might + plain verbMaybe yes, maybe no. You are not sure, and you are not promising anything.The flight might be delayed. · She might have a room.
NegativePut not after the modal. The verb still does not change.You should not wait here. · The desk might not be open.
QuestionThe modal goes first. No "do" and no "does" -- the modal does that job itself.Should I ask for the manager? · Could you help me, please?
The verb after
is naked		No to, no -s, no -ing. And the modal is the same for I, you, he, she, we.She should call (never "should to call", never "should calls")

The one rule to remember: ask yourself how sure you are. Sure it is the right thing = should. One possible way, or a polite question = could. Not sure at all = might. And one trap: agree is already a verb, so it is "I agree" and "I disagree" -- never "I am agree".

Stage 3: Pronunciation

Speaking

Listen to the model, then record yourself. Take your time: listen twice before you speak. Two things to watch: in real speech "should" and "could" lose the L completely -- say SHUD and CUD, never shoo-LD. And "might be" often sounds like one word: MIGHT-bee.

Quiz

A "This is unacceptable! You lost my room!"

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt. There is no right or wrong version. Speak for 60 seconds, with no script.

Tell me about a real trip when something went wrong -- in Italy, in Paris, in Brazil, anywhere. What was the problem: the hotel, the restaurant, the airport, your luggage? Who complained -- you, or somebody else for you? What did they say, and did anybody resolve it? Was there an apology, or compensation? Then the important part: imagine it happens again next month, and this time you are alone at the desk, at eleven at night. What will you do, and what will you say? Use "should" for the right next step, "could" for your options, and "might" for the things you do not know yet. Finish with "I agree" or "I disagree": some people say you have to shout to be helped. Do you? Take your time and do not read from a script.
