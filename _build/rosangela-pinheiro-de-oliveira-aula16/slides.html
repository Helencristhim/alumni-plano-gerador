From Freezing to Describing

Walk Around the Missing Word

You already know two thousand words. But in every language, in every conversation, one word will go missing right when you need it. That is normal — it happens to people born speaking English too. The skill is not to have every word. The skill is to walk around the one you lost: to describe the thing until the other person understands. Today you get the toolkit that saves any conversation.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"It's a thing that you use in the rain."

"It's a place where you buy medicine."

"It's a person who cuts your hair."

"It's a small tool, used for opening a bottle."

Each sentence describes a thing without its name. Look at the green words: what comes after a thing, after a place, after a person? And what shape does the verb take after used for?

When you describe a...use...Example
thingthat / whichIt's a thing that you use in the rain.
placewhereIt's a place where you buy medicine.
personwhoIt's a person who cuts your hair.
its jobused for + -ingIt's used for opening a bottle. (never "for open")

Things take that. Places take where. People take who. And after used for, the verb wears -ing — just like after "enjoy" last time.

The Anatomy

Four Cards, One Toolkit

The Toolkit

Six Bridges Across the Gap

When the word is gone, you have six bridges. Pick one, and cross — the other person follows you every time.

Point at the family

"It's a kind of small tool."
"It's a thing that you use in the rain."

Say the job, and the shape

"It's used for opening bottles."
"It's made of metal, small and round."

And when you are still stuck, compare: "It's similar to a spoon," or "It's the opposite of hot." One bridge is always enough — you almost never need all six.

The Function Frame

It's Used for -ing

The fastest way to describe a thing is to say what it does. And after used for, the verb always wears -ing — the same shape as after "enjoy" last time.

It's used for opening bottles.

used for + verb-ing, never "used for open"

"It's used for cutting paper."
"It's used for cleaning your glasses."

You almost never need the name of a thing if you can say its job. Say it three times, and it stops feeling strange.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"It's used for open the door."
"It's used for opening the door." — after "for", the verb wears "-ing".
"It's a thing who you use in the rain."
"It's a thing that you use in the rain." — a thing takes "that", never "who".
"It's similar with a spoon."
"It's similar to a spoon." — always "similar to", never "similar with".

Three little checks: after "for", is it -ing? For a thing, is it that? And is it similar to? Answer those, and these cannot beat you.

Expression of the Day

Let Me Describe It

Sorry, I don't know the word — let me describe it.

= stay with me one second — I am going to tell you what the thing is like

This one sentence is the door. It buys you the two seconds you need, and it tells the other person: don't fill in the word for me — stay, and I will get there.
It is the opposite of going quiet. It is the moment you stop waiting for the word and start walking around it, out loud, by yourself.

Your turn, out loud: think of a thing whose name you forget — how would you describe it?

Listening

The Shopkeeper's Secret

Twenty years behind a counter, and the one thing he wishes every tourist knew. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does Marco see arrive on a tourist's face, and what do some of them do because of it?
He sees fear arrive. Some of them turn red, say "sorry, sorry", and walk out with empty hands -- they go home without the thing they came for, because they gave up too soon.
2. What do the clever tourists do instead? Give the two examples he uses.
They describe the thing. One says: "It's a small thing, made of metal, used for opening a bottle of wine" -- a corkscrew. Another says: "It's a kind of sticker, used for a small cut on your finger" -- a plaster. In one second, Marco understands.
3. In twenty years, why does Marco say nobody has ever left his shop with empty hands?
Never because their grammar was wrong. Only because they gave up too soon. He does not need you to know the word -- he needs you to describe the thing: what it's made of, what it's used for, what it's similar to, or the opposite of.
Listening 2

Walking Around the Hole

Dulce is sixty-nine, and for years one lost word could close her mouth for the whole day. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. For years, what happened to Dulce when a word disappeared in the middle of a sentence?
She would stop and go quiet. She looked at her hands, felt small and stupid, said nothing, and let the moment pass -- and somebody else spoke for her. She believed that talking meant knowing every word.
2. What did the young teacher tell her that changed her trips?
That she already knew two thousand words, and she would always be missing the exact one she wanted -- even in Portuguese. That is normal; even people born speaking English forget words and go around them. The trick is not to have every word; the trick is to never stop.
3. What does Dulce do now when a word disappears, and what are her examples?
She describes and keeps walking, like walking around a hole in the road. Lose "umbrella"? "The thing you open when it rains." Lose "pharmacy"? "The place where you buy medicine." She uses: it's a kind of, it's used for, it's made of, it's similar to.

Comprehension

About What the Shopkeeper Understood

1. What was the first thing she described, and how did she describe it?
A plug adapter. She said it was a small gadget, made of plastic, used for putting her Brazilian charger into the walls, because the holes are different there.
2. How did she describe the corkscrew, and what did Tomás say about her description?
A little tool, made of metal, used for opening a bottle of wine -- a kind of spiral that goes into the cork. Tomás said it was a perfect description, and he understood right away.
3. How did she describe the plasters?
A kind of small sticker for your skin, similar to a little bandage, that you put on a small cut on your finger.
4. What does Tomás say most people do, and why was she different?
Most people just give up. She described three things she could not name -- and she got all three, without knowing a single one of the words.

"Made of" gives the material (cloth and metal). "Used for" gives the job (keeping the rain off). Same object, described from every side.

"It's used for keeping the rain off." The verb wears -ing (keeping), because after "for" the verb always takes -ing -- never "used for keep".

Self-Assessment

What I Can Do Now

When a word disappears, I do not go quiet — I say "let me describe it" and keep talking.
I can say what a thing is used for: "It's used for opening bottles" — with -ing after "for".
I can say what a thing is made of and what it looks like: "It's made of metal, small and round."
I use "that" for a thing, "where" for a place, and "who" for a person — never "who" for a thing.
I can compare to be understood: "It's similar to a spoon," or "It's the opposite of hot."
