Lesson 16 -- Pre-class

Saying It Better -- When You Don't Have the Word

A small shop, three things you need, and not one name you know -- and instead of going quiet, you describe each one until the other person understands. Key words: a gadget, stuff, to describe, similar to, a kind of, round, flat, made of, used for, the opposite of. The toolkit for a missing word: "It's a kind of...", "It's used for opening...", "It's made of...", "It's a thing that / a place where / a person who...", "It's similar to...", "It's the opposite of...". Things take "that", places take "where", people take "who", and after "used for" the verb wears -ing. Expression: Sorry, I don't know the word -- let me describe it.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to every word and read the example. These are your tools for describing a thing when the exact word is gone. Watch used for: the verb after it always wears -ing (opening, cutting) -- the same shape you met last time after "enjoy".

A gadget -- a small tool or machine that does one useful job
"It's a little gadget you use to open a bottle of wine."
Stuff -- things in general, when you don't name them one by one
"I need some stuff to clean my glasses."
To describe -- to say what something is like, without using its name
"I don't know the word, so let me describe it."
Similar to -- almost the same as something you already know
"It's similar to a spoon, but smaller."
A kind of -- a type of; one thing in a group of similar things
"It's a kind of bag that you wear on your back."
Round -- shaped like a circle or a ball
"It's small and round, and it's made of metal."
Flat -- smooth and level, with no bumps; not tall
"It's flat, like a card, and you put it in your wallet."
Made of -- the material a thing is built from, like metal or plastic
"It's made of plastic, and it's used for the rain."
Used for -- the job a thing does; what you do with it
"It's used for opening a bottle of wine."
The opposite of -- completely different; the far other side
"It's the opposite of hot -- very, very cold."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

Rosângela is standing in a small shop in Porto, and she needs three things she cannot name. The old Rosângela would have said "sorry" and walked out. This time, she uses the words she has. "Let me describe it," she says to Tomás. "It's a small gadget, made of plastic, and it's used for putting my charger into your walls." Tomás smiles: "An adapter." Then the second thing: "It's a little tool, made of metal, used for opening a bottle of wine. It's a kind of spiral." "A corkscrew," he says.

The last one is harder. "It's a kind of small sticker for your skin. It's similar to a little bandage, for a small cut." "Plasters," says Tomás, and there they are. Three things she could not name, all three on the counter. She did not know a single word -- adapter, corkscrew, plaster -- and she got every one, because she told him what each thing was made of, what it was used for, and what it was similar to.

For forty years, a forgotten word was a locked door, and her son would finish the sentence for her. But the word was never the point. You will always be missing the exact one -- the skill is to walk around it, out loud, and keep going. A thing takes that, a place takes where, a person takes who, and after used for the verb wears -ing. Six little frames, and no locked door ever again.

"It's used for putting my charger into the walls." Why "putting" and not "put"?
A Because "used" is a question word.
B Because after "for" the next verb always wears -ing: used for putting, used for opening, used for cutting.
C Because "put" has no other form.
"It's a thing that you use in the rain." Why "that", and not "who"?
A Because it is a thing, not a person -- things take "that" or "which", and only people take "who".
B Because "who" is only for questions.
C Because "that" and "who" mean different things.
She says the plaster is "similar to a little bandage." What is she doing here?
A Naming the exact word for it.
B Comparing it to something Tomás already knows, so he can picture it without the exact word.
C Asking Tomás for the word.
Tomás understands all three things. Why, if her English is not perfect?
A Because she described each one -- what it is made of, used for, and similar to -- so he could picture it.
B Because she said each word perfectly, with no mistakes.
C Because her son was there to translate for her.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Describing Without the Name

GRAMMAR

When a word is gone, you do not need it. You describe the thing with a little frame, and the other person understands. Here are the six frames, and the two small rules that keep them right.

FrameWhat it givesExample
It's a kind of... / a type of...the familyIt's a kind of bag. · It's a type of coat.
It's used for + -ingthe jobIt's used for opening bottles. · It's used for cutting paper.
It's made of...the materialIt's made of metal. · It's made of plastic.
a thing that / a place where / a person whoa little sentencea thing that you use in the rain · a place where you buy medicine · a person who cuts your hair
It's similar to... / the opposite of...a comparisonIt's similar to a spoon. · It's the opposite of hot.

The two rules to remember: (1) after used for, the verb always wears -ing -- "used for opening", never "used for open". (2) A thing takes that (or "which"), a place takes where, a person takes who -- never "a thing who". Pick one frame, cross the gap, and keep going.

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt. There is no right or wrong version. Speak for 60 seconds, with no script.

Look around the room you are in right now. Choose three objects -- but do NOT say their names. Describe each one so a person who does not know the word could understand: say what it is a kind of, what it is made of, what it is used for, what it is similar to, whether it is round or flat. Start each one with "Sorry, I don't know the word -- let me describe it." Use at least four of today's words. For years, a forgotten word closed your mouth. This time, walk around it, out loud, by yourself. Take your time and do not read from a script.
