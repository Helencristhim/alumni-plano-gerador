Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Ouça cada palavra, leia o exemplo e a tradução. Toque em Listen para ouvir.

Standpoint -- the position or angle from which you see and judge something (PT: ponto de vista)
"From my standpoint, the tool will save us time."
Perspective -- the particular way a person sees or understands a situation (PT: perspectiva)
"From my perspective, we should test it first."
Valid -- based on good reasons or facts; fair and worth listening to (PT: válido / justo)
"That is a valid concern, and we should look at it."
Reasonable -- sensible, fair and not extreme; based on clear thinking (PT: razoável / sensato)
"A six-week rollout sounds reasonable to me."
Soften -- to make a statement less strong or less direct, so it sounds polite (PT: suavizar)
"I soften my opinion when the team disagrees."
Hedge -- to say something with caution, without fully committing to it (PT: falar com cautela / sem se comprometer)
"I hedge when I am not certain: it might work."
Persuade -- to convince someone to agree with you by giving good reasons (PT: convencer / persuadir)
"Good arguments persuade the team to test the idea."
Hesitant -- unsure and slow to decide or speak; not yet confident (PT: hesitante / inseguro)
"I am a little hesitant about the deadline."
Downside -- the negative side or disadvantage of an idea or plan (PT: desvantagem / lado negativo)
"The downside is that it costs a little more."
Overstate -- to describe something as bigger, better or worse than it really is (PT: exagerar)
"I do not want to overstate the risk; it is small."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Leia o texto e responda às perguntas.

In the meeting, Daniela gave her opinion. "In my view, the new platform is a good idea," she said. Notice she opens with a clear phrase. Then she softened it: "It might take a little longer than planned." Words like might, could, perhaps and a little are softeners — they make her sound careful, not weak. When Marcus disagreed, she stayed polite: "I see your point, but I am not sure the team is trained yet." She never said "you are wrong". Remember: agree and disagree are verbs — we say "I agree", never "I am agree". A clear opinion, softened with respect, sounds like a senior manager.

1. Which phrase does Daniela use to OPEN her opinion?
A "In my view, the new platform is a good idea."
B "You are wrong about the platform."
C "The rollout takes six weeks."
2. What do softeners like "might" and "a little" do?
A They make her sound weak and unsure.
B They make her sound careful and professional.
C They are only used in writing, never speaking.
3. Which sentence is correct?
A "I am agree with you."
B "I agree with you."
C "I am agreeing you."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip — Opinion Phrases, Hedging & Softeners

GRAMMAR

Como dar opinião de forma profissional (bilingue EN + PT).

MoveHow it worksExample
OpenStart with a clear opinion phrase"In my view, / From my perspective, / It seems to me that..."
Soften (hedge)Add might / could / perhaps / a little / I think when you are not certain"It might take a little longer."
Disagree politelyAcknowledge, then offer your view"I see your point, but I am not sure that..."
Regra (PT)agree e disagree são VERBOS: diga "I agree" / "I disagree", NUNCA "I am agree". Para soar profissional, ABRA com uma frase de opinião, SUAVIZE com might/perhaps quando não tiver certeza, e DISCORDE com respeito ("I see your point, but...") — nunca "you are wrong".

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Escolha a melhor resposta para cada situação.

A stakeholder asks for your opinion. How do you open it professionally?
A "From my perspective, we should test it first."
B "You must do what I say."
C "I am agree with the project."
You are not 100 percent sure the deadline is realistic. How do you hedge?
A "The deadline is impossible."
B "The deadline might be a little tight."
C "The deadline is perfect, no problem."
A colleague says "We should launch now." You disagree. What is the polite reply?
A "I see your point, but I would test it first."
B "You are wrong, we cannot launch."
C "No. That is a bad idea."
The risk is actually small. How do you avoid making it sound too big?
A "This is a huge, terrible risk."
B "I do not want to overstate the risk; it is small."
C "The risk is the biggest problem ever."
