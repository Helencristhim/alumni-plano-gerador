Introducing yourself and your work in quality assurance to an international team. Key words: quality assurance, to test, to validate, software, bug, requirement, deadline, remote work, feature, to deploy, scenario, stakeholder. Structures: present perfect for experience (for/since) vs present simple (routine) vs past simple (finished time).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Quality assurance -- the process of testing software to make sure it works before it reaches the user
"I have worked in quality assurance for six years."
To test -- to try software to see if it works and to find problems
"I test every new feature before it goes live."
To validate -- to check that something meets the requirements and works as expected
"We validate the system in expected and unexpected situations."
Software -- the programs and code that run on a computer
"Our team builds software for banks around the world."
Bug -- an error or fault in a program that makes it behave incorrectly
"My job is to catch every bug before the user does."
Requirement -- something the system must do, agreed with the client
"Each test checks one requirement from the client."
Deadline -- the date by which work must be finished
"We have a tight deadline for the release on Friday."
Remote work -- working from home or another place, not the office
"I am looking for full remote work with a company abroad."
Feature -- a specific function or part of a product
"The new payment feature needs more testing."
To deploy -- to release software so that users can use it
"We deploy the new version every two weeks."
Scenario -- a possible situation you test the system in
"I test the login in every scenario, expected and unexpected."
Stakeholder -- a person with an interest in the project: a client, manager or user
"I report bugs clearly so every stakeholder understands the risk."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Ana is a systems analyst who works in quality assurance. Every day, she tests and validates new features before her team deploys them. "I have worked in QA for six years," she says. Earlier in her career, she worked at WIPRO, an Indian company, for six years, and she learned a lot about international teams. Right now, she is testing a new payment feature, and the team has just found a critical bug before the deadline. Ana has always used English for technical reading, but now she wants to speak it too, because she is looking for full remote work with a company abroad. Next week, she has a video interview with a stakeholder from another country.

1. Why do we say "I have worked in QA for six years"?
A The experience started in the past and continues up to now (present perfect + for).
B The action is finished and has no link to the present.
C It is a routine she does every single week.
2. "She worked at WIPRO for six years" uses the past simple because:
A The experience continues today.
B It is a finished job, in a finished period of the past.
C It is happening right now.
3. Which sentence about her daily routine is correct?
A "She has tested new features since every day."
B "She tests new features every day."
C "She is testing new features every day for years."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Perfect vs Present Simple vs Past Simple

GRAMMAR

How to talk about your career with the right tense.

FormUseExample
Present Perfect
have / has + past participle		Experience and duration up to now, with for / since, when the time is not finished.I have worked here for 6 years.
just + present perfectVery recent news.We have just deployed the new version.
Present Simple
I / you / we + verb (he/she + s)		Facts and routines, what you do every day.I test software every day.
Past Simple
verb + -ed		A finished action, in a finished time (in 2018, at WIPRO, yesterday).I worked at WIPRO for six years.
for vs sincefor + a period (for 6 years) · since + a starting point (since 2018)
