Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

The language of raising money from an investor. Tap Listen to hear each word. / O vocabulario de captar investimento.

Investor -- investidor(a)
a person or company that puts money into a business hoping it will grow
"She's an investor who backs premium brands."
Equity -- participacao societaria
ownership in a company, usually measured in shares or a percentage
"In exchange for the money, we give up some equity."
Stake -- fatia / parte da empresa
a share of ownership in a company that someone holds
"We're offering a fifteen percent stake."
Valuation -- avaliacao / valor da empresa
the total amount a company is considered to be worth
"We're raising at a valuation of ten million."
Traction -- tracao / prova de crescimento
real evidence that a business is growing, such as sales or repeat customers
"Our repeat clients are the clearest traction we have."
Scalable -- escalavel
able to grow much larger without a big rise in cost or loss of quality
"With more capacity, the model is fully scalable."
Backer -- apoiador(a) / financiador(a)
a person or fund that supports a business, usually with money
"The right backer brings contacts, not just cash."
Dividend -- dividendo
a share of a company's profit paid out to its owners or shareholders
"Early on, we'd reinvest profit rather than pay a dividend."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read how Milton frames his pitch, then answer. Notice the emphasis (cleft) sentences. / Leia como o Milton estrutura o pitch e responda; observe as frases de enfase.

In front of the investor, Milton kept it sharp. "What sets us apart is the margin," he began. "It is our craftsmanship and our story that let us price above the market and still sell out." For proof, he pointed to the traction: revenue up forty percent and more than half of buyers reordering. When she doubted whether a handmade brand was scalable, he answered calmly: "It is the bottleneck, not demand, that holds us back." Then came the ask: one and a half million for a fifteen percent stake, at a valuation of ten million. "For the right backer, the return is clear," he closed. He explained that early on they would reinvest profit rather than pay a dividend, giving up a little equity now to grow far bigger later.

1. "What sets us apart is the margin." What is this structure doing?
A An emphasis (cleft) sentence -- it spotlights one idea (the margin).
B A reported-speech sentence.
C A first conditional.
2. In "It is the bottleneck that holds us back," which connector is correct?
A It is the bottleneck which holds us back.
B It is the bottleneck that holds us back. (use "that" in this cleft)
C It is the bottleneck what holds us back.
3. What exactly is Milton offering the investor?
A A dividend of fifteen percent.
B A fifteen percent stake for one and a half million, at a ten million valuation.
C All of the equity in the company.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Emphasis (Cleft) Sentences

GRAMMAR

Split one plain sentence into two parts to push one idea into the spotlight. Use them at the moments that matter most.

PatternFrameExample
What-cleftWhat + clause + is/are + focusWhat sets us apart is the margin.
It-cleftIt is + focus + that + clauseIt is the bottleneck that holds us back.
What we needWhat we need is + focusWhat we need is capacity.
Common mistake: never drop the linking verb (What sets us apart is the margin, not "What sets us apart the margin"); the verb agrees with the focus (What we need is capacity); and in "It is... ", the connector is that, not which. PT: frases de enfase dividem uma frase comum em duas para destacar UMA ideia. Use "What + oracao + is/are + foco" ou "It is + foco + that + oracao". Nunca esqueca o verbo de ligacao (is/are), faca o verbo concordar com o foco, e use "that" (nao "which") na forma "It is...". Use com moderacao, nos momentos-chave: seu diferencial, sua prova, seu pedido.
