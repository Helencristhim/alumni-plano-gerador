Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each expression and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

What brings you here? -- a light, warm way to ask why someone is in a place
"What brings you here today -- work or a little vacation?"
I couldn't help but notice -- a soft, polite way to start a comment about something you saw
"I couldn't help but notice your bag -- is it from that new shop?"
To hit it off -- to like someone and get along well, right from the start
"We started talking about coffee and just hit it off."
Small world! -- a warm reaction when you discover an unexpected connection
"You know my cousin? Small world!"
No kidding! -- a friendly reaction showing interest or mild surprise
"You lived in Tokyo too? No kidding!"
That reminds me -- a bridge to a new topic that the conversation just sparked
"That reminds me -- have you tried the bakery next door?"
Speaking of -- a smooth link to a topic that was just mentioned
"Speaking of coffee, do you know a good place near here?"
Filler words -- small words that give you time and signal you are engaged
"I was, you know, a little nervous, but it went well."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

At a cafe in Brooklyn, I saw a woman with a book from a store I love. I couldn't help but notice it, so I said hello. When she said she was from Sao Paulo, I said, no kidding -- my sister lived there. Small world! We hit it off right away. I told her she might want to try the cold brew, and that we could visit the bakery next door. Speaking of the bakery, that reminds me -- their croissants are amazing. I said, if I lived here, I'd come every morning. A few filler words here and there kept it easy. After ten minutes, I said I should probably get going, but it was great talking to her.

1. When the writer says "you might want to try the cold brew", is it an order or a soft suggestion?
A A soft, friendly suggestion.
B A strong order.
C A complaint.
2. In "if I lived here, I'd come every morning", does the writer live there now?
A Yes, she lives there now.
B No -- it is imaginary (second conditional).
C It is a real future plan.
3. Why does the writer use a few filler words?
A Because she made a mistake.
B To keep the conversation easy and show she was engaged.
C To end the conversation quickly.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Soft Modals & Second Conditional (Review)

GRAMMAR

Nothing new today -- just two tools you already have, used socially. Use soft modals (might want to, could, should probably) to suggest gently instead of giving orders. Use the second conditional (if + past, would/'d) to imagine a social what-if. Inside if, use the past -- never "would".

ToolSocial useExample
might want toa gentle suggestion"You might want to try that cafe."
coulda light, no-pressure offer"We could grab a coffee sometime."
should probablya soft intention"I should probably head out soon."
if + past, 'da social what-if (not real now)"If I lived here, I'd come every day."

Stage 2: Put the Steps in Order

Order

Listen first, then put the steps of a small talk in the correct order.

?Listen closely and react warmly, like "no kidding" or "that is interesting".
?Notice a small, safe opening, like a bag, a jacket, or the weather.
?Close in a friendly way, saying "it was great talking to you".
?Open with a light comment or question, like "what brings you here".
?Keep it going with a follow-up, using "speaking of" or "that reminds me".

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best sentence for each casual situation.

You want to give a light tip about a restaurant. What do you say?
A "You must eat at that place."
B "You might want to try that place."
C "Eat at that place now."
You want to imagine living in the city. What do you say?
A "If I would live here, I would walk everywhere."
B "If I lived here, I would walk everywhere."
C "If I live here, I will walk everywhere."
A stranger says they also love your hometown. How do you react?
A "No kidding! Small world."
B "That is none of my business."
C "Please stop talking."
