Chapter 3 -- Comparatives

Grammar Discovery

Comparatives: -er than and more ... than

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

What do the orange words show about comparing?

"Science is harder than history."

"English is easier than math."

"History is more interesting than geography."

"A clear project is better than a long project."

Grammar Rule

The Comparative

TypeRuleExample
Short adjectiveadj + er + thanhard → harder than
Short + double letterdouble + erbig → bigger than
Adjective in -yy → ier + thaneasy → easier than
Long adjectivemore + adj + thanmore interesting than
Irregularspecial formgood → better, bad → worse

Key idea: use the comparative to compare TWO things. Short adjectives add -er (harder, bigger, easier). Long adjectives use more (more interesting, more difficult). Two adjectives are irregular: good → better, bad → worse. Always connect the two things with than.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"English is more easier than math." / "History is interestinger."
"English is easier than math." / "History is more interesting."

Use -er OR more, never both. Short adjectives take -er (easier, harder). Long adjectives take more (more interesting, more difficult).
