Lesson 06 -- Pre-class

Getting Around Like a Local -- Airports, Transit, and the Rules of the City

In Lesson 5 you chose the perfect week. Now you have to GET there — and the city has rules that nobody explains to you. From the check-in kiosk in Guarulhos to the turnstile in Manhattan. Key words: connecting flight, departure gate, check-in kiosk, rideshare, curbside, turnstile, to tap in, platform, express train, to transfer, uptown, shuttle. Structure: modals of obligation, permission and prohibition — the grammar of a rule ("You have to tap your card before the turnstile." / "You don’t have to buy a ticket." / "You can’t smoke on the platform." / "You are allowed to take a bike, but not at rush hour."). The point that decides everything: don’t have to (it is optional) and can’t (it is forbidden) are OPPOSITES — even though both of them are negative.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Take your time — listen twice, and say it out loud. These are the twelve words that live between the plane and the hotel: half of them are written on a sign, and nobody reads the sign for you.

Connecting flight -- the second flight you take, after the first one lands, on the same trip
"I have a connecting flight in Atlanta, and I have to change terminals."
Departure gate -- the door at the airport where you wait, and where you get on the plane
"You don't have to be at the departure gate two hours early."
Check-in kiosk -- the machine you use to check yourself in, without talking to anybody
"If the line at the counter is long, you can use the check-in kiosk instead."
Rideshare -- a car you order on an app, like Uber or Lyft, instead of a taxi
"At JFK you have to meet your rideshare on the second floor."
Curbside -- the edge of the street, where cars stop to pick you up or drop you off
"Rideshare cars are not allowed to wait at the curbside for more than a minute."
Turnstile -- the metal gate you go through to enter the subway, one person at a time
"You have to tap your card before you go through the turnstile."
To tap in -- to touch your card or your phone on the reader, to pay and go in
"You don't have to buy a ticket anymore -- you can just tap in with your credit card."
Platform -- the long floor next to the tracks, where you stand and wait for the train
"The uptown platform and the downtown platform are on different sides."
Express train -- a train that skips most stations and only stops at the big ones
"The express train doesn't stop at my station, so I have to take the local one."
To transfer -- to get off one train and get on another one, to finish your trip
"You have to transfer at Times Square if you want to go downtown."
Uptown -- toward the north of Manhattan, where the street numbers get bigger
"I was going uptown, but my hotel was downtown -- forty blocks the wrong way."
Shuttle -- a small bus or train that goes back and forth between two places only
"You have to take the shuttle from the terminal to the subway station."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

The line at the counter has forty people in it. The line at the check-in kiosk has two. Lucimara does not have to talk to anybody: she scans her passport, she prints her tag, and she walks to the departure gate. Her connecting flight leaves Atlanta at seven forty.

Eleven hours later, at Kennedy, her rideshare driver calls. He speaks fast, and he says he can’t wait. What nobody told either of them: at that airport, rideshare cars are not allowed to stop at the curbside on the arrivals level. He has to come up to Level 2. So he cancels, and she takes the shuttle to the station instead.

At the station there is a man in a booth. Her suitcase is too big for the turnstile. "You have to use the wide gate," he says, "and you have to tap in there too." She asks whether she has to buy a ticket. She doesn’t. She can just tap the credit card that is already in her hand.

Then she asks the question that saves the whole trip: which platform does she have to be on — uptown or downtown? Both platforms say E. Both trains are the same color. One goes to her hotel in Midtown. The other one is an express train going forty blocks the wrong way, and it would not stop again for a long time. She takes the downtown side. She doesn’t have to transfer at all.

1. "She does not have to talk to anybody." What does that tell you about the counter with a person behind it?
A That she is NOT ALLOWED to talk to the agent at the counter.
B That talking to the agent is OPTIONAL — she can do it if she wants, but she does not need to. Don’t have to = there is no obligation (and nothing stops you).
C That her English is not good enough for that.
2. What is the difference between "he can’t wait" and "she doesn’t have to buy a ticket"?
A They are OPPOSITES. Can’t = forbidden or impossible (the rule stops him). Doesn’t have to = optional (nothing stops her — she simply does not need to).
B No difference — both of them are negative, so they mean the same thing.
C The first one is formal and the second one is informal.
3. "Which platform does she have to be on?" Why is there a does in the question?
A Because every question in English starts with does.
B Because have to is a NORMAL verb — and a normal verb needs do/does to make a question. That is why we say "Do I have to transfer?" and never "Do I must transfer?".
C Because the sentence is in the past.
4. Which sentence is correct?
A "You mustn’t buy a ticket — you can just tap your card." (this FORBIDS the person from buying a ticket!)
B "You don’t have to buy a ticket — you can just tap your card."
C "You don’t must to buy a ticket — you can just tap your card."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Have to / Don’t have to / Can’t / Be allowed to (the grammar of a rule)

GRAMMAR

A city is a set of rules. These are the five ways English says one.

FormUseExample
have to
(affirmative)		OBLIGATION — somebody else’s rule, not your own choice. This is the one an American SAYS (must is the one they write)."You have to tap your card."
mustThe SAME obligation, but you almost always read it: signs, regulations, contracts. Rare in speech."Passengers must hold the handrail." (a sign in the subway)
don’t have to
(negative 1)		NO OBLIGATION — it is OPTIONAL. You can do it if you want to: nothing stops you. This is NOT prohibition."You don’t have to buy a ticket." (you do not need one — but you may)
can’t / must not
(negative 2)		PROHIBITION — it is forbidden. If you do it, there is a consequence."You can’t smoke on the platform."
can /
be allowed to		PERMISSION — the rule says yes. Be allowed to is the more formal one (and the one you see on an official notice)."You are allowed to take a bike, but not at rush hour."
The questionDo I have to…? / Am I allowed to…? — have to is a normal verb: it needs do/does."Do I have to transfer?" — never "Do I must transfer?"
The sound
(the important one)		In an American mouth, have to does NOT sound like have to: it becomes HAFTA, with an F. Has to becomes HASTA. Got to becomes GOTTA. And don’t have to becomes DOAN-HAFTA, all in one piece. That is why you have never heard this grammar — it is everywhere, but it does not sound like the words you learned.

Watch out -- the classic mistake: both of them are negative, so the mind glues them together into one thing. In English they are OPPOSITES: don’t have to SETS YOU FREE (it is optional), mustn’t / can’t STOPS YOU (it is forbidden). If you tell a tourist "you mustn’t buy a ticket", you have just forbidden her to buy one. The question that always solves it: does this sentence FREE me, or does it STOP me?

Stage 3: Pronunciation

Speaking

Listen to the sentence, say it out loud, and only then record. Today the target is ONE sound: have to, glued together. In an American mouth, have to becomes HAFTA (with an F!), has to becomes HASTA, and don’t have to becomes DOAN-HAFTA, all in one piece. If it comes out as "have · to", separate, it is wrong — even when it is correct. It is not laziness: it is real English — and it is exactly why you do not hear the obligation when they speak.

Excuse me, do I have to transfer to get to Midtown?
Which platform do I have to be on -- uptown or downtown?
You don't have to buy a ticket -- you can just tap your credit card.
Am I allowed to take this suitcase through the gate?
Let me check I understood: the E train, downtown platform, no transfer.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer. Question 3 is the trap of this lesson: not every negative sentence is a "no".

You have a big suitcase and the turnstile is too narrow for it. You ask the station agent:
A "Do I must use another gate?"
B "Do I have to use another gate? This suitcase doesn't fit through the turnstile."
C "I am allowed use another gate?"
Two platforms, the same letter, the same color. You have four seconds. The question that saves the trip is:
A "Which platform do I have to be on -- uptown or downtown?"
B "How do I get to Midtown?" (and then comes a fifteen-second answer that you do not understand)
C "Where is the train?"
A tourist behind you is in line at the machine, about to buy a ticket she does not need. You want to SET HER FREE. You say:
A "You mustn't buy a ticket -- you can just tap your card." (this FORBIDS her from buying one!)
B "You don't have to buy a ticket -- you can just tap your credit card."
C "You can't buy a ticket -- you can just tap your card."
Your rideshare driver is waiting on Level 1, where pickup is forbidden, and the police are moving the cars along. You say:
A "You don't have to wait on Level 1 -- you have to come up to Level 2."
B "You can't wait on Level 1 -- you have to come up to Level 2. Pickup is only allowed at the curbside upstairs."
C "You are not allowed come up to Level 2."
The agent gave you the line, the platform and the transfer, all very fast. Before you walk away, you:
A Say thank you and walk away, hoping that you understood him.
B "Let me check I understood: the E train, downtown platform, no transfer. Is that right?"
C "Sorry, can you repeat everything again from the beginning?" (and he repeats it just as fast)

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer — speak for 90 seconds, with no script.

Explain the rules of your own factory in Guarulhos to a foreign visitor who has never been there. What does he have to do before he goes in? What does he NOT have to do (careful -- this is the hard one: it means it is optional, not forbidden)? And what is he not allowed to do, under any circumstances? Use have to, don’t have to, can’t and be allowed to. This is tonight’s grammar on the ground where you are the expert -- so take your time, and do not read from a script.
