Lesson 06 -- Pre-class

How Long Have You...? -- Hobbies You've Had for Ages

Talking about how long you have been doing the things you love. Key words: hobby, to be into, to tinker, workshop, for ages, to pick up, to get the hang of, spare time, to be hooked, to keep at it, a knack, to lose track of time. Structure: present perfect continuous (have/has been + -ing) with for and since, and how it differs from the simple present perfect.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Hobby -- an activity you do for pleasure in your free time
"Furniture restoration started as a small hobby."
To be into -- to be very interested in and enthusiastic about something
"I have been into old furniture since I was a teenager."
To tinker -- to work on or adjust small things in a relaxed, curious way
"I love to tinker in the workshop on quiet afternoons."
Workshop -- the room or space where you make, fix or restore things
"I have turned the back room into a little workshop."
For ages -- for a very long time
"I have been meaning to finish that cabinet for ages."
To pick up -- to learn a skill slowly, without formal lessons
"I picked up most of it from videos and my own mistakes."
To get the hang of -- to learn how to do something after some practice
"It took a year to get the hang of sanding properly."
Spare time -- the free time when you are not working
"In my spare time, I sand, paint and repair."
To be hooked -- to enjoy something so much that you cannot stop
"After the very first chair, I was completely hooked."
To keep at it -- to keep doing something difficult without giving up
"Some pieces are hard, but I just keep at it."
A knack -- a natural skill for doing something well
"She has a real knack for fixing broken things."
To lose track of time -- to be so absorbed that you forget what time it is
"I lose track of time the moment I start a project."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Ana has been restoring old furniture for about six years. She has been doing it since she moved to the countryside, and she still tinkers in her workshop almost every day. She has been teaching herself the whole time, mostly from videos. Notice: we say for six years (a period) but since 2019 (a starting point). When she wants to count finished pieces, she switches to the simple form: "I have restored about twenty chairs." The continuous answers how long; the simple answers how many. She was hooked from the very first chair, and she often loses track of time the moment she starts.

1. Why is it "for six years" but "since she moved"?
A for + a period of time; since + a starting point.
B They mean the same thing and are interchangeable.
C "since" is only used in questions.
2. She says "I have restored about twenty chairs." Why the simple form here?
A Because there is a date in the sentence.
B Because she is counting finished results (how many), not duration.
C Because "restore" has no continuous form.
3. Which question asks about duration?
A "How long have you been restoring furniture?"
B "How many pieces have you restored?"
C "Which piece did you restore first?"

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Perfect Continuous

GRAMMAR

The tense for an activity that started in the past and is still going.

FormUseExample
Present Perfect Continuous
have / has been + verb-ing		An activity that started in the past and is STILL going. Focus on duration.I have been restoring furniture.
for+ a period of time (how long it has lasted)for six years, for ages
since+ a starting point (when it began)since 2019, since I moved here
QuestionHow long + have/has + subject + been + -ingHow long have you been doing this?
Continuous vs SimpleContinuous = how long (activity). Simple = how many (finished result).I have been painting all day. / I have painted three chairs.
In one lineStill going + how long → have been doing. for = period, since = point.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each real conversation.

Someone asks how long you have had your hobby. You answer:
A "I have been restoring furniture for six years."
B "I am restoring furniture for six years."
C "I restore furniture for six years."
You want to give a starting point, not a period. The correct word is:
A "I have been doing this for I moved here."
B "I have been doing this since I moved here."
C "I have been doing this from I moved here."
You want to count finished results, not duration. You say:
A "I have been finishing twenty chairs."
B "I have finished twenty chairs."
C "I am finishing twenty chairs since years."
You want to ask a friend about the duration of their hobby. You ask:
A "How long have you been playing the guitar?"
B "How long you are playing the guitar?"
C "Since how long you play the guitar?"
