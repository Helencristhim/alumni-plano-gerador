Chapter 3 -- The Short-Form Code

Brief, but Never Cold

Drop the scaffolding, keep the politeness

Discover the Pattern

Email vs Chat

Email: "I would be grateful if you could send me the deck."  →  Chat: "Could you send the deck when you get a sec?"

Email: "Please be advised this is not urgent."  →  Chat: "No rush on this one."

Email: "I am writing to inform you the deck is ready."  →  Chat: "Quick heads-up — deck is ready."

Email: "Thank you in advance for your assistance."  →  Chat: "Thanks! Really appreciate it."

The Rule

The Short-Form Code

In chat you DROPIn chat you KEEP
The long salutation ("Dear Mr. Costa,")A softener: "Could you...", "Would you mind..."
Heavy formal verbs ("I would be grateful")A "please" or a "thanks"
The full sign-off ("Best regards, Eduardo")A warm closer: "No rush", "Appreciate it"
Full sentences where a phrase worksContractions ("I'm on it", "you're right")

Key idea: short-form is not lazy writing. You cut the formal scaffolding, but you always keep one polite word — a "could you", a "please", a "no rush". That single softener is what keeps a two-line message warm instead of cold. Read the channel: the more senior the reader, the more you soften.

Watch Out

Common Mistake

Send the deck.
Could you send the deck when you get a sec? No rush.

Short is good; a bare command is not. "Send the deck." reads as an order. One softener — "Could you..." plus a "no rush" — keeps the message just as fast but warm. Brevity without politeness tips into rudeness, especially in a channel a partner can read.

Practice

Soften the Message

"_____Could you send the deck when you get a sec?" (softener)
"Quick _____heads-up — the partner is in this channel now." (early warning)
"Take your time, _____no rush on this one." (warm closer)
"On it — I'll _____circle back by end of day." (return later)

Before You Send
