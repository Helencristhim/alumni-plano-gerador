Lesson 09 -- Pre-class

Talking About Requirements -- Asking & Confirming

Joining a requirements call with a product owner to clarify a new feature before you test it: the use case, the end user, the scope, the constraints and the acceptance criteria. Key words: specification, scope, acceptance criteria, use case, assumption, constraint, end user, to gather, to sign off, to double-check, ambiguous, feasible. Structure: question forms and indirect questions (Could you tell me what the client wants?).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Specification -- a detailed written description of what the software must do
"The specification says the report must be a PDF."
Scope -- the boundaries of what a project includes and excludes
"Do you know if this feature is in scope?"
Acceptance criteria -- the conditions a feature must meet to be accepted
"The acceptance criteria are a PDF, under ten megabytes."
Use case -- how a user interacts with the system to reach a goal
"Could you tell me what the main use case is?"
Assumption -- something you treat as true without checking it
"Never make an assumption; ask instead."
Constraint -- a limit or restriction you have to work within
"The file size is a hard constraint."
End user -- the real person who will actually use the software
"Who is the end user of this feature?"
To gather -- to collect information from different people or sources
"A good analyst gathers the requirements by asking."
To sign off -- to formally approve that something is correct or complete
"The client will sign off on the final requirements."
To double-check -- to check something again to be completely sure
"Let me double-check the requirements with you."
Ambiguous -- not clear because it has more than one possible meaning
"The ticket was a little ambiguous, so I asked."
Feasible -- possible to do within the time and the limits you have
"The constraints decide what is feasible."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Ana is on a requirements call with Bruno, the product owner. She never makes an assumption; she asks. First she reads the specification, but it is a little ambiguous, so she asks questions. "Could you tell me what the main use case is?" she asks. Bruno explains that the end user clicks Export and gets a PDF. "Do you know if the report has to include the charts?" Ana asks. Then she checks the scope and the constraints: "Can I ask what the constraint on the file size is?" Before the call ends, she repeats everything back to confirm her understanding and to double-check the acceptance criteria. Notice how her polite questions use statement word order: "what the use case is", not "what is the use case".

1. Which is the correct indirect question?
A "Could you tell me what does the client want?"
B "Could you tell me what the client wants?"
C "Could you tell me what want the client?"
2. For a yes/no idea, which indirect question is correct?
A "Do you know is the feature in scope?"
B "Do you know if the feature is in scope?"
C "Do you know the feature is in scope if?"
3. In an indirect question, the word order after the wh-word is...
A the same as a statement: subject before verb, no do/does.
B always inverted, like a direct question.
C with an extra "do" or "does" added.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Question Forms & Indirect Questions

GRAMMAR

How to ask polite, clear questions to gather requirements.

TypeDirect questionIndirect (polite) question
Wh- questionWhat does the client want?Could you tell me what the client wants?
Yes/No questionIs the feature in scope?Do you know if the feature is in scope?
Word orderAfter the wh-word or "if/whether", use statement order (subject + verb). Drop do/does/did: "where the file is", not "where is the file".
OpenersCould you tell me... · Do you know... · Can I ask... · I was wondering whether...

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer.

Imagine a product owner gives you a new feature to test, but the ticket is ambiguous. Ask three questions to clarify the requirements: one about the use case, one about the scope, and one about a constraint. Use indirect questions ("Could you tell me...", "Do you know if..."). Then confirm the acceptance criteria back in one sentence. Take your time.
