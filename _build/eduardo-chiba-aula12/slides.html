Chapter 2 -- Data Words

The Language of Charts

Twelve words to describe numbers and movement

Vocabulary -- Part 1

Words for the Chart

A picture of numbers
Chart
A picture that shows numbers as bars, lines or slices
"This chart shows our revenue by quarter."
The general direction
Trend
The general direction numbers move in over time
"The trend is clearly upward this year."
A single number on a slide
Figure
A single number, especially an important business number
"Sixty-five million is our key figure this quarter."
A number out of one hundred
Percentage
A part of a whole, written out of one hundred
"Revenue grew by a high percentage this year."
The highest point
Peak
The highest point a number reaches on a chart
"Sales reached their peak in December."
The parts of a total
Breakdown
A split of a total into its separate parts
"Here is the breakdown of revenue by region."

Vocabulary -- Part 2

Words for Movement

To go up
Rise
To go up; an increase in a number
"Revenue continued to rise all year."
To go down
Fall
To go down; a drop in a number
"Costs began to fall after the new deal."
To become two times bigger
Double
To become two times as big
"Our user base doubled in twelve months."
To cut by half
Halve
To cut a number down to half its size
"We managed to halve our cost per lead."
Fast and large
Sharp
Fast and large; a quick, big change
"There was a sharp rise in the first quarter."
Slow and even
Steady
Slow, even and without big changes
"Growth was steady through the second quarter."

Chapter 3 -- Trends & Comparison

The Comparison Code

A number means more when you compare it

Discover the Pattern

What Do the Orange Words Share?

"Our growth was faster than the market."

"This figure is much higher than last year."

"Costs are more efficient than before."

"Our churn is as low as the best in the segment."

The Rule

Comparatives for Data

TypeFormExample
Short adjectiveadj + -er + than"Revenue is higher than last year."
Long adjectivemore + adj + than"Costs are more efficient than before."
Equalas + adj + as"Churn is as low as the best."
Emphasismuch / far + comparative"Much higher than the market."

Key idea: a number is just a number until you compare it. Use comparatives to give every figure meaning, and use much or far to show how big the gap is.

Watch Out

Common Mistake

Revenue is more higher than last year.
Revenue is much higher than last year.

"Higher" is already a comparative, so we never add "more". To make it stronger, use "much" or "far" before it.

Practice

Complete the Comparison

"This quarter is _____stronger than the last one." (short adjective + than)
"Our model is _____more scalable than theirs." (long adjective + than)
"Costs this year are _____as low as our target." (equal comparison)
"The gap is _____far wider than last year." (emphasis + comparative)

"Good. But you have ten numbers on that slide. Which one is the story?"

S

"Exactly. Then highlight that one figure, and hide the rest. Let the trend do the talking."

"Got it. One key figure per slide, one clear comparison, one simple message."
