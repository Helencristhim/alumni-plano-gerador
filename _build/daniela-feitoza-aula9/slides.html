The -ED Code

Three sounds, one ending

Sound Discovery

Listen and Notice

"We worked all day." — sounds like workt

"They played well." — sounds like playd

"I wanted coffee." — sounds like want-id

"She needed help." — sounds like need-id

When does -ed add a whole extra syllable?

Sound Rule

The Three -ED Sounds

Last sound of the verb-ed sounds likeExample
Voiceless (p, k, s, sh, ch, f)/t/ (no extra syllable)worked, finished, stopped
Voiced (vowels, b, g, l, n, v)/d/ (no extra syllable)played, lived, called
t or d/id/ (one extra syllable)wanted, needed, started
Key pointOnly verbs ending in t or d get a whole extra syllable. All others just add a quick /t/ or /d/.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

work·ed (two syllables)

✓ RIGHT

workt (one syllable)

✗ WRONG

"I tink so." (t sound)

✓ RIGHT

"I think so." (tongue between teeth)

End on the consonant sound, with no extra vowel. For th, let your tongue touch your teeth.

Chapter 5

Tricky Sounds

Drill it until it feels easy

Quick Fire

Say It Right

1 / 5

1. Say it with the TH sound: "I think we should meet on Thursday."

Tongue between your teeth: THink, THursday — not "tink, Tursday."

2. Say the -ed with no extra vowel: "We finished the report."

finished = finisht (one syllable, /t/ sound).

3. Which has an extra syllable: started or stopped?

started = start-id (extra syllable). stopped = stopt (no extra syllable).

4. Say the difference: "I live here, but I leave at six."

live = short i (quick); leave = long e (longer). Two different sounds.

5. Find the silent letter: "I cannot climb that wall."

The b in climb is silent: say "clime", not "climb-uh."
