Aula 16 -- Pre-class

Making Requests & Proposals

Ask for what you need politely and propose ideas the professional way -- Could I, Could you, Would you mind, I'd like to propose -- then put a proposal in writing. New vocabulary: request, proposal, favor, willing, approval, budget, resource, justify, prioritize, draft.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Request -- a polite act of asking for something
"I have a small request for the committee." (Pedido / solicitação)
Proposal -- a plan or idea you formally suggest, often in writing
"I have prepared a one-page proposal." (Proposta)
Favor -- a small kind thing you ask someone to do for you
"I need a small favor from you." (Favor)
Approval -- official permission to go ahead with something
"I am asking for your approval to move forward." (Aprovação)
Budget -- the amount of money available for something
"I know the budget is tight." (Orçamento)
Resource -- something useful you need: people, time or equipment
"The console frees up a critical resource." (Recurso)
Justify -- to give good reasons that show something is right or worth it
"Let me justify the cost of the console." (Justificar)
Prioritize -- to treat something as more important and deal with it first
"I would like to prioritize patient safety." (Priorizar)
Willing -- happy and ready to do something
"I am willing to lead the project." (Disposto / disposta)
Draft -- a first version of a document, before the final one
"I will send you a draft by Wednesday." (Rascunho / minuta)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Andreia wants a second training console, so she prepares a proposal. First she opens with a polite request: "Could I ask the committee to consider a second console?" Note: Could is softer than Can, so it sounds more polite. Then she gives the reason -- the team shares one console -- and explains the cost. The budget is tight, so she must justify it: the console frees up a critical resource and saves time. Above all, she wants to prioritize patient safety. She also asks her colleague David for a small favor: "Would you mind reviewing the draft?" Note: after Would you mind, the verb takes the -ing form (reviewing). David is willing to help. Finally, Andreia asks for approval to move forward, with a clear deadline.

1. Andreia says "Could I ask the committee to consider a second console?" Why "Could", not "Can"?
A "Could" is softer and sounds more polite at work.
B "Could" means the request is in the past.
C There is no difference between "Could" and "Can".
2. After "Would you mind", what form of the verb do we use?
A The base verb: "Would you mind review it?"
B The -ing form: "Would you mind reviewing it?"
C The infinitive: "Would you mind to review it?"
3. Why does Andreia need to justify the cost?
A Because the budget is tight, so she gives good reasons for the cost.
B Because the console is free and needs no reason.
C Because the committee already approved the budget.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Polite Requests & Proposing

GRAMMAR

Como pedir com educação e propor uma ideia. / How to make polite requests and propose an idea.

Function / FunçãoForm / FormaExample / Exemplo
Pedir permissãoCould I + verbo baseCould I ask for a second console?
Pedir a alguémCould you + verbo baseCould you review the proposal?
Muito educadoWould you mind + verbo-INGWould you mind reviewing it?
Propor uma ideiaI would like to propose...I would like to propose a console.

Atenção / Note: Could é mais suave que Can -- soa mais educado. Depois de Could I / Could you, use o verbo base (Could you send it?). Depois de Would you mind, use o verbo no -ING (Would you mind sending it?). Para propor, use I would like to propose + ideia, ou Would it be possible to + verbo base. / "Could" is softer than "Can"; use the base verb after Could you; use the -ing form after Would you mind.
