The Rule

Defining Relative Clauses

Points toWordExample
A thingwhich / that"the clause that limits liability"
A thingwhich / that"the provision which caps our exposure"
A personwho / that"the party who signs first"
No commait defines, not adds"any provision that stipulates a deadline"

Tip: a defining relative clause tells you which exact one you mean. Use who for people, which for things, and that for both. It defines, so there is no comma, and you never repeat the subject: "the clause that limits liability" -- never "the clause that it limits liability".

Common Mistake

Don't Repeat the Subject

"The clause that it limits liability. The provision who caps our exposure."
"The clause that limits liability. The provision which caps our exposure."

The relative word (that / which / who) is already the subject -- never add it. And use which or that for a thing, who or that for a person.

About the Brief

1. Which clause does the counsel say to read first, and why?

The clause that limits liability -- liability is who pays if something goes wrong, so the provision which caps it protects the company.

2. What does the counsel say to watch for, and what does it mean to waive a right?

Watch what you give up: a clause that asks you to waive a right removes a protection. Stipulate means to state clearly as a requirement, like the closing date or the payment terms.

3. What two questions should you always ask, and what is the golden rule?

Who enforces this, and what happens on a breach? The golden rule: never sign a binding agreement you have not understood -- read clause by clause.
