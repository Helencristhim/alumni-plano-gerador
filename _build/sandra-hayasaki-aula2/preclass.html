Aula 02 -- Pre-class

My Story So Far -- Past Simple vs Present Perfect (Life Experiences)

Talk about your life experiences: contrast finished moments in the past (past simple) with experiences that still matter now (present perfect) -- ever, already, yet, lately. New words: abroad, achievement, ever, already, yet, lately, experience, accomplish. New structure: past simple for a finished moment with a clear time, present perfect (have / has + past participle) for a life experience when the exact time does not matter.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

The language of your life story. Tap Listen to hear each word.

Abroad -- in or to a country that is not your own (no exterior, fora do pais)
"Last year I traveled abroad for the first time."
Achievement -- something good and difficult that you succeeded in doing (conquista, realizacao)
"Speaking English is a real achievement for me."
Ever -- at any time in your life, used mostly in questions (alguma vez)
"Have you ever lived in another city?"
Already -- before now, often sooner than you expected (ja)
"I have already finished my coffee."
Yet -- up to now, used in questions and negative sentences (ainda)
"I have not become fluent yet, but I am close."
Lately -- in the recent past, during the last days or weeks (ultimamente)
"Lately I have felt more confident in English."
Experience -- something you have done, seen, or lived through (experiencia)
"Traveling alone was a wonderful experience."
Accomplish -- to finish something difficult and do it well (realizar, alcancar)
"This year I want to accomplish a real conversation."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read Sandra's story and answer the questions.

A few years ago, Sandra started learning English because she wanted to travel more freely. Back then she only knew a few words. Since that day, she has made many mistakes, but each one has taught her something new. Last year, she finally took her first trip abroad, and it was one of the proudest moments of her life. She ordered food in English, she asked for directions, and people understood her. "I have not become fluent yet," she says, "but I have already accomplished things I never thought I could do." Lately, she has felt more confident, and she knows her biggest achievement is not perfect grammar. It is the courage to keep speaking. "Have you ever felt nervous in a new language?" she asks. "It has been the hardest and most beautiful experience of my life."

1. Why does the text say "Last year she took her first trip" and not "she has taken"?
A "Last year" is a finished, clear time, so we use the past simple.
B "Took" is wrong; it should always be "has taken".
C The past simple is only for the future.
2. What does "She has made many mistakes" tell us?
A She made the mistakes at one exact, finished time.
B It is a life experience that still connects to now (present perfect).
C It means she will make mistakes tomorrow.
3. Which form correctly follows "have" in the present perfect?
A The base verb (I have take).
B The past participle (I have taken, I have gone, I have made).
C The simple past form (I have took, I have went).

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Past Simple vs Present Perfect

GRAMMAR

Use these two pasts to tell your story so far.

FormPast SimplePresent Perfect
Affirmative"I traveled abroad last year.""I have traveled abroad."
Negative"I did not travel last year.""I have not traveled yet."
Question"Did you travel last year?""Have you ever traveled?"
Time wordslast year, yesterday, in 2020ever, already, yet, lately
Watch the traps: use the past simple for a finished moment with a clear time, and the present perfect (have / has + past participle) for a life experience with no exact time. Use the past participle after "have" -- "gone", not "went". PT: use o past simple para um momento terminado com tempo claro (last year, yesterday) e o present perfect (have/has + participio passado) para uma experiencia de vida quando o tempo exato nao importa; ever, already, yet e lately andam com o present perfect.
