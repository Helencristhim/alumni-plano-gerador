Lesson 17 -- Leading the Room: Chairing & Steering a Meeting

Series
The West Wing / Succession — any scene where one person chairs a tense, high-stakes meeting

Watch how a strong chair opens with a clear purpose, cuts dominators off without insulting them, pulls the quiet expert in, and forces the room to a decision before time runs out. Notice the soft imperatives — "shall we", "would you mind", "could we" — that steer without barking orders.

Tip: pause whenever the chair redirects the room and label the move — open, bring in, park, protect the clock, close.

Podcast
HBR IdeaCast / WorkLife with Adam Grant — any episode on running better meetings or facilitation

Experts unpack what separates a chair from a participant: own the purpose and the clock, manage the floor deliberately, park what's off-topic, and never let a meeting dissolve without a recap and owners. The exact toolkit from this class, in real leadership voices — ideal prep before Victor chairs his cross-regional reviews at Brink's.

Tip: write one go-to line for each job — open, bring in, park, protect the clock, close — that you would actually say in the chair.

YouTube
Harvard Business Review / TED — "How to Run a Meeting" or "How to Save the World (or at Least Yourself) from Bad Meetings"

A short, practical guide to chairing: open with a stated purpose, time-box the agenda, steer the floor, and close with decisions and owners. A clean bridge from winning peers over without authority (Lesson 16) to running the very room you brought them into.

Tip: draft a five-line run sheet for a real meeting you will chair — one line per job — then read it aloud and check each soft imperative.
