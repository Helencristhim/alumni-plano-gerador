Leading the Room -- Chairing & Steering a Meeting

Chair and steer a meeting from start to finish. Open with a clear purpose and a timed agenda, manage the floor by bringing quiet people in and parking digressions as sidebars, protect the clock, drive the room to a decision, and close with a recap of decisions and owners before you adjourn. New words: convene, agenda, quorum, steer, digress, sidebar, recap, adjourn. Grammar focus: chairing language — soft imperatives "shall we / why don't we + bare verb", polite control "would you mind + -ing" and "could we + verb", plus steering markers "let's move on" and "in the interest of time".

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

The language a chair uses to run a room. Tap Listen to hear each word.

Convene -- to formally call people together to hold a meeting
"I'll convene the regional leads on Thursday at ten."
Agenda -- the planned list of items a meeting will work through
"Three items on the agenda, a time box on each."
Quorum -- the minimum number of people needed for a decision to count
"We have quorum, so we can decide today."
Steer -- to guide a meeting in the right direction and keep it on course
"When it drifts, I steer us back to the decision."
Digress -- to wander away from the main topic of the discussion
"He started to digress, so I gently brought us back."
Sidebar -- a side discussion set aside to deal with separately, not in the main meeting
"Let's take that offline as a sidebar after the call."
Recap -- to briefly go back over the main points and decisions at the end
"Let me recap: here's what we decided and who owns it."
Adjourn -- to formally end a meeting, usually on time
"We've decided everything; let's adjourn on time."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the chairing debrief and answer the questions.

The Growth Manager convened the regional review with three items on the agenda and a time box on each. He opened with the purpose, then ran the floor like a job. When a lead began to digress into the budget, he did not bark an order; he said, "Would you mind parking that as a sidebar for after?" — notice that after would you mind we use the -ing form, not to. To bring a quiet colleague in he asked, "Why don't we hear from Carla?" and to keep moving he said, "Shall we move on to the next item?" — after why don't we and shall we, the verb is bare, with no to. Whenever the room drifted, he guarded the clock: "In the interest of time, could we come back to that later?" Finally he refused to let it end loose: "Let me recap the decisions and owners," and once it was clear, he adjourned on time. The room had quorum, so the date was decided that hour — and for once nobody left unsure of what had been agreed.

1. Why does he say "Would you mind parking that?" instead of "Stop talking about budget"?
A A soft imperative keeps a senior peer on side; a bare order creates friction.
B The two sentences mean exactly the same and feel the same.
C Because he wants the budget discussed right now.
2. Which form is correct after "would you mind"?
A "Would you mind to keep it brief?" (mind + to + verb)
B "Would you mind keeping it brief?" (mind + -ing)
C "Would you mind keep it brief?" (mind + bare verb)
3. What form follows "shall we" and "why don't we"?
A The -ing form ("shall we moving on").
B The bare verb, with no "to" ("shall we move on").
C "to" + verb ("shall we to move on").

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Soft Imperatives & Steering Language

GRAMMAR

A chair instructs the room constantly — but always as an invitation.

StructureJobExample
Shall we / Why don't we + bare verbpropose the next step"Shall we move on to item two?"
Would you mind + -ingask politely to do / stop something"Would you mind keeping it brief?"
Could we + bare verbredirect gently"Could we come back to that later?"
In the interest of time / Let's move onsteering markers (guard the clock)"In the interest of time, let's move on."
Watch the trap: after would you mind use the -ing form ("mind keeping"), NOT "to". After shall we / why don't we / could we use the bare verb, NO "to" ("shall we start"). PT: o chair instrui a sala o tempo todo, mas sempre em forma de convite. Use "shall we / why don't we + verbo base" para propor ("shall we start?"), "would you mind + -ing" para pedir com educacao ("would you mind keeping it brief?") e "could we + verbo base" para redirecionar ("could we come back to that?"). Cuidado: depois de "would you mind" vem o -ing, NUNCA "to"; depois de "shall we / could we" vem o verbo base, sem "to". Marcadores como "in the interest of time" e "let's move on" protegem o relogio. Mesmo controle, muito menos atrito — a sala segue porque voce convidou, nao porque mandou.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best response for each chairing situation.

You're chairing a meeting that hasn't started. How do you open?
A "Let's just see where the conversation goes today."
B "We're here to lock the date and leave with an owner per region."
C "I don't really know why we're here, but let's start."
A senior peer keeps digressing. What's the cleanest way to steer?
A "Stop talking about that, it's off-topic."
B "Would you mind parking that as a sidebar for after?"
C "Would you mind to park that for after?"
You want to bring a quiet, well-informed colleague into the discussion. What's best?
A "Does anyone have anything to add? No? Moving on."
B "Why don't we hear from Carla — what's your read?"
C "Shall we to ask someone else for once?"
The meeting is ending. How do you make sure it actually counts?
A "We're out of time — bye, everyone."
B "Let me recap the decisions and owners, then we'll adjourn."
C "We'll figure out what we decided over email later."
