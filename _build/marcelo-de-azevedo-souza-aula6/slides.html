Chapter 4: The Code

Hedge and Concede

It could be argued · That said

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"It could be argued that tourism helps."

"There might be a downside to consider."

"Even though it creates jobs, prices rise."

"That said, I see your point."

Why green to soften an opinion, but blue to admit the other side?

ToolUseExample
Hedge (modal)softens a claim so it is not absoluteIt could be argued that ... / There might be ...
Concessionadmits the other side before your pointEven though ... / That said, ... / On the other hand, ...

A strong opinion, softened and balanced, sounds far more convincing than a loud, absolute one.

Language Focus

Hedge or Concession?

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ TOO ABSOLUTE

"Tourism is bad, period."

✓ HEDGED

"It could be argued that tourism does more harm than good."

✗ INCOMPLETE

"Even though it creates jobs."

✓ COMPLETE

"Even though it creates jobs, prices rise for locals."

Hedge an opinion so it is not absolute. And after a concession like Even though, finish the thought with your point.

Dialogue

A Dinner in Tirana

P
You have cycled through half the Balkans, Marcelo. Be honest, is all this tourism good for these little towns, or are we ruining them?
M
It is not simple. It could be argued that visitors bring money and jobs. In an emerging economy, that really matters.
P
True, but critics claim it raises prices and turns a real culture into a performance.
M
I acknowledge that. And I would point out that the money often flows away, deepening inequality. That said, some towns handle it well.
P
So what is your real perspective? Give me a straight answer.
M
The honest answer has more nuance than either of us wants. The towns with resilience, the ones that set their own terms, do fine. It depends on how you look at it.
