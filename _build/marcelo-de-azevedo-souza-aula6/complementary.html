Aula 6 -- Talking About the World

Docuseries
Explained (Netflix / Vox) -- any episode

Short, sharp episodes that take one current-events topic -- geopolitics, inequality, an emerging economy, a global debate -- and lay out both sides with real nuance. It is a model of the exact skill in this lesson: presenting an argument, acknowledging the other perspective, and hedging where the evidence is not clear.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Write down two phrases the narrator uses to hedge (it could be argued, some claim) and one that admits the other side (on the other hand).

Podcast
Throughline -- NPR, any episode

Two historians explain how the past shapes the news of today -- geopolitics, borders, inequality, resilience. They constantly weigh perspectives against each other, exactly the balanced, hedged thinking you practiced tonight, in clear, well-paced American English.

Tip: pick one episode on a topic you already follow. Pause twice and summarize the argument out loud, using "It could be argued that..." and "That said...".

Julia Galef: Why you think you're right -- even if you're wrong | TED

A clear, twelve-minute TED talk on the exact mindset behind tonight's lesson: how to notice your own bias, acknowledge the other side, and hold an opinion without clinging to it. Natural, well-paced American English on the art of thinking, and arguing, well.

Tip: watch once, then take one opinion you hold strongly and try to argue the other side out loud for two minutes.
