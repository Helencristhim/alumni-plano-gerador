Lesson 01 -- Pre-class

Who Is Lucia Serra in English? -- Mapping the Gap

Introducing yourself through your career, not just your job title. Key words: background, career, to lead, executive, achievement, challenge, presentation, audience, confident, fluency, to stand out, strength. Structures: present simple (facts about your career) vs present continuous (what you are working on these days), plus state verbs that never take -ing.

Vocabulary

Background -- your education, skills and past work experience

Career -- the series of jobs and roles you have over your working life

To lead -- to be in charge of a team or a project and guide it
"For years I led large engineering teams in three countries."
Executive -- a senior leader who makes big decisions in a company
"As an executive, I speak with boards and clients."
Achievement -- an important result that you worked hard to reach
"Building a global security team was a real achievement."
Challenge -- a difficult situation that tests your skills
"AI safety is the biggest challenge of my career."
Presentation -- a talk where you share your ideas with a group of people
"I give executive presentations almost every month."
Audience -- the people who watch and listen to you
"I want an American audience to trust me quickly."
Confident -- sure of yourself, your value and your ideas
"I sound confident in Portuguese, and I want the same in English."
Fluency -- the ability to speak smoothly, without stopping to search for words
"My goal this year is fluency in real interviews."
To stand out -- to be easy to notice because you are better or different
"I want my answers to stand out in an interview."
Strength -- something you are naturally very good at
"Clear thinking under pressure is one of my strengths."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Lucia has a long career in technology. She is a senior executive and, for many years, she led large teams in several countries. Today she works at a big tech company, in the safety of artificial intelligence. That is the biggest challenge of her career. These days she is preparing for the American job market. Right now she is improving her English, because she gives important presentations and she wants every audience to trust her. In Portuguese she is calm and confident; in English she still searches for words. Her goal is simple: the same fluency, in both languages.

1. Why does the text say "she is preparing for the American job market" and not "she prepares"?
A It is a current process, happening these days (present continuous).
B It is something she does every single day.
C It happened once, a long time ago.
2. "She works at a big tech company" uses the present simple because:
A It is happening at this exact second.
B It is a permanent fact about her job.
C It is temporary, just for this week.
3. The text says "she wants every audience to trust her", never "she is wanting". Why?
A Because the sentence is about the past.
B Because "want" is an irregular verb.
C Because "want" is a state verb, and state verbs do not take -ing.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Simple vs Present Continuous

GRAMMAR

How to say what is always true about your career and what you are working on right now.

FormUseExample
Present Simple
I / you / we + verb (he / she + -s)		Facts, routines and permanent situations - always true.I work in AI security.
Present Continuous
am / is / are + verb-ing		Right now, today, these days - temporary or unfinished.I am preparing for an interview.
Negativedo not / does not + verb · am not / is not / are not + -ingI do not read from a script. / She is not leading that team now.
QuestionDo / Does + subject + verb · Am / Is / Are + subject + -ingDo you lead a team? / Are you preparing for interviews?
State verbslike, love, want, need, know, believe, understand almost never take -ing: "I want a confident voice" (not "I am wanting").
Time wordsevery day, always, usually, never → simple · right now, today, this year, these days → continuous
