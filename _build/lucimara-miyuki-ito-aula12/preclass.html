Lesson 12 -- Pre-class

Hotels and Accommodation -- Checking In and Making Requests Like a Pro

In Lesson 11 you got off the plane at the airport. Now you arrive at your hotel in New York — and something is always a little off: the name is misspelled, the room faces the street, the air conditioning is faulty. This is the lesson for September 2026, the night you walk up to the front desk and handle everything yourself. Key words: lobby, key card, vacancy, minibar, double occupancy, adjoining room, rollaway bed, faulty, to settle the bill, to extend a stay. Structure: the indirect question — the polite way to ask for anything ("Could you tell me where the elevator is." / "I was wondering if I could move rooms." / "Do you know if breakfast is included."). The rule that catches everybody: after the soft opener, the word order goes back to normal ("where the elevator is", never "where is the elevator").

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Take your time — listen twice, and say it out loud. These are the ten words you will use at a hotel front desk, from the lobby to the moment you settle the bill.

Lobby -- the large open area near the entrance of a hotel, with seating and the front desk
"She crossed the bright lobby and gave her name at the front desk."
Key card -- the flat plastic card that unlocks your hotel room door
"He slid a key card across the counter for room three-oh-four."
Vacancy -- a room that is free and available to book at the hotel
"Luckily the hotel had a vacancy on a higher, quieter floor."
Minibar -- the small fridge in a hotel room with drinks and snacks you can buy
"She noticed the minibar, the courtyard view, and the quiet."
Double occupancy -- a room booked and priced for two people sharing it
"The courtyard room was the same rate for double occupancy."
Adjoining room -- a room connected to the one next to it by an inside door
"She asked whether they could set up an adjoining room for her colleague."
Rollaway bed -- an extra folding bed the hotel brings up for one more guest
"For a third guest, the hotel can bring up a rollaway bed."
Faulty -- not working correctly; broken (for a device, like an air conditioner)
"The air conditioning was faulty: it hummed loudly and blew warm air."
To settle the bill -- to pay the final total for your whole stay, usually at check-out
"He moved everything onto a single bill she could settle when she left."
To extend a stay -- to stay for one or more extra nights beyond your original booking
"I was wondering if I could extend my stay by one night, until Sunday."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions. Notice how every request is a polite indirect question.

Lucimara reaches the front desk in the lobby and gives her name. The clerk cannot find it. "Could you tell me if the reservation might be under a different spelling?" she asks. "It's Ito, I-T-O." He checks again, and there it is. He hands her a key card for a room on the first floor, facing the street.

She does not demand a better room. She asks. "I was wondering if it would be possible to move to a higher, quieter floor. Do you know whether anything is available facing the back?" The clerk finds a vacancy on the fourteenth floor at the same rate for double occupancy.

In the new room, the air conditioning is faulty. She calls the desk. "I'm sorry to bother you, but the AC seems to be broken. Would you mind sending someone up, and could you tell me how long it might take?" An engineer arrives in fifteen minutes.

On the last morning she has one more request. "Could you tell me if I could extend my stay by one night, and settle the bill when I leave on Sunday?" The clerk confirms the room is free and moves everything onto one bill. Two years ago, she would have pointed and hoped. This week she asked, politely, and got everything she needed.

1. "Could you tell me where the elevator is?" Why is it "where the elevator is" and not "where is the elevator"?
A Both are the same; the order never changes.
B After a soft opener, the word order goes back to normal — subject ("the elevator") before verb ("is"), with no inversion.
C Because "elevator" is a plural word.
2. "Do you know if breakfast is included?" Why is "if" there?
A Because every polite question needs "if".
B Because it is a yes/no question — a yes/no question takes "if" or "whether" after the opener.
C Because "if" means the same as "where".
3. Why does "I was wondering if I could move rooms" sound more polite than "Give me another room"?
A The soft opener turns an order into a favor, so the other person wants to help.
B It is longer, and longer is always better.
C They mean exactly the same thing and sound the same.
4. A vacancy and an adjoining room — what is the difference?
A They are two words for the same thing.
B A vacancy is any free, available room; an adjoining room is one connected to yours by an inside door.
C A vacancy is on the first floor and an adjoining room is on a higher floor.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Indirect Questions (the polite way to ask)

GRAMMAR

Put a soft opener in front of your question, then say the rest as a calm, normal sentence. The opener does all the polite work.

PartFormExample
The soft openerCould you tell me / Do you know / I was wondering if / Would it be possible to"Could you tell me…"
Wh- questionopener + where/when/how + normal order"Could you tell me where the elevator is?"
Yes / No questionopener + if / whether + normal order"Do you know if breakfast is included?"
A requestI was wondering if I could… / Would it be possible to + base verb"I was wondering if I could move rooms."
The one ruleAfter the opener, drop the inversion: the verb comes AFTER the subject, exactly like a normal sentence. "Where is it?" → "…where it is." "Is it ready?" → "…if it is ready."

Watch out -- the classic mistakes: (1) keeping the inversion — "Could you tell me where is the elevator" should be "where the elevator is". (2) forgetting "if"/"whether" in a yes/no question — "Do you know breakfast is included" should be "Do you know if breakfast is included".

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer. Question 1 is the trap: after the opener, never keep the question word order.

You want to ask, politely, where the gym is. You say:
A "Could you tell me where is the gym?"
B "Could you tell me where the gym is?" (normal order after the opener)
C "Where is gym, please tell me now?"
Your room faces a noisy street. The most natural, polite request is:
A "I was wondering if it would be possible to move to a higher, quieter floor."
B "Give me another room, this one is bad."
C "I want quiet room now."
You want to check whether breakfast is included. The correct indirect question is:
A "Do you know is breakfast included?"
B "Do you know if breakfast is included?" (yes/no → if)
C "Do you know breakfast included?"
The air conditioning does not work. The best way to report it is:
A "Your air conditioning is broken, fix it."
B "I'm sorry to bother you, but the air conditioning seems to be faulty. Would you mind sending someone up?"
C Say nothing and just open the window.
It's your last morning and you want one more night. You say:
A "Could you tell me if I could extend my stay by one night and settle the bill on Sunday?"
B "I stay more. You change it."
C "Could you tell me if can I extend my stay?"
