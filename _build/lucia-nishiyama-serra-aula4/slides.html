Chapter 4: The First Conditional

If + present, will + verb

Predictions, risks, and recommendations

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"If we implement this framework, we will reduce our exposure."

"If leadership aligns on this, we will be able to scale."

"If we do not act, our exposure will grow."

"If you ask me about governance, I will tell you why it matters."

What tense comes after if? What comes in the other half of each sentence?

PartFormExample
The condition
(the "if" part)		If + present simpleIf we enforce this framework...
The result
(the prediction)		will + base verb...we will reduce our exposure.
Connectorsunless = if ... not (a warning) · as long as / provided that = only on this condition.

Real and likely future. After if, use the present -- never will. Use will (not would) to sound confident.

Grammar Practice

Say It in First Conditional

Say the full sentence out loud, then tap to check

"If we ___enforce the policy, teams will follow it." (enforce -- the condition)
"If we set a clear threshold, we ___will know when to pause a project." (will / know)
"___Unless leadership aligns, we won't be able to scale." (a warning = if ... not)
"As long as we ___stay compliant, we will keep their trust." (stay -- the condition)
"If the board approves, we ___will roll out the framework." (will / roll out)
Detail

Unless vs As Long As

UNLESS = if ... not

A warning

"Unless we act, we will fall behind."

= If we do not act, we will fall behind.

AS LONG AS = only if

A promise / a condition

"As long as we stay compliant, we will keep their trust."

= only on this one condition.

Both keep the same shape: the condition uses the present, the result uses will.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"If we will act now, we will reduce the risk."
"If we act now, we will reduce the risk."
"If we enforce this, no one follows it." (weak, no prediction)
"If we do not enforce this, no one will follow it."
"If we roll this out, we would reduce our exposure."
"If we roll this out, we will reduce our exposure."

After if, use the present, never will. In a real, likely plan, the result takes will -- would is the second conditional, for hypothetical ideas.

Dialogue

A Board Q&A

M
Thanks for the briefing, Lucia. Let me push back a little. Why should the board fund this now?
L
Fair question. If we act this quarter, we will get ahead of the risk. If we wait, our exposure will only grow.
M
And if we do fund it, what changes in practice?
L
If we roll out this framework and enforce it, we will mitigate most of the risk within one quarter.
M
Some people will say this is too strict. What do you tell them?
L
Unless we set a clear threshold, every team will guess. As long as the rule is the same for everyone, people will trust it.
M
That is a strong case. You have the room.
Did You Catch It?

About the Q&A

1. What does Mark do at the start of the briefing?
He pushes back and asks why the board should fund the plan now.
2. What concern does Mark raise about the plan?
That some people will say it is too strict.
3. How does Mark end the conversation?
He says it is a strong case and tells her she has the room.

0:000:00
1. Why does the speaker say exposure is too high right now?
Because there is a framework on paper, but no one enforces it.
2. What does he say will happen if they roll out the new controls?
They will mitigate most of the risk within one quarter.
3. What is his warning if the team does not align now?
Their exposure will only grow, and it will be much harder to fix next year.
Listening 2

A Coach's One Tip

A presentation coach shares one key tip. Sound first -- no text.

0:000:00
1. What small grammar change does the coach recommend?
Stop saying "maybe" and use the first conditional: if we do this, we will get that.
2. Why does "will" work so well, according to her?
Because "will" sounds like a decision, not a doubt.
3. What does she suggest you do before your next presentation?
Write three first-conditional sentences and practice them out loud until they feel natural.
