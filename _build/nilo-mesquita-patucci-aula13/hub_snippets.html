Handling Questions -- Answering with Confidence

Handle the Q&A after a presentation: ask politely with indirect questions (Could you tell me..., May I ask..., Do you know if...), clarify, and stay calm under pressure. New Q&A vocabulary: rephrase, address, break down, clarify, concern, fair point, get back to, off hand, follow-up, bring up, go into detail, hot seat.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Rephrase -- to say the same idea with different words
"Sorry, could you rephrase the question?" (Reformular)
Address -- to deal with a question or topic directly
"Let me address your point about the budget." (Tratar / responder)
Break down -- to divide something complex into smaller parts
"Let me break down your question into two parts." (Dividir em partes)
Clarify -- to make something clearer and easier to understand
"Could you clarify what you mean by risk?" (Esclarecer)
Concern -- a worry or problem someone raises
"I understand your concern about the cost." (Preocupação)
Fair point -- a reasonable comment you agree with
"That is a fair point. Let me explain." (Boa observação)
Get back to -- to give someone an answer at a later time
"Let me get back to you by email tomorrow." (Retornar depois)
Off hand -- right now, from memory, without checking
"I do not have that figure off hand." (De cabeça / na hora)
Follow-up -- a further question about the same topic
"Do you have a follow-up question?" (Pergunta de acompanhamento)
Bring up -- to start talking about a subject or topic
"Thank you for bringing up the timeline." (Trazer à tona / mencionar)
Go into detail -- to explain something with a lot of information
"I can go into detail after the meeting." (Detalhar / aprofundar)
Hot seat -- the difficult position of answering tough questions
"In the Q and A, you are in the hot seat." (Saia justa / berlinda)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

After his presentation, Nilo faces the committee in the Q&A. To sound polite and professional, he avoids blunt, direct questions and uses indirect ones. Instead of "What is the budget?", he says "Could you tell me what the budget is?" Instead of "Why do you think that?", he says "May I ask why you think that?" Notice the pattern: after the polite opener, the words follow statement order (the budget is, not is the budget), and there is no do or does. For yes/no questions, he adds if: "Do you know if the report is ready?" When a question is hard, Nilo stays calm: "That is a fair point, but I do not have that figure off hand. Let me get back to you by email."

1. Which sentence is the correct indirect question?
A Could you tell me what the budget is?
B Could you tell me what is the budget?
C Could you tell me what does the budget?
2. For a yes/no question, which word does Nilo add?
A Do you know that the report is ready?
B Do you know if the report is ready?
C Do you know is the report ready?
3. What does Nilo do when he does not have an answer?
A He says it is a fair point and offers to get back to them by email.
B He guesses a number to sound confident.
C He ignores the question and moves on.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Indirect Questions

GRAMMAR

Usado para perguntar de forma educada e profissional. / Used to ask politely and professionally.

Direct / DiretaPolite opener / Início educadoIndirect / Indireta
What is the budget?Could you tell meCould you tell me what the budget is?
Why do you think that?May I askMay I ask why you think that?
Is the report ready?Do you know ifDo you know if the report is ready?
Can you clarify that?I was wondering ifI was wondering if you could clarify that.

Key point / Ponto-chave: depois do início educado, use ordem de afirmação (the budget is, não is the budget) e sem do / does. Para perguntas de sim/não, acrescente if ou whether. / After the opener, use statement order with no do/does, and add if for yes/no questions.
