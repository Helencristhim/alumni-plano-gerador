The Distinction

Hold Point vs Witness Point

Hold point

The work STOPS. Nobody continues until you have inspected and released it.

You have the power. The supplier waits for you.

Witness point

You are invited. If you do not come, the work goes on without you.

The supplier has the power. You lose the moment.

This is why a supplier will quietly turn your hold points into witness points in the ITP — and why you push back on it.

Chapter 3: The Code

Judgment Without Accusation

"You didn't deliver."  →  "This should have been delivered."

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"The supplier should have submitted the ITP by last Friday."

"There must have been a miscommunication about the hold points."

"The waiver might have been approved by the wrong authority."

"They could not have run the FAT — the cabinet was still in the factory."

All four look back at the past. But should, must, might and could not do four different jobs. What are they?

FormFunctionExample
should have + past participleAn obligation that was NOT met. The criticism is on the action, not on the person."The ITP should have been submitted by March 10th."
must have + past participleA logical deduction: I am almost sure this is what happened, from the evidence."There must have been a misunderstanding on your side."
might / may have + past participleAn uncertain possibility. You open a door without accusing anyone."The drawings might have been sent to the wrong contact."
could not have + past participleImpossibility. It was simply not possible — the evidence rules it out."They could not have run the FAT without the cabinet."
In speech"have" reduces to /əv/. This is what you actually hear on a call."It should've been flagged up." · "They must've missed it."
Why it matters"You didn't deliver" attacks a person and the meeting stops. "This should have been delivered" states a fact about the process — and the supplier can agree with it without losing face. That is how you get a corrective action instead of a fight.
Meaning

Four Modals, Four Jobs

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"The supplier should had submitted the ITP last Friday."
"The supplier should have submitted the ITP last Friday."
"It must be a miscommunication about the hold points last month."
"There must have been a miscommunication about the hold points last month."

After a modal, the verb is always have — never had. And to deduce about the past, you need must have been, not must be.

Dialogue

The Status Call

J
Thanks for joining, Mei. Let's start with the open items. I'd like to follow up on the revised baseline schedule — it was due three weeks ago.
M
Yes, I know. The schedule is still with our engineering team in Shanghai.
J
I understand. But it should have reached us on June 10th, and we are now planning the site acceptance test without a date. There must have been a bottleneck on your side.
M
Possibly. My team might have been waiting for the interface document from your side.
J
To some extent I understand your position, however the interface document was signed in April. I have to push back on that. And I'd like to flag up a second issue: the inspection test plan you sent has no hold points.
M
That was intentional. We proposed witness points instead, to protect the production schedule.
J
That is the elephant in the room, and it should have been discussed with us before the ITP was submitted. So let's agree on a corrective action: you re-issue the ITP with the four hold points, and you send the schedule by Friday. Can we reach a consensus on that?
M
Friday is tight, but yes. I will send both by Friday, and I will copy my director, so that nobody is surprised later.
Comprehension

About Mei

1. Where is the revised baseline schedule, according to Mei?
Still with her engineering team in Shanghai.
2. How does Mei explain the delay — and why is that a problem?
She suggests her team might have been waiting for the interface document, which moves the responsibility to Motiva. It is not true: the document was signed in April.
3. Why did she remove the hold points from the ITP?
It was intentional. She proposed witness points to protect her own production schedule.
4. What exactly does she commit to at the end?
To send the re-issued ITP and the schedule by Friday, and to copy her director.

Listening 2

Three Rules About Escalation

Your project director, before the call. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does the director say escalation is NOT?
A punishment. It is simply the next step in the process when a supplier misses a date twice.
2. Why must you never escalate a problem you have not flagged up first?
Because your counterpart would hear about it from his own director. That is an ambush, and he will never help you again.
3. What does he say about waivers?
A waiver that saves the supplier three days in the factory can cost three weeks during the site acceptance test. Push back on it.
