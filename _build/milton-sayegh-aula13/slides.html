A Good Deal Is Not a Win

In a real negotiation, both sides give a little and both sides leave with something. The goal is not to crush the buyer — it is to close a deal you can both keep.

Key Expressions

"I can make one concession on payment if you can move on volume." — trades, not gives
"Would you be open to paying in three installments instead of a deeper discount?" — reframes the ask

Chapter 3

Asking Softly

Polite & indirect questions

Grammar Discovery

Same Ask, Softer Tone

Both columns ask for the same thing — why is the right one easier to say yes to?

Direct: "How much can you discount?"

Could you tell me how much you can discount?

Direct: "Will you lower the markup?"

Would you mind lowering the markup?

Direct: "Can you accept installments?"

I was wondering if you could accept installments.

Soft opener + the rest in normal statement order = a polite, indirect question.

Grammar Rule

Polite & Indirect Questions

OpenerWhat followsExample
Could you tell me... / Do you know...subject + verb (statement order, no inversion)"Could you tell me what the markup is?" (NOT "what is the markup")
Would you mind...verb + -ing"Would you mind lowering the price a little?"
I was wondering if...subject + could / would + base"I was wondering if you could accept installments."
Watch the formNo "do / does / did" inside"Do you know when they decide?" (NOT "when do they decide")
Watch Out

Common Mistake

Could you tell me what is the markup?
Could you tell me what the markup is?

Inside an indirect question, keep statement order (subject + verb) and drop do / does / did. The opener already makes it a question.

Practice

Soften the Question

Direct: "How big is the discount?" → Could you tell me _____? → how big the discount is (statement order)
Direct: "Will you lower the markup?" → Would you mind _____? → lowering the markup (mind + -ing)
Direct: "Can you accept installments?" → I was wondering if _____. → you could accept installments
Direct: "When do they decide?" → Do you know _____? → when they decide (no "do", statement order)
Chapter 4

At the Table

Milton and Diane settle the price

Dialogue

Milton & Diane

D
Milton, we love the collection. But I have to negotiate the price — your markup is high for our floor.
M
I understand. Could you tell me how much room you need on price?
D
My counteroffer is twelve percent off. In return, we'd commit to a larger volume.
M
Twelve is a real concession to ask for. I was wondering if eight would work with that volume.
D
Let's compromise. Ten percent — and would you mind splitting it into three installments?
M
That's fair. Ten percent over three installments — let's not reach a deadlock over the last point. We can settle this today.

Practice

Your Turn to Negotiate

From quick replies to a full counteroffer

Quick Fire

Negotiate on the Spot

1 / 5

1. The buyer asks for fifteen percent off. Reply with a lower counteroffer, politely.

"That's a real concession to ask for — I was wondering if eight would work."

2. You want to know their maximum volume. Ask with an indirect question.

"Could you tell me what volume you can commit to?"

3. You'd rather give better payment terms than a deeper discount. Propose the compromise.

"Would you be open to three installments instead of a bigger discount?"

4. The talk is stuck on one point. Keep it from breaking down.

"Let's not reach a deadlock over the last point — we can settle this today."

5. You're ready to close. Ask them to confirm, politely and indirectly.

"I was wondering if you'd be happy to settle on ten percent over three installments."

Spot the Error

Fix the Question

"Could you tell me what is the markup?"
"Could you tell me what the markup is?" (statement order, no inversion)
"Do you know when do they decide?"
"Do you know when they decide?" (drop "do" inside)
"Would you mind to lower the price?"
"Would you mind lowering the price?" (mind + -ing)
"I was wondering you could accept installments."
"I was wondering if you could accept installments." (add "if")
