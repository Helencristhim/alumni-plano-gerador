Aula 14 -- Pre-class

Damage Control -- Delivering Bad News & Reassuring Stakeholders

Deliver bad news the right way and steady the room. Lead in with an honest softening opener, state the setback plainly, take ownership in active voice without over-apologizing, then reassure with a concrete forward plan. New words: setback, shortfall, mitigate, contingency, reassure, accountability, transparent, root cause, on track, course-correct, downside, head off. Grammar focus: active voice for owning a miss (vs passive blame-shifting), will for firm promises, going to / be + -ing for arranged steps, and the first conditional for contingencies.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

The language that delivers bad news cleanly and keeps trust. Tap Listen to hear each word.

Setback -- a problem that delays or reverses progress on a plan
"The supplier delay was a real setback for the rollout."
Shortfall -- the amount by which a result falls below the target or budget
"We had a shortfall of about ten percent against the target."
Mitigate -- to reduce the seriousness or impact of a problem
"We mitigated the loss by shifting onto two core accounts."
Contingency -- a backup plan ready in case the first plan fails
"Our contingency is to fall back on the reserve budget."
Reassure -- to remove someone's worry and restore their confidence
"I reassured the client that the core service would not be touched."
Accountability -- being answerable for a result and willing to own the outcome
"Accountability means I name the miss before anyone asks."
Transparent -- open and honest, sharing the full picture without hiding it
"Being transparent about the shortfall earned me more trust."
Root cause -- the real, underlying reason behind a problem, not the symptom
"The root cause was a late forecast, not the team's effort."
On track -- progressing as planned, likely to meet the goal on time
"After the fix, the recovery plan is back on track."
Course-correct -- to adjust the plan to get back toward the goal after a miss
"We're meeting the supplier on Thursday to course-correct."
Downside -- the worst-case loss or risk if things go wrong
"I want to know the downside is covered if Thursday fails."
Head off -- to prevent a problem before it has the chance to happen
"A summary tonight will head off any surprises at the board."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the damage-control debrief and answer the questions.

The quarter came in below plan, so the Growth Manager had to call a key stakeholder before the board did. He led in honestly: "There's no easy way to say this." Then he owned it in active voice — "We missed the target" — instead of the passive blame-shift "mistakes were made." He showed accountability, named the root cause, and stayed transparent about the shortfall. To reassure her, he switched to the future: "We will close the shortfall by quarter end" (a firm promise), and "We're meeting the supplier on Thursday to course-correct" (an arranged step). To mitigate the downside, he added a contingency with the first conditional: "If the pilot slips again, we will pull the reserve." A one-page summary that night would head off any surprises and keep the recovery on track.

1. Why does he say "We missed the target" instead of "Mistakes were made"?
A Active voice owns the miss directly; the passive shifts blame and sounds evasive.
B The two mean the same; he just likes shorter sentences.
C "Mistakes were made" is grammatically wrong.
2. Why does he use "will" for closing the shortfall but "we're meeting" for Thursday?
A The two are interchangeable; he chose at random.
B "Will" is a firm promise; "we're meeting" is an arranged step already fixed (be + -ing).
C "Will" is past and "we're meeting" is future.
3. How does he cover the downside if Thursday does not work?
A He promises nothing will ever go wrong.
B He adds a first-conditional contingency: if the pilot slips, they will pull the reserve.
C He refuses to discuss the downside.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Damage-Control Toolkit

GRAMMAR

Each move in delivering bad news has its own structure.

MoveStructureExample
Lead inSoftening opener + the plain fact"There's no easy way to say this: we came in below plan."
Own it (active)We + past simple, active voice + I take responsibility"We missed the target, and I take responsibility."
Firm promisewill + base verb"We will close the shortfall by quarter end."
Arranged stepgoing to / be + -ing (a fixed plan)"We're meeting the supplier on Thursday."
ContingencyIf + present simple, … will + base"If the pilot slips, we will pull the reserve."
Watch the trap: own the miss in ACTIVE voice ("we missed it"), never passive blame-shifting ("mistakes were made"). Apologize once, not five times. In the first conditional, the if-clause stays in the present, NOT "if it will slip". PT: cada movimento de uma ma noticia tem sua estrutura -- abra com um lead-in honesto, assuma o erro na voz ativa (nunca no passivo "mistakes were made", que soa evasivo), peca desculpa UMA vez, e ai pivote para o futuro: will para uma promessa firme, going to / be + -ing para um passo ja agendado, e o first conditional (if + presente, will + base) para a contingencia. Seja transparente sobre o passado e concreto sobre o futuro.
