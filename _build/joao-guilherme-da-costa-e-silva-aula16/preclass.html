Lesson 16 -- Pre-class

Holding Your Ground -- Defending Technical Decisions Under Pressure

A Spanish signaling supplier says the four-week overrun on Line 4 is Motiva's fault and files an extension-of-time claim you do not accept. This is how you hold your ground without conceding: acknowledge the concern once, dispute the claim with a dated record, and close with a next step. Key words: schedule overrun, extension of time, burden of proof, to substantiate, to dispute, unfounded, to attribute, to reserve your rights, without prejudice, to rebut, back-charge, concurrent delay. Structure: the mandative subjunctive -- "It is essential that the supplier submit the plan", "We recommend that the team be notified".

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Last week you made your case in a presentation. Tonight the case comes back at you: a supplier claims the delay was your fault and files a claim. These are the words of a formal dispute -- how you name a delay claim, how you challenge it, and how you protect your position without closing the door. Listen to each term and read the example.

Schedule overrun -- the amount of time a project runs past its agreed completion date
"The schedule overrun is four weeks, and the baseline shows the cause was not on our side."
Extension of time -- extra contract time a supplier formally requests to cover a delay
"The supplier has filed an extension-of-time claim we do not accept."
Burden of proof -- the duty to prove that a claim is true
"The burden of proof for an extension of time rests with the party requesting it."
To substantiate -- to support a claim with documented evidence
"A claim you cannot substantiate from the sign-off log will not stand."
To dispute -- to formally challenge that a claim or charge is correct
"We dispute the claim that the delay was caused by our documentation."
Unfounded -- not supported by facts or evidence
"The claim that we approved the drawings late is unfounded on the record."
To attribute -- to state that something was caused by a particular party or thing
"The supplier attributes the delay to us; the timeline attributes it to the late units."
To reserve your rights -- to keep the option of taking formal action later
"We reserve our rights under the penalty clause while we resolve this in good faith."
Without prejudice -- said or written so it cannot be used against you later in a dispute
"Without prejudice, I can offer to share the cost of the retest while we review the log."
To rebut -- to give evidence or arguments that show a claim is wrong
"Let me rebut that with the inspection log carried out on the nineteenth."
Back-charge -- a cost the buyer deducts from a supplier for work the buyer had to correct
"If the units fail again, Motiva will raise a back-charge for the rework."
Concurrent delay -- a delay for which both parties are partly responsible at the same time
"There is no concurrent delay here; the record points to one side only."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

Ibersign, a Spanish signaling supplier, has filed an extension of time for a four-week schedule overrun on Line 4, and it attributes the delay to Motiva's late approval of the interface drawings. João Guilherme's job this afternoon is not to win the argument and not to give in. It is to hold his ground: acknowledge the concern once, then dispute the claim with the dated record.

The grammar that makes a position sound like a requirement instead of a plea is the mandative subjunctive. After words of urgency or requirement, the verb drops its ending and stays in the base form for every subject. "It is essential that the supplier submit the revised plan" -- submit, not submits. "We recommend that the team be notified in writing" -- be, not is. The negative is simply not + base: "We request that the units not be shipped until the fix is confirmed" -- no do or does. The base form is what turns an opinion ("the supplier needs to submit") into a stated term.

So João holds the line. The burden of proof for the extension rests with Ibersign, and the claim is unfounded on the sign-off log: the drawings were approved on the fourth, and the units arrived six weeks later, so there is no concurrent delay on Motiva's side. He can rebut the claim, substantiate every date, and still keep the relationship: without prejudice, he recommends both sides review the log before anyone talks about a back-charge, and he reserves Motiva's rights under the penalty clause. Firm on the facts, warm on the person, the door still open.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Mandative Subjunctive

GRAMMAR

After certain words of requirement, the verb loses its ending and its do/does. One question decides the form: is this a stated requirement, or just an opinion? If it is a requirement, use the base form.

TriggerSubjunctive formWhy
it is essential / critical / important that…the supplier submit the planBase form for every subject → no -s on he/she/it. It states a requirement.
require / recommend / insist / request / propose that…the team be notifiedThe subjunctive of be is simply be for all persons → not is / are.
the negativethe units not be shippedJust not + base → no do/does. "not ship", "not be filed".
opinion vs requirement"the supplier submits" → opinion"the supplier submit" → a stated term. Same words, different force.
Why it mattersIn a dispute, the subjunctive lets you be firm and impersonal at once. "It is critical that the schedule remain unchanged" is not an attack and not a plea — it is a condition you are setting. It is the grammar of a person who sets the terms, not one who reacts to them.

Watch out (habits carried over from Portuguese): (1) "It is essential that the supplier submits…" — after "essential that", drop the -s: "the supplier submit". (2) "We recommend that the team is notified" — use be: "the team be notified". (3) "We request that the units do not ship" — the negative is not + base: "the units not be shipped".

Diplomatic hedging (firm but not aggressive): "To some extent I understand the pressure, however…", "One could argue that the baseline was already agreed", "With respect, the record doesn't support that", "I hear the concern, but I can't concede a point the record doesn't support."

The question to ask before you write a formal clause: "Is this a requirement, or my opinion?" A requirement → base form (submit, be, remain). That one test gives you the whole grammar.

Stage 2: Hold Your Ground in Order

Order

Put the moves of holding your ground in order -- from acknowledging the concern to closing with a next step.

?State the requirement in the subjunctive: it is essential that any claim be substantiated from the log first.
?Acknowledge the concern once, genuinely: "I understand, and I've taken it seriously."
?Close with a next step, not a verdict: without prejudice, review the log together and share the retest cost.
?Dispute the claim with the dated record: the drawings were approved on the fourth, countersigned.
?Hold the line on the burden of proof: I can't concede a point the record doesn't support.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer for every real moment of the claim meeting -- the accusation, the requirement, the pressure on cost, and the close.

The supplier says: "The delay is on your side." Your strongest first move is:
A "Well, maybe we were a little late, I'm not sure…"
B "I understand the concern, and I've taken it seriously — but the dated sign-off log doesn't support it."
C "That's absurd — it's your equipment that failed."
You need to state a formal requirement about the revised plan. You say:
A "It is essential that the supplier submits the plan."
B "It is essential that the supplier submit a revised plan that accounts for the two failed units."
C "It is essential that the supplier is submitting the plan."
The supplier pushes: "Someone has to pay for this retest." You hold the line by saying:
A "To some extent I understand the pressure — but the burden of proof rests with the party requesting the extension, and I can't concede what the record doesn't support."
B "Fine, we'll cover it — I don't want a conflict."
C "That's your problem, not ours."
The claim is unfounded on the log. To rebut it without heat, you say:
A "You're wrong and you know it."
B "Let me rebut that with the record: the drawings were approved on the fourth, so the timeline attributes the overrun to the late delivery."
C "I'd rather not get into the details."
You want to protect Motiva without closing the door. You say:
A "We reserve our rights under the penalty clause, but — without prejudice — I recommend we review the log together and share the retest cost pending that review."
B "This is final. There's nothing more to discuss."
C "Let's just forget the whole thing."
