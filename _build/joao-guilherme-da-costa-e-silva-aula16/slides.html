Impact

Three Ways to Answer the Same Claim

Rank them, say why, then click to compare

A. "Well… maybe we were a little late on the drawings, I'm not sure, we can look into it…"
Conceding. It sounds reasonable and humble, but the moment you agree to a point you have not checked, you pick up the burden of proof and carry it for them. The back-charge conversation is now lost — you conceded before you read the record.
B. "That's absurd. It's your equipment that failed, not our approval. Don't put this on us."
Attacking. You may be right on the facts, but you have made it personal, and next year you still have to work with this supplier. You win the argument and lose the relationship — and a defensive tone reads as someone who is not sure of the record.
C. "I understand the concern, and I've taken it seriously. But the dated sign-off log doesn't support it — and I'd rather review it together than trade blame."
Holding ground. You acknowledge the concern once, you hold firm on the facts with the record, and you keep the door open. Firm on the facts, soft on the person. This is the whole skill of tonight.

The Tool

The Defender's Move

1 — Acknowledge

Once, genuinely. "I understand the concern, and I've taken it seriously." It costs nothing and it lowers the temperature.

2 — Hold on the Facts

The burden of proof is on them. Put the record on the table. "The drawings were approved on the fourth; the units arrived six weeks later."

3 — Stay Diplomatic

Firm on the point, soft on the person. "To some extent I understand the pressure — but I can't concede what the record doesn't support."

4 — A Next Step

Not a verdict. "Let's review the sign-off log together before anyone talks about a back-charge." The door stays open.

Most engineers either concede too early — and pick up the burden of proof — or turn defensive and lose the relationship. Acknowledge once, hold on the facts, stay warm, offer a next step. That is what holding your ground actually looks like.

Chapter 3: The Code

The Verb That Sets the Terms

The mandative subjunctive: "It is essential that the supplier submit" — base form, no -s.

Grammar Discovery

Why Does the Verb Look Wrong?

"It is essential that the supplier submit the revised test plan."

"Motiva requires that Ibersign account for the two failed units."

"We recommend that the team be notified in writing."

"We request that the units not be shipped until the fix is confirmed."

Every green verb is in the base form — submit, not submits; be, not is; not be shipped, not does not ship. What word comes just before "that" each time — and why does the ordinary -s and do/does disappear?

TriggerSubjunctive formWhy
it is essential / critical / important that…the supplier submit (base form, no -s)The verb loses its ending for every subject → it states a requirement, not an opinion.
require / recommend / insist / request / propose that…the team be notified (be, not is/are)Verbs of requiring take the same base form → be is the subjunctive of be for all persons.
the negativethe units not be shipped (not + base)No do/does. Just not + base verb → "not ship", "not be filed".
compare: ordinary present"the supplier submits" → my opinion"the supplier submit" → a stated term. Same words, very different force.
Why it mattersIn a dispute, the subjunctive lets you be firm and impersonal at once. "It is essential that the schedule remain unchanged" is not an attack and not a plea — it is a condition you are setting. It is the grammar of a person who sets the terms, not one who reacts to them.
Meaning

One Form Sets the Terms

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"It is essential that the supplier submits the revised plan."
"It is essential that the supplier submit the revised plan."
"We recommend that the team is notified in writing."
"We recommend that the team be notified in writing."

After it is essential / we require / we recommend that, the verb stays in the base form for every subject: submit, not submits; be notified, not is notified. The reflex to add an -s or an is comes straight from Portuguese agreement — and it turns a stated requirement back into a casual opinion.

About the Meeting

Did You Follow It?

1. What does Carmen claim, and what does she want from Motiva?
She claims Motiva approved Ibersign's interface drawings late, causing the four-week overrun. She is filing an extension-of-time claim and expects Motiva to carry the retest cost.
2. What single piece of the record does João use to rebut the claim, and what does the timeline show?
The dated sign-off log: the drawings were approved on 4 March, countersigned by Ibersign's own project engineer. The units left Shenzhen six weeks later, so the timeline attributes the overrun to the late delivery, not to Motiva — the claim is unfounded on the record.
3. How does João acknowledge Carmen's concern without conceding the point?
He says he understands the concern and has taken it seriously, and admits the pressure she is under — but he still disputes the claim, keeps the burden of proof on the party requesting the extension, and refuses to concede a point the record does not support.
4. What next step does João offer, and how does he keep the door open?
Without prejudice, he proposes that both sides review the sign-off log together before any back-charge, and that the retest cost be shared pending that review. Motiva reserves its rights under the penalty clause, but the offer keeps the relationship and the commissioning date intact.

Listening 2

Three Rules for Holding the Line

A claims advisor. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What is the first rule — separating the claim from the person — and why does it help?
When the supplier says the delay is your fault, that is a negotiating position, not an attack. The engineer who takes it personally defends his character instead of the facts — and the facts are the only thing that wins. Hear the claim as information about what she wants, and your shoulders come down.
2. What is the difference between acknowledging and conceding, and why does it matter?
Acknowledging costs nothing ("I understand why you see it that way, let me look at the record") and keeps the burden of proof on the party making the claim. Conceding ("yes, that was probably our fault") picks the burden up and carries it for them. Never concede what you have not verified; if a claim is unfounded, say so plainly but without heat.
3. What is the third rule about "the grammar of authority", and what example does he give?
The subjunctive. "It is essential that the supplier submit the revised test plan" — the base form (submit, not submits) makes it sound like a norm, not a favor. "It is critical that the schedule remain unchanged." "We recommend that the team be notified." It is the language of a person who sets the terms, not one who reacts to them.
