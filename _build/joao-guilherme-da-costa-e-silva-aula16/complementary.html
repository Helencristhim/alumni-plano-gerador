Talk
Chris Voss — "Never Split the Difference" (TEDxUniversityofNevada, ~17 min)

A former FBI lead hostage negotiator explains the exact skill you drilled tonight: how to stay firm on your position while staying warm on the person across the table. His "tactical empathy" is what turns "I understand the concern, and I've taken it seriously" from a soft phrase into a tool — you acknowledge the other side fully, and it lowers the temperature without conceding an inch. Watch it as the mindset behind holding your ground: the hostile claim is information about what they want, not an attack on you.

Tip: as you watch, write down two of his phrases for acknowledging the other side. Then rewrite your opening to Carmen using them — acknowledge the concern, then dispute the claim with the record.

Stanford's Matt Abrahams takes apart the moment you rehearsed tonight: the conversation where the other side thinks the problem is yours. The through-line is exactly your skill — what makes a dispute hard is rarely the topic, it is the inner reaction each person is having. The episode gives concrete moves for staying productive under that pressure: acknowledge before you argue, separate the position from the person, and keep the relationship intact while you hold your line.

Tip: pick one tool from the episode and script it into your Ibersign meeting. Draft the two sentences you would say to acknowledge Carmen's concern before you put the sign-off log on the table.

Grammar
Cambridge Dictionary — "The subjunctive" (English Grammar Today, ~10 min read)

The exact grammar of tonight, at your level, from a reference you can trust. It lays out the one move that made your claim letter sound authoritative: after essential / important / recommend / insist / request that, the verb stays in the base form for every subject — "It is essential that he submit", "We recommend that the team be notified". It also covers the negative ("that he not be present") and shows why formal English prefers this to "should". Read it after the lesson and the corrective-action letter will look transparent.

Tip: after reading, take three "should / needs to" sentences from a real Motiva document and rewrite each as a subjunctive requirement — "the supplier should submit" → "it is essential that the supplier submit". Producing them by hand is what moves the grammar from recognised to owned.
