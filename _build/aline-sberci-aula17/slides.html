Chapter 3 -- The Code

Grammar Discovery

Comparing two options with comparatives

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

What do the orange words have in common?

"Option B is cheaper than Option A."

"The direct flight is faster than the other one."

"Option A is more expensive than Option B."

"The layover makes Option B longer than Option A."

Grammar Rule

Comparing with Comparatives

TypeFormExample
Short adjectiveadjective + -er + thancheaper / faster than
Ends in -y-y → -ier + thaneasy → easier than
Long adjectivemore + adjective + thanmore expensive than
Irregularspecial form + thangood → better, bad → worse

Key idea: to compare two things, add -er to short adjectives (cheaper, faster) or use more before long ones (more expensive), then add than. Say "more expensive", never "expensiver".

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"Option B is more cheaper than Option A."
"Option B is cheaper than Option A."

Use -er (cheaper) or more (more expensive), never both. And long adjectives take more, not -er.

Dialogue

Booking the Flight

T

"Hi Aline. I found two flights for the trip. Let's compare them before I book."

A

"Sure. Option A is the direct flight, so it is faster. But the fare is more expensive, right?"

T

"Exactly. Option B is cheaper, but it has a layover in Zurich, so it is longer."

A

"Option B is refundable, and the baggage allowance is bigger. For a work trip, I think it is safer."

T

"Good point. Let's book Option B then. Do you want an aisle seat for the long flight?"

A

"Yes, an aisle seat, please. And add travel insurance. Send me the itinerary when it is done."
