Chapter 4: The Code

Relative Clauses

who, which, that, where, whose · adding information to a noun

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"It is the town that stayed with me."

"The cafe where we met is still there."

"A woman who ran the cafe waved me in."

"Bergen, which sits between mountains, is stunning."

Each blue word joins two ideas and adds information about a noun.

PronounPoints toExample
whopeoplethe woman who ran the cafe
which / thatthingsthe town that stayed with me
whereplacesthe square where we sat
whosepossessiona bakery whose owner knew my name

A defining clause is essential and takes no commas ("the town that stayed"). A non-defining clause adds extra information, takes commas, and uses which/who, never that ("Bergen, which...").

Language Focus

Defining or Non-defining?

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"The place what I loved most."

✓ RIGHT

"The place that I loved most."

✗ WRONG

"Bergen, that is beautiful, stayed with me."

✓ RIGHT

"Bergen, which is beautiful, stayed with me."

Never use what as a relative pronoun — use that or which. And in a non-defining clause (with commas), use which or who, never that.

Dialogue

A Walk Through Her Town

S
So this is the square that I told you about. That weathered old house over there, which is nearly three hundred years old, is where I grew up.
M
It is beautiful. And that little shop on the corner, the one whose windows are full of bread?
S
That is the bakery that everyone loves. The baker, who has worked there for fifty years, still remembers every name. This is a sleepy place, but it grows on you.
M
I can see why it stayed with you. It is so unassuming, and yet it is steeped in history.
S
Exactly. The tourists all go to the city that has the famous cathedral. But this is the place that I would never trade. It is my haven.
M
Back home I have a street like this, a narrow one whose cafes I have known since I was a boy. Some places just leave a mark on you.
Did You Catch It?

About the Town

1. What is special about the old house Sigrid points out?
It is nearly three hundred years old, and it is where she grew up.
2. What does she say about the baker?
He has worked at the bakery for fifty years and still remembers every name.
3. Why does she call the town her haven?
The tourists all go to the city with the famous cathedral, but this is the place she would never trade.
Listening 1

Sigrid's Village

The place she would go back to tomorrow. Sound first, no text.

0:000:00
1. What is unusual about the village's harbor?
It is so small that only three boats fit.
2. What is special about the wooden house?
Her grandmother was born in it, and the whole family still gathers there every summer.
3. Why does she say the place stayed with her?
Not because of what is there, but because of who is there, like the baker who remembers everyone's name.
Listening 2

Why Places Stay

A host on the places that refuse to leave us. Sound first, no text.

0:000:00
1. What happens to the great famous cities, according to the host?
They blur together in memory.
2. What kind of places actually stay with us?
Small towns whose names nobody recognizes, tied to a little square or a cafe and its owner.
3. Why does the host say a place becomes unforgettable?
Because of who you met and how they made you feel, not because of what is built there.

I can form defining relative clauses with who, which, that and where.
I can add extra information with a non-defining clause, using commas and which or who.

I do not use "what" as a relative pronoun, or "that" in a non-defining clause.
