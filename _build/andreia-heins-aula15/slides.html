Language Focus

Agree, Disagree, Decide

Polite phrases to agree and disagree, and to assign action items.

Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I think you're right about the console." (agree)

"I see your point, but the date is tight." (disagree politely)

"As a compromise, we add a buffer." (meet in the middle)

"Can you follow up with cardiology?" (assign an action item)

Language Rule

Run a Decision

FunctionPhraseExample
AgreeI agree / Good pointI agree, that is the consensus.
Disagree politelyI see your point, but...I see your point, but the date is tight.
CompromiseAs a compromise...As a compromise, we add a buffer.
AssignCan you / Would you + verb?Can you follow up by Friday?

Key idea: to disagree politely, recognize the other person first ("I see your point", "I understand"), then add your view with but. To assign an action item, use Can you or Would you + the base verb, and always name the owner and a deadline: "Would you confirm the loan by Thursday?"

Watch Out

Watch Out

✗ "I am agree with you."

✓ "I agree with you." (agree is a verb, no "am")

✗ "No, you're wrong." (too direct)

✓ "I see your point, but I'm not sure." (polite disagreement)

Reaching the Decision

L

"Andreia, on the workaround, do we have a consensus?"

A

"Yes, I agree. Borrowing a console is the right move. That is the consensus."

L

"Good. Some people want to push the practice to next month. What do you think?"

A

"I see your point, but a full month is too long. As a compromise, why don't we keep the date and add a three-day buffer?"

L

"That works for me. Let's assign the action items."

A

"I'll set the vendor deadline by Friday. Can you follow up with cardiology and confirm the loan by Thursday?"

L

"Done. We finalize the plan today, with a follow-up on Monday."
