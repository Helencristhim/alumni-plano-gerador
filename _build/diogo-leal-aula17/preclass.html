Dead Air -- The Two Minutes Before the Call Starts

Fill the two minutes before the call: open, react, add something about you, and bridge to the agenda. New vocabulary: break the ice, catch up, chat, awkward, casual, by the way, anyway, weekend.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Say it out loud after the audio.

Break the ice -- to make the first, easy conversation
"Somebody has to break the ice, and today it is you." (Quebrar o gelo)
Catch up -- to hear a person's news, after some time
"It is good to catch up before we start." (Colocar o papo em dia)
Chat -- an easy, friendly conversation about nothing important
"We had a quick chat about the weekend." (Bate-papo)
Awkward -- uncomfortable, and a little embarrassing
"Silence on a video call is awkward." (Constrangedor)
Casual -- relaxed, not formal
"Keep it casual: this is not the meeting yet." (Informal / descontraído)
By the way -- used to add something, or to change the topic
"By the way, how was the conference?" (A propósito)
Anyway -- used to close the small talk and start the meeting
"Anyway, shall we start? We have a full agenda." (Enfim / bom)
Weekend -- Saturday and Sunday: the safest topic there is
"Did you have a good weekend?" (Fim de semana)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

It is 2:58, and the silence on the call is awkward. Somebody has to break the ice, and today it is Diogo. He keeps it casual: "How is your week going so far?" Sarah answers in three words -- busy, same as always. A weaker speaker would go silent here. Diogo does two things instead: he reacts and uses a question tag ("End of quarter for you too, isn't it?"), and then he adds something about himself: his flight was three hours late. Now it is a chat, not an interview. "By the way, did you have a good weekend?" She played tennis, and they catch up for a minute about his daughter. At two minutes, he closes it himself: "Anyway, we have a full agenda -- shall we start?"

1. Why does Diogo talk about his own flight?
A Because if he only asks questions, it becomes an interview.
B Because he wants to complain about the airline.
C Because he is late for the meeting.
2. Which sentence is correct?
A How is your week going?
B How is going your week?
C How your week is going?
3. What does "Anyway" do at the end?
A It closes the small talk and starts the meeting.
B It changes the subject to another personal topic.
C It asks the other person to speak more.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Open, React, Bridge

GRAMMAR

Os dois minutos antes da call: abrir, reagir, acrescentar algo seu e voltar à pauta. / The two minutes before the call.

MoveEstrutura / StructureExemplo / Example
Abrir / OpenHow is your week going? (sujeito antes do verbo)How is your week going so far?
Manter vivo / Keep it aliveReagir + question tag: isn't it? / doesn't it?Busy quarter for you too, isn't it?
Voltar à pauta / BridgeBy the way, ... / Anyway, shall we start?Anyway, shall we start?
Ponto-chave / Key pointO "né?" brasileiro não existe em inglês: usa-se question tag (isn't it? / doesn't it? / didn't you?). E a regra de ouro do small talk: responda, e acrescente algo sobre você -- senão vira interrogatório.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

2. She answers: "Busy. Same as always." You:

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Two recordings. Speak first, do not write. This is the exercise that matters most this week.

Take a real American colleague or client. Do the two minutes out loud: (1) open with a real question, (2) react to the answer, (3) add something about your week, (4) ask one more question -- weekend, travel, sport, (5) bridge to the agenda. Record it.
Now she gives you nothing: "Fine. Busy. Same as always." Keep the conversation alive out loud for one more minute, without going silent and without asking three questions in a row. Record it.
