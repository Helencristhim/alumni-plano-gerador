Chapter 3

The What-If Engine

The second conditional: selling a hypothetical future

Grammar Discovery

Spot the Pattern

What do these imagined pitches have in common?

"If we placed kiosks in the busiest branches, we would cut wait times in half."

"If we invested two million now, we would see a return inside eighteen months."

"If the board backed a pilot, we would lower the risk of a full rollout."

None of this has happened. We are imagining it to persuade.

Grammar Rule

The Second Conditional

PartFormExample
If-clauseIf + past simple"If we invested in kiosks,"
Resultwould + base verb"we would cut wait times."
Useimagined present / futurepersuading, proposing, exploring

Second vs third conditional:

Second = an imagined present or future (it could still happen): "If we invested, we would grow." Third = an unreal past (it never happened): "If we had invested, we would have grown." In formal pitches, use "If I were" rather than "If I was".

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"If we would invest now, we would grow."

✓ RIGHT

"If we invested now, we would grow."

✗ WRONG

"If we ran the pilot, we will lower the risk."

✓ RIGHT

"If we ran the pilot, we would lower the risk."

In a second conditional, the if-clause uses past simple, never "would". The result uses "would + base verb", not "will" (will would make it a real, first-conditional promise).

Chapter 4

The Boardroom

Pitching the kiosk idea to the CFO

Dialogue

The Pre-Board Pitch

S
Victor, you have five minutes before the board. Give me the idea, and tell me why I should fund it.
V
Smart self-service kiosks in our fifty busiest branches. The value proposition is simple: faster service for clients, and our teams freed for the work that actually grows revenue.
S
Nice in theory. But what would it actually do to our numbers?
V
If we placed the kiosks in those branches, we would cut transaction time in half. The bottom line is a return inside eighteen months, and the model is fully scalable.
S
Two million is a lot of conviction for a maybe. Why now?
V
The momentum is already here. Three branch managers asked for exactly this. If we moved now, we would lead the market instead of chasing it.
S
The board will say it is too risky for a full launch.
V
I would argue the opposite. If we ran a two-branch pilot first, we would learn fast and lower the risk before any rollout. The ask is small; the upside is not.
Comprehension

Did You Catch It?

1. What does Sophie ask Victor to do in the first line?
Give her the idea and explain why she should fund it.
2. What objection does Sophie raise about the cost and the timing?
That two million is a lot to spend on a "maybe", and she asks why it has to be now.
3. What concern does Sophie predict the board will raise?
That a full launch would be too risky.

Chapter 5

Sharpen the Pitch

Persuasion, at speed

Quick Fire

Pitch It in One Line

1 / 5

1. Hook: sell the kiosk speed benefit (second conditional, "place / cut").

"If we placed the kiosks, we would cut wait times in half."

2. Numbers: sell the return on the investment (second conditional, "invest / see").

"If we invested two million, we would see a return in eighteen months."

3. De-risk: sell the pilot (second conditional, "run / lower").

"If we ran a two-branch pilot, we would lower the risk before any rollout."

4. Reframe the objection: the cost of waiting (second conditional, "delay / hand").

"If we delayed this, we would hand the advantage to a competitor."

5. Win buy-in: tie an incentive to the pilot (second conditional, "tie / earn").

"If we tied an incentive to the pilot, we would earn the teams' buy-in fast."

Detective

Spot the Error

0 / 4 errors found

"If we would launch the pilot, we would learn fast."
"If we launched the pilot, we would learn fast." (if-clause = past simple, never "would")
"If we invested now, we will see a return."
"If we invested now, we would see a return." (imagined result = would, not will)
"If the board backs it, we would scale to other regions."
"If the board backed it, we would scale to other regions." (keep it hypothetical: past simple in the if-clause)
"If we had run a pilot, we would win the board over tomorrow."
"If we ran a pilot, we would win the board over tomorrow." (future possibility = second, not third conditional)
