Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

The language of a persuasive pitch. Tap Listen to hear each word.

Pitch -- a short, persuasive presentation of an idea or proposal
"I have ninety seconds to pitch the kiosk idea to the board."
Persuade -- to convince someone to agree, believe, or take action
"I need to persuade the CFO that the pilot is worth funding."
Influence -- the power to shape the decisions, opinions, or behavior of others
"The regional director has real influence over the budget."
Value proposition -- the clear reason an idea is worth choosing over the alternatives
"The value proposition is simple: faster service at half the cost."
Buy-in -- agreement and active support from the people involved in a decision
"Without the CFO's buy-in, the pilot will never start."
Incentive -- something that motivates a person to act or decide in a certain way
"A faster bonus cycle is a strong incentive for the branch teams."
Rationale -- the underlying reasons or logic that justify a decision or plan
"Let me give the rationale before I show the numbers."
Momentum -- the force that keeps progress, energy, or support building over time
"Three managers already asked for this, so the momentum is there."
Upside -- the potential benefit, gain, or advantage of a choice
"The ask is small, but the upside is enormous."
Scalable -- able to grow significantly without losing efficiency or quality
"The model is fully scalable to the other LATAM regions."
Game changer -- something that dramatically shifts a situation or the way things are done
"Self-service kiosks would be a real game changer for our branches."
Tangible -- real and clearly measurable, rather than vague or abstract
"Pair every benefit with a tangible number the board can trust."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the pitch summary and answer the questions.

The Growth Manager walked into the pre-board meeting with a single pitch: smart self-service kiosks for the busiest branches. He opened with the value proposition instead of the background. "If we placed the kiosks in those branches, we would cut transaction time in half," he said. The rationale was clear and the numbers were tangible: "If we invested two million now, we would see a return inside eighteen months, and the model is fully scalable." He knew the cost would draw pushback, so he reframed it: "If we delayed this, we would hand the advantage to a competitor." Then he kept the momentum going by shrinking the ask. "If we ran a two-branch pilot first, we would lower the risk before any rollout." The CFO smiled. The upside was obvious, the ask was small, and the idea sounded less like a gamble and more like a game changer.

1. Why does the manager say "If we placed the kiosks, we would cut transaction time" rather than "we will cut"?
A It is a second conditional: an imagined result that depends on a hypothetical action.
B It is a third conditional, describing a past that did not happen.
C It is a firm promise that the kiosks are already installed.
2. What does "If we delayed this, we would hand the advantage to a competitor" do in the pitch?
A It apologizes for the cost.
B It reframes the objection by showing the cost of waiting.
C It reports what the board already decided.
3. In a second conditional, what form follows "if"?
A "would" plus the base verb.
B The past simple (placed, invested, ran).
C "had" plus the past participle.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Second Conditional

GRAMMAR

Use it to sell an imagined present or future: a result that could still happen.

FormPatternExample
AffirmativeIf + past simple, would + base"If we invested, we would grow."
NegativeIf + did not + base, would not + base"If we did not act, we would not lead."
QuestionWhat would + subject + base, if...?"What would it cost, if we started now?"
Formal "were"If I / he / she + were"If I were the sponsor, I would fund it."
Watch the traps: the if-clause uses past simple, never "would"; the result uses "would + base", not "will". Second conditional = imagined now or future (it could happen); third conditional = unreal past (it never happened). PT: o second conditional vende um futuro hipotetico -- "se fizessemos X, conseguiriamos Y" (if + passado simples, would + verbo base); nao confunda com o third conditional, que fala de um passado que NAO aconteceu.

Lesson 7 -- Pitching with Impact: Persuasion, Influence & the Second Conditional

Series
Shark Tank (ABC) -- any episode of Season 10

Every founder who walks in does exactly what you practiced: open with a value proposition, sell a hypothetical future, and ask for a specific investment. Listen for the hooks ("if you backed us, we would corner this market") and watch how the investors push back on cost and risk -- and how the strongest founders reframe instead of getting defensive. A masterclass in pitching under pressure.

Tip: pause after each pitch and write the founder's value proposition in one sentence, plus one second-conditional line they used ("If we... we would...").

Podcast
HBR IdeaCast (Harvard Business Review) -- "How to Pitch a Brilliant Idea"

This episode breaks down why some ideas win backing and others die in the room. The hosts cover how to frame a value proposition, how to make the upside feel tangible, and how to build momentum before the meeting even starts. It is the exact mindset Victor needs to take a kiosk idea from "interesting" to "funded".

Tip: after listening, draft a three-line pitch for one real idea at Brinks: the value proposition, one second-conditional hook, and one small ask.

YouTube
"The Second Conditional -- If I had... I would..." -- BBC Learning English

A clear, compact lesson on the exact structure from this class: if + past simple, would + base verb. The presenter shows how the second conditional describes imagined or unlikely situations, contrasts it with the first and third conditionals, and flags the most common error Brazilian professionals make -- putting "would" inside the if-clause.

Tip: watch with a notebook. After each example, write one sentence pitching a real idea at work using the same "If we... we would..." pattern.
