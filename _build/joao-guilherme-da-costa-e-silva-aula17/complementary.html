Lesson 17 -- The Numbers Behind the Project: Presenting KPIs, Milestones and Budget Deviations

TED Talk
Melissa Marshall -- "Talk nerdy to me" (TED, ~4 min, free, plays in the browser)

This is your lesson in four minutes, from the audience's side of the table. Marshall's message to every technical expert is the one Ingrid gives you on the dashboard: your board is fascinated by the work, but they need it in a form they can act on. She shows why leading with jargon and building up to the point loses the room, and why you start with what matters -- the bottom line -- and translate the detail only when it earns its place. Everything you drilled tonight (number first, own the deviation, forecast in plain terms) is the same move she is teaching scientists to make.

Tip: watch once with English subtitles and note the two or three things she says a technical presenter must change. Then take your own Line 4 status report and rewrite the opening line the way she would want it -- the single sentence a finance director hears before anything else. Record that opening out loud, three times, until it lands in under ten seconds.

Podcast
Think Fast, Talk Smart (Stanford GSB) -- Ep. 189, "Numbers Need Narrative: Use Data to Influence and Inspire" (~25 min, free, plays in the browser)

Miro Kazakoff of MIT Sloan joins Matt Abrahams to answer the exact question behind your lesson: a dashboard is objective, but the board is not, so how do you present a KPI, a variance or a burn rate so a room actually acts on it? He argues that a number without a narrative is ignored, and that the best data presenters are the ones who listen first -- who know which figure moves the needle for THIS audience and lead with it. Native English, real pace, and it maps straight onto the board review you simulated tonight.

Tip: listen once and write down every technique he gives for turning a number into a story an executive remembers. Then take the three figures from tonight's status report -- the earned value, the cost variance and the forecast date -- and write one sentence of narrative for each. Record all three, and make sure the forecast one is in the future perfect: "by the end of Q3 we will have..."

YouTube
"Future Continuous and Future Perfect Explained" -- Advanced English Grammar (~15 min)

The exact grammar of tonight's lesson, at your level, from a clear native teacher. It puts the future continuous (will be + -ing, in progress at a future point) and the future perfect (will have + past participle, finished by a future point) side by side, with the timeline diagrams that make the difference stick -- which is precisely what turns a forecast into a commitment instead of a hope. Watch it AFTER the lesson to lock in the two errors Brazilians make most: using the plain future ("we will complete by Q3") where the future perfect is needed, and saying "until" where the deadline word is "by".

Tip: after the video, write 8 sentences from your real work at Motiva -- four with the future perfect ("by [date] we will have closed/completed/delivered...") and four with the future continuous ("at [moment] the team will be testing/running/working..."). Record them fast, letting "will have" collapse into "we'll've", and say each one as if Ingrid had just asked you for a date.
