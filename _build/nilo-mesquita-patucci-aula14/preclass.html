Negotiation Basics -- Opening the Deal

Open a negotiation and propose terms politely with soft language (We would like to propose..., We could offer...) and conditional offers (If you accept..., we would...). New negotiation vocabulary: propose, offer, counteroffer, terms, common ground, meet halfway, compromise, leverage, deadlock, bargain, walk away, deal.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Propose -- to formally suggest a deal, plan, or idea
"We would like to propose a three-year deal." (Propor)
Offer -- what you are willing to give in a deal
"Their first offer was lower than we expected." (Oferta / proposta)
Counteroffer -- a new offer you make in response to theirs
"My counteroffer was more visibility for the same price." (Contraproposta)
Terms -- the conditions of a deal: price, time, rights
"We were hoping to agree on better terms." (Condições / termos)
Common ground -- an idea or point both sides can agree on
"We started to look for common ground." (Ponto em comum)
Meet halfway -- to agree by each side giving up part of what it wants
"We decided to meet halfway on the rights." (Ceder de ambos os lados)
Compromise -- an agreement where each side gives a little
"In the end we found a fair compromise." (Acordo / meio-termo)
Leverage -- the power or advantage that helps you negotiate
"A strong brand gives Corinthians leverage." (Poder de barganha / vantagem)
Deadlock -- a point where neither side will move forward
"For a while we were close to a deadlock." (Impasse)
Bargain -- to discuss in order to get a better price or terms
"We bargained for two extra categories." (Barganhar / negociar)
Walk away -- to leave a negotiation without making a deal
"Know when a deal is good and when to walk away." (Desistir / abandonar)
Deal -- the final agreement that both sides accept
"We shook hands on a fair deal." (Acordo / negócio)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

At the negotiation table, Nilo never makes hard demands. He softens every proposal with would and could. Instead of "We propose a deal", he says "We would like to propose a three-year deal." Instead of "We offer more visibility", he says "We could offer more visibility." When he wants to trade one thing for another, he uses a conditional offer: "If you accept a longer contract, we would lower the price." Notice the pattern: after if, the verb is present simple (you accept), and the result uses would + a base verb (we would lower) -- never "would" after "if". To suggest a middle point, he asks politely: "Would you be open to a compromise?" These soft forms keep the whole negotiation friendly and professional.

1. Which sentence is the correct soft proposal?
A We would like to propose a three-year deal.
B We would to propose a three-year deal.
C We could to propose a three-year deal.
2. Which is the correct conditional offer?
A If you would accept a longer contract, we would lower the price.
B If you accept a longer contract, we would lower the price.
C If you accept a longer contract, we would to lower the price.
3. Why does Nilo use would and could at the table?
A To soften his proposals and keep the negotiation friendly and professional.
B To sound stronger and make hard demands.
C To ask about the past.
