Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

To suggest -- to hint that something is probably true, without stating it directly
"The logs suggest a timeout, but it is not confirmed."
Tentative -- not final or certain; said or done with caution
"I only have a tentative answer for now."
Apparently -- based on what seems true, though not fully confirmed
"Apparently the bug only happens under heavy load."
Presumably -- most likely; used when you assume something is true
"Presumably the test will take about two hours."
To imply -- to communicate something indirectly, without saying it openly
"I did not say no; I implied we needed more time."
Cautious -- careful to avoid mistakes or risks; not rushing to conclusions
"I am cautious about promising a fix today."
Seemingly -- in a way that appears to be true, on the surface
"A seemingly small change broke the login."
To downplay -- to make something seem less important or serious than it is
"Do not downplay the risk just to sound positive."
Roughly -- approximately; not exactly
"It will take roughly two hours to test."
To soften -- to make a message gentler or less direct
"I softened my email so it did not sound like a complaint."
Understated -- expressed in a calm, restrained way, without exaggeration
"Her understated reply hid how serious the issue was."
To qualify -- to add a detail that limits a statement and makes it less absolute
"Let me qualify that: it works, but only on the latest version."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

In her release standup, Ana wants to flag a risk without sounding blunt, so she softens what she says. Instead of "this is a bug", she says it seemingly only happens under heavy load, and the logs suggest a timeout. She is cautious, so she keeps her view tentative: "I am not sure yet." When her manager asks how long a test takes, she does not pretend to be exact; she says roughly two hours, and presumably they will know by the afternoon. She does not downplay the risk to look positive, and she does not overstate it either. When she disagrees, she does not say "you are wrong"; she softens it to "I'm not sure that's quite right." Notice the pattern: a modal (might, could), a reporting verb (seem, suggest), a cautious adverb (apparently, roughly), or a tentative opener (I'd say) turns a hard claim into a measured one. One hedge is usually enough; too many and you sound unsure.

1. Which sentence sounds the most measured and diplomatic?
A "This is a bug and the release will fail."
B "This might be a bug, so it could be worth another test."
C "You are wrong, the release will definitely fail."
2. How many hedges does the text recommend per sentence?
A Usually one; too many sound unsure.
B As many as possible, to be safe.
C None; hedging is always weak.
3. To disagree diplomatically, Ana says...
A "I'm not sure that's quite right."
B "You are completely wrong."
C "That is impossible."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Hedging Toolkit

GRAMMAR

Four ways to soften a claim or an opinion so you sound measured, not blunt.

DeviceFunctionExample
Modal: might / could / maysoften a claim you are not sure ofThis might be a regression.
seem / appear / suggest / tend toreport cautiously, not as factThe logs suggest a timeout.
Adverbs: apparently / presumably / roughly / seeminglyadd caution to a statementIt is apparently intermittent.
Tentative openers: I'd say / it could be worthsoften an opinion or suggestionI'd say we hold the release.
Key ruleOne hedge per sentence is usually enough. Modals do not stack (never might can). To disagree, soften instead of contradicting: I'm not sure that's quite right.
