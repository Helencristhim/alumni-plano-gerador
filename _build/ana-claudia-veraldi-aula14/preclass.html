Lesson 14 -- Pre-class

Advanced Modals -- Would Rather, Had Better, May As Well

Ana's remote team must choose a testing tool for a new module. She writes a recommendation: her preference, her warning, and her pragmatic call. New words: preference, to recommend, advisable, worthwhile, to opt for, viable, upside, rationale, reluctant, to lean toward, prudent, compelling. Grammar: advanced modals for recommending and preferring (I would rather use the free tool; we had better decide this week; we may as well set it up now).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Learn the words for making a strong recommendation. Tap Listen to hear each one.

Preference -- a thing you like or want more than another
"My preference is the open-source tool."
To recommend -- to advise something as the best choice
"I would recommend the free tool for our team."
Advisable -- wise or sensible to do
"It is advisable to decide before the deadline."
Worthwhile -- worth the time, money or effort
"Setting up the tool now is worthwhile."
To opt for -- to choose one option instead of others
"In the end, the team opted for the open-source tool."
Viable -- realistic and able to work as a choice
"Both tools are viable, but one is faster to set up."
Upside -- the positive part or advantage of a choice
"The upside of the free tool is that everyone knows it."
Rationale -- the reasons behind a decision
"Let me explain the rationale for my recommendation."
Reluctant -- not wanting to do something; hesitant
"Some teammates were reluctant to switch tools."
To lean toward -- to prefer or be inclined to one option
"I am leaning toward the open-source option."
Prudent -- careful and sensible; avoiding risk
"It was more prudent to pick a good-enough tool now."
Compelling -- strong enough to convince you
"She made a compelling case for the free tool."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Ana had to recommend a testing tool, so she used three modals to be clear. To share her preference, she wrote, "I would rather use the open-source tool than the paid one" — would rather + base verb, with no "to". To warn the team about the deadline, she added, "We had better decide this week" — strong advice, almost a warning. And because the team was already blocked, she suggested, "We may as well set up the tool now" — there was no better option, so why not. All three take the base verb: would rather use, had better decide, may as well set up.

1. When Ana says "I would rather use the open-source tool", she is expressing:
A a preference -- what she likes more.
B a command to the team.
C a report about a past decision.
2. "We had better decide this week" is used to:
A describe a daily routine.
B give firm advice, almost a warning.
C ask a polite question.
3. What verb form comes after would rather, had better and may as well?
A to + verb (to use, to decide).
B the base verb with no "to" (use, decide, set up).
C the -ing form (using, deciding).

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Advanced Modals

GRAMMAR

Three modals for recommending and preferring. All take the base verb with no "to".

ModalUse / FormExample
would ratherpreference: would rather + base verb (than...)I would rather use the free tool than the paid one.
had betterstrong advice / warning: had better + base verbWe had better decide this week, or we will miss the deadline.
may as wellno better option: may as well + base verbWe are blocked, so we may as well set up the tool now.
Common mistakeDo not add "to": not "would rather to use" — say "would rather use". And it is had better, never "would better".

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each recommendation situation.

A teammate asks which tool you prefer. You answer:
A "I would rather use the free tool, because the team already knows it."
B "I would rather to use the free tool."
C "I used the free tool last year."
The deadline is close and nobody has decided. The best warning is:
A "We would better decide this week."
B "We had better decide this week, or we will miss the deadline."
C "There is no hurry, we can decide anytime."
The team is blocked, waiting on an API. You suggest a good use of the dead time:
A "We are blocked anyway, so we may as well set up the tool now."
B "We are blocked, so let us do nothing."
C "We may as well to wait for the API."
You want your recommendation to be trusted. The most compelling line is:
A "Just trust me, it is the best tool."
B "The upside is fast setup; the downside is less support -- but it is the more prudent choice for us."
C "There are no downsides at all, it is perfect."
