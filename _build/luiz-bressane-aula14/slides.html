Chapter 2: Words of Punishment

The Words of the Sentence

Vocabulary

Words 1-4

Sentencing
The stage after a guilty verdict, when the court decides the punishment
"At the sentencing, the defence argues for a lighter penalty."
Mitigation
Facts that argue for a lighter sentence, in the defendant's favour
"In mitigation, counsel pointed to his clean record."
Aggravation
Facts that argue for a heavier sentence, against the defendant
"The breach of trust was treated as a factor in aggravation."
Remorse
Deep, genuine regret for the harm one has caused
"The judge accepted that his remorse was sincere."

Chapter 3: Perfect Modals

Judging the Past

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

The state should have offered treatment years earlier.

He could have fled, but he stayed and confessed.

He might have caused far greater harm.

The court ought to have shown more leniency.

ModalPatternMeaning
should haveshould have + past participlecriticism / regret
could havecould have + past participlepast possibility, not taken
might havemight have + past participlespeculation about the past
ought to haveought to have + past participlemoral duty in the past

Perfect modals judge a finished past: modal + have + past participle (run, offered, caused). In sentencing you weigh what was done against what could, should, or might have been done. Beware "should of" — it is always "should have".

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"The state should of acted sooner."

✓ RIGHT

"The state should have acted sooner."

✗ WRONG

"He could ran, but he stayed."

✓ RIGHT

"He could have run, but he stayed."

The contraction "should've" sounds like "should of" — but in writing it is always "have", followed by the past participle (run, acted, offered), never the past simple.

Chapter 4: Before the Judge

The Plea in Mitigation

Dialogue

The Sentencing Hearing

J
The verdict is guilty. Counsel, before I pass sentence, what do you say in mitigation?
L
Your Honour, I do not dispute the gravity of this offence. But in mitigation, my client could have fled and instead he stayed and confessed.
L
He has shown genuine remorse, and he has already begun to make amends to the victim. The system should have offered him treatment years earlier.
J
The prosecution points to aggravation — a breach of trust. How do you answer that?
L
I concede it, Your Honour. But he might have caused far greater harm, and he chose to stop. A custodial term would not rehabilitate him; it would only deepen the harm.
L
I ask the court for leniency: a proportionate sentence, perhaps probation, that punishes without ending a young life.
J
I accept the gravity, counsel, but also the remorse. The court will consider a sentence below the maximum.
Comprehension

The Hearing

1. What did counsel say the client could have done, but did not?
He could have fled, but he stayed and confessed.
2. What aggravating factor did the prosecution raise?
A breach of trust.
3. Why did counsel argue against a custodial sentence?
It would not rehabilitate the client; it would only deepen the harm.
4. What did the judge accept on both sides?
The gravity of the offence and the client's remorse.

Chapter 5: Drill

Sharpen the Plea

Detective · Transformation · Building

Spot the Error

Fix the Modal

"The court should of shown more leniency."
The court should have shown more leniency. (have, not of)
"He could have ran, but he stayed."
He could have run, but he stayed. (past participle)
"The state should offered him treatment earlier."
The state should have offered him treatment earlier. (add have)
"She might caused greater harm."
She might have caused greater harm. (add have)
Transformation

Judge the Past

"It was possible for him to run, but he did not." → could have
He could have run, but he stayed.
"It was wrong of the state not to act sooner." → should have
The state should have acted sooner.
"Perhaps she did not understand the threat." → might have
She might not have understood the threat.
"It was the court's duty to weigh his remorse." → ought to have
The court ought to have weighed his remorse.
