Lesson 11 -- Pre-class

Predictions & Promises

Talking about the future in everyday life. Key words: prediction, to promise, to guess, to expect, to doubt, likely, certain, forecast, to wonder, to warn, spontaneous, to offer. Structure: will and won't for predictions, promises, and spontaneous decisions, always followed by the base verb.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Prediction -- a statement about what you think will happen in the future
"My prediction is that it will rain this afternoon."
To promise -- to say firmly that you will certainly do something
"I promise I will call you tonight."
To guess -- to give an answer when you are not completely sure
"I can't be sure, but I'll guess it's about five o'clock."
To expect -- to think that something will probably happen
"I expect the train will be a little late again."
To doubt -- to think that something is probably not true
"I doubt it will snow here in April."
Likely -- probably going to happen
"It is likely that prices will rise next year."
Certain -- completely sure about something
"I am certain she will win the game."
Forecast -- a report that says what will happen, especially the weather
"The forecast says it will be sunny tomorrow."
To wonder -- to think about something you are curious about
"I wonder if he will come to the party."
To warn -- to tell someone about a possible danger or problem
"I must warn you: the roads will be icy tonight."
Spontaneous -- decided suddenly, without planning it before
"It was a spontaneous decision to travel this weekend."
To offer -- to say you are happy to do something for someone
"She offered to drive me home after work."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Ana Paula is planning a weekend picnic, so she checks the weather forecast. The prediction is good: it will be sunny and warm on Saturday. She is certain the day will be perfect, and she expects at least ten friends. She doubts that anyone will stay home, because rain is not likely. Her friend Ben is nervous, so she makes a promise: she will bring a big salad and she will help him set everything up. She also has one small warning: their friend Chris will probably be late, as always. It was a spontaneous idea, but Ana Paula is sure it will be a wonderful afternoon.

1. Why do we say "it will rain" and not "it will rains"?
A After will, we use the base verb, with no -s.
B "Rain" has no third-person form.
C "Will" is only used with "it" and "you".
2. In "she will bring a big salad", what does "will" show here?
A A plan she made a long time ago.
B A promise or an offer to help.
C Something that happened in the past.
3. Which sentence makes a correct prediction?
A "I think it will to rain."
B "I think it will rain."
C "I think it will raining."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Will and Won't

GRAMMAR

Use will for predictions, promises, and decisions you make right now. After will, use the base verb.

UseAffirmativeNegative & Question
PredictionIt will rain later.
what you think happens		It won't rain today.
Will it rain?
Promise & decisionI will call you.
I'll help you now.		I won't forget.
Will you help me?
After willAlways the base verb: no to, no -ing, no -s. "She will call" (not "calls", not "to call"). In speech: I'll, she'll, they'll.
