Lesson 07 -- Pre-class

Speaking Up with Confidence -- Hotels, Reservations, and When Things Go Wrong

In Lesson 6 the city gave you the rules. Now the hotel gives you nothing — you have to ask for what you want, and in English, HOW you ask changes the answer. Key words: amenities, suite, housekeeping, valet, surcharge, late checkout, an upgrade, room service, a wake-up call, a deposit, spotless. Grammar: polite requests and indirect questions — the difference between an order and a favor ("Could you send someone up?" / "Would you mind moving me to a quieter room?" / "I was wondering if you could check the bill." / "Do you know if the room is ready?"). Firm, never rude — the guest people want to help.

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

Lucimara reaches the front desk a little early. She does not say "give me my room." She says, "Do you know if the room is ready yet? I'm happy to wait." Notice the shape: inside the question, the words stay in normal order — if the room is ready, not is the room ready. It is a question wearing the clothes of a statement, and that is exactly what makes it sound polite.

The clerk has good news. They have given her an upgrade — a junior suite, already cleaned by housekeeping, spotless and quiet. But there is a surcharge on the reservation for valet parking, and Lucimara did not bring a car. She does not get angry. She opens softly: "I was wondering if you could take a look at this. There seems to be a valet charge, and I didn't use the parking." Same message as "this is wrong" — but now they are on the same side of the desk.

Then the small favors. "Would you mind adding a wake-up call for six?" she asks — and after would you mind, the verb always takes -ing, never to add. "And could I possibly have a late checkout on Friday? My flight is in the evening." The clerk cannot promise the late checkout, but he flags it. He takes a refundable deposit, removes the surcharge, and thanks her.

Nothing she asked for was unreasonable. What made the difference was not the words — it was the wrapper around them. She asked like someone who expected a yes, and she got one. The amenities were the same for every guest; the upgrade, the removed charge, the flagged late checkout were not. They went to the guest who was calm, clear, and kind.

1. "Do you know if the room is ready?" Why is it the room is and not is the room?
A Because it is a mistake — questions always invert the verb.
B Because it is an indirect question: the real question is "do you know...?", and inside it the words keep normal statement order. This is what makes it sound polite.
C Because "the room is" is more formal than "is the room".
2. "Would you mind adding a wake-up call?" Why adding and not to add?
A After would you mind, the verb always takes -ing. It is a fixed rule: would you mind waiting / sending / moving.
B Because add has no infinitive.
C Because adding is in the past tense.
3. Lucimara had a wrong charge on her bill. What made her complaint work?
A She raised her voice so the clerk would take her seriously.
B She opened softly ("I was wondering if you could...") and then stated the fact clearly ("I didn't use the parking"). Kind frame, firm content.
C She apologized for the charge and paid it anyway.
4. Which request is correct?
A "Would you mind to move me to another room?"
B "Would you mind moving me to another room? And could you tell me what time checkout is?"
C "Would you mind moving me, and could you tell me what time is checkout?"

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Polite Requests and Indirect Questions

GRAMMAR

Same request, different wrappers. The bigger the favor, the softer you go.

WrapperForm / UseExample
Could you...?Could you + base verb. The everyday polite request. Neutral, works everywhere."Could you send someone up, please?"
Would you mind...?Would you mind + verb-ing. Softer. Careful: "Sure" or "Not at all" means yes, I'll do it."Would you mind moving me to a quieter room?"
I was wondering if...I was wondering if you could + base verb. The softest — keep it for a bigger favor."I was wondering if you could check the bill for me."
Indirect questionDo you know if / Could you tell me + normal word order inside. No inversion, no do/does."Do you know if the room is ready?" / "Could you tell me what time checkout is?"
Softening a complaintThere seems to be + problem. It states the fact without accusing anyone."There seems to be a surcharge here, and I didn't use the parking."
The one ideaEvery wrapper does the same job: it leaves the other person a way to say yes. You are not weaker for being polite — the soft version is the one that gets the upgrade, the late checkout, the charge removed.

Watch out -- the two classic mistakes: (1) would you mind to move — after would you mind the verb always ends in -ing: would you mind moving. (2) The inverted indirect question: "do you know where is the gym?" — inside an indirect question the words stay in normal order: "do you know where the gym is?". Learn those two, and you sound polished, not just correct.

Stage 2: Handle the Problem, in Order

Order

This is how you speak up about a wrong charge and win: in the order that actually works — from the soft opening to the calm confirmation.

?Make the request: "Would you mind taking it off my bill?"
?Open softly, not with the problem: "I was wondering if you could help me."
?Confirm at the end: "Let me check I got that -- the charge is gone. Is that right?"
?State the fact clearly: "There seems to be a valet charge, and I didn't bring a car."
?Stay firm if they hesitate: "I understand, but I really didn't use the parking."

Speaking

Listen to the sentence, say it out loud, and only then record. Today the target is the melody of a polite ask: let your voice rise gently at the end, and slow down. The same words said flat sound like an order; said with the rising tune, they sound like a favor.

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer — speak for about 90 seconds, with no script.

You are at a hotel front desk, and your bill has two charges that are wrong: a valet fee for a car you never had, and a minibar charge for a minibar you never opened. Speak up, out loud, as if the clerk were in front of you. Open politely, name both problems with the facts, ask for them to be removed, and confirm at the end. Use at least two of today's wrappers: "I was wondering if you could...", "Would you mind...", "There seems to be...", "Could you tell me...". Be firm and kind at the same time. Take your time and do not read from a script.
