Sound

The Melody of a Polite Ask

In English, politeness is not only the words — it is the music. The voice rises at the end, and it slows down.

Could you send someone up, please? — the voice lifts on please
Would you mind moving me to a quieter room? — slow, and rising
I was wondering if you could help me. — the softest opening of all
Do you know if the room is ready yet? — a question inside a question

Chapter 3: The Code

How to Ask Without Ordering

Same request. Two tones. English hears which one you chose.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

Could you send someone up to look at the heating?

Would you mind moving me to a quieter room?

I was wondering if you could check the bill for me.

Do you know if the room is ready yet?

Not one of them is an order. Each one wraps the request in something softer. What are the four wrappers?

WrapperFormWhen you use it
Could you...?Could you + base verb
Could you send...		The everyday polite request. Neutral, works everywhere.
Would you mind...?Would you mind + verb-ing
Would you mind moving...		Softer. Careful: here "Sure" or "Not at all" means YES, they will do it.
I was wondering if...I was wondering if you could + base verbThe softest. Save it for a bigger favor — an upgrade, a late checkout.
Indirect questionDo you know if / Could you tell me + normal word order
...if the room is ready		To ask for information gently. The inside keeps statement order.
The one ruleInside an indirect question, the words go in normal order, like a statement — "if the room is ready", never "is the room ready". And after would you mind, the verb always takes -ing.

One idea holds them together: you leave the other person a way to say yes. That is what makes it polite.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Would you mind to send someone up?"
"Would you mind sending someone up?"
"Do you know where is the gym?"
"Do you know where the gym is?"

Two rules, and only two: after would you mind, the verb ends in -ing. Inside an indirect question, the words stay in normal order — exactly as in a statement.

Blunt: "Give me a late checkout."  →  "Could I have a late checkout, please?"
Blunt: "Fix the heating."  →  "Could you send someone up to look at the heating?"
Blunt: "Take this charge off my bill."  →  "I was wondering if you could take this charge off my bill."
Blunt: "Is breakfast included?"  →  "Do you know if breakfast is included?" (normal word order)
Blunt: "Move me to another room."  →  "Would you mind moving me to another room?" (-ing)
The Ladder

Same Request, Five Temperatures

The bigger the favor, the higher you climb.

1 · Blunt: "Move me to another room." — sounds like an order
2 · Can you: "Can you move me to another room?" — okay, but plain
3 · Could you: "Could you move me to another room?" — the safe default
4 · Would you mind: "Would you mind moving me to another room?"
5 · I was wondering: "I was wondering if you could move me to another room." — the softest

A towel? Stay low. A free upgrade? Climb high. The mistake is using the same step for everything.

The gym, wifi, and breakfast are included. The deposit and the valet surcharge are charged separately.
2. There is one charge on here you should question. Which one, and why?
The valet surcharge — forty-two dollars a night. She booked without a car, so she should ask them to remove it.
3. You want to know if you can check in before 3 p.m. Ask it as an indirect question.
"Could you tell me whether I could check in before three?" or "Do you know if the room would be ready earlier?"

Listening Strategy

Catch the Soft Words

The politeness lives in tiny words — and in fast speech, they melt together.

Would you mind waiting? — "would you" runs together: wouldja
Do you know if it is included? — "do you" shrinks to d'you
Could you hold on one second? — "could you" becomes couldja
Let me check that for you. — "let me" becomes lemme

The words do not disappear — they glue together. Learn the glued shape, and the polite frame stops hiding from you.

Comprehension

What Did David Say?

1. What room did David give her, and was it ready?
A junior suite on the fourteenth floor. Yes — housekeeping had just finished it, so she was all set.
2. What did he say about the late checkout?
Checkout is at eleven. He could not promise a late checkout, but he would flag the request.
3. What did he take at check-in, and what did he say about it?
A deposit of a hundred and fifty dollars — refundable, returned to her at checkout.
4. How did he react when she questioned the valet charge?
He agreed at once — "you're absolutely right, my apologies" — and removed it.
