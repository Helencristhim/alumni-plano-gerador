Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. These are the eight words you use in a debrief, when someone asks you what happened.

Reconstruct -- to tell what happened again, step by step, from the start
"Let me reconstruct the meeting for you."
Clarify -- to explain something so that it becomes clear
"She clarified the date with our lawyer."
Sequence -- the order of the events, from the first one to the last one
"The sequence changed, but the whole thing worked."
Version -- one person's way of telling what happened
"My version is accurate -- I was there."
Detail -- a small piece of information in a story -- a time, a name, a number
"That is the detail I needed."
Accurate -- correct and exact, with no mistakes
"Her report was short, but it was accurate."
Witness -- a person who was there and saw what happened
"I was a witness -- I was in the room."
Debrief -- a meeting after an event, to talk about what happened
"The director asked for a debrief."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Bruno is helping Maria Clara reconstruct the round table in Seoul. At two o'clock, the team was setting up the room. Bruno was testing the microphones, and the Korean delegation was waiting outside. Everything was ready. Then, at two fifteen, the news arrived: the minister was one hour late. While they were changing the schedule, the rector made a decision — he started the session without her. Maria Clara was not in the room at that moment; she was talking on the phone. So she asks Bruno: "What were you doing when the news arrived?" Bruno gives her every detail, in sequence. Now her version is accurate, and she can tell the whole thing herself.

1. Why is it "the team was setting up the room" and NOT "the team set up the room" here?
A Because "set up" is an irregular verb.
B Because it was a long action in progress at two o'clock — the background of the story.
C Because the team is more than one person.
2. "While they were changing the schedule, the rector made a decision." Why is "made" past simple?
A Because it is a short, completed action — the event inside the background.
B Because the rector is only one person.
C Because the verb "make" cannot take -ing.
3. Which question is correct?
A "What you were doing when the news arrived?"
B "What were you doing when the news arrived?"
C "What were you do when the news arrived?"
4. Which sentence uses "when" and "while" correctly?
A "While the news arrived, we were changing the schedule."
B "When the news arrived, we were changing the schedule."
C "While the news arrived, we changed the schedule."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Background and the Event

GRAMMAR

Two questions build any story: What was happening? (the background, long) and What happened? (the event, short). This is how you put them together.

Use it forStructureExample
The background — a long action in progresswas / were + verb-ingAt two o'clock, we were setting up the room.
The event — short and completedpast simpleThe news arrived at two fifteen.
The event cuts the backgroundbackground + when + past simpleI was testing the microphones when the news arrived.
Two long actions at the same timewhile + was / were + -ingWhile we were changing the schedule, he was reading the agenda.
Open the scene (new today)What were + you + doing when...?What were you doing when the minister arrived?
Yes/No question & expressionWere + they + waiting when...? · I used to... for the past habitWere they waiting when you arrived? · I used to bring an interpreter.

Common mistake: many people say "What you were doing?" In a question, were comes BEFORE you: "What were you doing?" And remember the connectors: when goes before the SHORT event ("when the news arrived"), while before the LONG background ("while we were changing the schedule"). The idiom of the lesson: "The whole thing."

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each moment of the debrief.

The director asks about the round table. You start with the background at two o'clock. You say:
A "At two o'clock, we set up the room and the news arrived."
B "At two o'clock, we were setting up the room, and the delegation was waiting outside."
C "At two o'clock, we are setting up the room."
You need one detail from Bruno. You want to know his exact action at that moment. You ask:
A "What you were doing when the news arrived?"
B "What were you doing when the news arrived?"
C "What did you doing when the news arrived?"
Two long actions happened at the same time: the team was changing the schedule, and the rector was reading the agenda. You say:
A "While the team was changing the schedule, the rector was reading the agenda."
B "When the team changed the schedule, the rector read the agenda."
C "While the team was changing the schedule, the rector reads the agenda."
The background was calm, and then one short thing happened. Which sentence is correct?
A "While the phone rang, I was reading the report."
B "I was reading the report when the phone rang."
C "I was reading the report when the phone was ringing."
You want to say that in the past you always brought an interpreter, but you do not do it now. You say:
A "I was using to bring an interpreter."
B "I used to bring an interpreter. Not anymore."
C "I used to bringing an interpreter."

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer — speak for one or two minutes, with no script. Every attempt counts.

The council asks you to reconstruct one real event from your career: a meeting that changed, a trip with a problem, or a decision under pressure. Give your version in sequence: the background (what was happening — use was / were + verb-ing at least three times), the event (what happened — past simple), and what you did. Join two moments with "when" and two with "while", and give at least three details (a time, a name, a number). Close with the idiom of the lesson: "The whole thing." Take your time and do not read from a script.
