Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Your executive toolkit, reactivated. You have met every one of these before -- listen, read the example, and recall where each one lands in the meeting.

Mandate -- the authority and remit you were given to act on
"Let me frame my mandate: I was brought in to lead the cultural transformation of the Canadian division."
Stakeholder -- anyone with a stake in your work whose support you need
"Every stakeholder in that room has read the Brazil numbers; my job is to turn interest into buy-in."
Buy-in -- genuine agreement and commitment to a plan, not just permission
"My predecessor rolled out the framework before he had the buy-in, so it never gained momentum."
Framework -- a structured set of principles you use to organize and act on a problem
"I am not importing a Brazilian framework; I am importing a diagnostic method and letting your context write the answers."
Credibility -- the trust and standing you build before you spend it on change
"I earn credibility by listening first, and only then do I move the needle."
Psychological safety -- the felt safety to speak up, disagree and admit a mistake without fear
"Success is the day a Canadian leader disagrees with me directly, because the psychological safety is real."
Developmental feedback -- input aimed at someone's growth, not a verdict on their past
"I give developmental feedback that grows a person instead of shrinking them."
Actionable insight -- a conclusion from data that you can actually act on
"I tell a story with the data and tease out the actionable insight without reading too much into it."
Groundwork -- the early, unglamorous preparation that makes later change possible
"I would rather build on the groundwork my predecessor laid than start over."
To move the needle -- to make real, measurable progress on something that matters
"The fastest way to move the needle is to hear the real objection now, not after we launch."
To read the room -- to sense the unspoken mood of a group before you act
"I read 'ambitious' as a polite objection, so I read the room and asked one follow-up."
To empower -- to give someone the authority and confidence to act on their own
"I am not there to rescue the team; I am there to empower them to solve the next problem without me."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions. This is a review -- watch every structure you built doing its one job in the room.

It is nine o'clock on a Thursday in Toronto, and for the first time the room Danielle is opening is not in Portuguese. The VP has handed her the meeting, and she begins where a leader begins: with the mandate. “I was brought in to lead the cultural transformation of the Canadian division,” she says, and then, without apologising for the size of it, “by the end of my first year I will have run a full diagnostic and rolled out a new onboarding experience.” The future perfect is doing exactly one job there: projecting quiet confidence about what will already be true. Fifteen weeks ago, that sentence would have come out as a hope. Tonight it comes out as a plan.

A regional director asks the fair, sharp question: how is this different from what her predecessor tried and dropped? She does not trash him. “He had rolled out the framework before he had the buy-in,” she explains — the past perfect sequencing the backstory cleanly — “so it never gained momentum.” Then the cleft, placing the emphasis exactly where she wants it: “What I am changing is the sequence, not the destination.” And the advanced modal that guides without commanding: “I would rather build on the groundwork he laid than start over.” Same destination, a different order of operations; she earns credibility first, and only then does she move the needle.

The academic lead, a doctor, pushes back: a Brazil-designed framework may not transfer here. She concedes the fair part — “I take the point about cultural distance” — and holds her ground with a hedge, not a flat no: “where we may differ is on transfer.” If she had prescribed a Brazilian answer on day one, she would have lost the room; the third conditional lets her own that call without drowning in it. She is not importing conclusions, she says; she is importing a diagnostic method and letting their context read the room for itself.

Then Grant asks the question she could not prepare for: what does success look like for her, personally? She does not reach for the perfect sentence. She reaches for the true one. “Let me think on that out loud,” she says — the hedge that is honesty, not weakness — “I'm not fully sure yet how I'd measure it, but success is the day a leader here disagrees with me directly, because the psychological safety is real.” It is the whole program in one answer: developmental feedback, actionable insight, the confidence to empower a room to push back on her. Grant nods. “That's the answer. Not the polished one — the true one.” The English was only ever the vehicle. The leader was hers all along.

1. Why does “by year one I will have run a full diagnostic” use the future perfect?
A Because the diagnostic already finished last year.
B The future perfect (will have + past participle) projects confidence about what will already be true by a point in time — it turns a hope into a plan.
C Because “year” always requires the future perfect.
2. Why is “He had rolled out the framework before he had the buy-in” in the past perfect?
A The past perfect sequences the earlier action in a backstory: the roll-out happened before the (later) point she is describing, so it steps one tense further back.
B Because the sentence is a question.
C Because “framework” is an uncountable noun.
3. What does the cleft “What I am changing is the sequence, not the destination” achieve?
A It makes the sentence more polite by adding a filler.
B It spotlights the key idea — the sequence — and contrasts it with what is NOT changing, putting the emphasis exactly where she wants it.
C It puts the sentence into the passive voice.
4. When Grant asks the question she didn't prepare for, what does she do — and why is it senior?
A She hedges honestly (“let me think on that out loud… I'm not fully sure yet”) and then commits to the true part. Naming uncertainty and still committing is more senior than a polished but hollow answer.
B She invents a confident-sounding number to fill the silence.
C She refuses to answer and changes the subject.
5. What is the through-line of the whole simulation — the point of the program?
A That perfect grammar is what makes a room follow a leader.
B The English was only ever the vehicle: Danielle is the same senior leader in English that she already was in Portuguese — she saw people clearly and told the truth, and that is what the room follows.
C That she should run the Canadian division exactly as she ran Brazil, unchanged.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Whole Map, One Job Each

GRAMMAR

Nothing new here -- this is the consolidation. Across fifteen lessons you built these structures one at a time. In the room they are not rules to remember; each one simply does a single job. Read the map, then produce one sentence of your own with each structure.

StructureIts one jobExample (your Thursday meeting)
Future perfectProject confidence about what you will have deliveredBy year one I will have run a full diagnostic.
Past perfectSequence a backstory -- what had happened beforeHe had rolled out the framework before he had the buy-in.
Advanced modalsGuide and disagree without commandingI would rather build on it than start over.
Wish / if onlyVoice adaptation, not complaintI wish the objections came sooner, so I read the room instead.
Third conditionalOwn a past call without drowning in itIf I had prescribed, I would have lost the room.
Cleft sentencesPut the emphasis exactly where you want itWhat I am changing is the sequence, not the destination.
HedgingBe honest about uncertainty, then commit: “I'm not fully sure yet -- but here is what I am sure of.”

The only instruction for the room: do not reach for the perfect sentence — reach for the true one. Hedge honestly where you are genuinely unsure, then commit to what you know. The most senior thing in any language is a person who can say “I don't have a complete answer yet, and here is how I would find one.” You already do that in Portuguese; on Thursday you are just going to do it in English.
