Lesson 2 -- Reactivating Your Professional Voice

Series
Formula 1: Drive to Survive — any season (Netflix)

Engineers and team principals doing, episode after episode, exactly what you practiced today: explaining what went wrong in a race without destroying the supplier, the driver or the team. The third conditional shows up in the wild — "if we had pitted one lap earlier, we would have won the race".

Tip: watch with English subtitles and write down 3 sentences shaped like "if we had..., we would have...". Then rewrite each one, swapping the race for YOUR project at Motiva.

Podcast
HBR IdeaCast — "Why Project-Based Work Fails — and How to Get It Right"

A full episode on why projects run late: scope, suppliers, interfaces and the hard conversation nobody wants to have. It is the vocabulary of this lesson (workstream, procurement, handover, lead time) in the business English your next international interview will use.

Tip: listen to 15 minutes ONCE, without pausing. Then record 60 seconds summarizing the main argument out loud — the bottleneck is your speaking, not your ear.

YouTube
BBC Learning English — How to use the third conditional (6 Minute Grammar)

Six minutes, a clean British accent, and the exact structure of this lesson: if + had + past participle, would have + past participle. Pay close attention to the contractions in speech ("if they'd sent it, we'd have caught the error") — that is how the sentence reaches your ear on a call, never spelled out.

Tip: after the video, say 5 sentences about YOUR project — three in the third conditional (one of them negative) and two describing your role with "I'm responsible for" and "My role involves". Out loud, with nothing written down first.
