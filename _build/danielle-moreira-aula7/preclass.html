Lesson 07 -- Pre-class

Navigating Difficult Conversations -- Feedback, Accountability, and Conflict in a Canadian Context

Leading the conversation nobody wants to have: name a behavioral pattern with evidence, work through the defensiveness without wounding the person, close with a corrective action and a date on it — and then REPORT the conversation upward, with precision. Key words: constructive feedback, conflict resolution, blind spot, defensiveness, resistance to change, non-violent communication, growth mindset, behavioral pattern, performance gap, corrective action, to take ownership of, to address head-on, to follow through on, to sugarcoat, to beat around the bush. Structure: reporting verbs and their five complementation patterns (he acknowledged THAT... / he denied MISSING... / he apologized FOR reacting... / I encouraged him TO take... / he agreed TO follow through...).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the vocabulary of a difficult conversation that ends in an agreement, not in resentment.

Constructive feedback -- feedback that names the behavior and its impact, and leaves the person able to act on it
"Constructive feedback is not softer feedback. It is feedback the other person can actually do something with."
Conflict resolution -- the work of turning a disagreement into a decision both sides can live with
"Conflict resolution is not making the conflict disappear. It is making it produce something."
Blind spot -- the thing everyone on the team can see about you, except you
"His blind spot is that he hears a question about a deadline as an accusation about his character."
Defensiveness -- the reflex of protecting yourself instead of hearing the point
"Defensiveness is not disagreement. It is the door closing before the sentence has finished."
Resistance to change -- the pushback a team produces when a change arrives without them
"Resistance to change is rarely about the change. More often than not, it is about not having been asked."
Non-violent communication -- naming the observation, the impact and the request, and never the character of the person
"Non-violent communication separates the behavior from the person. That separation is the whole method."
Growth mindset -- treating an ability as something you build, not something you were issued at birth
"A growth mindset turns I am not good at this into I am not good at this yet."
Behavioral pattern -- a behavior that has happened often enough to stop being an accident
"One missed deadline is an incident. Three is a behavioral pattern, and it needs a different conversation."
Performance gap -- the distance between what was agreed and what was actually delivered
"Name the performance gap with a number, or the whole conversation will sound like an opinion."
Corrective action -- the specific step, with a date on it, that closes the gap
"A conversation without a corrective action and a date is a conversation you will simply have again."
To take ownership of -- to accept that the outcome was yours, without being asked to
"I am not asking you to agree with me. I am asking you to take ownership of the rollout."
To address head-on -- to raise the difficult thing directly, at the start, instead of circling it
"I would rather address it head-on today than manage the consequences of it for six months."
To follow through on -- to actually do the thing you agreed to do, after the meeting has ended
"He agreed to every action in that meeting. He followed through on none of them."
To sugarcoat -- to make the message so gentle that the other person never hears the problem
"If you sugarcoat this, he will leave the call happy and nothing at all will change."
To beat around the bush -- to talk around the difficult point instead of naming it
"Do not beat around the bush with this team. Name the issue first, then protect the person."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

The conversation lasted forty minutes, and Ines Correia lost it in the first ninety seconds. She had rehearsed the opening for two days. She had a performance gap with three dates behind it, and a behavioral pattern that was no longer arguable. And then, at the door of the sentence, she flinched. She spent four minutes on how much the team valued him, and by the time she arrived at the deadlines, he had already worked out where she was going and had built the wall. She had tried not to wound him. What she had actually done was sugarcoat it, and a message that is sugarcoated is a message that never lands.

Her director asked for the debrief on the Thursday, and that is where the second lesson arrived. He acknowledged that the dates had slipped, Ines said. He denied being resistant to the project. He pointed out that nobody had asked him whether the timeline was realistic, and he accused the head office of imposing a plan built without a single question to the people who had to run it. He apologized for raising his voice. He did not apologize for anything he had said. And then, at the end, he agreed to follow through on one revised date, and he promised to bring the plan himself.

Her director listened to all of it and asked one question. And what did you ask him? Nothing, Ines said. There had not been room. That, her director told her, is the blind spot: she had walked in to deliver a verdict and had walked out with a confession she had never requested. Defensiveness is information, he said. It is a man telling you, badly, that something happened to him that you do not know about. Next time, name the pattern in the first thirty seconds, address it head-on, and then be quiet for long enough that he tells you the thing that is not in your spreadsheet. I would encourage you to close with one corrective action and a date — and, above all, I would advise you not to beat around the bush with a man who is already braced for the blow.

1. “He denied being resistant to the project.” — why can you NOT say “He denied to be resistant”?
A Because deny never takes a complement of any kind, only a direct object.
B Because deny takes the -ing form (deny + -ing), never the infinitive. The pattern belongs to the VERB itself, and it has to be learned together with it.
C Because the sentence is in the past, and in the past the infinitive disappears.
2. The number one mistake of this lesson: why is “She suggested me to talk to him” wrong?
A Because suggest does NOT take an object + infinitive. It is “suggested that I talk to him” or “suggested talking to him”. The structure travels straight in from your first language and brings the whole sentence down.
B Because the correct form would be “suggested me talking to him”.
C Because suggest only exists in the passive voice.
3. According to the text, what did Ines actually do wrong in the first ninety seconds?
A She opened with the three dates before she had built any rapport with him.
B She spent four minutes praising him before naming the issue — so he saw the message coming and was already defending himself by the time it arrived.
C She raised her voice, and he raised his voice back.
4. What does the director mean when he says that defensiveness is information?
A That a defensive employee is always hiding something, and the manager should keep pushing until it comes out.
B That defensiveness should simply be ignored, because it is an emotional reaction and not a professional one.
C That the defensiveness is telling her, badly, that something happened to him which is not in her spreadsheet — so the right move is to name the pattern and then be quiet.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Reporting Verbs & Their Patterns

GRAMMAR

Every reporting verb carries its own pattern, and the pattern cannot be worked out from the meaning: it is learned together with the verb. This is the grammar of the debrief — the moment you tell your VP what was said in the room.

PatternVerbsExample
1. verb + that + clauseacknowledge, point out, explain, insist, claim, admit, addHe acknowledged that the deadlines had slipped.
2. verb + -ingdeny, admit, suggest, recommend, mentionHe denied missing the deadlines. · She suggested talking to him first.
3. verb + preposition + -ingapologize for, insist on, object to, accuse sb of, blame sb forHe apologized for reacting defensively. · He accused the head office of imposing it.
4. verb + object + to + infinitiveencourage, urge, advise, remind, ask, invite, warnI encouraged him to take ownership of the rollout.
5. verb + to + infinitiveagree, refuse, promise, offer, threaten, decideHe agreed to follow through on the corrective action.
BackshiftWhen you report in the past, the tense steps back ONE step: “the dates have slipped” → He acknowledged that the dates had slipped. But if the fact still holds today, keeping the present is perfectly acceptable: “He insists that the timeline is impossible.”

Watch out (mistake number one): suggest does NOT take an object + infinitive. “He suggested me to talk to her” is the structure of your first language arriving intact. The correct form is “He suggested that I talk to her” or “He suggested talking to her”. The same goes for recommend.

Watch out (mistake number two): after a PREPOSITION you always use the -ing form, never the infinitive. “He apologized to react defensively” → “He apologized for reacting defensively”. Store each preposition glued to its verb: apologize for, insist on, object to, accuse somebody of.

Why this matters to you: the reporting verb you choose IS ALREADY an assessment. “He said the timeline was impossible” is neutral. “He insisted that the timeline was impossible” is a man who will not move. “He acknowledged that the timeline was impossible” is a man conceding a fact against himself. Same event, three readings — and your Canadian VP will read exactly the one you chose.

The three-sentence debrief: what he acknowledged, what he denied, and what he agreed to do. In that order, and nothing else. It is the report your VP wants on Thursday.

Order
