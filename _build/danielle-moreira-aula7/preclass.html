Aula 07 -- Pre-class

Navigating Difficult Conversations -- Feedback, Accountability, and Conflict in a Canadian Context

Conduzir a conversa que ninguém quer ter: nomear um padrão de comportamento com evidência, atravessar a postura defensiva sem ferir a pessoa, fechar com uma ação corretiva com data — e depois REPORTAR a conversa para cima, com precisão. Key words: constructive feedback, conflict resolution, blind spot, defensiveness, resistance to change, non-violent communication, growth mindset, behavioral pattern, performance gap, corrective action, to take ownership of, to address head-on, to follow through on, to sugarcoat, to beat around the bush. Structure: reporting verbs e os cinco padrões de complementação (he acknowledged THAT... / he denied MISSING... / he apologized FOR reacting... / I encouraged him TO take... / he agreed TO follow through...).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Ouça cada termo e leia o exemplo. Este é o vocabulário de uma conversa difícil que termina em acordo, e não em ressentimento.

Constructive feedback -- feedback that names the behavior and its impact, and leaves the person able to act on it (feedback construtivo)
"Constructive feedback is not softer feedback. It is feedback the other person can actually do something with."
Conflict resolution -- the work of turning a disagreement into a decision both sides can live with (resolução de conflitos)
"Conflict resolution is not making the conflict disappear. It is making it produce something."
Blind spot -- the thing everyone on the team can see about you, except you (ponto cego)
"His blind spot is that he hears a question about a deadline as an accusation about his character."
Defensiveness -- the reflex of protecting yourself instead of hearing the point (postura defensiva)
"Defensiveness is not disagreement. It is the door closing before the sentence has finished."
Resistance to change -- the pushback a team produces when a change arrives without them (resistência à mudança)
"Resistance to change is rarely about the change. More often than not, it is about not having been asked."
Non-violent communication -- naming the observation, the impact and the request, and never the character of the person (comunicação não-violenta)
"Non-violent communication separates the behavior from the person. That separation is the whole method."
Growth mindset -- treating an ability as something you build, not something you were issued at birth (mentalidade de crescimento)
"A growth mindset turns I am not good at this into I am not good at this yet."
Behavioral pattern -- a behavior that has happened often enough to stop being an accident (padrão de comportamento)
"One missed deadline is an incident. Three is a behavioral pattern, and it needs a different conversation."
Performance gap -- the distance between what was agreed and what was actually delivered (lacuna de desempenho)
"Name the performance gap with a number, or the whole conversation will sound like an opinion."
Corrective action -- the specific step, with a date on it, that closes the gap (ação corretiva)
"A conversation without a corrective action and a date is a conversation you will simply have again."
To take ownership of -- to accept that the outcome was yours, without being asked to (assumir a responsabilidade por)
"I am not asking you to agree with me. I am asking you to take ownership of the rollout."
To address head-on -- to raise the difficult thing directly, at the start, instead of circling it (encarar de frente)
"I would rather address it head-on today than manage the consequences of it for six months."
To follow through on -- to actually do the thing you agreed to do, after the meeting has ended (cumprir / levar até o fim)
"He agreed to every action in that meeting. He followed through on none of them."
To sugarcoat -- to make the message so gentle that the other person never hears the problem (dourar a pílula / suavizar demais)
"If you sugarcoat this, he will leave the call happy and nothing at all will change."
To beat around the bush -- to talk around the difficult point instead of naming it (enrolar / não ir direto ao ponto)
"Do not beat around the bush with this team. Name the issue first, then protect the person."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Leia o texto e responda às perguntas.

The conversation lasted forty minutes, and Ines Correia lost it in the first ninety seconds. She had rehearsed the opening for two days. She had a performance gap with three dates behind it, and a behavioral pattern that was no longer arguable. And then, at the door of the sentence, she flinched. She spent four minutes on how much the team valued him, and by the time she arrived at the deadlines, he had already worked out where she was going and had built the wall. She had tried not to wound him. What she had actually done was sugarcoat it, and a message that is sugarcoated is a message that never lands.

Her director asked for the debrief on the Thursday, and that is where the second lesson arrived. He acknowledged that the dates had slipped, Ines said. He denied being resistant to the project. He pointed out that nobody had asked him whether the timeline was realistic, and he accused the head office of imposing a plan built without a single question to the people who had to run it. He apologized for raising his voice. He did not apologize for anything he had said. And then, at the end, he agreed to follow through on one revised date, and he promised to bring the plan himself.

Her director listened to all of it and asked one question. And what did you ask him? Nothing, Ines said. There had not been room. That, her director told her, is the blind spot: she had walked in to deliver a verdict and had walked out with a confession she had never requested. Defensiveness is information, he said. It is a man telling you, badly, that something happened to him that you do not know about. Next time, name the pattern in the first thirty seconds, address it head-on, and then be quiet for long enough that he tells you the thing that is not in your spreadsheet. I would encourage you to close with one corrective action and a date — and, above all, I would advise you not to beat around the bush with a man who is already braced for the blow.

1. "He denied being resistant to the project." — por que NÃO se diz "He denied to be resistant"?
A Porque "deny" nunca aceita complemento nenhum, só objeto direto.
B Porque deny pede verbo em -ing (deny + -ing), nunca infinitivo. O padrão do verbo é uma propriedade DELE, e tem de ser memorizado com ele.
C Porque a frase está no passado, e no passado o infinitivo desaparece.
2. O erro brasileiro nº 1 desta aula: por que "She suggested me to talk to him" está errado?
A Porque suggest NÃO aceita objeto + infinitivo. É "suggested that I talk to him" ou "suggested talking to him". O "sugeriu para eu falar" do português chega inteiro e derruba a frase.
B Porque o correto seria "suggested me talking to him".
C Porque "suggest" só existe na voz passiva.
3. According to the text, what did Ines actually do wrong in the first ninety seconds?
A She opened with the three dates before she had built any rapport with him.
B She spent four minutes praising him before naming the issue — so he saw the message coming and was already defending himself by the time it arrived.
C She raised her voice, and he raised his voice back.
4. What does the director mean when he says that defensiveness is information?
A That a defensive employee is always hiding something, and the manager should keep pushing until it comes out.
B That defensiveness should simply be ignored, because it is an emotional reaction and not a professional one.
C That the defensiveness is telling her, badly, that something happened to him which is not in her spreadsheet — so the right move is to name the pattern and then be quiet.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Reporting Verbs & Their Patterns

GRAMMAR

Cada reporting verb carrega o seu próprio padrão, e o padrão NÃO se deduz do sentido: aprende-se junto com o verbo. É a gramática do debrief — a hora em que você conta à sua VP o que foi dito na sala (explicação em inglês e português).

PatternVerbs / VerbosExample
1. verb + that + oraçãoacknowledge, point out, explain, insist, claim, admit, addHe acknowledged that the deadlines had slipped.
2. verb + -ingdeny, admit, suggest, recommend, mentionHe denied missing the deadlines. · She suggested talking to him first.
3. verb + preposição + -ingapologize for, insist on, object to, accuse sb of, blame sb forHe apologized for reacting defensively. · He accused the head office of imposing it.
4. verb + objeto + to + infinitivoencourage, urge, advise, remind, ask, invite, warnI encouraged him to take ownership of the rollout.
5. verb + to + infinitivoagree, refuse, promise, offer, threaten, decideHe agreed to follow through on the corrective action.
BackshiftReportando no passado, o tempo recua UM passo: "the dates have slipped" → He acknowledged that the dates had slipped. Mas se o fato ainda vale hoje, manter o presente é perfeitamente aceitável: "He insists that the timeline is impossible."

Atenção (o erro nº 1): suggest NÃO aceita objeto + infinitivo. "He suggested me to talk to her" é o português "sugeriu para eu falar" chegando inteiro. O certo é "He suggested that I talk to her" ou "He suggested talking to her". O mesmo vale para recommend.

Atenção (o erro nº 2): depois de PREPOSIÇÃO vem sempre -ing, nunca infinitivo. "He apologized to react defensively" → "He apologized for reacting defensively". Guarde as preposições coladas ao verbo: apologize for, insist on, object to, accuse somebody of.

Por que isto importa para você: o reporting verb que você escolhe JÁ É uma avaliação. "He said the timeline was impossible" é neutro. "He insisted that the timeline was impossible" é um homem que não cede. "He acknowledged that the timeline was impossible" é um homem que reconhece um fato contra si. Mesmo evento, três leíturas — e a sua VP canadense vai ler exatamente a que você escolheu.

O debrief de três frases: o que ele acknowledged, o que ele denied, e o que ele agreed to do. Nesta ordem, e nada mais. É o relatório que a sua VP quer na quinta-feira.

Stage 2: Put the Difficult Conversation in Order

Order

Coloque os cinco movimentos na ordem em que você vai conduzir o 1:1 com um coordenador canadense resistente à mudança.

?Hand him the microphone and go quiet: ask what you do not know, and listen past the defensiveness to the information underneath it.
?Debrief upward in three sentences: what he acknowledged, what he denied, and what he agreed to do.
?Address it head-on in the first thirty seconds: name the issue without sugarcoating it and without beating around the bush.
?Close with one corrective action and a date on it, and ask him to take ownership of that date in front of you.
?Put the evidence on the table: the behavioral pattern, the three dates, and the performance gap between what was agreed and what arrived.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Escolha a melhor resposta para cada momento real do seu 1:1 com um coordenador canadense.

You open the 1:1. He has missed three deadlines. The most senior first sentence is:
A “Before anything else, I want you to know how much this team values you and how happy we are with your work.”
B “I want to address something head-on. Three dates on the rollout have slipped, and what I have noticed is a pattern rather than an incident.”
C “There is a small thing I wanted to mention, and it is really not a big deal at all.”
He answers: “So you are building a case against me.” You say:
A “It is not the behavior itself that concerns me. It is the impact, and this conversation ends with one corrective action that you and I agree on together.”
B “If you are going to be defensive, there is no point in continuing this conversation.”
C “No, no. Forget the deadlines. Let us talk about something else.”
Your VP asks for the debrief. Which sentence reports the conversation with the most precision?
A “He said a lot of things, and in the end he was fine with everything.”
B “He suggested me to change the timeline, and he denied to be resistant.”
C “He acknowledged that the dates had slipped, he denied being resistant to the project, and he agreed to follow through on a revised plan by the fifteenth.”
Which sentence uses the reporting verb pattern correctly?
A “He apologized to react defensively in front of the team.”
B “He accused the head office of imposing a plan that nobody had been asked about.”
C “She recommended me to speak with him before the steering meeting.”
He goes quiet and then says: “Nobody ever asked me if that timeline was possible.” And he is right. You say:
A “You are right, and I am not going to argue with that. The pattern still stands, and so does the question I am asking you now: what would a realistic date look like?”
B “That is not relevant. The deadlines were the deadlines.”
C “You are completely right, and I am sorry. Let us forget the three dates.”
