Series
The Last Dance — Episode 7 (Netflix)

The episode where Michael Jordan is confronted with the question of this lesson: did my feedback build that team, or did it wound those people? Teammates describe a behavioral pattern; he answers, he defends himself, and at the end — in the closing scene, with the camera still running — he takes ownership. It is the difference between harshness and constructive feedback, on film. Connection to Lesson 7: watch his blind spot (he does not see what the whole room sees) and defensiveness as INFORMATION: every time he defends himself, he tells you something nobody had asked about.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Write down THREE lines in which a teammate describes a pattern (not an incident), and ONE in which Jordan drops the defensiveness and starts to take ownership of his own style. Then report the scene in three sentences, one for each reporting verb: what he acknowledged, what he denied, what he agreed to.

Podcast
WorkLife with Adam Grant — “How to Love Criticism” (TED)

Adam Grant walks into a company where criticizing your boss in public is an obligation, and takes apart why almost everyone confuses hard feedback with cruel feedback. It is the theory behind this lesson: why sugarcoating destroys the message, and why a culture that can take criticism needs psychological safety first. Connection to Lesson 7: this is your room in August — a team that has to hear something difficult from someone who has only just arrived.

Tip: listen once, with no pauses. Then summarize it out loud in ninety seconds, using THREE reporting verbs with different patterns (“He points out that...” / “He recommends ...ing” / “He encourages leaders to...”). Record it and listen back.

YouTube
LeeAnn Renninger — “The secret to giving great feedback” (TED, The Way We Work, 5 min)

Five minutes on the four-part formula that holds a difficult 1:1 together: the micro-yes, the specific data point, the impact, and the question that hands the floor back to the other person. It is the backbone of the conversation you are about to run with a resistant coordinator — and the answer to “how do I open without beating around the bush, and without wounding him?”. Connection to Lesson 7: her closing question is the exact moment when defensiveness turns into information.

Tip: watch it twice. On the second viewing, write your own thirty-second opening for the 1:1 with the coordinator, using her formula + address head-on + behavioral pattern. Bring it in writing to Lesson 8.
