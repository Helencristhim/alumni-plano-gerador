Get buy-in from people who do not report to you. Find the win for them first, frame so they own the how instead of giving orders, enlist an ally to champion the idea before the meeting, rally everyone around a shared goal, and use reciprocity. New words: advocate, champion, ally, enlist, rally, sway, endorse, reciprocity. "I rallied all three teams around the regional target."

"Their numbers swayed the room more than my slides did."

"Once the director endorsed it, the others fell in line."

"Reciprocity is real: back their priorities and they back yours."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the influence debrief and answer the questions.

The Growth Manager needed three teams for the regional rollout, and not one of them reported to him. He could not give orders, so he had to sway people who owed him nothing. First he found the win for each side — a cleaner forecast for finance, fewer fire drills for IT. Then, instead of commanding, he got people to act: "I got finance to back the plan" and "I'll have IT review it" — notice that after get we use to + verb, but after have we use the bare verb, with no to. He framed every request with a reason: "I shaped it so that they owned the how," and "I met them early in order to enlist their support." He enlisted one ally before the meeting and asked her to champion the idea, because one advocate in the room is worth ten slides. Finally he rallied the teams around a shared target and relied on reciprocity — he backed their priorities first, so they would back his. By the end, every team endorsed the plan as if it were their own.

1. Why does he say "I got finance TO back the plan" instead of "Back the plan"?
A He has no authority over finance; "get someone to" persuades, an order would fail.
B "Get someone to" and an order mean exactly the same thing.
C Because finance reports directly to him.
2. Which form is correct after "have" in the causative?
A "I'll have IT to review it." (have + to + verb)
B "I'll have IT review it." (have + bare verb, no "to")
C "I'll have IT reviewing it." (have + -ing)
3. What does "so that" add to "I shaped it so that they owned the how"?
A Nothing; it's just filler.
B The reason/result — the "why" that makes people want to act.
C It turns the sentence into a question.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Causatives & Purpose Clauses

GRAMMAR

When you cannot order, you get people to act — and give the reason why.

StructureFormExample
Get + person + to + verbpersuade / lead someone to act"I got finance to back it."
Have + person + verbarrange for someone to do it (NO "to")"I'll have IT review the plan."
… so that + clausethe reason / result (the "why")"I framed it so that they owned it."
… in order to + verbthe purpose (formal)"I met them early in order to enlist support."
Watch the trap: after get you DO use "to" ("get them to agree"); after have you do NOT ("have them agree"). And always attach the reason with "so that + clause" or "in order to + verb" — that "why" is what turns a request into something a peer actually wants to do. PT: quando voce nao pode mandar, voce CONSEGUE que a pessoa aja, em vez de ordenar. Use "get + pessoa + TO + verbo" ("I got them to agree") e "have + pessoa + verbo" SEM "to" ("I'll have IT review it"). Cuidado: depois de GET usa-se "to"; depois de HAVE, NAO. E sempre amarre o porque com "so that" + oracao ("so that they own it") ou "in order to" + verbo ("in order to win support"). Essa razao e o que transforma um pedido em algo que um colega quer fazer.
