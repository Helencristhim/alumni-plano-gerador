Chapter 3 -- The Code

Grammar Discovery

going to (decided plans) vs will (predictions and offers)

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

Is it a decided plan, or a prediction at the moment?

"I am going to launch the kickoff next quarter."

"I think the outcome will be excellent."

"We are going to allocate two senior nurses."

"Do not worry, I will send you the budget today."

Grammar Rule

going to & will

StructureWhen we use it
am / is / are + going to + verb (I am going to launch)A plan you already decided before now
going to (it is going to rain)A prediction with evidence you can see now
will + base verb (it will be excellent)A prediction or opinion: I think, probably, I am sure
will + base verb (I will help you)A decision made now, an offer, or a promise

Key idea: if the plan is already decided, use going to. If you predict, offer, promise, or decide at the moment of speaking, use will. After will, always use the base verb -- never will to.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"I will start the kickoff next Monday -- it is already on my calendar."
"I am going to start the kickoff next Monday."

A plan you already decided takes going to. Use will only for decisions you make at the moment, predictions, offers, and promises.

Artefact

Kickoff Agenda

ROBOTIC SURGERY TRAINING PROGRAM

Kickoff agenda -- Lead: Andreia Heins, RN -- Hospital Israelita Albert Einstein

Decided plans (going to)

We are going to launch the kickoff next quarter. We are going to set the main goal: forty nurses in two years. We are going to allocate one mentor to every two learners.

Predictions and promises (will)

We forecast that the first outcome will be visible in six months. We are sure the team will be ready for live procedures by December. We commit to support every nurse.

Roadmap: Phase 1 (theory) -- deadline: end of March. Phase 2 (simulators) -- estimated twenty hours per nurse. Phase 3 (live procedures) -- next steps to follow.
