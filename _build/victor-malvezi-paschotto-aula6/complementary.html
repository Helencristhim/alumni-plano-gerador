Lesson 6 -- The Mid-Block Review: Putting Lessons 1 to 5 Together

Series
Industry (HBO Max) -- Season 2, Episode 1: "Daybreak"

The young bankers at Pierpoint return for performance reviews, and every conversation is a live exam in the five tools from this block. Watch how they present achievements ("I have closed three accounts this quarter"), explain misses ("if I had flagged the risk earlier..."), speculate about a deal that went wrong ("the client must have changed his mind"), debrief what a managing director told them, and ask for support in carefully hedged language. Direct connection to Lesson 6: it shows the same review skills Victor needs, under pressure, in a real corporate setting.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Pause five times and label each line with one of the five tools: achievement, unreal past, speculation, debrief, or register.

Podcast
HBR IdeaCast (Harvard Business Review) -- "How to Run a Quarterly Business Review"

This episode walks through the structure of a strong quarterly review: how to open with results, how to take ownership of misses without being defensive, and how to frame next steps. The hosts model exactly the language Victor practiced -- present perfect for wins, careful speculation about causes, and diplomatic requests. A clear bridge from the block to a real review you might give at Brinks.

Tip: listen once for structure, then draft your own three-line review opener: one achievement, one honest setback, one next step. Read it out loud twice.

YouTube
"Present Perfect, Conditionals, Reported Speech: A B2 Grammar Review" -- BBC Learning English

A compact review of the exact structures consolidated in Lesson 6. The presenters contrast present perfect with past simple, walk through second and third conditionals side by side, and show how reported speech backshifts tenses. They use clear workplace examples and flag the most common mistakes Brazilian professionals make, such as adding a finished time to the present perfect or putting "would" in the if-clause.

Tip: watch with a notebook. Every time a structure appears, write one sentence about your own work using the same pattern.
