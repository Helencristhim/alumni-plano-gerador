Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Leia o texto e responda às perguntas.

English has a music. Two tools make you clear and confident. First, word stress: every long word has one strong syllable, louder and longer — we say con-FE-rence and re-com-men-DA-tion. Second, sentence stress: we stress the content words (nouns, verbs) and keep the small function words (the, to, of, is) weak — "The PROJECT is on SCHEDULE." Then there is intonation, the up and down of the voice: questions usually go up ↗ and statements go down ↘. And for confidence, use emphatic stress: push one word hard to contrast or correct — "I said the FIRST option, not the second." Never speak in a flat monotone.

1. How many strong syllables does a long word usually have?
A One strong syllable (e.g. con-FE-rence).
B Every syllable is equally strong.
C The last syllable is always strong.
2. In a sentence, which words do we keep weak?
A The content words (nouns, verbs).
B The small function words (the, to, of, is).
C All the words equally.
3. How does your voice usually move on a yes/no question?
A It falls ↘ at the end.
B It rises ↗ at the end.
C It stays flat (monotone).

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip — Stress & Intonation Rules

GRAMMAR

As regras de stress e entonação (bilingue EN + PT).

FerramentaRuleExample
Word stressone strong syllable per long wordcon-FE-rence / pri-O-ri-tize
Sentence stressstrong content words, weak function wordsThe PROJECT is on SCHEDULE.
Intonationrise ↗ for questions, fall ↘ for statementsAre you ready? ↗ / I am ready. ↘
Regra (PT)Toda palavra longa tem uma sílaba forte (mais alta e mais longa): con-FE-rence. Na frase, estresse as content words (substantivos, verbos) e deixe as function words pequenas (the, to, of, is) fracas. A voz sobe ↗ em perguntas yes/no e desce ↘ em afirmações. Para soar seguro, use emphatic stress: empurre uma palavra com força — "I said the FIRST option, not the second." Nunca fale num monotone plano.
