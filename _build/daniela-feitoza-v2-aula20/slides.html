Chapter 3

The Stress Code

Word stress, sentence stress, and rising vs falling tone

Stress Discovery

Listen and Notice

con · FE · rence — the strong syllable is in the middle

re · com · men · DA · tion — the strong part is near the end

The PROJECT is on SCHEDULE — small words stay weak

Are you ready? ↗   I am ready. ↘

One strong syllable per long word. Strong content words, weak small words. Up for questions, down for statements. What is the pattern?

Stress Code

Stress, Tone, Emphasis

ToolRuleExample
Word stressOne strong syllable per long word (louder + longer)con-FE-rence / pri-O-ri-tize
Sentence stressStrong content words, weak function wordsThe PROJECT is on SCHEDULE.
IntonationRise ↗ for yes/no questions, fall ↘ for statementsAre you ready? ↗ / I am ready. ↘
EmphaticPush ONE word hard to contrast or correct: "I said the FIRST option, not the second." It makes you sound sure, not nervous.

Content words carry meaning (nouns, verbs, adjectives). Function words are small (the, to, of, is) — keep them weak.

Common Mistake

Flat vs Alive

✗ WRONG STRESS

"CON-fe-rence" (stress on the first syllable)

✓ RIGHT STRESS

"con-FE-rence" (strong middle syllable)

✗ MONOTONE

"The. Project. Is. On. Schedule." (flat, every word equal)

✓ ALIVE

"The PROJECT is on SCHEDULE." (strong + weak)

One strong syllable per long word. Stress the big content words; keep small words weak. Never a flat monotone.

Long words have one strong syllable, louder and longer: con-FE-rence, re-com-men-DA-tion, pri-O-ri-tize.

We stress content words (nouns, verbs) and keep function words (the, to, of) short and weak: The PROJECT is on SCHEDULE.
