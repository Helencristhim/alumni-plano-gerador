The Proposal -- Persuasive Business Writing

Turn a summary into a proposal that wins approval: open with the rationale, frame the upside, use a persuasive if ("If you approve..., we will...") and a formal inversion ("Should you decide..."), add an incentive and ask the reader to endorse it. New vocabulary: rationale, compelling, persuade, incentive, endorse, justify, upside, viable.

Stage 1.1: Proposal Vocabulary

Vocabulary

Eight words for the language of persuasion in a written proposal. Listen to each one.

Rationale -- the logical reason behind a decision or proposal
"Lead with the rationale: why this matters to them." (Justificativa / razão)
Compelling -- so convincing that it drives the reader to act
"Make the benefit compelling, framed as their upside." (Convincente / irresistível)
Persuade -- to make someone decide to do or accept something
"A proposal has to persuade, not just inform." (Persuadir / convencer)
Incentive -- a reward offered to make someone agree or act
"Add an incentive, like a reduced fee for the first quarter." (Incentivo)
Endorse -- to publicly support or formally approve a plan
"Ask the partner to endorse the next step." (Endossar / aprovar)
Justify -- to show, with reasons, why something is right or worth it
"Every cost in a proposal has to justify itself." (Justificar)
Upside -- the benefit or gain the reader stands to get
"Frame the offer as their upside, not your revenue." (Vantagem / lado positivo)
Viable -- realistic and workable; able to be done in practice
"Keep every promise viable, nothing we cannot deliver." (Viável / factível)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

Grammar

Eduardo has to turn his summary into a proposal, so it must persuade, not just inform. He opens with the rationale: the partner loses two weeks on every onboarding. He frames the benefit as the partner's upside and makes it compelling: "If you approve the pilot, we will cut that time in half." For a more formal tone he writes, "Should you decide to proceed, we will assign a dedicated team." He adds an incentive to lower the barrier — "If you sign this quarter, we will waive the setup fee" — keeps every promise viable, and closes by asking the partner to endorse the plan.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip

Grammar

A condicional persuasiva liga uma ação do leitor a um benefício que você promete: depois de if usa-se o PRESENTE, e o will fica só na promessa. Para um tom mais formal e seguro, troque o if por uma inversão: Should you decide... ou Were we to... (sem a palavra if). / A persuasive conditional links the reader's action to a benefit you promise — present tense after "if", "will" only in the promise.

RegisterStructureExample
StandardIf + present, will + verb"If you approve, we will deliver in 30 days."
FormalShould + you + verb, will..."Should you decide to proceed, we will assign a team."
HypotheticalWere + subject + to + verb, would..."Were we to partner, your costs would fall."
Incentive closeIf + condition, will + reward"If you sign this quarter, we will waive the fee."

Key point / Ponto-chave: nunca diga "If you will approve" — depois de if o verbo fica no presente ("If you approve"). As formas formais Should you decide... e Were we to... substituem o if por completo e não levam to depois do sujeito ("Should you decide", não "Should you to decide").

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best line for each proposal moment.

You are opening a proposal to a partner. Which line persuades best?
A "We offer a B2B platform with many features."
B "Today your team loses two weeks on every onboarding — here is how to fix that."
C "Let us know if you are interested."
You want to promise a result in exchange for approval. Which is correct?
A "If you approve the pilot, we will deliver results in thirty days."
B "If you will approve the pilot, we deliver results."
C "You approve, we delivering results."
