Warm-up

When the Room Is Split

Winning the argument is easy. Aligning the room is hard. The skill is getting two dug-in sides to a yes they all actually own — not a yes half of them resent.

Chapter 3

The Grammar

Propose so it feels shared, not imposed

Grammar Discovery

Spot the Pattern

Three lines — which ones invite the room in?

"We will fund one region now." (a verdict — can feel imposed)

"What if we funded one region now?" (an invitation — what if + past)

"I'd suggest we cap the spend." (a soft proposal — I'd suggest / we could)

Soft on the outside, clear on the inside.

The Rule

The Consensus Toolkit

MoveStructureExample
AcknowledgeThere's merit in… / I hear both sides"There's merit in both positions."
ConvergeWhere we all agree is… / what we share is"Where we all agree is a healthy company in a year."
Propose (soft)What if we + past simple / suppose we / I'd suggest / we could"What if we funded one region first?"
Build onBuilding on X's point… / to add to that"Building on Adriana's point, we could cap the spend."
ConfirmCan everyone live with…? / are we all on board?"Can everyone live with that?"

The key thing to keep clean:

Propose with tentative language, not orders. "What if we funded…" uses the past simple as a softener — it is not real past time; it signals "this is a suggestion, not a verdict." The same job is done by suppose we, I'd suggest, and we could. Acknowledge first so people give ground, converge on the common ground, then propose softly — and always close by checking everyone can genuinely live with it. A lukewarm nod is not consensus.

Common Mistake

Steamroll vs Align

✗ IMPOSED AS AN ORDER

"We will fund all four regions. That's decided."

✓ PROPOSED, INVITING

"What if we funded one region first and reviewed it?"

✗ ASSUMED AGREEMENT

"So we all agree. Great, moving on." (nobody actually said yes)

✓ CONFIRMED BUY-IN

"Can everyone genuinely live with that? Adriana?"

Propose tentatively so people give ground. Then confirm a real yes, never a lukewarm shrug.

Chapter 4

Bringing Them Together

Two factions, one decision

Dialogue

The Split Room

A
Victor, I'll be blunt: Operations cannot back funding four regions. The margin is too thin this year.
V
I hear you, and there's real merit in that. Protecting the margin is not caution, it's responsibility. Growth has a point too, but I'm not going to pretend the risk isn't real.
A
I appreciate that. But we keep landing in a stalemate — your team pushes, mine holds, and nothing moves.
V
Then let's start from what we share. Where we all agree is a healthy company in eighteen months, not just a clean quarter. If that's the real goal, we're closer than it looks.
A
Agreed on the goal. So what's the actual proposal — and don't just tell me to approve it.
V
What if we funded one region now instead of four, and reviewed it at the half-year? That's the middle ground: growth gets traction, your margin stays protected.
A
That I can work with. If you'd cap the spend, I'd stop fighting the whole thing.
V
Building on your point, let's cap it and hold the review date firm. Before we lock it, can everyone genuinely live with this — a real yes, not a lukewarm one?
A
Yes — a real one. Capped, one region, reviewed at half-year. That's a win-win I'll defend to my team.
