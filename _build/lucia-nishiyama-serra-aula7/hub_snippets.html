Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

To evolve -- to change and develop gradually and naturally over time
"The role of a technology leader has evolved completely since the 1980s."
Landscape -- the whole picture of an area, such as the market or the technology, at one time
"The tech landscape used to be about mainframes; now it is about the cloud."
Era -- a long period of time marked by a particular style, event, or technology
"The mainframe era taught me a discipline I still use today."
Shift -- a change from one position, idea, or way of working to another
"The shift from on-premise systems to the cloud changed everything."
To transform -- to change something completely, usually into something better
"Cloud computing transformed how companies build software."
To reflect -- to think back carefully about something from the past
"When I reflect on 41 years, I see how much has changed."
Obsolete -- no longer used because something newer or better has replaced it
"The systems I used to manage are obsolete today."
Decade -- a period of ten years
"Each decade brought a completely new technology to learn."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

When Lucia describes her career, she paints a picture of change. "When I started, I used to manage entire data centers by hand. Every night I would stay late, checking each server one by one." Notice the two forms. Used to + a base verb describes a past state or habit that is no longer true -- how things were. Would + a base verb describes a repeated past action inside that story -- what she did again and again. But would only works for actions, never for states: she says "I used to be a junior developer," not "I would be." The whole landscape has transformed since then. Systems that she used to run every day are obsolete now, and the industry has evolved through several eras. When she reflects on forty-one years, the contrast between what she used to do and what she does today is exactly what gives her story authority.

1. Why does the text say "I used to be a junior developer" and not "I would be a junior developer"?
A "Be" is a state, and "would" only works for repeated actions, not states.
B "Would be" is too formal for an interview.
C The two sentences mean exactly the same thing.
2. What does "used to" tell the listener about the data centers Lucia managed by hand?
A That she still manages them the same way today.
B That it was true in the past but is no longer true now.
C That it only happened one single time.
3. According to the text, what gives Lucia's story its authority?
A The number of companies on her resume.
B Using difficult, technical vocabulary.
C The contrast between what she used to do and what she does today.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Used To and Would

GRAMMAR

Two forms for telling a story of change. Use them to show the contrast between the past and the present.

FormUseExample
used to + base verbPast states and habits that are no longer true.I used to be a developer.
I used to manage data centers.
would + base verbRepeated past actions only -- never states.Every night I would stay late.
We would guard our computer time.
Negative & questionDrop the "d" after did: I didn't use to trust the cloud. · Did you use to work with mainframes?
Why it matters"Used to" signals not anymore, so the listener feels the contrast with today. Simple past ("I managed data centers") just reports an event; "used to" tells a story of change.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each real interview moment.

You want to describe a past state that is no longer true. Which is correct?
A "I used to be a junior developer at IBM."
B "I would be a junior developer at IBM."
C "I use to be a junior developer at IBM."
An interviewer asks: "Did you use to work with mainframes?" Which reply is correct?
A "Yes, I would work with mainframes every single day, all day."
B "Yes, I used to manage them, and every night I would run the backups."
C "Yes, I use to manage them for many years."
You want to say a repeated past habit -- an action you did again and again. Which fits best?
A "When a server failed, someone would being at midnight."
B "When a server failed, someone would drive in at midnight to fix it."
C "When a server failed, someone would be a fixer at midnight."
You want the negative form of "used to." Which is correct?
A "I didn't used to trust the cloud."
B "I didn't use to trust the cloud."
C "I wouldn't be trusting the cloud."
