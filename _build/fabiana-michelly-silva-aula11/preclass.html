Vocabulary

Cross-cultural -- involving two or more different cultures
"Cross-cultural negotiation requires more than language skills." (Intercultural)
High-context culture -- a culture where meaning is communicated indirectly
"Japan is a high-context culture, which means a lot goes unsaid." (Cultura de alto contexto)
Low-context culture -- a culture where meaning is stated openly and directly
"Germany is a low-context culture, where directness is respected." (Cultura de baixo contexto)
Save face -- to protect someone's dignity in front of others
"Never correct a partner publicly; let them save face." (Salvar as aparências)
Read between the lines -- to understand a hidden or indirect meaning
"When they said maybe, I had to read between the lines." (Ler nas entrelinhas)
Etiquette -- the unwritten rules of polite, correct behavior
"Business etiquette varies enormously between countries." (Etiqueta)
Directness -- being clear, frank, and straightforward
"In some cultures, directness is a sign of respect." (Franqueza / objetividade)
Small talk -- light, informal talk before serious business
"In Brazil, small talk builds the relationship before the deal." (Conversa informal)
Reciprocity -- exchanging favors for mutual benefit
"Reciprocity matters: a concession should be returned." (Reciprocidade)
Common ground -- shared interests both sides can agree on
"We found common ground on delivery timelines first." (Pontos em comum)
Mediator -- a neutral person who helps two sides agree
"The mediator who joined us helped adjust our pace." (Mediador)
Accommodate -- to adjust your behavior to fit another's style
"A good negotiator accommodates the other side's pace." (Acomodar-se / adaptar-se)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Cross-cultural negotiation is an art. The manager who leads a meeting in a high-context culture rarely says no directly. A response that sounds like maybe is often a polite refusal, which you have to read between the lines. Silence, which many Westerners find awkward, is actually a sign of careful thinking. The senior manager whose approval the team needs usually speaks last. Etiquette matters enormously here, and the colleague who introduces you sets the tone. In a low-context culture, the buyer who leads the meeting gets straight to the numbers, which can surprise people used to small talk. The negotiator who reads the room, and who accommodates the culture in front of them, is the one who builds common ground. Tokyo, where the first deal was signed, taught the team the value of reciprocity.

1. In "the manager who leads a meeting", what does "who" refer to?
A A person -- "who" is used for people (defining clause).
B A thing -- "who" is used for objects.
C A place -- "who" is used for locations.
2. Why is "which" (not "that") used in "Silence, which many find awkward, ..."?
A Because "silence" is a person.
B It is a non-defining clause (extra info, with commas) -- which is required, never that.
C Because the sentence is in the past.
3. What does "whose" express in "the manager whose approval the team needs"?
A Possession -- the approval belongs to the manager.
B A place where something happens.
C The same as "who is".

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Relative Clauses

GRAMMAR

Pronomes relativos para descrever pessoas, coisas e lugares com precisão. / Relative pronouns to describe people, things, and places with precision.

Pronoun / PronomeUse / UsoExample / Exemplo
who / thatPessoas / PeopleThe manager who leads the call.
which / thatCoisas e ideias / ThingsThe deal that closes.
whosePosse / PossessionThe manager whose approval we need.
where / whenLugares e tempos / Places, timesThe room where terms are agreed.
Non-defining (com vírgulas)Info extra; use which, nunca that / Extra info, never thatSilence, which they find odd, is normal.
Ponto-chaveVírgulas = clause não-definidora (info extra, use which/who). Sem vírgulas = clause definidora (essencial).

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best response for each professional situation.

You want to identify a specific person -- the one who leads the call. The best sentence is:
A "The partner who leads the call rarely says no." (defining, person = who)
B "The partner which leads the call rarely says no." (which is for things)
C "The partner, who leads the call, rarely says no." (commas make it non-essential, but here it is essential)
You want to add extra, non-essential information about silence. You say:
A "Silence that they find awkward is normal here." (no commas + that = defining)
B "Silence, which they find awkward, is normal here." (non-defining = commas + which)
C "Silence, that they find awkward, is normal here." (non-defining cannot use that)
You want to talk about the manager and his team's responsibility. Use:
A "The manager whose team handles procurement asked pointed questions." (possession = whose)
B "The manager who's team handles procurement asked pointed questions." (who's = who is)
C "The manager which team handles procurement asked pointed questions." (which cannot show possession of a person)
