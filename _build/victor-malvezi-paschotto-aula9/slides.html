Chapter 3

Reading the Signals

Modals of deduction for interpreting behavior

Grammar Discovery

Spot the Pattern

How sure is the speaker in each reading?

"They haven't replied yet — they might be waiting for the whole team to agree."

"She asked about deadlines three times — punctuality must be a priority for them."

"He stayed silent, but that can't mean he disagrees; in his team, silence often means he is still thinking."

These are readings, not facts. Each one says how sure the speaker is.

Grammar Rule

The Deduction Scale

CertaintyFormExample
Possiblemight / may / could + base"They might be waiting for consensus."
Almost sure (yes)must + base"Punctuality must be important to them."
Almost sure (no)can't + base"That can't mean he disagrees."
Possibly notmay not / might not + base"They may not have the final say."

Reading the room is not guessing:

A signal tells you what might be true, never what is certainly true. Use might / may / could for a possible reading, must when the logic makes you almost sure, and can't for a confident no. Always use the base verb after the modal — "must be", never "must to be" or "must is". When in doubt, do not assume; check it.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"He must to be the decision-maker."

✓ RIGHT

"He must be the decision-maker."

✗ WRONG

"She runs the account, so she mustn't be junior."

✓ RIGHT

"She runs the account, so she can't be junior."

After a modal, use the base verb with no "to" (must be, not must to be). For a confident negative reading, use "can't", never "mustn't" — "mustn't" means it is forbidden, not that it is impossible.

Chapter 4

On the Call

Debriefing a cross-cultural conversation

Dialogue

After the Call

E
So, how did the call with Frankfurt and Tokyo go? I saw a few long silences on my screen.
V
Better than I expected. But you're right about the silence. When the Tokyo team went quiet, I almost misread it. I thought, "They might be unhappy." Then I remembered: in their team, a pause usually means they're still building agreement.
E
Exactly. A pause there isn't a no. And Frankfurt? They can be very direct.
V
Very. Klaus said the timeline simply "doesn't work." At first that came across as harsh, but it wasn't personal. For them, directness is just the norm — it's actually a sign of respect.
E
It must be hard to balance both styles in one call.
V
It was. So I adapted. With Frankfurt I went straight to the numbers; with Tokyo I slowed down and left room for them to think. And I made sure nobody had to lose face when we disagreed.
E
That's the skill. And a little small talk never hurts — here in Buenos Aires the rapport has to come first, or nothing moves.
V
I'm learning that. Read the room, adapt the style, protect the relationship. The etiquette changes, but respect is the one thing that travels everywhere.
