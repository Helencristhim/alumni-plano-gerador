Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the language of a leader who develops people in a one-on-one — the difference between telling a mentee what to do and coaching them into doing it themselves.

Developmental feedback -- input aimed at helping someone grow, not at correcting a single past error
"She reframed the review as developmental feedback, so Owen heard a path forward instead of a verdict on his past."
Growth edge -- the next skill or capacity a person is just ready to stretch into
"His growth edge was not effort; it was asking for help before a deadline slipped, and she named it gently."
Coachable moment -- a short opening when someone is genuinely open to learning something
"The moment Owen admitted he was struggling was a coachable moment, and she did not waste it with advice he had not asked for."
Active listening -- listening fully to understand the person, not to prepare your reply
"Active listening meant she let the silence sit until Owen said the thing he had almost swallowed."
Reframing -- offering a different, more useful way to see the same situation
"Instead of correcting him, she tried reframing: he was not failing, he was on the steep part of a learning curve."
Sounding board -- a trusted person you think out loud with to test your own ideas
"She offered to be a sounding board, not a judge, so he could think out loud without defending himself."
Stretch assignment -- a task set deliberately beyond someone's comfort zone to build capacity
"The stretch assignment was deliberately a size too big, because that is where he would actually grow."
Coaching mindset -- leading by asking questions instead of handing over answers
"A coaching mindset asks ‘what would it look like if...?’ before it ever offers an answer."
Boundary setting -- making your limits clear, kindly and firmly, so expectations stay realistic
"Boundary setting is not coldness; it is telling someone honestly what you can and cannot take on."
Career aspirations -- where a person genuinely hopes their career will go over time
"Once she understood his real career aspirations, the work she gave him finally pointed somewhere he wanted to go."
To empower -- to give someone the authority and the confidence to act on their own
"The goal of the 1:1 was not to fix Owen but to empower him to solve the next problem without her."
To check in -- to briefly and deliberately ask how someone is really doing
"She had made a habit of checking in before a deadline, not after, so problems surfaced while they were still small."
To play to someone's strengths -- to give someone work that uses what they are already best at
"Rather than force him into spreadsheets, she chose to play to his strengths and let him run the interviews."
To bite the bullet -- to face a hard, unavoidable task instead of putting it off
"Some conversations you can only postpone for so long; eventually you bite the bullet and name the issue."
To throw in the towel -- to give up and stop trying
"He had been ready to throw in the towel, and one honest hour was the difference between quitting and staying."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Owen, a junior coordinator Danielle mentors, had gone quiet for two weeks — deadlines slipping, replies getting shorter. In a voice note the night before their 1:1, he all but said he was ready to throw in the towel. Danielle knew the easy move was to fix it for him. She chose the harder one: a coaching mindset. She opened the meeting not with a verdict but with a question, and then did the thing that is hardest for a fast, capable leader — active listening, letting the silence sit until he named what was really wrong.

What came out was not laziness. Owen was drowning in tasks that did not play to his strengths, and he had been too proud to check in before things slipped. So she tried reframing: “You are not failing — you are on the steep part of the learning curve.” Then she guided, without commanding. “I would rather we solved the root cause together than patch the same problem next month. And honestly, you had better talk to your manager before the next deadline slips, not after — it is far easier now.” When Owen admitted he would have to redo a whole report anyway, she smiled: “If you are redoing it regardless, you may as well use it to show exactly what you have learned.”

This was the coachable moment, and she did not fill it with orders. She offered to be a sounding board, gave him developmental feedback instead of a scorecard, and named his real growth edge: not effort, but asking for help early. She set a stretch assignment just beyond his comfort — running the next round of interviews — because that is where growth actually happens, and it pointed straight at his career aspirations.

The point of the hour was never to rescue Owen. It was to empower him to solve the next problem without her. She modelled boundary setting too — telling him plainly what she could and could not take off his plate — so the help stayed real. He walked in ready to quit and walked out with one honest task and a reason to stay. Sometimes you have to bite the bullet and have the conversation; more often, you just have to ask the right question and then be quiet enough to hear the answer.

1. “I would rather we solved the root cause than patch it again” — what does would rather express here?
A A clear preference between two options — and with a different subject (we) it takes the past form (solved).
B An obligation, exactly the same in meaning as “must”.
C A question asking Owen for permission to continue.
2. “You had better talk to your manager before the deadline slips” — why had better and not just should?
A Because had better is only about the past — it means “it would have been better”.
B Because had better is stronger than should: it is urgent advice that carries a warning — do it, or something bad follows — and it is followed by the bare verb (talk), no to.
C Because had better can only be used with the word please.
3. “If you are redoing it anyway, you may as well use it to show what you learned.” What is the idea behind may as well?
A That the action is forbidden and should be avoided.
B That since there is no better alternative, you might as well do the thing — it costs nothing extra and there is an upside.
C That the speaker is unsure whether the action is even possible.
4. According to the text, what was the real point of Danielle's 1:1 with Owen?
A To take the difficult tasks off his plate and solve the problem for him.
B To deliver a firm scorecard so he knew exactly where he had fallen short.
C To empower him to solve the next problem himself — by listening, reframing, and naming his growth edge instead of giving orders.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Advanced Modals: would rather / had better / may as well

GRAMMAR

The modals that shift you from “making a suggestion” to “executive guidance” — warm, but directive. This is how a senior leader advises without commanding, and guides without shrinking the other person.

FormUseExample
would rather
+ bare infinitive		A clear preference (same subject). No to.I would rather grow you than replace you. · NOT “would rather to grow”.
would rather + subject
+ past form		A preference about what someone else does — the verb goes to the past.I would rather you didn't promise that date. · I would rather we solved the root cause.
had better
+ bare infinitive		Strong, urgent advice with a warning: do it, or a bad result follows. Negative: had better not.You had better loop in the team first. · You had better not wait until it slips.
may / might as well
+ bare infinitive		No better option exists, so you might as well do it — low cost, clear upside.If you are redoing it anyway, you may as well show what you learned.
Advisory subjunctive
suggest / recommend / insist + that + base verb		Formal directive language: the verb stays in the base form (no -s, be not is).I suggest that each mentee be given a clear growth edge. · I recommend that he take the interviews.
Why it sounds seniorwould rather owns a preference without an order; had better lands a warning without raising your voice; may as well makes a decision feel easy. Together they are the tone of a leader who is empathetic and precise at the same time — guidance, not commands.

Watch out (the number one slip): all three take the bare infinitive — no to. “You had better to talk” → “You had better talk”. “I would rather to reframe” → “I would rather reframe”.

Watch out (the other slip): with a different subject, would rather takes the past form. “I would rather you don't” → “I would rather you didn't”.

The line that wins the room: “I would rather grow you than replace you — but you had better ask for help before the next deadline, and if you are redoing the report anyway, you may as well use it to show me what changed.”

Order

Quiz

A “You are right, it is not for everyone — let us talk about an exit.”
B “Help me understand what got in the way. You are not failing — you are on the steep part of the learning curve, and I would rather grow you than replace you.”

A “You had better to talk to your manager today.”
