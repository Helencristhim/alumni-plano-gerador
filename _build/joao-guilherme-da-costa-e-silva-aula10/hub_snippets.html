The Interview Room -- Performing Under Pressure

The interview in Brussels went badly. This is the reverse-engineering: the STAR method for behavioral questions, and the grammar that narrates your evolution — used to / be used to / get used to. Key words: STAR method, behavioral question, situational question, transferable skills, gap analysis, selling point, culture fit, notice period, relocation, to cut to the chase, to read between the lines, to think on your feet, to come across as, to sell yourself short. Structure: used to + base verb (finished past) vs. be / get used to + -ing (accustomed), plus would rather / had better / may as well for positioning.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Last week you decoded native speech at full speed. Here you walk into the room. These are the words a hiring panel speaks: how they ask, what they judge, and the honest word for what you must never do to yourself. Listen to each term and read the example.

STAR method -- a four-part way to structure an interview answer: Situation, Task, Action, Result
"When they ask for an example, I answer with the STAR method so nothing is missing."
Behavioral question -- a question that asks what you actually did in a real past situation ("Tell me about a time...")
"A behavioral question wants a story, not a philosophy."
Situational question -- a question about a hypothetical case ("What would you do if...")
"A situational question tests judgment; a behavioral one tests track record."
Transferable skills -- abilities you built in one role that carry over to a different job or industry
"They hire for transferable skills, not for one specific platform."
Gap analysis -- comparing where you are now with where the role needs you to be, and naming the difference
"Before the interview I did a gap analysis: what the role wants, and what I still have to prove."
Selling point -- the specific strength that makes you the right person, stated so the panel remembers it
"My selling point is the international interface: I have run it in three languages."
Culture fit -- how well the way you work matches the way the company works
"Between two equal engineers, culture fit decides who gets the offer."
Notice period -- the time you must keep working for your current employer after you resign
"My notice period is thirty days, so I could start early next quarter."
Relocation -- moving to another city or country for a job
"The role is in Zurich, and relocation support is provided for the family."
To cut to the chase -- to skip the preamble and get straight to the point that matters
"Let me cut to the chase: my strongest area is the international interface."
To read between the lines -- to understand the real meaning that is implied but not stated
"Read between the lines: they care more about range than about their exact product."
To think on your feet -- to respond well and quickly to something you did not prepare for
"A panel is designed to make you think on your feet, so a short pause is allowed."
To come across as -- to give a certain impression to the person judging you
"A calm pause makes you come across as senior, not slow."
To sell yourself short -- to describe yourself as less capable than you really are
"Do not sell yourself short: you led the interface, so say you led it."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

An interview in Brussels went badly in March, and João Guilherme has decided to reverse-engineer it. A recruiter, Claire Mercier, has now invited him to a competency-based panel for a Senior Signaling Engineer role in Zurich, and this time he is preparing the version of himself that walks out in control. He starts with a gap analysis: what the role needs, and what he still has to prove — and he reads between the lines of the posting, which values transferable skills and culture fit over any single product.

The grammar of the interview is small, and it decides everything. Watch the word right after used to. "I used to work on electrification" — base verb — is a finished past habit: where he came from. "I'm used to managing suppliers in three time zones" — the -ing — is a state that is already normal for him. And "I got used to reading CENELEC standards in English" is the process in between: strange, then normal, over time. Same three words, three meanings, and a panel hears the difference. Together they narrate an arc — from, through, to — which is exactly what a hiring manager wants.

So he builds his answers. When a behavioral question lands ("Tell me about a time you managed a supplier conflict"), he uses the STAR method: he cuts to the chase, names the story first, then runs Situation, Task, Action, Result — because the Result is the sentence that buys the job. When a situational question lands ("What would you do if..."), he thinks on his feet and takes the short pause that makes him come across as senior. He positions himself without arrogance: "I would rather give you one real result than a list of skills", "I had better be specific here", "we may as well start with the hardest project I have run". And he refuses his oldest habit: he does not sell himself short. He owned the interface, so he says he owned it — and he closes the room on relocation and his notice period without hedging, landing on the one selling point the panel will remember.

1. "I used to work on electrification." What does this tell the panel?
A That he still works on electrification today.
B That it is a FINISHED past habit — used to + base verb means it was true before and is over now. It is where he came FROM.
C That he is currently learning electrification.
2. And "I'm used to managing suppliers in three time zones"? What CHANGES?
A Nothing — it means the same as "I used to manage".
B It is a mistake; after "used to" the verb should be the base form.
C EVERYTHING: be used to + -ING is a STATE that is already normal for him NOW. "I used to manage" = finished; "I'm used to managing" = normal today.
3. When a behavioral question lands, what is the best move?
A Cut to the chase, name the story first, and run STAR: Situation, Task, Action, Result — because the Result is what buys the job.
B Give your general philosophy of conflict, without a specific example.
C Say you cannot remember a specific time and describe your personality instead.
4. Which sentence is correct?
A "I would rather to give you one real result than a list of skills."
B "I would rather give you one real result than a list of skills."
C "I am used to give you one real result than a list of skills."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Used To / Be Used To / Get Used To

GRAMMAR

One little word after "used to" decides the meaning — a base verb for a finished past, an -ing for what is normal now.

StructureMeaningExample
used to + base verbA past habit or state that is now FINISHED — where you came from. Negative/question drop the -d: "I didn't use to...", "Did you use to...?""I used to work on electrification." — I did, and I stopped.
be used to + -ing / nounA STATE: it is already normal for you now. NOT the base verb — the -ing."I'm used to managing suppliers." — normal for me now.
get used to + -ing / nounThe PROCESS of becoming accustomed — from strange to normal, over time."I got used to reading CENELEC in English." — it became normal.
would rather / had better / may as well
+ base verb (no "to")		would rather = preference. had better = strong advice, almost a warning. may as well = no better option, so let's."I would rather give a result." · "I had better be specific." · "We may as well start with the hardest one."
Why it mattersThese structures narrate your evolution in one breath: "I used to work only in Portuguese; I got used to running calls in English; now I'm used to managing the interface across three countries." That arc — from, through, to — is exactly what a panel wants, and the verb form is the only thing that makes it come out right.

Watch out (habits carried over from Portuguese): (1) "I am used to manage" — after be/get used to the verb takes -ing: managing. Your first language uses one form for both, which tempts you into the base verb; English needs the -ing; (2) "I used to working" — a past habit takes the base verb: used to work; (3) "I would rather to give" — would rather / had better / may as well take the base verb with no "to".

Interview collocations: value transferable skills, come across as confident (never "come across confident"), serve / work a notice period, be shortlisted for a role, provide relocation support, cut to the chase, answer a behavioral question, think on your feet.

The question to ask before every answer: "Did I give them the Result?" Most candidates tell the Situation and stop. The STAR answer without the R is a story with no ending — and it is the R that buys the job.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer for every real moment of the panel — the opening, the behavioral question, the platform gap, the unplanned question, and the close on relocation.

The panel asks "Tell me about yourself." The strongest opening is:
A "I only did some international work, nothing major, my English is not so good."
B "I'm a signaling engineer at Motiva. I used to work on electrification, and over three years I got used to owning the interface, so now I'm used to running it in English."
C "I'm experienced, communicative and good with international teams."
A behavioral question lands: "Tell me about a time you managed a supplier conflict." You:
A Give your general opinion on how conflicts should be handled.
B Cut to the chase and run STAR: six units failed the EMC test (S), my task was to keep the design question open (T), I put the shared design on record (A), and all forty were re-verified at their cost (R).
C Say you would rather not talk about conflicts.
You have never worked on their exact platform. The best framing is:
A "So maybe I am not really qualified for this role."
B "You hire for range, not one platform. My transferable skills carry over, and I got used to new standards fast at Motiva."
C "I used to working on other platforms, so it is fine."
They ask something you did not prepare. You want to think on your feet without looking lost. You say:
A "I don't know, sorry."
B "That is a good question — let me think for a second, because I would rather give you a real example than a general answer."
C You stay completely silent and hope they move on.
Claire asks about relocation and your notice period. You answer:
A "I'm open to relocation — I got used to being deployed to site in 2023 — and my notice period is thirty days, so I could start early next quarter."
B "I am not sure, maybe I could move, I would have to think about it."
C "My notice period is a problem, so perhaps this will not work."
