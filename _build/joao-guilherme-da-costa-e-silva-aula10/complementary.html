Lesson 10 -- The Interview Room: Performing Under Pressure

Film scene
The Pursuit of Happyness — the job interview scene (Sony, 4 min)

Chris Gardner walks into the most important interview of his life straight from a night in a police cell, in paint-stained clothes, completely unprepared. Watch what he does with it. He does not apologize his way out of the room and he does not sell himself short: he thinks on his feet, tells the truth in one honest line, and turns a disaster into the answer they remember. It is a masterclass in how you come across as the right person even when nothing is going your way — and it is exactly the composure you rehearsed tonight for your own panel.

Tip: watch once, then pause after his answer about the shirt. Write down, in English, the single sentence he uses to reframe the situation. Then write YOUR version for a real question you fear ("Why did the Brussels interview go badly?") — one honest sentence, no apology, that makes you come across as senior, not defensive.

Podcast
B-Schooled — "The STAR method for essays and interview questions" (Episode 69, ~15 min, free)

Fifteen minutes on the exact tool you built tonight. The hosts break the STAR method down — Situation, Task, Action, Result — and, more usefully, they name the mistake you already know is yours: telling the Situation and stopping, so the Result never lands. Listen for how they answer a behavioral question out loud, and notice how much of the impact lives in the final sentence. This is the audio version of the artifact you filled in with the six-units story.

Tip: listen once. Then take your own supplier-conflict story from the lesson and record a 90-second STAR answer on your phone, out loud, in English. Play it back and check one thing only: is the Result there, with a number? If it is not, you have a story, not a STAR answer — fix it and record again.

YouTube
BBC Learning English — "'used to', 'be used to' and 'get used to'" (English Class, 8 min)

The exact grammar point of tonight, taught by native teachers at your level. They put the three structures side by side and drill the one thing that decides everything: the verb form. used to + base verb for the finished past, be / get used to + -ing for what is already normal — and why "I'm used to manage" is the mistake a hiring panel hears immediately. Watch it AFTER the lesson: it will sound obvious, and that is the point.

Tip: after the video, write six sentences about your own career — two with "used to" (where you came from), two with "be used to" (what is normal now), and two with "got used to" (what you adapted to). Read them aloud. Those six sentences are your "tell me about yourself" answer, already built.
