Lesson 13 -- Pre-class

Asking for Directions -- Finding Your Way Around the City on Foot

From the hotel door to the museum, on foot: how to ask for and understand walking directions in New York. Key words: intersection, crosswalk, landmark, sidewalk, pedestrian, traffic light, around the corner, to head. Grammar: prepositions of place and movement -- place (across from, next to, on the corner of) tells you where a landmark is; movement (along, past, through, toward) tells you how to walk to it. A clear route uses both.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Take your time -- listen twice, then say it out loud. These are the eight words that live on the street, between your hotel door and the museum.

Intersection -- the place where two streets cross each other
"Turn left at the intersection with 53rd Street."
Crosswalk -- the marked white lines where people cross the street on foot
"Wait for the light and cross at the crosswalk."
Landmark -- a well-known building or place you use to find your way
"The tall glass tower is a good landmark to look for."
Sidewalk -- the paved path beside the street where people walk (the L is silent)
"Stay on the sidewalk and watch for cars."
Pedestrian -- a person walking in the street, not in a car
"The drivers here do not always wait for pedestrians."
Traffic light -- the red-yellow-green signal that tells cars when to stop and go
"Cross when the traffic light turns red for the cars."
Around the corner -- very close, just past the corner of the street
"The museum is around the corner, on your right."
To head -- to go or move in a certain direction (head north, head toward the park)
"From the door, head north on Fifth Avenue."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

Lucimara's hotel is on Fifth Avenue, and the Museum of Modern Art is only two blocks away, so she decides to walk. She leaves the hotel and heads north along the avenue, staying on the sidewalk.

At the intersection with 53rd Street, she waits for the traffic light and crosses at the crosswalk with the other pedestrians. The museum is around the corner, on her right, across from a small park.

Notice the little words that do all the work. Some tell you where something is: across from the park, next to the bookstore, on the corner. Others tell you how you move: along the avenue, past the church, toward the river. These are prepositions of place and movement.

A year ago, Lucimara would have shown the address on her phone and hoped. Now she can ask for directions, understand the answer, and even give them back -- because a route is only clear when the little words are right.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Prepositions of Place and Movement

GRAMMAR

A direction is only clear because of small words. Place prepositions tell you where a landmark is; movement prepositions tell you how to walk to it. Use both, and any route becomes easy to follow.

TypeWordsExample
Place -- where it isacross from, next to, opposite, on the corner of, betweenThe museum is across from the park.
Movement -- how you goalong, past, through, toward, up / down, aroundWalk along Fifth, past the church.
The one ideaPlace tells you where a landmark is; movement tells you how to walk to it. A clear direction uses both.

Watch out -- the classic mistakes: it is "on Fifth Avenue", never "in Fifth Avenue"; you walk to the corner, never "walk until the corner"; and you cross at the crosswalk, not "in the crosswalk". And across means over: to say opposite, use across from.

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer -- speak for about 90 seconds, with no script.

Imagine a tourist stops you outside your hotel on Fifth Avenue and asks how to walk to the Museum of Modern Art. Give the whole route out loud: which way to head, where to cross, which street to turn on, the landmark to look for, and how far it is. Use at least four of today's new words and at least three prepositions -- a mix of place (across from, next to, on the corner) and movement (along, past, toward). Ask nothing; this time, you are the one who knows the way. Take your time and do not read from a script.
