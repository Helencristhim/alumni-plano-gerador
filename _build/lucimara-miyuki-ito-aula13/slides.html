Chapter 3: The Code

"Across From, Along, Past"

Prepositions of place and movement — the small words that make a route clear.

Grammar Discovery

Where It Is, and How You Get There

The museum is across from a small park, next to a bookstore.

Walk past the church and turn left.

Go straight along Fifth Avenue toward the park.

The restaurant is around the corner, between a bakery and a flower shop.

Some orange words tell you where a place is. Others tell you how you move. Which is which?

TypeWordsExample
Place — where it isacross from, next to, opposite, on the corner of, betweenThe museum is across from the park.
Movement — how you goalong, past, through, toward, up / down, aroundWalk along Fifth, past the church.
The one ideaPlace prepositions tell you where a landmark is; movement prepositions tell you how to walk to it. A clear direction uses both.

One trap: it is "on Fifth Avenue", never "in Fifth Avenue"; and you walk to the corner, never "walk until the corner".

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"The museum is in Fifth Avenue."
"The museum is on Fifth Avenue."
"Walk until the traffic light and turn left."
"Walk to the traffic light and turn left."

Two small words carry the street: it is on the avenue, not in it; and you walk to the corner, not until it.

Listening Strategy

Catch the Direction

In a direction, three things decide everything: which way, which street, and where it is.

"Turn left at the traffic light, then head north." — left, not right; north, not south
"Cross at 53rd Street and it is on your right." — fifty-THIRD; on your RIGHT
"It is across from the park, next to a bookstore." — across from = opposite; next to = beside
"Walk along Fifth for two blocks, past the church." — along = follow it; past = go by it

Catch the direction, the street number, and the last preposition — and the route stops hiding.

You have got it. If you get lost, just ask again — New Yorkers are friendlier than people think. Enjoy the museum!
