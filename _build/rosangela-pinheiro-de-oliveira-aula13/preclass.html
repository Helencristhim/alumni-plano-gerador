Lesson 13 -- Pre-class

Have You Just Arrived? -- The First Hour in the Hotel

Almost midnight, a long flight behind you, and a receptionist asking what you have already done and what you haven't done yet. Key words: to unpack, to settle in, to drop off, to freshen up, to check out, a wake-up call, a nap, jet-lagged, exhausted, spare. Structure: present perfect for recent events with the three little words -- just and already in the middle ("I have just arrived", "I have already checked in"), and yet at the end, only in questions and negatives ("I haven't had dinner yet", "Have you checked in yet?"). Expression: So far, so good.

Vocabulary

Listen to every word and read the example. These are the ten words of the first hour in a hotel. Watch number three: to drop off is not to take off (Lesson 11) — you drop OFF your bags (you leave them and go), and a plane takes OFF (it goes up). And number five: to check out is the door out at the end of the trip, the opposite of check-in from Lesson 2.

To unpack -- to take your things out of your suitcase
"I have already unpacked, and my clothes are in the wardrobe."
To settle in -- to get comfortable in a new place, little by little
"Take your time to settle in -- you can unpack tomorrow."
To drop off -- to leave your bags somewhere and go without them
"I have just dropped off my bags at the front desk."
To freshen up -- to wash your face and hands quickly and feel clean again
"I want to freshen up before dinner -- two minutes."
To check out -- to leave the hotel and pay at the end of your stay
"We check out on Friday, but we haven't checked out yet."
A wake-up call -- a phone call from reception that wakes you in the morning
"Could I have a wake-up call at seven, please?"
A nap -- a short sleep in the daytime, not at night
"I have just taken a short nap, and now I feel better."
Jet-lagged -- tired and confused because of the time change after a long flight
"I have just arrived from Brazil, and I am so jet-lagged."
Exhausted -- very, very tired, with no energy left
"It was an eleven-hour flight, and I am exhausted."
Spare -- one extra, kept in case you need it (a spare key)
"The hotel has already given us a spare key for my husband."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

It is almost midnight at the Hotel Lisboa, and Rosângela is standing at the front desk. Her husband is asleep in a chair by the window. The flight has just landed, and she is exhausted and jet-lagged. The receptionist looks at her and asks, "Have you just arrived?" And she says, "Yes. I have already checked in online, and I have already dropped off my bags — but I haven't had dinner yet."

"The kitchen has already closed," the receptionist says, "but I can send some soup up to the room." He gives her the key. "Your room is ready. We have already cleaned it, and there is a spare key in the envelope for your husband." Rosângela asks for one more thing: a wake-up call at eight, because she does not trust her phone with the jet lag. Tonight she is not going to unpack. She is going to drop off the bags, freshen up, and sleep. She can settle in tomorrow.

Four years ago, in Paris, her son did all of this for her. He said her name, he asked for the wake-up call, he explained about dinner. Tonight, in Lisbon, at midnight, alone, Rosângela has done the whole thing herself. She hasn't checked out yet — that is on Friday — and the trip has only just begun. But she looks at the key in her hand, and she thinks: so far, so good.

1. "The flight has just landed." Why "has just landed" and not "landed"?
A Because "just" always needs the past simple.
B Because it happened a moment ago and it matters right now — "just" sits in the middle: has + just + landed.
C Because planes always take the present perfect.
2. She says "I have already checked in." What does "already" tell the receptionist?
A That it is done, and done early — good news, so he does not have to do it now.
B That she is going to check in later tonight.
C That she never checks in online.
3. "I haven't had dinner yet." Where does "yet" go, and why is it here?
A In the middle, like "just" and "already".
B At the end — and only because the sentence is a "no". "Yet" is the thing still waiting to happen.
C At the start of the sentence, before "I".
4. The text says her son "did all of this" in Paris, but tonight she "has done" it. Why the two different tenses?
A Paris was a finished time (four years ago) — past simple. Tonight is fresh news, connected to now — present perfect.
B There is no reason — the two tenses are the same.
C Because her son is a man and she is a woman.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Perfect with just / already / yet

GRAMMAR

In Lesson 12 you learned the present perfect for a lifetime: "I have been to Italy." Today it is the same have / has + past participle, but with the clock very close: what happened a moment ago, and what has not happened yet. You only add one small word.

WordWhere & whenExample
justA moment ago. It stands in the middle: have + just + participle.I have just arrived. · The flight has just landed.
alreadyDone, and done early — good news. Also in the middle.I have already checked in. · They have already cleaned the room.
yetStill waiting. It stands at the end, only in the negative and in questions.I haven't had dinner yet. · Have you checked in yet?
Formhave / has + past participle. He / she / it takes has.I have eaten. · She has arrived. (never "has ate")
Negativehave not / haven't + participle, and "yet" at the end.We haven't checked out yet.
Careful: a finished timeWith a closed time (yesterday, in 2019, three days ago), use the past simple, not the present perfect.I arrived in Lisbon three days ago. (never "I have arrived three days ago")

The one rule to remember: just and already are a sandwich — they sit in the middle, between have and the verb. Yet is a door — it sits at the end, and it only opens for a "no" or a question. And after have, always the participle: eaten, not "ate"; arrived, not "arrive".

Stage 3: Pronunciation

Speaking

Listen to the model, then record yourself. Take your time: listen twice before you speak. Two things to watch: in real speech "have" is tiny and weak, and it joins the word before it — "I've just arrived", not "I HAVE just arrived". And "haven't" is one fast word, with the stress on the first part.
