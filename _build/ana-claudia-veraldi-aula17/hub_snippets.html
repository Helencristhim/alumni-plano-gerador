Reported Speech -- Capturing What Was Said in Meetings and Escalations

The morning after a night incident, Ana debriefs her manager on the escalation call: she reports what the client, QA and the on-call engineer said, shifting the tenses back and keeping the exact wording where it matters. Key words: to relay, to convey, to reiterate, to paraphrase, to notify, to acknowledge, recipient, thread, chain of command, discrepancy, wording, verbatim. Structure: reported speech (backshift across tenses).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

To relay -- to pass a message from one person to another
"I will relay the client's decision to the release manager."
To convey -- to communicate an idea or feeling so others clearly understand it
"I could not convey how urgent the outage really was."
To reiterate -- to say something again to make it clear or to stress it
"Let me reiterate the one decision we made on the call."
To paraphrase -- to restate what someone said in your own words
"I will paraphrase the long explanation, but keep the numbers exact."
To notify -- to formally tell someone that something has happened
"We must notify the incident manager before we close the ticket."
To acknowledge -- to confirm that you have received or noticed something
"Please acknowledge the alert so we know you saw it."
Recipient -- the person who receives a message or email
"The recipient of the report is the release manager."
Thread -- a connected series of messages or emails on one topic
"The whole decision is buried in a long email thread."
Chain of command -- the order in which people report to and answer to each other
"This issue has to go up the chain of command."
Discrepancy -- a difference between two accounts or numbers that should match
"There is a discrepancy between what he said and the logs."
Wording -- the specific choice of words used in a message
"The wording of the alert was confusing, so nobody acted."
Verbatim -- using exactly the same words; word for word
"I copied her commitment verbatim into the ticket."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

The morning after the outage, Ana had to relay to her manager what everyone had said on the escalation call. She was careful, because a report is only useful if it is faithful. On the call, Priya had said, "the fix is live", so Ana reported that Priya said the fix was live. David had said, "we deployed a bad config last night", so Ana reported that David said they had deployed a bad config the night before. Notice the pattern: when you report speech, the tense usually shifts one step back, and time words move too: today becomes that day, yesterday becomes the day before, will becomes would, can becomes could. The client had asked, "can you send a summary today?", so Ana reported that the client asked whether we could send a summary that day, with no question mark and normal word order. She used a clean paraphrase for the long parts, but she kept the client's commitment verbatim, because the recipient on the incident thread would act on it. When she spotted a discrepancy between what someone said and the logs, she flagged it instead of hiding it. That is what a good report does: it shifts the tense, keeps the wording honest, and sends the key facts up the chain.

1. Priya said, "the fix is live." How does Ana report it?
A Priya said the fix is live.
B Priya said the fix was live.
C Priya says the fix will be live.
2. The client asked, "can you send a summary today?" How does Ana report it?
A The client asked whether we could send a summary that day.
B The client asked can we send a summary today?
C The client asked if we can send a summary today.
3. Why does Ana keep the client's commitment verbatim?
A Because the recipient will act on the exact words.
B Because paraphrasing is always wrong.
C Because verbatim reports do not need a backshift.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Backshift Rule

GRAMMAR

When you report what someone said, the tense usually moves one step back.

Direct speechReported speechShift
"It is down."She said it was down.present → past
"We deployed it."He said they had deployed it.past → past perfect
"I will fix it."She said she would fix it.will → would
"Can you help?"He asked if I could help.can → could
Key ruleStatements: said (that). Questions: asked if / whether + normal word order, no question mark. Use tell with a person (told me), say without one. Time words shift too: today → that day, yesterday → the day before, tomorrow → the next day.
