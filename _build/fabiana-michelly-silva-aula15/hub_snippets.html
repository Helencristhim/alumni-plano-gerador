Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Friction -- ongoing tension or low-level conflict between people
"There is clearly some friction between the two teams." (Atrito)
Grievance -- a formal complaint about unfair treatment
"A couple of people raised a grievance about the delay." (Queixa formal)
Defensive -- reacting to criticism by protecting yourself instead of listening
"He got defensive the moment I raised the deadline." (Na defensiva)
De-escalate -- to reduce the intensity of a conflict or tense situation
"Our job today is to de-escalate, not to win." (Reduzir a tensão)
Accountability -- owning the result of your actions and decisions
"We want accountability for the process, not blame." (Responsabilização)
Candid -- honest and direct, even about hard things, but still kind
"I had to be candid about the impact of the slip." (Franco / sincero)
Empathy -- the ability to understand and share how another person feels
"Lead with empathy before you make your point." (Empatia)
Blame -- saying that someone is responsible for something bad
"Focus on the process, not on blame for the people." (Culpa)
Pushback -- firm resistance or opposition to an idea or decision
"I expected pushback on the new tracking standard." (Resistência)
Overstep -- to go beyond the limits of your role or authority
"I did not want to overstep, so I asked first." (Extrapolar / passar dos limites)
Assertive -- speaking up confidently and firmly, without being aggressive
"Be assertive about the standard, but never aggressive." (Assertivo)
Standoff -- a deadlock where neither side will give ground
"Last time the conversation ended in a standoff." (Impasse)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Giving difficult feedback is really about acknowledging one thing and countering another in the same breath. A skilled manager never just blames. She says, "Although the deadline slipped, I know you were working with a broken forecast." She concedes the pressure -- "While I understand the workload, the slip still hurt the client" -- and then contrasts two facts: "The cost stayed flat, whereas the lead time doubled." When she wants to keep the goodwill, she leads with the positive: "Despite the friction, your team responded well." Notice that although, even though, while and whereas are followed by a full clause, but despite and in spite of are followed by a noun. Used well, these linkers de-escalate the conflict and turn blame into shared accountability.

1. Why does the manager say "Although the deadline slipped, I know you had a broken forecast"?
A She acknowledges the problem first, then concedes a reason -- it softens the feedback.
B "Although" means she is not sure the deadline really slipped.
C The two clauses contradict each other and cancel out.
2. What is the difference between "despite" and "although" in the text?
A They are interchangeable in every position.
B "Despite" is followed by a noun, while "although" is followed by a full clause.
C "Despite" can only be used at the end of a sentence.
3. What does "whereas" do in "The cost stayed flat, whereas the lead time doubled"?
A It directly contrasts two facts inside one sentence.
B It shows that the two facts caused each other.
C It means "because" -- it gives the reason.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Concession & Contrast

GRAMMAR

Como reconhecer um ponto e contra-argumentar na mesma frase para suavizar uma crítica. / How to acknowledge a point and counter it in the same sentence to soften feedback.

Conector / LinkerO que vem depois / What followsExample / Exemplo
Although / Even thoughuma frase completa (sujeito + verbo) / a full clauseAlthough the team missed the deadline...
Whileuma frase; concede um ponto / a clause, concedes a pointWhile I understand the pressure...
Whereasuma frase; contraste direto de dois fatos / a clause, direct contrastCost stayed flat, whereas lead time doubled.
Despite / In spite ofum SUBSTANTIVO ou -ing (nunca uma frase) / a NOUN or -ingDespite the friction, we agreed.
Ponto-chaveCuidado: Despite the delay (substantivo) e correto, mas Despite the team was late é ERRADO -- use Although the team was late. / Despite + noun, not a clause.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best response for each professional situation.

A teammate missed a deadline, but the forecast was broken. You want to give feedback without blame. You say:
A "Although the deadline slipped, I know you were working with a broken forecast." (acknowledge + counter)
B "You missed the deadline. This is on you." (pure blame -- triggers defensiveness)
C "It does not matter, forget it." (avoids the feedback entirely)
There was tension, but the team still agreed. You want to use "despite" correctly. The best line is:
A "Despite there was friction, we agreed." (despite cannot take a clause)
B "Despite the friction, we agreed." (despite + noun)
C "Despite of the friction, we agreed." ("despite of" is not correct English)
The colleague gets defensive and feels blamed. You want to de-escalate. You say:
A "I am not here to blame anyone -- the accountability sits with the process." (redirects from people to process)
B "You are being too defensive about this." (escalates the conflict)
C "Fine, it is all your fault then." (sarcasm, pure blame)
