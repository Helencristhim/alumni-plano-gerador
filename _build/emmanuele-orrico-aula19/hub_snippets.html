How Much, How Many -- Talking About Quantities and Data with Global

Describe the Brazil numbers out loud for David and the Global team: how many patients, how much access, how many hospitals. Key words: quantity, average, portion, majority, minority, ratio, approximately, to measure. The grammar of the lesson is quantifiers. Use many and a few with things you can count ("many patients", "a few hospitals"). Use much and a little with things you cannot count ("not much time", "a little access"). Use a lot of with both ("a lot of demand"). Ask How many? for countable and How much? for uncountable.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. These are the eight words you use to talk about quantities and data.

Quantity -- how much of something there is
"We track the quantity of product in each region."
Average -- the normal or middle number
"The average is twelve field visits per week."
Portion -- one part of a larger amount
"A large portion of the private market has no coverage."
Majority -- more than half of a group
"The majority of our patients are in the public system."
Minority -- less than half of a group
"Only a minority of hospitals are ready for the launch."
Ratio -- how one number compares to another
"The ratio of public to private patients is three to one."
Approximately -- almost, but not exactly
"We have approximately two hundred patients on the program."
To measure -- to find the size or amount of something
"We measure our results every quarter."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

David, from the Global team, calls Emmanuele for the numbers. "How many patients are on the program?" he asks. "We have many patients — approximately two hundred, on average," she says. "And how much access do we have?" "We have a lot of demand, but only a little access in the private market." David asks about hospitals. "A few hospitals are ready for the launch, but the majority are not ready yet." Then he asks about time. "We have a little time before the deadline — a few weeks." David is happy: Emmanuele knows how much and how many, and she says it clearly.

1. "We have many patients." Why "many" and not "much"?
A Because "patients" is countable — we can count them.
B Because "many" is always more polite than "much".
C Because "patients" is uncountable.
2. "We have a lot of demand." Is "demand" countable or uncountable here?
A Countable — so we could say "many demand".
B Uncountable — that is why we use "a lot of" and "a little", not "many".
C It is a number.
3. "A few hospitals are ready." What does "a few" mean?
A Zero hospitals.
B A small number of countable things — but some, not none.
C All the hospitals.
4. "We have a little time." What does "a little" mean?
A A small number of things you can count.
B A small amount of something uncountable, like time.
C No time at all.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Quantifiers: much / many / a lot of / a few / a little

GRAMMAR

To say how much or how many, the word you choose depends on the noun: can you count it, or not?

QuantifierUse withExample
many / a fewcountable nouns (patients, hospitals)We have many patients.
Only a few hospitals are ready.
much / a littleuncountable nouns (access, time, demand)We don't have much time.
We have a little access.
a lot ofboth countable and uncountablea lot of patients · a lot of demand

Careful: ask How many? for countable ("How many patients?") and How much? for uncountable ("How much access?"). We usually use much in questions and negatives ("not much time"); in positive sentences, use a lot of.
