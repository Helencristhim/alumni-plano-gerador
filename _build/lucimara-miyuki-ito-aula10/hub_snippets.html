Talking About Your Work -- Explaining Your Role and Industry with Confidence

In Lesson 9 you learned to be direct and polite in a store. Now the subject is YOU — your company, your industry, your work. This is the lesson for the Lyon fair in November and the New York dinner in September, when someone asks, "So, what does your company do?" Key words: regulatory compliance, supply chain, raw material, manufacturing process, quality control, distributor, vendor, batch, certification, shipment. Structure: the passive voice — the grammar business runs on ("Our products are tested before distribution." / "Regulations are set by the health authority." / "The formula was approved last year."). The rule that catches everybody: after be, always the past participle ("are tested", never "are test"), and passive always needs the be verb.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Take your time — listen twice, and say it out loud. These are the ten words that describe your own industry, from the raw material to the final shipment. You already know them in Portuguese; today they get their English version.

Regulatory compliance -- following the rules and laws set by an authority
"In our market, no product is sold without full regulatory compliance."
Supply chain -- the whole path a product takes, from raw material to the customer
"Our supply chain reaches Europe, so we can deliver to Lyon."
Raw material -- the basic material a product is made from, before it is processed
"Most of our raw materials are sourced from vendors in Asia."
Manufacturing process -- the series of steps used to make a product in a factory
"The whole manufacturing process is monitored by our team."
Quality control -- the checks that make sure a product is good and safe before it is sold
"Every batch passes quality control before it leaves the plant."
Distributor -- a company that buys products and sells them on to stores or clients
"Daniel's company could become our distributor in France."
Vendor -- a company or person that sells goods or services to another company
"We source our raw materials from three trusted vendors."
Batch -- a quantity of a product made together at one time, as one group
"Every batch is tested before it is released to the market."
Certification -- an official document that proves a product or plant meets a standard
"The plant holds a certification to international standards."
Shipment -- a load of goods sent from one place to another
"The first shipment could be arranged for early next year."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions. Notice how many sentences use the passive voice.

At the Lyon fair, a man stops at Lucimara's booth and asks what her company does. This time she does not point at the brochure. "We specialize in specialty chemicals," she says. "Our products are manufactured in Guarulhos, and every batch is tested before it is released."

The man is interested. "And your supply chain? Where are the raw materials sourced?" Lucimara answers calmly, because she knows the words. "Most of them are sourced from vendors in Asia and Europe, and each shipment is checked when it arrives."

In his market, he explains, a product cannot be sold without proper certification and full regulatory compliance. Lucimara nods. "Every regulation in our sector is set by the health authority, and each one is followed. Last year the formula was approved, and our certification was renewed."

The man hands her his card. He is a distributor across France and Belgium, and he would like to talk. Two minutes, one language, and a booth she used to dread — and the whole manufacturing process of her company, explained in English.

1. "Our products are manufactured in Guarulhos." How is the passive voice built here?
A manufacture + -ing (the continuous form)
B a form of be (are) + the past participle (manufactured) — the product is the star, not the person.
C just the base verb: "Our products manufacture in Guarulhos."
2. "The formula was approved last year." Why was and not is?
A Because the passive voice always uses "was".
B Because it is a finished action in the PAST — past passive is was/were + past participle.
C Because "formula" is a plural word.
3. "Every regulation is set by the health authority." Why is "by" used here?
A Because WHO sets the rules matters — you add "by + the doer" only when the doer is important.
B Because every passive sentence must end with "by".
C Because "by" means the same as "from" for a place.
4. A vendor and a distributor — what is the difference in the text?
A They are two words for the same company.
B A vendor SELLS the raw materials to Lucimara; a distributor would BUY her products and sell them on across France and Belgium.
C A vendor is a customer, and a distributor is a factory.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Passive Voice (the grammar business runs on)

GRAMMAR

In business, we care about the process, not the person. The passive voice lets you talk about what is done, without naming who does it.

StructureFormExample
Present passiveam / is / are + past participle"Our products are tested before distribution."
Past passivewas / were + past participle"The formula was approved last year."
The "by"… by + the doer — only when who does it matters"Regulations are set by the health authority."
The place… from / in + a place (not "by")"The materials are sourced from vendors in Asia."
The one rulePassive = a form of be (am/is/are/was/were) + the past participle (test → tested, set → set, approve → approved). Choose the be for the time (present or past), and never drop the -ed.

Watch out -- the classic mistakes: (1) dropping the participle — "Our products are test" should be "are tested". (2) dropping the be verb — "The formula approved last year" should be "was approved". Without be, there is no passive.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer. Question 1 is the trap: after "be", the verb is never left bare.

A distributor asks how you make sure your products are safe. You want to sound professional. You say:
A "Our products are test before they are ship."
B "Every batch is tested before it is released, and the whole process is monitored."
C "We are testing our products, someone tests, it is tested by us always."
Someone at a New York dinner asks, "So, what do you do?" The best first answer is:
A "I'm a director at a chemical company — we specialize in specialty chemicals." (one clean field sentence)
B A ten-minute description of every step of the manufacturing process.
C "I work in chemicals." (and then nothing else)
You want to say the health authority makes the rules. In the passive, that is:
A "The regulations are set from the health authority."
B "The regulations are set by the health authority." (the doer matters, so we use "by")
C "The regulations set the health authority."
You want to say your materials come from Asia. The natural passive is:
A "The raw materials are sourced by Asia."
B "The raw materials are sourced from vendors in Asia." (from/in for a place)
C "The raw materials source in Asia."
The technical detail is getting heavy and your guest looks lost. The best rescue line is:
A Keep going with even more detail until they understand.
B "It's a fairly complex process, but basically we make the raw materials others can't make safely."
C Stop talking and hand them a brochure.

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer — speak for 90 seconds, with no script.

You are at the Lyon fair, and a potential European distributor stops at your booth and asks what your company does. Give a 90-second introduction: your field (one clean sentence), how your products are made and tested (use the passive voice — "are tested", "are sourced", "are set by"), your supply chain and certification, and one human sentence to make it simple. Close by offering to send your company profile. This is the exact moment you will live in November — so take your time, and do not read from a script.
