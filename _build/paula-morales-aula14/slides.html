Chapter 3: The Code

Two Kinds of Past

Uns só ganham -ed. Os outros mudam de forma — e você já sabe os seis.

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

As palavras verdes falam de agora ou de antes?

I worked at a small office.

I got a job at Gallagher.

I became a specialist.

Last year, I made the move.

Todas falam do passado — de algo que já terminou. Uma termina em -ed (worked). As outras três mudam de forma.

TipoPresente → PassadoRegra
Regularwork → worked · start → started · join → joinedSó ganha -ed no fim
Irregularget → got · become → became · go → wentMuda de forma — aprenda um por um
Irregularmake → made · leave → left · begin → beganMuda de forma — são os seis de hoje
O passado afirmativo é a MESMA forma para eu, você, ela, nós — I worked, she worked, we worked. Não muda com a pessoa. A única coisa a decidir é: o verbo é regular (ganha -ed) ou irregular (muda de forma)? Os seis de hoje são todos irregulares — e são os que você mais vai usar pra contar a sua história.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I go to Gallagher in 2015."
"I went to Gallagher in 2015."
"I becomed a specialist."
"I became a specialist."
"Last year I make the move."
"Last year I made the move."

Os três são o mesmo erro por dois caminhos. O primeiro e o terceiro usam o verbo pelado (go, make) quando a frase fala de algo terminado — aí o verbo precisa do passado: went, made. O do meio põe -ed num irregular (becomed) — e become não ganha -ed, ele muda de forma: became. O -ed é só pros regulares. Ninguém nasce sabendo qual verbo é qual — você aprende os seis de hoje, e o resto vem com o tempo.

Chapter 5: Their Voices

“When I Started, I Was Terrible”

Dialogue

The Call Before the Interview

G
Paula, hi, it's Greg. You have the big interview next Monday, right? I called to tell you one thing. When I started in this business, I was terrible.
P
You? Really? What happened?
G
My first month, a client asked me a simple question, and I froze. I said nothing for ten seconds. It felt like an hour.
P
So what did you do?
G
I took a breath, and I told him the truth. I said, “I'm new, but I will find the answer today.” And I did. I called three people, I found the number, and I sent it that afternoon.
P
And the client?
G
He stayed with me for ten years. He told me later he liked one thing: I did not guess. So here is my point, Paula.
P
Tell me.
G
Monday they will ask, “What did you do?” Do not give them a perfect person. Give them the real story: you began small, you worked, you became good. That is what they want.
P
Okay. I began small... I worked... I became good. I can say that.
G
You just said it. See you after the interview, Paula. You've got this.
Comprehension

About Greg

1. What happened to Greg in his first month?
A client asked him a simple question and he froze. He said nothing for ten seconds — it felt like an hour.
2. When Greg did not know the answer, did he guess? What did he do?
He did not guess. He took a breath, told the client the truth ("I'm new, but I will find the answer today"), then called three people, found the number, and sent it that afternoon.
3. How long did the client stay with Greg, and why?
Ten years. The client liked one thing: Greg did not guess — he told the truth and found the answer.
4. What is Greg's advice for Paula's interview?
Don't give them a perfect person — give them the real story: "you began small, you worked, you became good." That is what they want.

Listening

David: How It Really Happened

Sound first — no text. Leia as perguntas antes de ouvir.

0:000:00
1. Where did David begin, and what was his first job?
He began twenty years ago in a tiny office in Ohio. He got a job as a junior clerk and made photocopies all day. He was too afraid to speak in meetings.
2. What changed for David one day?
A manager asked him a question in front of everyone, and he answered. It was small, but something changed. After that, he worked hard, learned the business, became a broker, went to Miami, and became a director.
3. For a long time, what did David think about his career? What did he realize?
He thought it "just happened" to him — good luck, good timing, good people. Then he realized it did not just happen: he showed up, he answered the question, he made the moves. And now it is Paula's turn to tell her story.
Listening 2

Sofia: You Are the Subject

Sound first — no text. Leia as perguntas antes de ouvir.

0:000:00
1. For a year, how did Paula always tell her story?
The same way, as if everything happened to her: "They took me to Marsh. The clause closed the door. Zurich called me." Someone else always decided.
2. What does Sofia notice about the sentences Paula made in this lesson?
In every one — "I began, I got, I became, I left, I went, I made" — Paula is the subject, the one who acts. Sofia says that is not only grammar; it is the truth. Other people opened doors, but Paula walked through them and did the work.
3. What is Sofia's advice for the interview on Monday?
They will ask, "What did you do?" Don't give a list of things that happened to you — give your story, in your own voice, with you at the start of every sentence, because that is where you belong.

Free Practice

Your Whole Story — No Screen, No Notes

Scenario: The Zurich interview panel asks one question: "Tell us about your career. What did you do?" No screen, no notes. Tell your whole story in the past — from your first small office to Zurich — in sixty to ninety seconds.

One rule, and it is the whole lesson: start every sentence with you. I began. I got. I became. I left. I went. I made. Beginning, middle, and end — and the Zurich move was your own decision.
