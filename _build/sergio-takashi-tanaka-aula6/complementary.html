"How whistle-blowers shape history" -- Kelly Richmond Pope (TED)

A forensic accounting professor explains how fraud is found, disclosed, and reported inside real companies. Connection to Lesson 6: it is the human story behind every audit report -- what was hidden, what was disclosed, and what was required afterwards -- and it is full of the passive voice.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. After each case, say one passive sentence: "The fraud was found by...", "The report was submitted to...". Note three verbs used in the passive.

Podcast
"Planet Money" -- NPR

Short, well-produced episodes that take one company, one number, or one risk and explain what happened. Connection to Lesson 6: many episodes are small investigations -- something was found, something was disclosed, something was required -- so they are perfect practice for the passive voice of a report.

Tip: pick one recent episode, listen once for the main idea, then retell it in three passive sentences: "The problem was found...", "The money was moved...", "A fine was required..."

Read
Federal Reserve -- Press Releases

Free, official statements from the US central bank, written in exactly the formal, impersonal style you read on the risk desk. Connection to Lesson 6: reading is your strength, and these releases are a live source of the passive voice -- "a rule was adopted", "the bank is required to", "actions were taken".

Tip: read one release, underline every passive verb (was/were/is/are + past participle), then close it and summarize the decision out loud in three passive sentences.
