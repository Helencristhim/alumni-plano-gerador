Lesson 15 -- Pre-class

On Her Own -- The Whole Journey, Gate to Door

One travel day, from the plane to the hotel door, alone, with every tense of the program working together. Key words: on my own, to handle, to sort out, to go through, to keep track of, to double-check, right away, smooth. No new grammar -- today you choose the right tense for each moment: present perfect for what has just happened ("I have just landed"), past simple for a finished time ("I checked in this morning"), present continuous for right now ("I am looking for the train"), and going to for the next step ("I am going to take the train to the hotel"). Expression: One step at a time.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to every word and read the example. These are the eight words for doing a trip yourself, when nobody is helping. Watch number four: to go through means to pass through a checkpoint (passport control, security) — it is not to go off (Lesson 11, when an alarm rings). And number six: right away means immediately — it is about time, while right now is the present moment.

On my own -- alone, with nobody to help or translate
"My husband is not here — I am doing this trip on my own."
To handle -- to deal with a situation calmly and manage it yourself
"It was a big airport, but I could handle it."
To sort out -- to solve a small problem
"My phone had no signal, so I sorted out a SIM card at the airport."
To go through -- to pass through a checkpoint like passport control
"I have already gone through passport control — my passport is stamped."
To keep track of -- to watch and remember where your things and time are
"I keep track of my passport, my bag and the time."
To double-check -- to check something a second time, to be sure
"I always double-check the gate number before I sit down."
Right away -- immediately, at once, with no waiting
"The train was leaving, so I got on right away."
Smooth -- easy and calm, with no problems
"The whole trip was smooth — nothing went wrong."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

It is one o'clock in the afternoon in the Lisbon arrivals hall, and Rosângela is doing something she has never done before: a whole trip on her own. Her husband is at home; he does not speak English, and this time he could not come. The plane has just landed. She checked in a bag in São Paulo this morning, and she has already gone through passport control — her passport is stamped. But her suitcase hasn't come out yet, so right now she is standing at belt four, watching the bags go round.

She is calm, because she has a little card with the steps in order, and she is doing them one at a time. "First the bag," she thinks, "and then I am going to sort out a SIM card, because my phone has no signal, and I need the maps right away. Then I am going to take the train to the center, and I am going to walk to the hotel." She keeps track of her passport and her ticket in her hand, and she double-checks the platform number on the screen, because a young man at the information desk told her it can change.

Fifteen lessons ago, at a gate just like this one, her son said her name for her and did all the talking. Today nobody does it for her. She is handling a whole international airport, alone, in English — the plane, the passport, the bag, the phone, the train, the door. And when she finally reaches the hotel and closes the door of her room, she is going to think the same thing Inês thought: the trip was completely smooth, and the only hard part was ever believing she could.

1. "The plane has just landed," but "she checked in this morning." Why two different tenses?
A There is no reason — both are the same tense.
B "Just landed" is fresh news right now (present perfect); "this morning" is a finished time, so it takes the past simple.
C Because planes take the present perfect and people take the past simple.
2. "Right now she is standing at belt four." Why the -ing form here?
A Because it is happening at this exact moment — right now takes the present continuous (am/is/are + -ing).
B Because it is a plan for later.
C Because it is a finished action from this morning.
3. "I am going to take the train." What does "going to" tell us about this action?
A It is happening at this second.
B It is the next step, the plan — something she has decided to do, but has not done yet.
C It finished this morning.
4. "She has already gone through passport control." Why "gone" and not "went"?
A After "have / has" comes the past participle — "gone", never "went".
B Because "already" always needs "went".
C There is no difference between "gone" and "went".

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- One Journey, Four Tenses

GRAMMAR

There is no new grammar today. A travel day is a line of moments, and each moment has its own tense — and you have already learned them all. The only question is when: answer when, and the tense chooses itself.

WhenTenseExample
Just now, still freshPresent perfect: have / has + just / already / yet + participleThe plane has just landed. · I have already gone through.
A finished time (you can say when)Past simpleI checked in this morning. · The flight took off at ten.
Right now, as you speakPresent continuous: am / is / are + verb-ingRight now I am looking for the train.
The next step, the planGoing to + verbThen I am going to take the train.
Careful: participle after havehave / has + past participle, never the past simpleI have gone through (not "went"); I have done it (not "did").

The one rule to remember: do not ask "which grammar?" — ask "when?" Just now → present perfect. A finished time → past simple. Right now → am/is/are + -ing. The next step → going to. Answer when, and the tense is already chosen for you.

Stage 3: Pronunciation

Speaking

Listen to the model, then record yourself. Take your time: listen twice before you speak. Two things to watch: "going to" in fast speech sounds like "gonna" — "I'm gonna take the train"; and in "have gone" the "have" is small and weak — "I've already gone through".

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt. There is no right or wrong version. Speak for 60 seconds, with no script.

Imagine you have just landed in Europe, on your own, and you are crossing the airport to your hotel. Tell the whole travel day, out loud, in order: what you did this morning (past simple), what has just happened (present perfect), what you are doing right now (present continuous), and what you are going to do next (going to). Use three of today's words — on my own, handle, sort out, go through, keep track of, double-check, right away, smooth — and finish with "one step at a time". For forty years somebody did all of this for you. This time, do it yourself. Take your time and do not read from a script.
