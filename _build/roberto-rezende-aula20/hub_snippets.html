Advanced Negotiation: Pushing for a Better Deal

Block four begins. Push for a better deal without breaking the relationship: use your leverage, trade a concession for a concession, and protect your bottom line. Talk about what could have happened differently with the third conditional (If you had ordered earlier, we would have offered a bigger discount). New vocabulary: leverage, concession, trade-off, bottom line, deadlock, walk away, non-negotiable, sweeten the deal.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Leverage -- an advantage that gives you power in a negotiation
"On-time delivery is our leverage in this deal." (Vantagem / poder de barganha)
Concession -- something you give up to reach an agreement
"I will make a concession on price if you sign longer." (Concessão)
Trade-off -- a balance where you give one thing to gain another
"A lower price for a bigger volume is a fair trade-off." (Troca / contrapartida)
Bottom line -- the lowest result you can accept in a deal
"My bottom line is a five percent discount, no more." (Limite mínimo)
Deadlock -- a situation where neither side will move and talks stop
"We reached a deadlock on the final number." (Impasse)
Walk away -- to leave a negotiation without an agreement
"If they go below my limit, I am ready to walk away." (Desistir / sair da mesa)
Non-negotiable -- a point that is fixed and cannot be changed
"The delivery date is non-negotiable." (Inegociável)
Sweeten the deal -- to add something to make an offer more attractive
"I will sweeten the deal with free training." (Tornar a oferta mais atraente)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

The Vinex client pushed hard on price, so Roberto started from his leverage: on-time delivery and high quality for three years. He never gives a concession for nothing, so he offered a trade-off: a lower price for a longer contract. But five percent was his bottom line, and below that was non-negotiable. When they reached a deadlock on the final number, he stayed calm and was ready to walk away. Instead, he chose to sweeten the deal with free training if they signed that month. Later he thought: "If I had prepared the volume numbers earlier, I would have closed the deal even faster." Notice: to talk about a different past and its result, English uses the third conditional -- if + past perfect, would have + past participle.

1. What was Roberto's leverage?
A A very low price.
B On-time delivery and high quality for three years.
C He had no leverage.
2. Which sentence is a correct third conditional?
A "If I would have prepared, we would closed it."
B "If I had prepared, we would have closed it."
C "If I prepared, we will close it."
3. How did Roberto break the deadlock?
A He gave the price away for nothing.
B He sweetened the deal with free training if they signed that month.
C He walked away from the deal.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Third Conditional

GRAMMAR

Use o third conditional para falar de uma condição do passado que NÃO aconteceu e do seu resultado imaginado. / Use the third conditional for a past condition that did not happen and its imagined result.

Forma / FormIf + past perfect, would have + past participle
CondiçãoIf you had ordered earlier,
Resultadowe would have offered a bigger discount.
NegativaIf we had not waited, we would not have lost the deal.
PerguntaWould you have signed if we had moved faster?

Key point / Ponto-chave: na condição use had + past participle (NUNCA "would have known"). No resultado use would have + past participle (offered, closed) -- NUNCA o verbo na base sozinho. As duas partes falam do passado. Formas curtas: I'd ordered = I had ordered; we'd have offered = we would have offered.
