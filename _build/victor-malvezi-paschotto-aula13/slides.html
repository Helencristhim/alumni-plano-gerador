Chapter 3

The Grammar

Indirect questions, hedging, buying time

Grammar Discovery

Spot the Pattern

Three lines, three tools — what is different about each?

"Could you tell me what the cost is?" (indirect — polite, no inversion)

"As far as I can tell, the risk is contained." (hedge — not fully certain)

"That's a fair question. Let me think for a second." (buy time — before the answer)

The right tool buys you calm and credibility.

The Rule

The Q&A Toolkit

ToolStructureExample
Indirect questionCould you tell me / I was wondering + normal word order (no inversion, no do/does)"Could you tell me what the cost is?"
Clarify the questionCould you clarify whether… / what you mean by…"Could you clarify whether you mean cost or timing?"
Hedge (qualifier)As far as I can tell / To the best of my knowledge / It is likely that"To the best of my knowledge, the figure holds."
Buy timeThat is a fair question + Let me think / Let me make sure I understand"That is a fair question. Let me think for a second."
Defer cleanlyI don't have X to hand, but I'll circle back / get you the figure"I'll get you the precise number after the session."

The key thing to keep clean:

In an indirect question, the verb does not invert and you drop do/does: "Could you tell me what the cost is" (not "what is the cost"). A hedge is honest, not weak — a qualifier shows you know the limit of your certainty. And buying time is not stalling: a short bridge earns you a calm, accurate answer instead of an off-the-cuff guess.

Common Mistake

Blunt vs Smooth

✗ INVERTED

"Could you tell me what is the cost?"

✓ NORMAL ORDER

"Could you tell me what the cost is?"

✗ OVERCOMMITTED

"The risk is definitely zero, no question."

✓ HEDGED

"As far as I can tell, the risk is low."

Indirect question = normal word order, no do/does. Unsure = add a qualifier, never overcommit.

Chapter 5

Sharpen the Skills

All four reflexes, at speed

Quick Fire

Field the Question

1 / 5

1. A hard question lands. Buy yourself a moment.

"That's a fair question. Let me make sure I understand it first."

2. The question is vague. Clarify it with an indirect question.

"Could you clarify whether you mean this quarter or the year?"

3. You are not fully sure. Hedge with a qualifier.

"To the best of my knowledge, the supplier is still on schedule."

4. They try to pin you to an exact number you don't have. Defer cleanly.

"I don't have it to hand, but I'll get you the precise figure today."

5. The question assumes something false. Correct the premise, then answer.

"With one caveat: that assumes the pilot has failed, and it hasn't."
