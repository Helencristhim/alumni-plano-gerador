Aula 13 -- Pre-class

Handling Q&A -- Tough Questions Under Pressure

Field tough questions under pressure without losing your footing. Buy a moment instead of answering off the cuff, clarify a vague or loaded question with an indirect question, hedge anything you are not certain about with a qualifier, and defer cleanly when you cannot commit. New words: loaded question, probe, hedge, ballpark, defer, circle back, fielding, pin down, off the cuff, qualifier, presumptuous, caveat. Grammar focus: indirect questions (normal word order, no do/does), hedging language, and time-buying phrases.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

The language that keeps a Q&A calm and credible. Tap Listen to hear each word.

Loaded question -- a question built on a hidden assumption or trap
"Why is the rollout already behind? was a loaded question."
Probe -- to ask follow-up questions that dig deeper into a vague or loaded point
"I probed the question to find out what she was really asking."
Hedge -- to answer carefully, without committing to what you are unsure of
"I hedged the number: as far as I can tell, it's around fifteen percent."
Ballpark -- a rough, approximate figure rather than an exact one
"I gave a ballpark and promised the precise number later."
Defer -- to put off an answer until later, openly and on purpose
"I deferred the exact figure to after the session."
Circle back -- to return to a point or question later, once you have the answer
"Let me check and circle back to you after lunch."
Fielding -- taking questions as they come and answering on the spot
"Fielding questions live is harder than the talk itself."
Pin down -- to press someone for an exact, committed answer
"He tried to pin me down on a date before I had the data."
Off the cuff -- spoken without preparation, on the spur of the moment
"I answered off the cuff and overcommitted — never again."
Qualifier -- a word or phrase that limits how certain a statement is
"As far as I can tell is a qualifier that signals honest doubt."
Presumptuous -- assuming something is true when it is not, taking too much for granted
"The question was presumptuous, so I corrected the premise first."
Caveat -- a warning or condition you attach to limit a statement
"With one caveat: that figure assumes the pilot holds."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the Q&A debrief and answer the questions.

The Growth Manager finished his slides, but the real test was fielding the questions. A board member opened with a loaded question about risk, so instead of answering off the cuff, he bought a second: "That's a fair question. Let me think." Then he used an indirect question to clarify: "Could you tell me what you mean by exposure?" Once he understood it, he hedged the part he was unsure of with a qualifier: "As far as I can tell, the risk is contained." When she tried to pin him down on an exact figure, he gave a ballpark and deferred the precise number: "I'll confirm it and circle back." Finally, when a follow-up was presumptuous — it assumed the pilot had already failed — he added a caveat and corrected the premise before answering.

1. Why does he say "Could you tell me what you mean by exposure?" instead of "What do you mean?"
A The indirect question is more polite and gives him room to clarify a loaded question.
B The two are identical; he just prefers longer sentences.
C "What do you mean?" is grammatically wrong.
2. Why does he add a qualifier ("as far as I can tell") to his answer about the risk?
A To sound less confident on purpose, even when he is sure.
B To hedge honestly — signalling he is fairly sure but has not fully confirmed it.
C Because qualifiers are required in every business sentence.
3. What does he do when she tries to pin him down on an exact figure?
A He invents a precise number on the spot to look confident.
B He gives a ballpark and defers the precise figure, promising to circle back.
C He refuses to answer and changes the subject.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Q&A Toolkit

GRAMMAR

Each move in a tough Q&A has its own structure.

ToolStructureExample
Buy timeThat's a fair question + Let me think / make sure I understand"That's a fair question. Let me think for a second."
Indirect questionCould you tell me + normal word order (no inversion, no do/does)"Could you tell me what the cost is?"
ClarifyCould you clarify whether / what you mean by…"Could you clarify whether you mean cost or timing?"
Hedge (qualifier)As far as I can tell / To the best of my knowledge + statement"As far as I can tell, the risk is contained."
Defer cleanlyI don't have X to hand, but I'll circle back / get you the figure"I'll circle back with the precise number today."
Watch the trap: in an indirect question the verb does NOT invert and you drop do/does: "Could you tell me what the cost IS" (not "what is the cost"). A hedge is honest, not weak. And buying time is not stalling. PT: cada movimento de um Q&A dificil tem sua estrutura -- ganhe um segundo ("that's a fair question, let me think"), use indirect questions (ordem normal, sem inversao e sem do/does) para perguntar com educacao, faca hedge do que nao tem certeza com um qualifier ("as far as I can tell"), e adie de forma limpa ("I'll circle back"), sempre com um compromisso. Nunca responda off the cuff nem prometa demais.
