Precision

Three Ways to Write the Same Requirement

Rank them, say why, then click to compare

A. "Please fix the units with the interference problem as soon as possible."
Vague. It never says which units, against what criterion, or by when. The supplier reads it, picks the smallest number it can defend, and answers in three weeks. A requirement that can be interpreted will be interpreted — in their favor.
B. "The interlocking units, which failed the EMC test, must be reworked at the supplier's cost."
Worse than vague, because it looks precise. Those two commas say that ALL forty units failed — and the supplier will quote that sentence back at you the moment you ask for forty. One comma, and you have written a claim you cannot prove.
C. "The six interlocking units that failed the EMC test on 14 July must be reworked at the supplier's cost, and the grounding configuration, which is common to all forty units, must be re-verified."
Precise. The first clause restricts — only those six. The second, inside commas, adds a fact about all forty. Two clauses, two different scopes, and nothing left for anybody to interpret.

The Distinction

RFI vs RFP

Request for information

A question about something that already exists: a design, a test, a clause. You are asking them to explain, not to sell.

Inside the contract. It costs you nothing.

Request for proposal

An invitation to price work that has not been awarded. You are asking them to propose — and a proposal comes with a number.

New scope. It opens a commercial door.

If the rework is already inside the contract, an RFP is an invitation to charge you twice for it. Ask yourself one question before you send: am I asking them to explain what they owe, or to price something new?

Chapter 3: The Code

The Comma That Changes the Contract

"The units that failed"  ≠  "The units, which failed,"

Grammar Discovery

Same Words. One Comma.

"The interlocking units that failed the EMC test must be reworked."

"The interlocking units, which failed the EMC test, must be reworked."

"The report issued on 14 July does not cover the grounding configuration."

"Please confirm the standard to which the design was certified."

Sentences 1 and 2 have the same words. In the first, how many units failed? In the second? — And in sentence 3, what happened to which was?

FormWhat it doesExample
Defining relative clause
no commas		It restricts: it tells you WHICH ones. Remove it and you no longer know what you are talking about. Use that or which; who for people."The units that failed the EMC test must be reworked." — only those. Six.
Non-defining relative clause
two commas		It adds a fact about ALL of them. Remove it and the sentence still stands. Never that — only which / who."The units, which failed the EMC test, must be reworked." — all of them. Forty.
Reduced relative
past participle		Drop which was / that were when the meaning is passive. The compression of every technical document you read."the report which was issued on 14 July" → "the report issued on 14 July"
Reduced relative
-ing participle		Drop which is / that are when the meaning is active. The unit is doing it, not receiving it."the units which are radiating above the limit" → "the units radiating above the limit"
Preposition + whichThe formal register of specifications and RFIs. The preposition goes before which — and after a preposition, it is never that."the standard to which the design was certified" · "the conditions under which the test was performed"
Why it mattersSix units failed. Forty were built to the same grounding configuration. "The units that failed" commits the supplier to six. "The units, which failed," says all forty failed — which is not true, and the first thing their lawyer will point out. And "the grounding configuration, which is common to all forty units, must be re-verified" gets you the forty on the right grounds. The grammar is not decoration. It is the scope of the work.
Meaning

Four Clauses, Four Jobs

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"The interlocking units, which failed the EMC test, must be reworked at your cost."
"The interlocking units that failed the EMC test must be reworked at your cost."
"The cable installing last week is out of specification."
"The cable installed last week is out of specification."

Those two commas claim that all forty units failed — and only six did. And a reduced relative is passive with the past participle: "the cable installed" is the cable that was installed. "The cable installing" says the cable is installing something else.

Dialogue

The Clarification Call

J
Thank you for joining, Ingrid. I want to be precise, so let me start with the scope. The six interlocking units that failed the EMC test on 14 July are the ones we are asking you to rework.
I
Good. That is what our response says. Six units, and the rework is at our cost. I am glad we agree.
J
We agree on the six. We do not agree on why. Those six units were built to the grounding configuration, which is common to all forty. So the question I am raising is not about six units. It is about a design.
I
Thirty-four units passed, João. If the configuration were the cause, all forty would have failed. The other six sit next to the traction return cable. It is an installation issue, and installation is your contractor.
J
That is a reasonable hypothesis, and it may well be right. But it is a hypothesis. Until somebody pins down the source of the interference, nobody can say whether it is the configuration or the position — and thirty-four units passing a test does not prove a design.
I
Then let us be practical. My team has devised a workaround: ferrite filters on the six units. They can be fitted in a week and the line runs.
J
I will take the filters for the trial run. I will not take them for the handover, because a workaround does not meet the acceptance criteria that apply at handover, and those criteria are in clause 7.4. What I need in writing is the standard to which the design was certified, and the conditions under which you tested it.
I
You have written this very carefully, haven't you. All right. You will have the certification standard and the test conditions in ten days. And I will run the emission scan on four of the units that passed — because if you are right, I would rather find it myself.
Comprehension

About Ingrid

1. What does Ingrid accept at the start of the call?
The six units. She confirms that the six that failed will be reworked at the supplier's cost — and she is glad to agree, because agreeing to six is how she avoids being asked about forty.
2. What is her argument for keeping the grounding design out of scope?
That thirty-four units passed. If the configuration were the cause, she says, all forty would have failed — so it must be the position of the six, next to the traction return cable, which makes it an installation issue and therefore the client's contractor.
3. What does she offer instead of a fix?
A workaround: ferrite filters on the six units, fitted in a week, so the line can run. It solves her problem — the schedule — and it leaves the design question unanswered.
4. What does she finally commit to, and why?
The certification standard and the test conditions in ten days, and an emission scan on four of the units that passed. Her reason is professional, not generous: if João is right about the design, she would rather find it herself than have him find it.

Listening 2

Before You Submit That RFI

Your systems assurance lead. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does he say the comma does in a technical document?
It sets the scope, and therefore the money. Without commas the clause restricts — only those units. With commas it says the fact is true of all of them. He calls it the cheapest mistake to make and the most expensive one to argue about.
2. Why does he say an RFI must never contain the word "problem"?
Because "problem" is not a measurement. An RFI states what was found, against which criterion, on which date — and asks a question that can only be answered one way. A problem invites an opinion; a finding invites a response.
3. What is his rule about workarounds?
Accept a workaround only with the date it dies. A workaround that is written down without an end date stops being temporary: it becomes the design, and the client pays for it for thirty years.
