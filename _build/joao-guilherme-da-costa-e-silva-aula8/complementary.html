Lesson 8 -- Say Exactly What You Mean: Writing the RFI

Documentary
Downfall: The Case Against Boeing (Netflix, 89 min)

Your lesson at the scale of two disasters. The 737 MAX was certified as if it were the SAME aircraft as before — and the question you learned to ask today is the exact question nobody asked in time: the standard to which the design was certified, and the conditions under which it was tested. Watch what the documents did NOT say: MCAS existed, MCAS acted on its own, and MCAS was simply not in the manual. And watch how the company applies old acceptance criteria to a new design, and treats a design difference as if it were a minor variation. It is a documentary about ambiguity in technical documentation — and about who ends up paying for it.

Tip: watch with English subtitles and write down 8 sentences with a relative clause said by the engineers and the investigators ("the system that pushed the nose down", "the pilots, who had never been told about it, ..."). For EACH one, decide: does the clause DEFINE (say which ones) or ADD (talk about all of them)? Then rewrite THREE of them, moving the commas, and watch the meaning — and the liability — change owner.

Podcast
Cautionary Tales (Tim Harford) — "Cleared For Take-Off? (Tenerife Air Disaster 1)" (~40 min, free)

The most expensive case of ambiguity in the history of engineering, and it fits into four words. In 1977, at Tenerife, a Dutch captain said over the radio "We are now at take-off" — and the tower understood that he was HOLDING at the take-off point, when he meant that he was TAKING OFF. 583 people died. Nobody got the English wrong: the sentence was grammatically perfect and it could, in good faith, be understood in two ways. It is the exact definition of what you saw today — and it happens between non-native speakers, under schedule pressure, in technical English: your world, with Germans, French and Chinese engineers on the same call.

Tip: listen ONCE without pausing. Then stop at the fatal sentence and write THREE versions of it that could NOT be read in two ways. Next, open the last technical email you sent to a supplier and find ONE sentence of your own that allows two readings. Rewrite it. That is the whole skill.

YouTube
Defining and Non-Defining Relative Clauses — Essential B2 First (FCE) Grammar · SMASH English (12 min)

The exact structure of the lesson, at the exact level, from the same channel as the videos in lessons 6 and 7. The two clauses side by side, with the rule that decides everything: with no comma the clause DEFINES (it says which ones) and accepts that; with commas it ADDS (it talks about all of them) and that becomes impossible — only which. He also covers reduced relatives and the preposition in front of which, which is the register of the RFI. Watch it AFTER the lesson: the video will sound obvious, and that is the point.

Tip: after the video, open the last REAL RFI or technical note you wrote at Motiva and audit it: underline every relative clause and ask yourself "how many units did I just commit?". Then write 6 new sentences about your own project — two defining, two non-defining, one reduced, and one with preposition + which. Those six sentences are the RFI you are going to send for real.
