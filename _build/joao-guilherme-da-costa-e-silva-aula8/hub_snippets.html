Say Exactly What You Mean -- Writing the RFI

Six interlocking units failed the EMC test — and all forty were built to the same grounding configuration. How to write the RFI that locks the scope onto the six, puts the fact about the forty on record, and cannot be read in two ways. Key words: request for information (RFI), request for proposal (RFP), interoperability, electromagnetic compatibility (EMC), grounding, interlocking, fail-safe, redundancy, acceptance criteria, workaround, ambiguity, to spell out, to devise, to pin down. Structure: relative clauses — defining vs. non-defining (the comma that rewrites the contract), reduced relatives, and preposition + which.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

In lesson 7 you turned memory into a record. Here you write the document — and a technical document is not read, it is interpreted. These are the words of the formal clarification: what you ask, what you measure, and what you accept only as a temporary arrangement. Listen to each term and read the example.

Request for information (RFI) -- a formal written question sent to a supplier when a document does not tell you enough
"The RFI that we submitted on Monday has not been answered."
Request for proposal (RFP) -- a formal invitation asking suppliers to propose a solution, with a price, for work not yet awarded
"The rework is inside the contract, so this is an RFI, not an RFP."
Interoperability -- the ability of two different systems to work together and understand each other
"Each system passed alone. What has not been demonstrated is interoperability."
Electromagnetic compatibility (EMC) -- whether equipment can operate without disturbing other equipment through electrical noise
"Six units failed the electromagnetic compatibility test on 14 July."
Grounding -- the connection that gives electrical noise a safe path away from the equipment
"It was the grounding configuration, common to all forty units, that caused the interference."
Interlocking -- the signaling logic that makes two conflicting movements impossible at the same time
"The interlocking units that failed are on the northbound platform."
Fail-safe -- designed so that when it fails, it fails into the state that is safe
"A signal that loses power goes to red. That is what fail-safe means."
Redundancy -- a second, independent path that keeps the system working when the first one fails
"The redundancy that protects the northbound line has not been demonstrated."
Acceptance criteria -- the written conditions a deliverable must meet before anybody is allowed to accept it
"The acceptance criteria that apply to this test are in clause 7.4, not in the email."
Workaround -- a temporary way of operating that lets you carry on while the real fix is still missing
"A workaround is acceptable for the trial run. It is not acceptable for the handover."
Ambiguity -- when a sentence is clear, but can honestly be understood in two different ways
"The ambiguity in clause 7.4 is what the supplier is relying on."
To spell out -- to state something in complete detail, leaving nothing for the reader to assume
"The RFI has to spell out which units, against which criterion, and by when."
To devise -- to think out and design a solution that did not exist before
"The workaround that your team devised is not written down anywhere."
To pin down -- to identify something exactly, when it has been vague or moving until now
"Until we pin down the source of the interference, any fix is a guess."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

Forty interlocking units were delivered for Line 5, and on 14 July six of them failed the electromagnetic compatibility test. João Guilherme has to write the request for information that goes to the German supplier, and he has one afternoon to decide what the document actually asks for. The six that failed are the six installed closest to the traction return cable. But all forty were built to the same grounding configuration — and that is the sentence that will cost somebody money.

Read the two versions carefully, because they contain the same words. "The interlocking units that failed the EMC test must be reworked." No commas: the clause RESTRICTS. It says WHICH units — those six, and nobody else. Now the second: "The interlocking units, which failed the EMC test, must be reworked." Two commas, and the clause no longer restricts anything: it simply ADDS a fact about all of them. It says that all forty failed. And all forty did not fail, so João has just written a claim he cannot prove, and the first person to notice will be their lawyer. One comma. Thirty-four units.

So he writes it the way it has to be written. The six units that failed are to be reworked at the supplier's cost. The grounding configuration, which is common to all forty units, must be re-verified — a fact about the whole batch, stated as a fact, without pretending the whole batch failed. Then he compresses, the way every standard on his shelf compresses: the report issued on 14 July does not cover the configuration, and the units radiating above the limit are the six on the northbound platform. And he closes with the two questions that no ambiguity survives: please confirm the standard to which the design was certified, and spell out the conditions under which the EMC test was performed — on the bench, or as installed? The supplier's answer arrives nine days later. Their team has devised a workaround, ferrite filters, and they are careful too: the filters will meet the acceptance criteria applicable to the trial run. Not to the handover. In a technical document, nobody writes a relative clause by accident.

1. "The interlocking units that failed the EMC test must be reworked." How many units does the supplier have to rework?
A All forty — the clause simply describes the units in general.
B SIX — with no commas, the clause DEFINES: it says WHICH units. Take the clause out and you no longer know what is being talked about.
C It depends on the contract; grammar does not decide it.
2. And in the version "The interlocking units, which failed the EMC test, must be reworked"? What CHANGES?
A Nothing — in English the comma is only a reading pause.
B It sounds more formal, but the meaning is the same.
C EVERYTHING: with the two commas the clause stops defining and starts ADDING a fact about ALL of them. The sentence now states that all forty failed — which is false, and it will be used against whoever wrote it.
3. "The report issued on 14 July does not cover the grounding." What structure is this?
A A REDUCED relative: which was has dropped out and the PAST participle is left, with a PASSIVE meaning — the report THAT WAS issued. This is how every technical standard is written.
B A mistake: the main verb is missing.
C An active -ing form: the report is issuing something.
4. Which sentence is correct?
A "Please confirm the standard to that the design was certified."
B "Please confirm the standard to which the design was certified."
C "Please confirm the standard which the design was certified to it."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Relative Clauses & Precision

GRAMMAR

How the comma decides the scope — and why one sentence, with it and without it, commits the supplier to six units or to forty.

StructureWhat it doesExample
DEFINING relative
no commas
(that / which / who)		It DEFINES: it says WHICH ones. Take the clause out and you no longer know what is being talked about. This is the clause that sets the SCOPE of the work — and scope is money."The six units that failed the EMC test must be reworked." — those six, and no others.
NON-DEFINING relative
between two commas
(NEVER that)		It ADDS a fact about ALL of them. Take the clause out and the sentence still stands. After a comma, only which (things) or who (people)."The units, which failed the test, must be reworked." — ALL forty failed.
REDUCED relative
past participle		which was / that were drops out when the meaning is PASSIVE. It is the compression that every technical standard on his shelf is built on."the report which was issued on 14 July" → "the report issued on 14 July"
REDUCED relative
-ing		which is / that are drops out when the meaning is ACTIVE: the unit is DOING it, not receiving it."the units which are radiating above the limit" → "the units radiating above the limit"
Preposition + whichThe formal register of the RFI and of the specification. The preposition comes BEFORE which — and that can never follow a preposition."the standard to which the design was certified" · "the conditions under which the test was performed"
Why it mattersSix units failed. All forty share the same grounding. "The units that failed" commits the supplier to six. "The units, which failed," states that all forty failed — which is false, and their lawyer will point it out on the first reading. And "the grounding configuration, which is common to all forty units, must be re-verified" gets all forty for the right reason. The grammar here is not decoration: it is the scope of the contract.

Watch out (three habits carried over from Portuguese): (1) "The RFI that we submitted it on Monday" — the object pronoun is NOT repeated in English: that is already the object; (2) "the configuration, that is common to all forty" — after a comma, that is impossible: use which; (3) "the cable installing last week" — the cable is not installing anything: it WAS installed, so it is installed (past participle). One form covers both cases in your first language; English needs two.

RFI collocations: submit an RFI (never "send"), respond to technical queries (never "answer to"), verify compliance with the specs (never "compliance TO"), ensure interoperability, validate the design, exceed the emission limit, implement a workaround, fall outside the scope, meet the acceptance criteria.

The question to ask before you hit send: "Can this sentence be read in a second way that costs me money?" If it can, it is not ready. Ambiguity is not confusion: an ambiguous sentence looks completely clear — and that is precisely why it gets through.

Stage 2: Put the RFI in Order

Order

Put the steps of the RFI in the correct order — from locking the scope down to the date on which the workaround dies.

?Close the workaround: the ferrite filters are accepted for the trial run only, and not for the handover.
?Add the fact about all forty: the grounding configuration, which is common to the whole batch, has not been verified as installed.
?Compress the evidence: the report issued on 14 July does not cover the configuration.
?Ask formally: please confirm the standard to which the design was certified.
?Restrict the scope: the six interlocking units that failed the EMC test are to be reworked.
?Ask formally again: please spell out the conditions under which the test was performed.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer for every real moment of the technical clarification call — including the moment the supplier argues that thirty-four passes prove the design, or offers a temporary fix as if it were the solution.

You want the supplier to rework ONLY the six units that failed. You write:
A "The interlocking units, which failed the EMC test, must be reworked at your cost."
B "The six interlocking units that failed the EMC test on 14 July must be reworked at your cost."
C "Please rework the units with the interference problem as soon as possible."
You want to put on record that the grounding is the SAME on all forty — without stating that all forty failed:
A "The forty units, which failed the EMC test, share the same grounding configuration."
B "The grounding configuration, which is common to all forty units, has not been verified in the installed condition."
C "All the units have a grounding problem that needs to be looked at."
Ingrid says: "Thirty-four units passed, so the design is correct." The strongest reply is:
A "That is a reasonable hypothesis, and it may well be right. But thirty-four units passing a test does not demonstrate a design — and two of them passed by almost nothing."
B "That is not true and you know it."
C "All right. If thirty-four passed, let us close the design question."
She offers ferrite filters "that fix it in a week". You answer:
A "Perfect. If the emissions come down, we can consider it closed."
B "I will take the filters for the trial run. I will not take them for the handover, because a workaround does not meet the acceptance criteria that apply at handover."
C "We do not accept workarounds under any circumstances."
You need to know HOW they ran the test (on the bench or as installed). You ask:
A "How did you do the test exactly? Can you explain it to me?"
B "Please spell out the conditions under which the EMC test was performed, and confirm the standard to which the design was certified."
C "The conditions which you tested the design in them are not clear."

Podcast
Cautionary Tales (Tim Harford) — "Cleared For Take-Off? (Tenerife Air Disaster 1)" (~40 min, free)

The most expensive case of ambiguity in the history of engineering, and it fits into four words. In 1977, at Tenerife, a Dutch captain said over the radio "We are now at take-off" — and the tower understood that he was HOLDING at the take-off point, when he meant that he was TAKING OFF. 583 people died. Nobody got the English wrong: the sentence was grammatically perfect and it could, in good faith, be understood in two ways. It is the exact definition of what you saw today — and it happens between non-native speakers, under schedule pressure, in technical English: your world, with Germans, French and Chinese engineers on the same call.

Tip: listen ONCE without pausing. Then stop at the fatal sentence and write THREE versions of it that could NOT be read in two ways. Next, open the last technical email you sent to a supplier and find ONE sentence of your own that allows two readings. Rewrite it. That is the whole skill.
