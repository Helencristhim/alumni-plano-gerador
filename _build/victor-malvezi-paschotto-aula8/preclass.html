Aula 08 -- Pre-class

Negotiating the Deal -- Holding Your Ground & Trading Concessions

Close a deal without giving anything away for free. New words: concession, trade-off, counteroffer, deadlock, common ground, bargaining chip, non-negotiable, sticking point, meet halfway, hold firm, in good faith, give and take. New structure: the first conditional for real trades (if + present simple, will/can + base) plus the connectors unless, as long as and provided that -- contrasted with the softer second conditional from Lesson 7.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

The language of trading concessions. Tap Listen to hear each word.

Concession -- something you give up or agree to in order to reach an agreement
"I can offer one concession on price, but only in exchange for volume."
Trade-off -- a balance in which you accept a disadvantage to gain an advantage
"A longer contract is the trade-off for a lower unit price."
Counteroffer -- a new offer you make in response to one you did not accept
"Their price was too high, so I came back with a counteroffer."
Deadlock -- a situation where neither side will move and the talks stop
"We hit a deadlock on price, so I changed a different variable."
Common ground -- the interests or points that both sides already agree on
"Let's start from the common ground: we both want a long-term partnership."
Bargaining chip -- something of value you can offer or hold back to gain an advantage
"Our annual volume is the strongest bargaining chip we have."
Non-negotiable -- a condition that cannot be changed, traded, or given up
"The service guarantee is non-negotiable for us."
Sticking point -- the single issue that is blocking the agreement
"Lead time is the sticking point; everything else is agreed."
Meet halfway -- to make equal compromises so that both sides gain something
"We were close, so we agreed to meet halfway on the rate."
Hold firm -- to refuse to change your position, even under pressure
"On the guarantee, I had to hold firm and not give way."
In good faith -- with honest intentions to reach a fair agreement
"Both sides came to the table in good faith."
Give and take -- mutual compromise, where each side offers something to the other
"A good deal needs give and take from both sides."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the negotiation summary and answer the questions.

The Growth Manager sat down with the supplier in good faith and opened from the common ground: both sides wanted a long-term partnership. He knew the talk would be all give and take, so he led with a trade-off. "If you bring the lead time down to four weeks, we will commit to the full volume," he said. That volume was his strongest bargaining chip. The supplier pushed back, and for a moment they reached a deadlock on price. So he set a clear condition: "We can't sign unless the rate drops by five percent." He offered a longer contract as a concession, but only with a condition attached: "We'll sign for two years, as long as the service guarantee stays exactly as written" -- because the guarantee was non-negotiable, and on that he had to hold firm. The only real sticking point left was three percent on the rate. So he made his final counteroffer: they would meet halfway, and the deal was done.

1. Why does the manager say "If you bring the lead time down, we will commit" rather than "If you will bring it down"?
A It is a first conditional: the if-clause uses the present simple, never "will".
B Both clauses should use "will" for emphasis.
C It is a third conditional about the past.
2. What does "We can't sign unless the rate drops" mean?
A They will sign even if the rate stays the same.
B They will only sign if the rate drops ("unless" = "if not").
C They have already signed the contract.
3. Which form correctly follows "as long as" in a real trade?
A "will" plus the base verb.
B The present simple (stays, holds, drops).
C "had" plus the past participle.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Conditionals for Trading

GRAMMAR

Use the first conditional to put a real trade on the table: a give for a get.

FormPatternExample
AffirmativeIf + present simple, will/can + base"If you cut the price, we will sign."
unless (= if not)will not + base, unless + present"We won't commit unless the lead time drops."
as long as / provided that+ present simple (one condition)"We'll renew, as long as you hold the rate."
Softer (second cond.)If + past simple, would + base"If you dropped it, we would consider it."
Watch the traps: the if-clause uses the present simple, never "will"; "unless" already means "if not", so never add a second negative. First conditional = a real offer on the table now; the second conditional (Lesson 7) is softer and more tentative, to float an idea politely. PT: o first conditional poe uma troca real na mesa -- "se voce fizer X, nos fazemos Y" (if + presente, will/can + verbo base); use o second conditional quando quiser soar mais cauteloso e diplomatico.
