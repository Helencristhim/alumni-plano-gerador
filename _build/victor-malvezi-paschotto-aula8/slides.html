Key Expressions

Phrases That Trade

"Let's find some common ground." — opens by naming what you already agree on
"I can do that, if you can do this." — trades a concession for a concession
"Where can we be flexible?" — invites both sides to find room to move
"That's where I have to hold firm." — names a line you will not cross, calmly
"Can we meet halfway?" — proposes a fair split when you are close
Chapter 3

The Trade Engine

Conditionals for trading concessions

Grammar Discovery

Spot the Pattern

What do these real, on-the-table trades have in common?

"If you bring the lead time down to four weeks, we will commit to the full volume."

"We can offer a longer contract, as long as you hold the current rate."

"We can't sign unless the price drops by five percent."

These are real offers, on the table right now. Each give earns a get.

Grammar Rule

The Trade Conditional

PartFormExample
If-clauseIf + present simple"If you drop the price,"
Resultwill / can + base verb"we will sign today."
unless= if not (one condition)"We can't commit unless the lead time drops."
as long as / provided that= on this one condition"We'll renew, as long as you hold the rate."

First vs second conditional at the table:

First conditional = a real offer on the table now: "If you reduce the price, we will sign." Second conditional (last class) = softer and more tentative, useful to test an idea diplomatically: "If you reduced the price, we would sign." Use the first to close; use the second to float a possibility without committing.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"If you will reduce the price, we will sign."

✓ RIGHT

"If you reduce the price, we will sign."

✗ WRONG

"Unless you don't lower the lead time, we can't commit."

✓ RIGHT

"Unless you lower the lead time, we can't commit."

In a first conditional, the if-clause uses the present simple, never "will". And "unless" already means "if not", so it never takes a second negative.

Chapter 4

Across the Table

Negotiating the supplier contract

Dialogue

The Supplier Deal

P
Victor, good to see you. I'll be honest: our price is firm this year. I have very little room to move on the rate.
V
Understood, and thank you for being direct. Let's start from the common ground: we both want a long-term partnership. So let's see where each of us can be flexible.
P
Fair enough. What did you have in mind?
V
Here is a trade-off. If you bring the lead time down to four weeks, we will commit to the full annual volume. That volume is our strongest bargaining chip, and it is real money for you.
P
Four weeks is tight. I could maybe reach five, but then I would need the two-year contract, not one.
V
I can live with two years, as long as the service guarantee stays exactly as written. That part is non-negotiable for us, and that is where I have to hold firm.
P
The guarantee is fine; we never planned to touch it. So our only real sticking point is the rate.
V
Then let's meet halfway. If you give us a three percent cut, we will sign the two-year deal today, full volume, guarantee included. Do we have an agreement?
Comprehension

Did You Catch It?

1. What is Priya firm about at the start of the meeting?
The price: she says her rate is firm and she has very little room to move on it.
2. What does Priya ask for in exchange for a five-week lead time?
A two-year contract instead of a one-year one.
3. Once the guarantee is settled, what does Priya say the only remaining sticking point is?
The rate (the price).

Chapter 5

Sharpen the Deal

Trading, at speed

Quick Fire

Trade It in One Line

1 / 5

1. Trade volume for a shorter lead time (first conditional, "bring / commit").

"If you bring the lead time down, we will commit to the full volume."

2. Set a single condition with "unless" (price must drop to sign).

"We can't sign unless the price drops by five percent."

3. Offer a longer contract with "as long as" (rate held).

"We can offer a two-year contract, as long as you hold the current rate."

4. Name your non-negotiable calmly and hold firm.

"The service guarantee is non-negotiable, and that is where I have to hold firm."

5. Propose a fair split to close (meet halfway, three percent).

"Let's meet halfway: if you give us three percent, we will sign today."
