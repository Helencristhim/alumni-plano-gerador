Chapter 4: The Strength of Advice

How Advisors Recommend

Modals of recommendation

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"You could go with the cheaper option."

"I'd recommend the premium plan."

"You should compare the two costs first."

"You had better decide soon, or you'll lose the discount."

StructureStrengthWhen to use
couldSofter optionOne possibility among others: "You could test both."
I'd recommend / shouldStandardA clear recommendation: "I'd recommend the cloud plan."
had betterStrong (warning)Advice with a consequence: "You had better decide, or you'll lose the deal."

After could / should / had better, use the base verb (no to). I'd recommend takes a noun or the -ing form: "I'd recommend testing it."

Meaning Guide

How Strong Is the Recommendation?

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"I recommend you to take the cloud option."

✓ RIGHT

"I recommend taking the cloud option."

✗ WRONG

"You could to compare the costs."

✓ RIGHT

"You could compare the costs."

Use recommend + -ing or recommend that you + verb, never "recommend you to". And no to after could.

Dialogue

Choosing the Right Tool

J
Helen, I'm stuck. We need a new project tool, and I have two options on the table.
H
Okay. Tell me about each one. What's the main benefit, and what's the drawback?
J
Option A is cheap and simple, but it has fewer features. Option B is powerful, yet it's expensive.
H
I see. For a small team, I'd recommend starting with the simple one.
J
But the powerful tool could save us time later. Isn't that worthwhile?
H
It could be. Still, you should test the cheaper option first, before you spend more.
J
Good point. And if the team grows, we can always upgrade.
H
Exactly. My advice is simple: pick the solution that fits today, not the one that looks impressive.
J
That's really helpful. You'd make a great consultant, Helen.
