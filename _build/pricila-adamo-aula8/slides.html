Chapter 3: Relative Clauses

Saying Exactly Which One

defining (no commas) · non-defining (commas, never that)

Discover the Code

Listen and Find the Pattern

Take the street that leads to the station. (which street? — defining)

The driver who picked us up knew a shortcut. (which driver? — defining)

The old bridge, which closes at rush hour, is faster. (extra info — non-defining)

That is the square where the market is held. (place — defining)

Defining (essential)Non-defining (extra info)
No commasAlways commas
who / which / that / wherewho / which / where — never that
Object pronoun can be dropped: "the road (that) I took"Nothing can be dropped

Use who for people, which for things, where for places. Commas mean the clause is just bonus information; remove it and the sentence still makes full sense.

Common Mistake

Wrong vs Right

✗ WRONG

"My hotel, that is near the river, is quiet."

✓ RIGHT

"My hotel, which is near the river, is quiet."

✗ WRONG

"The street which it leads downtown is closed."

✓ RIGHT

"The street which leads downtown is closed."

✗ WRONG

"Take the bus, that stops here, to the centre."

✓ RIGHT

"Take the bus that stops here to the centre."

Chapter 4: Around the City

In the Taxi

Pricila works out the route with a local driver

Dialogue

Working Out the Route

P
Hello, could you take me to the Belmonte Hotel, which is near the old market square?
D
Of course. The road that usually goes there is closed for repairs, so we'll take a small detour. Don't worry, I know a shortcut.
P
Thank you. Is there much congestion at this time? The driver who brought me yesterday got stuck for ages.
D
It's rush hour, so the centre, which is always busy, can be gridlock. But my route avoids the big roundabout where everyone gets stuck.
P
Perfect. Could you pull over at the crosswalk near the hotel? I'd rather walk the last bit, which gives me a feel for the area.
D
Good idea. Just remember it's a one-way street, so walk against the traffic. Here we are -- that's the lane that leads straight to the square.
Listening

The Driver's Advice

Listen first. Sound first, no text.

0:000:00
1. Why does the driver take a detour?
The road that usually takes you downtown is closed for repairs.
2. What does the driver say about the roundabout?
The third exit, which is the one they want, leads straight to the hotel.
3. What is the driver's advice for getting around the city?
Never drive yourself; use public transit or hail a cab like a local.
Comprehension

About the Conversation

1. Where is the Belmonte Hotel, and how does Pricila describe it?
It is near the old market square -- "the hotel, which is near the old market square".
2. Why does the driver avoid the big roundabout?
Because it is the roundabout where everyone gets stuck in rush-hour gridlock.
3. Describe it: what does Pricila ask the driver to do at the end?
To pull over at the crosswalk near the hotel, because she wants to walk the last bit, which gives her a feel for the area.

The Directions

Read a Real Set of Directions

⚲

Route to Belmonte Hotel · 1.4 km

Estimated 8 min · light traffic

"Leave the station and take the road that runs along the river. Avoid the main bridge, which is closed for repairs, and follow the detour signs instead. At the roundabout, take the second exit onto the one-way street where the market is held. Use the crosswalk by the cafe, then continue on foot -- this is the quiet lane that avoids the worst congestion. The hotel, which is the building with the green awning, is the third on your left. In rush hour, public transit is faster than any cab."

Describe it: why should you avoid the main bridge?
Because it is the bridge which is closed for repairs, so you follow the detour signs instead.
Which exit do you take at the roundabout, and where does it lead?
The second exit, which goes onto the one-way street where the market is held.

Tools:

the road that... the driver, who... the square where...

Scenario: Tell the teacher how you get around a city you know well -- the traffic, the best routes, the places to avoid at rush hour. Use relative clauses to describe the streets, the people and the shortcuts.
