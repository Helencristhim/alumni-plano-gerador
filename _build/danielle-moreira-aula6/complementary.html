Film
Moneyball (2011) — Netflix

Billy Beane trades the scouts’ opinion for an instrument of measurement — and then spends the whole film defending that instrument in front of a room that has already decided he is wrong. It is your September meeting, in baseball. Connection to Lesson 6: notice that the resistance is never against the numbers, but against WHO decided to use them. Beane calculates nothing himself: he has it calculated (causative), and he answers for it.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Write down TWO lines where somebody mistakes a symptom for a root cause, and ONE where Beane defends the instrument without apologizing for the number.

Podcast
WorkLife with Adam Grant — “How to Trust People You Don’t Like” (TED)

Adam Grant, an organizational psychologist, on how trust is built between people who disagree — astronauts from different countries locked inside a space station. It is the ground under the trust index you will measure in Canada, and the executive English used to talk about it. Connection to Lesson 6: trust is not warmth. It is predictability — which is why the single thing that moves your response rate most is saying, up front, what happens to the findings.

Tip: listen to five or seven minutes with no pauses. Then summarize it out loud in sixty seconds, using ONE causative (“They had the crew trained by...”) and ONE hedge (“The data would suggest that...”). Write down three expressions you had never heard before.

YouTube
Amy Edmondson — “Building a psychologically safe workplace” (TEDxHGSE, 11 min)

The Harvard researcher who turned a concept into an INSTRUMENT: she explains how she measured hospital teams and found that the better teams reported MORE errors, not fewer. It is a lesson in how a counterintuitive finding is defended. Connection to Lesson 6: her number said the opposite of the obvious, and she did not soften the number — she went after the root cause. It is exactly what Claire asked for in the voice note.

Tip: watch it once, all the way through. Then write THREE sentences about your own diagnostic using her pattern: the finding, the hedge, and the root cause underneath — and bring them to Lesson 7.
