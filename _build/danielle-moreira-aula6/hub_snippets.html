Key words: cultural assessment, diagnostic tool, baseline, response rate, focus group, trust index, root cause, gap analysis, findings, benchmark, to run a diagnostic, to gather insights, to surface an issue, to come up with, to open a can of worms. Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the vocabulary of the deck the Canadian leadership reads — and from September on, it is the deck you write.

Cultural assessment -- a structured study of how an organization actually works, rather than how it says it works
"A cultural assessment does not ask people to describe the culture. It puts the culture in a position where it has to show itself."
Diagnostic tool -- the instrument you choose to see with: a questionnaire, an interview protocol, a focus group
"The diagnostic tool is never neutral. Choose the wrong one and you will collect politeness and call it data."
Baseline -- the measurement you take before you change anything, so that later you can prove what moved
"Have the baseline taken in September. Without it, every improvement in March is just your opinion."
Response rate -- the share of people who actually answered, and the first thing a skeptical room will attack
"Thirty-one percent is not a technical failure. The response rate is the first finding."
Focus group -- a small room, a facilitator, and the sentences a questionnaire can never capture
"A survey tells you how many. A focus group tells you why, and it tells you in ninety minutes."
Trust index -- a single number that tracks whether people believe what leadership tells them
"Whatever the trust index comes back as, it will be lower than the leadership team believes."
Root cause -- the thing underneath the symptom, and the only thing worth fixing
"Attrition is not a root cause. It is what a root cause looks like nine months later."
Gap analysis -- the distance between where the culture is today and where the strategy needs it to be
"The gap analysis is the only slide the board will remember, because it is the only one with a distance on it."
Findings -- what the data actually says, before anyone decides what to do about it
"Nobody in that faculty was ever told what the findings were. That is why they will not answer you now."
Benchmark -- an external number you compare yourself to, so that good stops being an opinion
"Sixty-two means nothing on its own. Against the education benchmark of seventy-one, it means a great deal."
To run a diagnostic -- to put an organization through a structured examination before prescribing anything
"We are not here to run a diagnostic on you. We are here to run one with you."
To gather insights -- to collect what people mean, not only what they answered
"The survey gathers numbers. The focus groups are where we gather insights."
To surface an issue -- to bring a problem out into the open, where the room can finally work on it
"A good focus group does not collect opinions. It surfaces the issue that everybody already knew about."
To come up with -- to produce an idea or a framework, usually under pressure
"Give me three weeks and I will come up with an instrument this faculty will actually answer."
To open a can of worms -- to raise one question that releases a dozen you did not plan for
"I know I am opening a can of worms. I would rather open it on a Thursday than read it in an exit interview in March."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Every People and Culture leader who arrives in a new division wants to run a diagnostic. Very few of them stop to ask what a diagnostic does to the people being diagnosed. The diagnostic tool is never neutral. The moment a questionnaire lands in an inbox, the organization starts answering a question nobody wrote: is it safe to be honest here?

This is why the response rate is the first finding, and not a footnote. A team that has had its opinion collected before and was never told the outcome will answer at thirty percent — and the thirty percent who do answer are not a random sample. They are the angriest and the most loyal, and the middle of the organization is missing from the data. The data would suggest that a low response rate is not a technical failure at all. It is a trust index with no survey attached to it.

So the sequence matters more than the instrument. Have the baseline taken before you change anything, so that in March you can prove what moved instead of arguing about it. Have the expectations set out in writing before the first question is asked: who reads the findings, when, and what happens to them. One director in Toronto had the questionnaire designed by an external specialist and then had it reviewed by two people inside her own building, precisely so that the faculty could not dismiss it as a document written somewhere else. To some extent, the findings indicate that this single decision did more for her response rate than the questions ever did.

And when the results arrive, resist the symptom. Attrition is not a root cause; it is what a root cause looks like nine months later. A good focus group will not collect opinions — it will surface issues that have been quiet for a decade, and some of them will not be culture problems at all. You will open a can of worms. The gap analysis you present to the board will be clean; the room you leave behind will not. That is not a reason to skip the assessment. It is a reason to come up with a plan for the second week, and not only for the first.

1. Why do we say “We had the questionnaire designed by a specialist” and not “We had designed the questionnaire by a specialist”?
A Because in the causative passive the past participle comes after the object (have + object + past participle). “We had designed” is past perfect, and it means WE did the designing.
B Because “design” cannot be used in the passive voice in English.
C Because the subject is plural, which forces inversion.
2. “I had Marc review the questions.” — why do we NOT say “I had Marc to review the questions”?
A Because “review” is a noun in that position.
B Because after have + person the verb takes the base form, with no “to”. That “to” is a direct translation from your first language.
C Because Marc is a proper name, and proper names are never followed by an infinitive.
3. According to the text, why is a low response rate a finding rather than a technical failure?
A Because the questionnaire was too long and people gave up halfway through it.
B Because a team that was asked once and never told the outcome answers at thirty percent — so the rate is a trust index with no survey attached to it.
C Because thirty percent is statistically sufficient for any cultural assessment.
4. What does the Toronto director's decision (an external specialist, then an in-house review) actually buy her?
A A cheaper instrument, because the review work was not paid for.
B A shorter questionnaire, because two reviewers cut half of the questions.
C Credibility — the faculty could not dismiss the survey as a document written somewhere else, and the text says it did more for the response rate than the questions did.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Causative Passive & Hedging Data

GRAMMAR

The grammar of the person who has it done: the subject does not perform the action, but answers for it — which is exactly where a People & Culture leader stands.

FormUseExample
Causative Passive
have + object + past participle		You arranged it; somebody else performed it. The action is theirs, the decision is yours.We had the questionnaire designed by an external specialist.
Causative Active
have + person + base verb		You name the person you called on. No “to”. Never “to review”.I had Marc review the questions before they went out.
Deadline pattern
to have + X + completed by		The delivery commitment, without promising who does what.Our goal is to have the baseline completed by the end of September.
get instead of haveSame structure, less formal, and it hints at effort: “I finally got the 2024 data re-coded.” Use have with the board; get with your team.
Plain passive vs causative“The survey was designed by a consultant” names the person who typed it — and erases you. “We had the survey designed” names the person who DECIDED. In a diagnostic room, nobody asks who typed it.
Hedging on dataA reading of the data, not a verdict. An interpretation, which the room can still argue with.The data would suggest that... · To some extent, the findings indicate...
Collocationrun a diagnostic · gather insights · surface an issue · come up with a framework (never “come up a framework”) · open a can of worms

Watch out (the number one slip): the ORDER. In English the past participle comes AFTER the object: “had the questionnaire designed”. “We had designed the questionnaire” is past perfect — it says you designed it yourself, which is not what happened.

Watch out (the other slip): no “to” after have + person. “I had Marc to review” is a word-for-word translation arriving whole. The correct form is “I had Marc review”.

The combination you want: a causative and a hedge in the SAME sentence. “The data would suggest that we should have the baseline taken before September, rather than after.” It is the most senior line in this lesson.
