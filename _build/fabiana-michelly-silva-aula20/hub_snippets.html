End-to-End Communication -- Advanced Discourse Markers (signposting a coherent, extended turn)

Advanced discourse markers for telling the whole story coherently: sequence (to begin with / moving on), contrast (that said / nonetheless), result (consequently / as a result), and summary (to sum up / the upshot is) -- the four moves that hold an extended turn together. New vocabulary: discourse marker, cohesion, signpost, throughline, synthesize, recap, segue, crux, overarching, upshot, standpoint, coherent.

Cohesion -- the quality of parts hanging together as a whole

Signpost -- to mark out the stages so listeners can follow

Throughline -- the single idea that runs through the whole story
"The throughline is simple: every decision was underpinned by real figures." (Fio condutor)
Synthesize -- to combine separate parts into one clear whole
"In the recap, I synthesize the whole programme in two sentences." (Sintetizar)
Recap -- a short summary of what was already said
"To finish, a quick recap of the headline figures." (Recapitulação / resumo)
Segue -- a smooth transition from one topic to the next
"That is a natural segue from the negotiation to the results." (Transição suave / gancho)
Crux -- the single most important point of the matter
"The crux of the problem was duplication across eleven suppliers." (Âmago / ponto central)
Overarching -- covering and connecting everything; top-level
"The overarching aim was simple: fewer partners, better rates." (Abrangente / global)
Upshot -- the final result or conclusion that matters
"The upshot is that both sides signed in good faith." (Resultado final / conclusão)
Standpoint -- a particular point of view on a matter
"From a cost standpoint, the consolidation is clearly the right call." (Ponto de vista / ótica)
Coherent -- clear, logical, and easy to follow as a whole
"A coherent story takes them from the problem to the signature without gaps." (Coerente)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

An extended turn -- presenting a whole programme from problem to result -- only works if it is coherent. The tool that creates that cohesion is the discourse marker: a word or phrase that signals what comes next. There are four key functions. To signpost a new stage, use a sequence marker: "To begin with, the context. Moving on, the proposal." To turn the direction, use a contrast marker: "The savings were clear. That said, the negotiation was tough." (Others: nonetheless, whereas, having said that.) To show an effect, use a result marker: "We held firm on the cap. Consequently, the contract is balanced." (Others: as a result, in light of this.) And to close, use a summary marker: "To sum up, four million in savings." (Others: in essence, the upshot is.) Match the marker to the function -- a contrast marker where you mean a result simply confuses the listener. Used well, these markers give your story a clear throughline from the first word to the final recap.

1. "We held firm on the cap. Consequently, the contract is balanced." What does "Consequently" signal?
A a result -- the balanced contract follows as an effect of holding firm.
B a contrast -- the two ideas push against each other.
C a new, unrelated topic.
2. Which marker best opens a new stage of the story (sequence)?
A "That said," -- this signals a contrast.
B "Moving on," -- this signals the next stage.
C "As a result," -- this signals an effect.
3. Why do speakers use discourse markers in an extended turn?
A To create cohesion and a clear throughline so listeners can follow from start to finish.
B To make the talk longer with no purpose.
C To hide the main point from the audience.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best response for each professional situation.

You are opening the first stage of an end-to-end presentation. You say:
A "Let me signpost where we are going. To begin with, the context: eleven overlapping suppliers." (clear sequence marker)
B "So, there is a lot of stuff and I will just say it all and see how it goes." (no signposting)
C "As a result, the context is eleven suppliers." (result marker used for an opening -- wrong function)
You want to connect a tough negotiation to a successful outcome. The best version is:
A "The negotiation was tough. That said, both sides signed." (contrast marker used for a result)
B "The negotiation was tough. As a result, both sides signed in good faith." (correct result marker)
C "The negotiation was tough both sides signed." (no marker, no comma -- unclear)
You are closing the whole story with a sharp recap. You say:
A "To sum up: from eleven suppliers to four, four million in savings, contract signed." (summary marker + synthesis)
B "Moving on, from eleven suppliers to four." (sequence marker used to close -- wrong function)
C "And there were some numbers and it went fine, I think." (no synthesis, no figures)
