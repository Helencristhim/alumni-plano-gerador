Chapter 3

Wish & If Only

The grammar of regret: now, the past, and other people

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I wish I had more time before the launch."

"I wish I had asked for help sooner."

"If only I hadn't ignored my gut feeling."

"I wish investors would decide faster."

MeaningStructureExample
Regret about NOWwish + past simple (were)I wish I had more time. / I wish I were braver.
Regret about the PASTwish / if only + past perfectI wish I had asked sooner.
Wanting OTHERS to changewish + would + base verbI wish they would decide.

"If only" works exactly like "wish", but feels stronger. Use "were" for all persons after wish (I wish I were), and never "would" for your own state.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

I wish I have more time before the launch.
I wish I had more time before the launch.
I wish I would have asked for help sooner.
I wish I had asked for help sooner.
I wish I would be more confident.
I wish I were more confident.

Three rules: a present wish uses the past simple ("I wish I had"); a past regret uses the past perfect ("I wish I had asked"), never "would have"; and for your own state, use "were", never "would".

Looking Back

H
You have come a long way, Eliane. Looking back on the year, is there anything you would do differently?

H

You have grown. The founder who wishes she had known everything is not the one who wins — it is the one who keeps learning.
