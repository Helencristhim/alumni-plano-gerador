Lesson 14 -- Pre-class

Checking In and Speaking Up -- Asking for Clarification in Global Meetings

The class that ends the freezing on a Global call. Three small toolkits keep you in the room: clarify ("Sorry, I didn't catch that. Could you repeat that?", "What do you mean by...?"), hold the floor ("Let me finish, please.") and buy time ("That's a good question. Let me think for a second."). Key words: clarify, concern, wrap up, misunderstanding, interrupt, raise, feedback, summarize. This is functional language — no new grammar — with present continuous (we are changing the plan) and going to (I'm going to follow up) in review. Idiom of the lesson: "Let's take that offline."

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. These are the eight words of a meeting — what you do, and what you ask for on a Global call.

Clarify -- to explain something again so it is clear
"Could you clarify that last number for me?"
Concern -- a worry or a problem you want to talk about
"I have one concern about the timeline."
Wrap up -- to finish a meeting or a topic
"Let's wrap up and agree on the next steps."
Misunderstanding -- when two people understand the same thing in different ways
"Sorry, that was a small misunderstanding."
Interrupt -- to stop someone while they are speaking
"Sorry to interrupt, but I have a quick question."
Raise -- to bring up a point or a question in a meeting
"I would like to raise one point about the budget."
Feedback -- comments about your work, to help you improve
"Thank you for the feedback on my report."
Summarize -- to say the main points again, in a short way
"Let me summarize the three action points."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

It is a Global call, and Marc is talking fast. "We are changing the launch plan, and I am going to send the new figures after the call." Emmanuele misses the last part. The old Emmanuele would stay quiet and understand it later. The new one speaks up: "Sorry, Marc, I didn't catch that. Could you repeat that, please?"

Marc repeats it, and she checks the word: "What do you mean by 'the new figures' — the sales, or the targets?" Then he asks a hard question, and she does not freeze. She buys time: "That's a good question. Let me think for a second." She gives her opinion, and adds a concern about the timeline: "The public market is slower, so I am going to follow up with the access team this week."

Then someone interrupts her. The old Emmanuele would stop. The new one holds the floor: "Sorry, let me just finish my point, please." Nobody minds. In fact, the room respects it. She did not understand every word — but she stayed in the meeting from the first minute to the last.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Three boxes, and the tenses you already know

GRAMMAR

There is no new grammar today — only three ready toolkits, plus two tenses you already have. In a meeting you use the present continuous for what is happening now ("we are changing the plan") and going to for what you will do ("I am going to follow up"). The toolkits are the phrases that keep you in the room when you miss a word.

The toolkitWhen you use itWhat you say
CLARIFYYou miss a word, or a point is not clear"Sorry, I didn't catch that. Could you repeat that?" · "What do you mean by...?" · "Just to clarify..."
HOLD THE FLOORSomeone interrupts you"Let me finish, please." · "Can I just add one thing?"
BUY TIMEYou don't know the answer yet"That's a good question. Let me think for a second."
The one ruleLearn one phrase from each box by heart. Add the soft words — sorry, please, could, just — and you always sound professional, never abrupt.

Common mistake: under pressure, it is easy to cut the words and sound hard: "Repeat!", "What?", "No, I talk!". The message is fine, but the tone sounds rude. The fix is not more grammar — it is the small words. "Repeat!" becomes "Sorry, could you repeat that, please?". "What?" becomes "Sorry, what do you mean by that?". Same idea, but now you sound like the calmest professional in the room.
