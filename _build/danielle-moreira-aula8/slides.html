Expressions

Five for the Review

to take stock of

to fall short of

“We fell short of the response rate I projected in June, and I would rather report that than bury it.”

to build on

to use what already worked as the foundation of the next step

“The focus groups worked. What I propose is to build on them rather than start again.”

Chapter 4: The Code

The Grammar of the Executive Summary

Having reviewed the findings, I would propose... · Given the response rate... · ...leaving us nine points below

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

Having reviewed the findings, I would propose that we build on the focus groups.

The trust index came back at fifty-eight, leaving us nine points below the benchmark.

Asked about the timeline, most of the faculty said exactly the same thing.

Given the response rate, the caveat belongs on the first slide, not the last.

Each of these was two sentences before. Something was deleted from all four — the same two things, every time.

FormWhat it doesExample
Perfect participle
Having + past participle		The action that came first. Packs the entire past into five words.“Having reviewed the findings, I would propose...”
= After I reviewed the findings, I would propose...
Present participle
verb + -ing		The result of what you just said. Almost always after the comma.“...came back at fifty-eight, leaving us nine points below.”
= ...and this left us nine points below.
Past participle
participle alone		Passive: the subject receives the action.“Asked about the timeline, the faculty said...”
= When they were asked about the timeline...
Fixed openers
Given / Based on / Drawing on		Anchors the recommendation in the evidence, before you say what it is.“Given the response rate...” · “Based on the preliminary findings, I am proposing...”
The golden ruleThe participle and the main clause must share the SAME SUBJECT. Whoever performs the participle must be the subject of the sentence that follows. Break this and the sentence does not sound odd in English — it sounds wrong.

What disappeared in all four: the subject of the first clause, and the connector (and / after / because / when). That is the entire trick — and it is why a participle clause buys you back the seconds a senior room will not give you twice.

Register

Narrate, or Summarize

Narrating — the room waits

Every event in the order it happened. Nothing is wrong here except the cost: your eleven minutes.

“First we ran four focus groups, and then we analyzed them, and after that I prepared a proposal, and my proposal is that we should build on the focus groups.”

Summarizing — the room decides

The past goes into a participle clause. The weight stays on the recommendation.

“Having run four focus groups, I would propose that we build on them rather than start again.”

Thirty-one words become fifteen, and not one fact is lost. The first version tells a COO what you did with your autumn. The second tells him what to do on Monday — and that is the only sentence he was ever going to repeat to the board.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Having reviewed the findings, the response rate was disappointing."
"Having reviewed the findings, I found the response rate disappointing."
"Having review the data, I am proposing two quick wins."
"Having reviewed the data, I am proposing two quick wins."

The first error is the dangling participle, and it is the one that will catch you: the sentence says that the response rate reviewed the findings. Ask “who did it?” — the answer must be the subject of the main clause. The second is simpler: having takes a past participle — reviewed, never “review”.
