Lesson 08 -- Pre-class

Midpoint Review -- Consolidating HR Leadership Language for Canada

The midpoint: the lesson where you stop, take stock and PRESENT what you found — without inflating what worked or burying what fell short. All the language of lessons 1 to 7 comes back here as REVIEW (causative passive, cleft, inversion, third conditional, reporting verbs, hedging), inside a single executive presentation. Key words: takeaway, caveat, rationale, trade-off, traction, quick win, roadblock, track record, preliminary, tangible, to take stock of, to fall short of, to build on, to iron out, the jury is still out. New structure: participle clauses — the grammar of the executive summary (Having reviewed the findings... / ...leaving us nine points below the benchmark / Asked about the timeline... / Given the response rate...).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the vocabulary of someone who presents a midpoint review — and survives the questions that come after it.

Takeaway -- the one thing you want the room to still remember tomorrow morning
"If they remember one takeaway from this review, it should be that the culture is not broken -- it is unheard."
Caveat -- the limit you attach to your own claim, before somebody else attaches it for you
"One caveat: these are preliminary numbers, and the November survey may move them."
Rationale -- the reasoning behind a decision, stated out loud so the room can argue with it
"Nobody objected to the plan. They objected to never having been given the rationale for it."
Trade-off -- what you agree to lose in order to gain something you want more
"Every people strategy is a trade-off. A leader who presents one without naming the cost is presenting a wish."
Traction -- the point at which an initiative starts moving without you pushing it
"The mentorship program is finally gaining traction: three teams asked to join without being invited."
Quick win -- a small, fast, visible result that buys you the credit to attempt the slow one
"I need one quick win before December, or nobody will fund the two-year work."
Roadblock -- the obstacle that stops the work, as opposed to the one that merely slows it
"The real roadblock was never the budget. It was that two directors had stopped speaking."
Track record -- the history of what you have actually delivered, which speaks before you do
"In a new country, you have no track record. The first review is where you begin building one."
Preliminary -- true so far, and openly not final yet
"These are preliminary findings. Treating them as conclusions is how a review loses a room."
Tangible -- concrete enough to be counted, shown, or disagreed with
"Give the board one tangible number per slide, or the whole review sounds like a feeling."
To take stock of -- to stop, and honestly assess where you actually are
"Eight lessons in, it is worth taking stock of what has moved and what has not."
To fall short of -- to miss a target you had publicly committed to
"We fell short of the response rate we projected, and I would rather report that than bury it."
To build on -- to use what already worked as the foundation of the next step
"The focus groups worked. What I propose is to build on them rather than start again."
To iron out -- to resolve the small remaining problems in something that basically works
"The framework is sound. We have six weeks to iron out the details before the launch."
The jury is still out -- it is genuinely too early to say, and pretending otherwise costs you credibility
"On whether the new onboarding is working, the jury is still out. Ask me again in March."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Mariana Vidal had twenty-two slides and eleven minutes, and she lost the room on slide three. Having spent six months on the most careful cultural work of her career, she opened the midpoint review by explaining her methodology. By the time she arrived at what she had actually found, the Chief Operating Officer had stopped taking notes. The work was excellent. The review was a failure, and the two facts have nothing to do with each other.

The mistake was not nerves, and it was not her English. It was sequence. A senior room does not want the journey; it wants the takeaway, and it wants it first. Asked afterwards what he had needed, the COO was blunt: one tangible number, the rationale behind her recommendation, and the caveat she was clearly holding back. Given eleven minutes, he said, spend one of them telling me what you found and ten defending it.

She rebuilt the deck in a single evening. The new version opened with a sentence, not a slide: the culture here is not broken, it is unheard, and the response rate is the proof. Then the number, then the trade-off. Based on the preliminary findings, she proposed two quick wins and one long piece of work, naming what each would cost. She said out loud that the survey had fallen short of the target she had promised in June, leaving her with a smaller sample than she had wanted — and that she would rather report that than bury it. On the new onboarding, she said, the jury is still out: ask me again in March.

The COO approved the plan in four minutes. What convinced him was not the confidence. It was the caveat. A leader who names the limit of her own data is a leader whose numbers can be trusted the next time she brings some — and that, more than any single result, is how a track record begins.

1. "Having spent six months on the work, she opened by explaining her methodology." — what is the perfect participle (having + past participle) saying here?
A That both actions happened at the same time: she spent six months while she was opening the presentation.
B That the participle action came FIRST: the six months of work, then the opening. It is how you pack the whole story into one subordinate clause and go straight to the point.
C That she is still spending the six months, because having marks the present continuous.
2. "Asked afterwards what he had needed, the COO was blunt." — why Asked and not Asking?
A Because the subject (the COO) RECEIVES the action: somebody asked HIM. A passive participle = past participle. "Asking" would mean HE was the one asking.
B Because after a comma English always requires a past participle.
C Because "ask" is an irregular verb and does not take an -ing form.
3. According to the text, why exactly did Mariana lose the room on slide three?
A Because her English was not precise enough for a senior Canadian audience.
B Because of sequence: she opened with her methodology instead of her takeaway, and by the time she reached what she had found, the COO had stopped listening.
C Because the cultural work itself was weak, and the room could see it immediately.
4. What actually convinced the COO to approve the plan in four minutes?
A Her confidence, and the fact that she never admitted any weakness in the data.
B The two quick wins, which meant the project would show results before December.
C The caveat — because a leader who names the limit of her own data is a leader whose numbers can be trusted the next time, and that is where a track record begins.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Participle Clauses

GRAMMAR

The grammar of the executive summary: it takes TWO sentences and turns the less important one into a short clause, with no subject and no connector — leaving the main clause with all the weight. It is what separates someone who NARRATES from someone who SUMMARIZES.

FormUseExample
1. Perfect participle
Having + past participle		The action that came FIRST. It packs the whole past into five words.Having reviewed the findings, I would propose two quick wins.
= After I reviewed the findings, I would propose...
2. Present participle
verbo + -ing		The RESULT or consequence of the main clause. It almost always comes AFTER the comma.The index came back at fifty-eight, leaving us nine points below the benchmark.
= ...and this left us nine points below.
3. Past participle
the participle alone		A PASSIVE meaning: something was done TO the subject.Asked about the timeline, most of the faculty said the same thing.
= When they were asked about the timeline...
4. Fixed openings
Given / Based on / Drawing on		The executive opening. It anchors the recommendation in the evidence, before you say what it is.Given the response rate, the caveat belongs on slide one.
Based on the preliminary findings, I am proposing...
The golden ruleThe participle and the main clause must share the SAME SUBJECT. If they do not, the sentence collapses (the classic dangling participle) — and in English it does not sound odd: it sounds wrong.

Watch out (mistake no. 1 — the dangling participle): "Having reviewed the findings, the response rate was disappointing" is WRONG: YOU reviewed the findings, not the response rate. The subject of the main clause has to be the one who performs the participle. Correct: "Having reviewed the findings, I found the response rate disappointing." Foolproof test: ask "who did it?" → the answer has to be the subject of the main clause.

Watch out (mistake no. 2): having takes a PAST PARTICIPLE, never an infinitive or a base form. "Having review the data" → "Having reviewed the data". And the passive participle is the participle alone, without being: "Being asked about the timeline" is possible, but the executive register prefers the dry "Asked about the timeline".

Why this matters to you: you have eleven minutes and a COO who stops taking notes on slide three. The participle clause is the tool that compresses "We reviewed the findings, and after that I prepared a proposal" into "Having reviewed the findings, I would propose..." — and gives your seconds back to the only part the room wants to hear: what you found, and what you are going to do.

Review of lessons 1-7 (today it all comes back): causative passive (We had the survey designed by...), cleft (What the team needs is...), inversion (Never have I seen...), third conditional + modal perfects (If we had asked earlier, we would have known / we should have addressed it), reporting verbs (He acknowledged that... / She agreed to...) and hedging (It would appear that... / To a certain extent...). This is not new content: these are your tools. Today you use them ALL in a single presentation.

Stage 2: Put the Midpoint Review in Order

Order

Put the five moves in the order you will run the midpoint review with the Canadian leadership. Remember Mariana's mistake: the room wants the end first.

?Name the caveat yourself, before anyone else does: what you fell short of, and what the jury is still out on.
?Close by asking for the one decision you actually came for, with a date attached to it.
?Open with the takeaway, in one sentence, before a single slide: the culture is not broken, it is unheard.
?Propose what comes next: two quick wins to build on, one long piece of work, and the trade-off each one costs.
?Put one tangible number on the table, and give the rationale behind it before anybody has to ask.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each real moment of your midpoint review with the Canadian leadership.

You have eleven minutes with the COO. Your first sentence is:
A “Let me start by walking you through the methodology we used and the four phases of the assessment.”
B “The takeaway first: the culture here is not broken, it is unheard — and the response rate is the evidence. Everything else I say today defends that sentence.”
C “Thank you all so much for your time today. I know how busy everyone is at this time of year.”
Which sentence uses the participle clause correctly?
A “Having reviewed the findings, the response rate was disappointing.”
B “Having reviewed the findings, I would propose that we build on the focus groups rather than start again.”
C “Having review the findings, I am proposing two quick wins.”
You promised a seventy percent response rate in June. It came back at thirty-eight. The COO has not asked about it yet. You:
A “One caveat, and I would rather give it to you than have you find it: we fell short of the rate I projected in June. It leaves me a smaller sample, and it is itself a finding about how heard this team feels.”
B Say nothing about it. He has not asked, the work is strong, and the number would only distract from the recommendation.
C “The response rate was low, but honestly that happens with every survey, so I would not read anything into it.”
He asks: “Is the new onboarding working?” You genuinely do not know yet. The most senior answer is:
A “Yes, definitely. Everyone I have spoken to seems very positive about it.”
B “I have no idea. It is impossible to measure something like that.”
C “On that one, the jury is still out. The first cohort finishes in February, so I will have a tangible number for you in March — and not before.”
He pushes back: “Two quick wins? I want the whole framework by Q2.” You say:
A “We can do that, and I would want you to see the trade-off: pulling the framework into Q2 means the baseline gets taken after the change rather than before, which costs us the ability to prove that any of it worked.”
B “That is not possible. Q2 is far too early for something of this size.”
C “Of course, no problem at all. I will have the whole framework ready by Q2.”
