Series
The Diplomat — Season 1, Episode 1 (Netflix)

Kate Wyler spends the whole series doing what you will do in November: walking into a room of senior people, with little time and INCOMPLETE information, and saying what she knows, what she does not know yet, and what she wants them to decide. This is Lesson 8, filmed. Connection to Lesson 8: watch how she separates what is preliminary from what is a conclusion, and how the caveat (“we think, we do not know”) is exactly what makes the room TRUST her — not the other way around.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Write down THREE moments where somebody delivers information with a caveat (“as far as we know...”, “preliminary reports suggest...”) and ONE where somebody over-promises and pays for it later. Then rewrite the over-promiser's line with a caveat and a participle clause.

Podcast
HBR IdeaCast (Harvard Business Review) — any episode on leadership and executive presentation

The corporate English your Canadian room speaks, undiluted: twenty to twenty-five minutes, two executives, zero teaching. It is the natural home of hedging — nobody on this podcast says “this will work”; they say “it would appear that”, “the evidence suggests”, “more often than not”. Connection to Lesson 8: it is Warren's exact register, and the exact vocabulary of your midpoint review (takeaway, trade-off, traction, quick win).

Tip: listen to five minutes of an episode and write down THREE hedging expressions the guest uses — and, next to each one, what the sentence would say WITHOUT the hedge. The gap between the two columns is the distance between sounding junior and sounding senior. Bring it to Lesson 9.

YouTube
Patrick Winston — “How to Speak” (MIT, 60 min — watch the first 15)

The most famous MIT lecture on public speaking, and it opens with exactly the thesis of Lesson 8: start with the promise — what the room will take away — and NEVER with the methodology. It is Mariana Vidal's mistake, explained by an MIT professor fifteen years before you walk into that room. Connection to Lesson 8: his “empowerment promise” is your takeaway in the first sentence, before the first slide.

Tip: watch the first fifteen minutes with English subtitles. Then write YOUR own “empowerment promise” for the November review — ONE sentence, no number, no methodology — and record it out loud five times, until it comes out with no hesitation. It is the first sentence Warren will hear.
