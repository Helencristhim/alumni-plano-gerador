Lesson 08 -- Pre-class

Better, Bigger, Best -- Comparing Results in the Business Review

The business review: not one number, but a comparison. How Brazil compares to the region — bigger or smaller than Mexico, higher or lower growth — which area has the best performance, and where the biggest gap is. Key words: performance, market share, figure, trend, gap, improvement, leader, region, priority, significant. Structures: comparatives (bigger, higher, better than) and superlatives (the biggest, the most important, the best) — one question decides all of them: is the word short or long? Expression of the lesson: "In my opinion..."

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. These are the ten words of a business review — the words you compare with when you present to the Global team.

Performance -- how well a product, a team or a market works and sells
"This quarter, asthma has the best performance."
Market share -- the part of the whole market that one company has
"Our market share in dermatology is bigger than last year."
Figure -- a number in a report or a chart
"Twelve thousand is a big figure for this market."
Trend -- the way the numbers change over time, up or down
"The trend is very positive this year."
Gap -- the difference, or the empty space, between two numbers
"There is a significant gap between the public and the private market."
Improvement -- when something becomes better than before
"We see a clear improvement in the private market."
Leader -- the product, team or country that is number one
"Brazil is the leader in Latin America."
Region -- a large area, like a group of countries
"Latin America is the region with the highest growth."
Priority -- the most important thing, the thing you do first
"Atopic dermatitis is our most important priority."
Significant -- big and important, not small
"This is a significant trend for next year."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

It is the business review, and Marc is on the call. He does not want one figure alone — he wants a comparison. "So, how does Brazil compare to the region?" he asks. Emmanuele opens her scorecard. "In my opinion, Brazil is the leader in Latin America. It is the biggest market, and this year it has the best performance."

"Bigger than Mexico?" — "Yes, bigger than Mexico. But Mexico has a higher growth rate, so the gap between us is smaller than last year." Marc writes it down. "And your most important priority?" — "Atopic dermatitis. The trend is very positive: the numbers are better than last quarter, and it is our biggest opportunity."

Then the difficult part, and she does not hide it. "Our biggest challenge is the public market share. There is a significant gap there. But in my opinion, it is also the best opportunity for next year, because the improvement can be the fastest of all." Marc nods. Bigger is not always better — and she just proved she knows it.

1. Why does Emmanuele say "the biggest market" and not "the most big market"?
A Because "big" is a SHORT word (one syllable), so it changes its end: big → bigger → the biggest.
B Because "market" is a big place.
C Because every superlative in English uses "the most".
2. And why does she say "the most important priority" and not "the importantest priority"?
A Because "priority" is a plural word.
B Because "important" is a LONG word (three syllables), so it takes "the most": the most important.
C Because the sentence is a question.
3. She says the numbers are "better than last quarter". Why "better", and not "gooder"?
A Because "good" is a short word, so it becomes "gooder".
B Because after a number you always say "better".
C Because "good" is irregular: good → better → the best. You learn it by heart. And a comparison always ends with "than".
4. Which comparison is correct?
A "Brazil is more big that Mexico, and it has the most best performance."
B "Brazil is bigger than Mexico, and it has the best performance."
C "Brazil is the bigger than Mexico, and it has more good performance."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- One question decides everything: is the word short or long?

GRAMMAR

Before you compare, count the beats of the adjective. big — one beat, a short word: it changes its end (bigger, the biggest). im-por-tant — three beats, a long word: it takes a helper (more important, the most important). This one question gives you the answer every time.

Type of wordComparative (two things) + thanSuperlative (number one) + the
SHORT word (1 syllable): big, high, low, fast, smalladd -er: bigger than, higher thanthe + -est: the biggest, the highest
LONG word (2+ syllables): important, significant, expensivemore + word: more important thanthe most + word: the most important
IRREGULAR (learn by heart)good → better than · bad → worse thangood → the best · bad → the worst
The two rules you must never breakA comparison always ends with than (never "that"). A superlative always starts with the. And never both roads at once: never "more bigger", never "the most biggest".

Common mistake: "more big" and "the more important" — both are wrong. In your first language one word can work for every case, so it feels natural to put "more" in front of everything. But English has two roads: short words change their end (big → bigger), long words take a helper (important → more important). Never mix them, and never use both (never "more bigger"). And the smallest, most expensive mistake of all: "than" vs "that". A comparison finishes with than — "bigger than Mexico" — never "bigger that Mexico".

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each real moment of the business review with the Global team.

Marc asks: "How does Brazil compare to Mexico in size?" You answer:
A "Brazil is more big than Mexico."
B "Brazil is bigger than Mexico — it is the biggest market in Latin America."
C "Brazil is the bigger that Mexico."
He asks about the growth rate. Mexico is growing faster. You say:
A "Mexico has a more high growth rate."
B "Mexico has a higher growth rate, but the gap is smaller than last year."
C "Mexico is the most fast in growth."
You want to name your number-one priority. The best way is:
A "Atopic dermatitis is our most important priority this quarter."
B "Atopic dermatitis is our importantest priority this quarter."
C "Atopic dermatitis is our the more important priority."
Marc asks which area performs best. Asthma is number one. You report:
A "Asthma has the most best performance."
B "Asthma has the gooder performance of the three."
C "Asthma has the best performance of the three areas."
You want to give your opinion about the trend, with authority but not arrogance. You say:
A "This is the most significant trend, and I am 100% sure of everything."
B "In my opinion, this is the most significant trend for next year."
C "This trend is the more significant of all, believe me."

Reflection
