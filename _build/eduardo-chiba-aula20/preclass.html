Stage 1.1: Communication Audit Vocabulary

Vocabulary

Eight terms every business writer uses. Listen to each one.

Register -- the level of formality you use, chosen to fit the reader
"For a senior partner, raise the register and avoid slang." (Registro / nível de formalidade)
Concise -- short and clear, saying only what is needed
"Keep the email concise: three sentences, not three paragraphs." (Conciso)
Salutation -- the opening greeting of an email or letter
"Match the salutation to the reader: Dear Mr. Costa, not Hi." (Saudação de abertura)
Sign-off -- the polite phrase that closes a message before your name
"End with a clear sign-off: Best regards, Eduardo." (Despedida / fecho)
Attachment -- a document or file sent together with an email
"The full proposal is in the attachment for your review." (Anexo)
Proofread -- to read a text carefully to find and fix mistakes before sending
"Always proofread for typos before you hit send." (Revisar)
Bottom line -- the most important point or result, stated simply
"The bottom line: we should sign by Friday." (Ponto principal)
Persuasive -- written in a way that convinces the reader to agree or act
"A persuasive proposal links each feature to a clear benefit." (Persuasivo)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

Grammar

Before Eduardo sends an important email, he audits it. First he checks the register: for a senior partner he raises it, changing the salutation to "Dear Mr. Costa" and softening the request — "I was wondering if you could send the file." Then he checks the structure: he leads with the bottom line, then points to the attachment. He makes the case persuasive: "This plan will cut your costs and save hours." He keeps it concise, ends with a clear sign-off, and proofreads once more before he hits send.

2. Why does he say "I was wondering if you could"?

B To soften the request for a senior reader.
C To ask for the price.
3. What does he do just before sending?
A He adds more paragraphs.
B He proofreads once more and adds a clear sign-off.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip

Grammar

Uma auditoria de comunicação reúne o Bloco 4 inteiro num só checklist. Ajuste o register com saudação e pedidos suavizados (I was wondering if), lidere com o bottom line, torne a proposta persuasive ligando plano a benefício (will + verbo), e escolha o canal certo. / A communication audit combines the four Block 4 skills into one checklist.

CheckFromExample
The registeremail tone"I was wondering if you could send the file."
The structureexecutive summary"The bottom line is that we should sign by Friday."
The casethe proposal"This plan will cut costs and save hours."
The channeldigital communication"Quick update: deck is ready, no rush."

Key point / Ponto-chave: a auditoria não é uma habilidade nova — é o Bloco 4 inteiro num só checklist. Ajuste o register, lidere com o bottom line, torne a mensagem persuasive, e escolha o canal certo.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best line for each writing moment.

You are emailing a senior partner. Which opening is best?
A "Hey, send me the contract asap, thx."
B "Dear Mr. Costa, I was wondering if you could send the contract."
C "Contract. Now."
A busy executive needs a one-line summary. Which works?
A "The bottom line is that we should sign by Friday."
B "There are many details to read in the report."
C "I will explain everything in a long meeting."
You want a proposal line to persuade. What is best?
A "This plan will cut your costs and save your team hours."
B "This plan has many features."
C "This plan is a plan."

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection
Take one real message you write at work and audit it out loud in English. Set the right register (salutation, softened request, sign-off), lead with the bottom line and keep it concise, make the case persuasive (this plan will...), choose the right channel, and proofread it before you imagine sending. Use audit words like register, attachment, persuasive and proofread.
