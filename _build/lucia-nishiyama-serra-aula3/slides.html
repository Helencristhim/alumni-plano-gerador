Chapter 3: Archive vs Argument

Did vs Have Done

I led in 2015 · I have led ever since

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I led the LATAM team at Microsoft in 2015."

"I have led teams across five continents."

"We deployed the framework last quarter."

"I have carried that lesson into every role since."

Why blue when there is a date, but green when it still matters now?

FormUseExample
Past Simple
verb + -ed / irregular		A finished action with a clear past time — the factI led that team in 2015.
Present Perfect
have / has + past participle		Experience or a result that still matters now, no exact dateI have led teams ever since.
Signal wordsPast: in 2015, last quarter, that year, when I was at Amazon · Present perfect: so far, up to now, since, throughout my career

In an interview: past simple is your archive · present perfect is your argument for today.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I have led the LATAM team in 2015."
"I led the LATAM team in 2015."
"I work in AI safety since 2020."
"I have worked in AI safety since 2020."
"Did you ever lead a global team?"
"Have you ever led a global team?"

A finished time — in 2015, last year — forces the past simple. Since, so far and ever (life experience, no date) invite the present perfect.

The Signal Word Decides

in 2015 · last year → past  |  since · so far → present perfect

"I ___delivered the outcome last year." (deliver)
"So far, I ___have delivered every project on time." (deliver)
"I ___have carried that lesson into every role since." (carry)
"That year, we ___hit every single deadline." (hit)
Chapter 4: The Interview

Meet David

A recruiter for an AI Safety leadership role

Dialogue

The Interview

D
Thanks for making the time, Lucia. I am David, a recruiter for the AI Safety leadership team here. This is a senior role, so let me start simple: walk me through your background.
L
Of course. I have spent almost forty years in technology, and I have led teams on three continents. Most recently, I moved into AI safety, and I have owned that work ever since.
D
Good. Give me one concrete example. Tell me about a time you led a major transformation.
L
In 2015, I led a difficult migration at a large company. We deployed a new security framework across four countries, and we delivered every outcome on time. Since then, I have carried that discipline into every project.
D
I like that. Here is what I care about most in this role: accountability. When something breaks, who owns it?
L
I do. I take full ownership. Over the years, I have learned that a leader who shares the credit must also carry the blame. That is how I have built trust with every stakeholder.
D
That is exactly what we look for. One more thing: why is your past experience relevant to a role that did not exist five years ago?
L
Because the tools change, but the judgment carries over. I have managed risk and aligned people my whole career, and that is precisely what AI safety needs today.
Listening

What a Recruiter Listens For

A recruiter explains the two things he wants to hear. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. In the past simple, what does he want to hear?
The facts: what you actually did — you led a team, you shipped a product, you closed a deal in a specific year.
2. What does the present perfect do, in his words?
It builds the bridge: it shows why that experience still matters, here and today, for this role.
3. What makes him "lean in"?
When a candidate says, "I have carried that lesson into every project since."

Listening 2

A Model Answer

A senior leader answers "walk me through your background." Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Which tense does she use for the facts, and why?
The past simple, because they are finished events: she joined in 2015 and led the migration that year.
2. How does she connect her past to now?
With the present perfect: "since that project, I have owned every rollout I touch."
3. What does she say the difference is?
The past is an archive; the present perfect is an argument for who she is today.
