Chapter 3 -- The Polite Question Code

Grammar Discovery

Indirect questions -- asking politely in a meeting

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

What are the orange words? After them, does the word order change?

Could you tell me how the testing is going?

I would like to know whether the budget is approved.

Do you know when we will get the sign-off?

Direct: "How is the testing going?" (less formal)

Grammar Rule

Indirect Questions

OpenerThenExample
Could you tell me + question wordsubject + verb (normal order)"...how the testing is going."
Do you know + question wordno do/does/did inside"...when we will get the sign-off."
I would like to know + if / whetherfor yes/no questions"...whether the budget is approved."
Direct questionverb before subject (less formal)"How is the testing going?"

Key idea: open with Could you tell me, Do you know, or I would like to know, and then use normal word order (subject + verb). Do not use do/does/did inside the question. For yes/no questions, use if or whether. Indirect questions sound more polite and professional when you lead a meeting.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"Could you tell me where is the report?"
"Could you tell me where the report is?"

After the opener, use normal word order (subject + verb) and no do/does/did. For yes/no, use if or whether.

Grammar Practice

Make It Polite

"Could you tell me _____how the testing is going?" (from: How is the testing going?)
"Do you know _____when the call starts?" (from: When does the call start?)
"I would like to know _____whether the budget is approved." (yes/no: Is the budget approved?)
"Could you tell me _____where the report is?" (from: Where is the report?)

Dialogue

Leading the Meeting

N

"Thanks for joining. Let me start with a quick summary, then we will go through the agenda. First agenda item: could you tell me how the testing is going?"

T

"It is going well. But before that, what about the new vendor contract?"

N

"That is a good point, but it is a little off-topic for now. Let me defer it and log it as a follow-up, so we can steer back to the testing update first."

T

"Fair enough. On testing, then -- do you know exactly how much it has cost so far?"

N

"I do not have the exact figure to hand, so I would rather not guess. Let me double-check it after the call and send it to you today."

N

"Let us close out. We have two follow-ups: I will double-check the testing cost, and we will revisit the vendor contract next week. Thank you all."
