Lesson 17 -- Pre-class

Checking In, Speaking Up -- Hotels, Reservations, and Handling Problems Confidently

The front desk of a New York hotel: your reservation is wrong, the Wi-Fi code fails, and the checkout time is off -- and you fix all three without raising your voice. Key words: to apologize, inconvenience, to accommodate, courtesy, to resolve, discrepancy, to escalate, reasonable. Grammar: modal verbs for polite requests and advice -- Could you upgrade my room?, Would it be possible to check in early?, You should speak to the manager. -- firm about the problem, kind to the person.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Take your time -- listen twice, then say it out loud. These are the eight words that live at a front desk, in the space between a problem and its solution.

To apologize -- to say you are sorry for a mistake or a problem
"The manager called to apologize for the mix-up with my room."
Inconvenience -- trouble or difficulty caused to someone
"We are sorry for the inconvenience, and we will fix it right away."
To accommodate -- to give a guest what they need or ask for
"Could you accommodate an early check-in, please?"
Courtesy -- polite behavior, or a kind gesture offered for free
"As a courtesy, the hotel added a free breakfast."
To resolve -- to find a solution to a problem
"I am sure we can resolve this here at the front desk."
Discrepancy -- a difference between two things that should match, like a bill
"There is a discrepancy on my bill -- a charge I do not recognize."
To escalate -- to take a problem to someone with more authority, like a manager
"If the clerk cannot help, I can escalate it to the duty manager."
Reasonable -- fair and sensible
"A free breakfast sounds like a reasonable way to fix it."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

Lucimara arrives at the Harborview Hotel in New York after a long flight. She booked a superior room with an ocean view, but the clerk offers her a standard room on the ground floor. A year ago she would have accepted it in silence. Tonight she speaks up — firmly, and politely.

"There seems to be a discrepancy," she says. "I booked a superior room. Would it be possible to move me?" Notice the little word that does all the work: would. It turns a demand into a request the clerk wants to say yes to. She does not attack him; she names the problem and asks for the fix.

The clerk checks and finds she is right. "I apologize for the inconvenience," he says. "Could I offer you a complimentary breakfast as a courtesy?" That sounds reasonable, and she accepts. He resolves the room in two minutes.

If the clerk had not been able to help, she knew what to do: "You should ask for the manager," a friend once told her. When a problem is too big for the front desk, you escalate it — calmly, and with the same three small words: could, would, and should.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Modal Verbs (could, would, should)

GRAMMAR

A modal verb softens your words: you sound firm about the problem, but kind to the person. Use could and would for polite requests, and should for advice.

ModalUse it forExample
could you...?a polite request (softer than can)Could you help me, please?
would it be possible to...?a very polite requestWould it be possible to move me?
you should...advice (a recommendation)You should ask for the manager.
The one ideaThe modal makes you firm about the problem and kind to the person — and that is what gets a yes.

Watch out -- the classic mistakes: after a modal, use the base verb with no to — you should speak, never "you should to speak". Use only one modal at a time — not "Would you can help me?" but Could you help me?. And a modal never changes with he/she: she should ask, not "she shoulds ask".

Stage 2: The Four Moves, in Order

Order

This is how you handle a hotel problem well -- in the order it actually happens, from naming it to accepting the solution.

?If the clerk cannot help, escalate: "Could I speak with the duty manager, please?"
?Name the problem: "There seems to be a discrepancy -- I booked a superior room."
?Accept a fair solution: "A free breakfast sounds reasonable -- thank you."
?Request the fix politely: "Would it be possible to move me to the room I booked?"
?Confirm and move on: "Thank you for resolving it so quickly."
