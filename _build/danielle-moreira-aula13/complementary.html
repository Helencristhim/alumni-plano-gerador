Series
Ted Lasso — Season 1, official trailer & series (Apple TV+)

This is your first month in Canada, played for comedy. An American coach lands inside a British football club and gets every unwritten rule wrong — he mistakes reserve for coldness, misses the understatement, and only wins the room once he stops resisting the culture and starts reading it. Watch how the newcomers who complain lose the room, and how Ted himself builds rapport through relentless small talk. Connection to Lesson 13: the whole show is the difference between “this place is wrong” and “I wish it worked differently — and here is what I am doing meanwhile”.

Tip: watch the trailer here, then find the full first episode on Apple TV+. As you watch, write TWO sentences with “I wish” from Ted’s point of view — one about the present (“I wish the team trusted me”) and one regret (“I wish I had understood the sarcasm sooner”).

Podcast
WorkLife with Adam Grant — “Authenticity is a double-edged sword”

Organizational psychologist Adam Grant examines what happens when “just be yourself” collides with a culture that expects something different — exactly your dilemma in a Canadian office where directness reads as rude and silence reads as consent. He makes the case for adapting how you present without losing who you are: the professional version of code-switching. Connection to Lesson 13: it is the argument behind the whole lesson — you can wish the rules were different and still adapt to them, and that is not fakeness, it is leadership.

Tip: listen once with no pauses. Then record ninety seconds answering: “What is one unwritten rule of the Canadian office you wish you had understood sooner?” Use “if only” + past perfect at least once, and the words understatement, to read the room and reciprocity.

YouTube
Pellegrino Riccardi — “Cross cultural communication” (TEDxBergen, 20 min)

A funny, precise talk on why every culture runs on invisible rules that feel like “just common sense” to the people inside it — and why a newcomer only sees them by breaking them. Riccardi lived the exact move you are about to make, and he explains directness, small talk, punctuality and formality as CHOICES a culture makes, not right-and-wrong. Connection to Lesson 13: it gives you the calm, non-judgmental frame for the whole lesson — the rules are not wrong, they are just not yours yet, and adapting to them is the work.

Tip: watch it twice. On the second pass, list three unwritten rules of the Canadian office and, for each, write the “wish + own + invite” reframe: name what you wish were different, what you are already doing about it, and how you would invite the other person — then bring it to Lesson 14.
