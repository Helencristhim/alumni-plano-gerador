Lesson 13 -- Pre-class

The Unwritten Rules -- Rapport and Etiquette in the Canadian Office

Decoding the etiquette no document gives you: the small talk that has to come before business, the flat, egalitarian way people speak to their VP, the punctuality that is expected not admired, the understatement that hides a real objection. And the grammar to voice your own adaptation without sounding like you are complaining. Key words: rapport, small talk, egalitarian, cordiality, punctuality, formality, understatement, code-switching, etiquette, reciprocity, to break the ice, to read the room, to hit it off, to save face, the unwritten rules. Structure: wish / if only — wish + past simple (I wish the team made...), wish + past perfect (I wish I had asked...), wish + would (I wish people wouldn't...), if only for emphasis. The verb steps back in time because the sentence admits: this is not how things are, this is how I would want them.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the vocabulary of the office culture nobody writes into a handbook.

Rapport -- an easy, trusting connection built with someone through repeated warm contact
"In Brazil the rapport came fast; here it is built more slowly, mostly in the small talk before the meeting starts."
Small talk -- light, informal conversation that builds a connection before the real business starts
"The six minutes of small talk are not filler here; they are how the team warms up and how trust is earned."
Egalitarian -- treating everyone as equals, with little visible distance between senior and junior people
"The office is strikingly egalitarian: the VP is on a first-name basis with everyone and lines up for coffee like the rest of us."
Cordiality -- friendly, polite warmth in professional dealings, without being close friends
"The cordiality is real but measured; people are warm to you long before they are actually close to you."
Punctuality -- the habit of starting and finishing exactly on time
"Punctuality is not a virtue you get credit for here; it is the baseline, and arriving late reads as disrespect."
Formality -- the degree of official, reserved behavior a setting expects, in titles, dress and tone
"I arrived with Brazilian meeting formality and here it created the opposite of respect, a distance I did not intend."
Understatement -- deliberately describing something as smaller or milder than it really is
"'It's a bit of a concern' was an understatement; it meant the whole plan was in trouble, and I read it too literally."
Code-switching -- adjusting how you speak or behave to fit a different cultural setting
"I already code-switch between Portuguese and English; now I am learning to code-switch between two office cultures."
Etiquette -- the unwritten code of polite behavior expected in a particular group
"There is a whole etiquette here that no onboarding document mentions, and breaking it quietly costs you."
Reciprocity -- the give-and-take of trust and favors that keeps a relationship balanced
"The reciprocity is real: invest in the small talk first, and people invest back in you."
To break the ice -- to ease the initial awkwardness when people do not yet know each other
"A question about the weekend is how you break the ice here, and the meeting goes better for it."
To read the room -- to sense the unspoken mood and dynamics of a group before you act
"I have to read the room, not just the words, because the real objection is rarely said out loud."
To hit it off -- to form a warm rapport with someone very quickly
"I hit it off with the operations lead over coffee, and that one conversation opened three doors."
To save face -- to protect your own or someone else's dignity in front of others
"I raised the problem privately so the coordinator could save face, and she fixed it the same day."
The unwritten rules -- the implicit norms everyone is expected to follow without being told
"The unwritten rules are where the real culture lives, and they are exactly what no document ever gives you."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

On her first Monday in the Toronto office, Danielle walked into the nine o'clock meeting with her agenda open and ready. Nobody else opened one. For six minutes the room talked about the weekend, the snow, someone's dog. She waited, a little impatient, and then did the efficient thing: she started on item one. She could feel the temperature of the room drop. It took her three days and a quiet word from Nathan to understand why. In Brazil, getting straight to business is a way of respecting people's time. Here, skipping the small talk does not read as focused. It reads as cold, and the rapport she needed was being built in exactly the minutes she had tried to skip.

There is a way to talk about a frustration like that which keeps you inside the culture instead of standing outside it, judging — and it is a grammar. She did not say “these meetings are inefficient”. She said, “I wish the meetings got to the point a little faster — but the small talk is where the trust is built, so I'm learning to break the ice first.” Notice the verb. She means now, yet the verb is got, in the past. That backward step is the whole point: it is English admitting this is not how things are; this is how I would want them. And by the time she adds what she is already doing about it, she is no longer a newcomer complaining. She is a leader adapting.

The etiquette kept surprising her. The office was strikingly egalitarian — the VP went by her first name and stood in the coffee line like everyone else — so the formality Danielle had arrived with created distance, not respect. And the disagreement was almost never on the table. When a colleague called her plan “interesting” and “not quite there yet”, that was an understatement: a real objection wearing a polite coat. “If only I had read the room,” she told Nathan afterwards, “I would have caught it.” The regret takes had read, the past perfect, because she is looking back and wanting a different past — and naming a mistake that cleanly, without drowning in it, is one of the most senior things a leader can do.

The rule that took the longest to trust was reciprocity. The cordiality was real but measured; people were warm long before they were close. And there was one thing she wanted other people to change, not just survive: “I wish people wouldn't read my silence as agreement.” When she went quiet, she was weighing something, not consenting. So she began saying “let me think on that out loud” — it let everyone save face and it kept things clear. None of this meant she was becoming someone else. She was code-switching, the same way she already switched between Portuguese and English. And Nathan was right: within a month, she had hit it off with the team, because she had learned the one thing no handbook teaches — that the culture lives in the unwritten rules, and you read them by wishing out loud what you would change, and adapting while you do.

1. Why does “I wish the meetings got to the point faster” use “got” and not “get”, even though she means now?
A Because the meetings got faster in the past and are slow again now.
B Because after “wish”, a present situation you want to be different steps back into the past simple. The backward verb is English admitting this is not reality — it is what she would want.
C Because “wish” can only be followed by an irregular verb.
2. Mistake number one of this lesson: why is “I wish I would have asked about the etiquette” wrong?
A A regret about the past takes the past perfect: “I wish I had asked”. “Would have asked” is the classic error — save “would” for a behavior in others you want changed.
B Because “etiquette” cannot be the object of “ask about”.
C Because a regret must always use the present perfect, “have asked”.
3. Mistake number two: why is “I wish people will stop reading my silence as agreement” wrong?
A Because “silence” is uncountable and cannot be read.
B When you want SOMEONE ELSE to change a behavior, wish takes would, not “will”: “I wish people wouldn't read my silence as agreement.”
C Because the sentence should be in the passive voice.
4. What did the colleague actually mean by calling the plan “interesting” and “not quite there yet”?
A That the plan was genuinely intriguing and almost finished.
B It was an understatement — a real objection said softly. In this culture the disagreement is often in the room, not on the table, so she had to read the room, not the words.
C That the plan had already been approved by the VP.
5. What is the difference between the newcomer who resists a culture and the leader who adapts to it?
A The leader hides every frustration and pretends everything is fine.
B The newcomer says “this is wrong” and stands outside judging. The leader says “I wish it worked differently, and here is what I am doing meanwhile” — same frustration, but inside the culture, working with it.
C The leader simply imposes the Brazilian way until the team adjusts.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Wish & If Only

GRAMMAR

“Wish” and “if only” let you name a frustration precisely without complaining. The trick is that the verb after them steps BACKWARD in time — that backward step is the language marking “this is not how things are”. Which tense you step back to depends on what you mean.

You meanFormExample (your Canadian office)
1. A present situation you want to be differentwish + past simpleI wish the team made more small talk.
2. Same, more formal (all persons)wish + wereI wish I were further along with the etiquette.
3. A regret about the pastwish + past perfect (had + p.p.)I wish I had asked about the etiquette on day one.
4. You want someone else to change a behaviorwish + would + base verbI wish people wouldn't read my silence as agreement.
5. Emphatic wish or regretif only + the same tense rules: If only I had read the room.

Watch out (mistake number 1): after “wish”, a present you want changed goes into the past simple. “I wish I know the rules” is WRONG → “I wish I knew the rules”. Portuguese uses a present/subjunctive here, and translating literally keeps the verb in the present. English needs the backward step.

Watch out (mistake number 2): a past regret takes the past perfect, never “would have”. “I wish I would have asked” → “I wish I had asked”. Keep would for a behavior in OTHERS you want changed — and there it is “would”, never “will”: “I wish they would listen”.

Why this matters to you: “This office is wrong” puts you outside the culture, judging it — and no one lets an outsider change anything. “I wish it worked differently, and here is what I am doing meanwhile” puts you inside it, adapting. Same frustration, opposite impression. The backward-stepping verb is what signals you know it is your wish, not their failing.

The three-move reframe: WISH (name the frustration, verb in the past) → OWN (what you are already doing about it) → INVITE (open the door without imposing your norm). Skip the middle move and it is a complaint; skip the last and it is a demand.

Stage 2: Put the Reframe in Order

Order

Put the five moves in the order that turns a frustration about the Canadian office into a leader's adaptation.

?Own the adaptation you are already making: so I am learning to break the ice first and treat the small talk as where the rapport is built.
?Close on reciprocity, not resentment: the more I invest in the culture first, the more the team invests back, and I am code-switching into it, not resisting it.
?Name the frustration as a wish, verb stepped back in time: I wish the meetings got to the point a little faster.
?Invite the other person without imposing your norm: and when you do have a real concern, I would genuinely rather you told me plainly.
?Acknowledge the reason the rule exists here: but the small talk is not filler, it is how this team builds trust.
