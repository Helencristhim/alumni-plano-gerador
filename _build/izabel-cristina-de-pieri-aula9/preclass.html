Aula 09 -- Pre-class

The What-If Room -- Weighing Hypotheticals with the Second Conditional

Thinking in hypotheticals: what AVRT would do if an unlikely scenario happened. Key words: trade-off, bottleneck, overhead, incentive, worst-case scenario, hedge, feasible, leverage, concession, bargaining chip, undercut, walk away. Structure: the second conditional -- if + past simple, would/could + base verb -- for unreal, hypothetical situations (If we had more capacity, we would take the contract).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Trade-off -- a balance where you give up one thing to gain another (compromisso / troca)
"Last class you planned for likely conditions. Today, every option is a trade-off between speed and cost."
Bottleneck -- a point that slows everything down and limits output (gargalo)
"If we took the whole contract, it would create a bottleneck in production."
Overhead -- the ongoing fixed costs of running a business (custos fixos / despesas gerais)
"If we opened a second plant, our overhead would rise sharply."
Incentive -- something that motivates a person to act (incentivo)
"If we offered a small incentive, the buyer would commit sooner."
Worst-case scenario -- the most unfavorable possible outcome (pior cenário possível)
"In the worst-case scenario, we would lose our biggest account."
Hedge -- to protect yourself against loss or risk (proteger-se / mitigar risco)
"I would hedge: take half the volume and keep the rest of our capacity free."
Feasible -- possible to do successfully; realistic (viável / factível)
"A second plant would only be feasible if the volume held for two years."
Leverage -- the power to influence a negotiation in your favor (poder de barganha / alavancagem)
"If I sold now, I would lose my leverage over the roadmap."
Concession -- something you give up to reach an agreement (concessão)
"If they made one concession on delivery, I would sign."
Bargaining chip -- something valuable you use to gain an advantage in a deal (moeda de troca / trunfo)
"Our lead time would be my strongest bargaining chip."
Undercut -- to sell at a lower price than a competitor (praticar preço mais baixo que)
"If a rival undercut us, we would not follow them down."
Walk away -- to leave a negotiation without making a deal (sair da mesa / desistir do acordo)
"If the terms were not right, I would walk away."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Andy opens the strategy session with a what-if drill. "Suppose that global buyer offered you the big contract," he says. Izabel is careful. "If we took the whole contract, it would create a bottleneck in production," she explains. "But if they made one concession on delivery, I would sign for half the volume. Our lead time would be my strongest bargaining chip." On competition, she is just as clear: "If a rival undercut us, we would not follow them down. If we cut our price to match, our overhead would eat the margin, so we would compete on quality." And the acquisition offer? "If I were in their position, I would wait. Every option is a trade-off. If the terms were not right, I would walk away."

1. "If we took the whole contract, it would create a bottleneck." Is Izabel describing something real or hypothetical?
A Something real that is happening now.
B A hypothetical, unreal situation (second conditional: if + past simple, would + base).
C A finished action in the past.
2. Look at "If a rival undercut us, we would not follow." Why "would" and not "will"?
A The situation is imagined / unlikely, so the result uses "would + base verb".
B Because "will" is never correct in English.
C Because "follow" cannot go with "will".
3. Izabel says "If I were in their position..." Why "were" and not "was"?
A Because "I was" is grammatically impossible anywhere.
B In hypotheticals we use "were" for every subject (If I were, if he were).
C Because the sentence is about the past.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Second Conditional

GRAMMAR

The hypothetical conditional (explicação em inglês e português).

FormUse / UsoExample
Afirmativa: If + past simple, would + baseSituação hipotética / irreal no presente ou futuroIf we had more capacity, we would take it.
Negativa: ..., would not (wouldn't) + baseResultado imaginado que NÃO aconteceriaIf a rival undercut us, we would not follow.
Interrogativa: What would you do if...?Perguntar sobre um cenário hipotéticoWhat would you do if they offered more?
Verbo "be": were p/ todosHipotético formal: use "were", não "was"If I were you, I would wait.
Cuidado / Watch outNUNCA use "would" depois de "if": errado "If we would have..." → certo "If we had...". O resultado usa would, não will.
