Stakeholder -- anyone with an interest in the outcome of the project

Compliance -- doing exactly what a standard or a contract requires
"Compliance with the European standard is mandatory."
Inspection -- a formal check to confirm quality before you accept the work
"The inspection takes place at the factory."
Specification -- the written document that defines what the equipment must do
"The specification says the response time is two seconds."
Scope -- everything the project includes, and nothing more
"That request is outside the scope of the contract."
Deadline -- the date by which the work must be finished
"The supplier missed the deadline again."
To oversee -- to be in charge of a process and supervise it
"I oversee the commissioning of the signaling system."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

João Guilherme is a signaling engineer at Motiva. He oversees the commissioning of the signaling system on two metro lines, and he runs a weekly status meeting with the suppliers. "I have worked in railway signaling for eight years," he says, "and I have been working with German, French and Chinese suppliers since 2019." In 2023, he spent two months in San Francisco, where he commissioned equipment in the field. Today his week is a schedule: he reviews the specification, he prepares the factory inspection, and he checks compliance with the European standard, because compliance is mandatory. The next milestone is three weeks away and the test report, the first deliverable, has not arrived yet. The team has been waiting for the revised specification since March, the deadline is fixed, and the extra training the client asked for is outside the scope. Every stakeholder wants a schedule they can trust.

1. Why do we say "I have worked in railway signaling for eight years" and not "I work in railway signaling for eight years"?
A It started in the past and it is still true today — present perfect with for.
B The action is over and it has no connection with the present.
C It is a routine he repeats every week.
2. "In 2023, he spent two months in San Francisco" takes the past simple because:
A The experience is still going on today.
B It is a closed period, inside a finished time frame (in 2023).
C It is happening right now.
3. "The team has been waiting for the revised specification since March." What does this sentence tell you?
A The team waited in March and the document has already arrived.
B The wait started in March and it is STILL going on — the document has not arrived.
C The team will start waiting in March.
4. Which sentence about his current role is correct?
A "He is overseeing the commissioning since eight years."
B "He oversees the commissioning and runs a weekly status meeting."
C "He has overseen the commissioning in 2023."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Present Simple vs Present Perfect vs Present Perfect Continuous vs Past Simple

GRAMMAR

How to tell your career story with the right tense — the very thing every interviewer and every supplier tests in the first two minutes of a call.

FormUseExample
Present Simple
I / you / we + verb (he/she + s)		Current role and routine.I oversee the commissioning.
Present Perfect
have / has + past participle		Experience and duration up to now (for / since).I have worked in signaling for eight years.
Present Perfect Continuous
have been + verb-ing		An activity that started in the past and is still going on right now.I have been working with German suppliers since 2019.
Past Simple
verb + -ed / irregular		Finished action, finished time frame (in 2023).I spent two months in San Francisco in 2023.
Negative (present perfect)have / has + not + past participle. In speech, contracted: haven't.We haven't received the specification.
QuestionHow long + have + subject + past participle?How long have you been in signaling?
for vs sincefor + a period of time (for eight years) · since + a starting point (since 2019)

Watch out — the classic Brazilian trap: your first language expresses duration up to now with a PRESENT tense verb, so "I am an engineer for eight years" and "I work with Germans since 2019" feel natural. Both are wrong. In English, duration up to now never takes the present simple: say "I have been an engineer for eight years" (in speech, I've been) and "I have been working with German suppliers since 2019".
