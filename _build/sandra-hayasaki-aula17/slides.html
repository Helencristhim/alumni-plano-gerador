Chapter 3 -- The Pattern

I Wish + Past Simple

For wishes about the present -- and "if only" for stronger feelings

Listen and Notice

What Do the Orange Words Share?

Each sentence is a wish about now. Notice the two orange parts.

"I wish I had more time."

"I wish I lived closer to the sea."

"I wish I knew more French."

"If only I were a better cook."

The Rule

Wishes About Now

PartFormExample
The wishI wish + subject + past verb"I wish I had more time."
Meaningpast form, but about now"I wish I lived by the sea." (I do not, now)
"to be"use were for all people"I wish I were a better cook." (not "was")
"If only"same form, stronger feeling"If only the days were longer."

Tip: The past verb does NOT mean the past. It means a wish about now. Compare: If I had time, I would travel (imagining a result) vs I wish I had time (the wish itself).

Common Mistake

Past Verb, Not Present

"I wish I have more time." / "I wish I was a better cook."
"I wish I had more time." / "I wish I were a better cook."

After I wish, use a past verb, even though you mean now. For to be, always use were.

1. What does Sandra wish about learning English?

She wishes she had started learning English much earlier. She sometimes wonders how it would feel to be fluent already.

2. What does she long for, and how does she feel about her life?

She longs for the sound of water and wishes she lived closer to the sea. Still, she is content; her life is good and full, and her wishes are gentle, not heavy regrets.

She would read every book on her shelf. Since the days are not longer, she simply makes the most of each evening, one page at a time.
