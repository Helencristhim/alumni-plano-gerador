Comparatives and superlatives — 6 Minute Grammar (BBC Learning English)

Two teachers show exactly the grammar of Lesson 6: how to compare two things. They explain when a short adjective takes -er (cheap → cheaper) and when a long adjective takes more (more expensive) — and the irregular ones like good → better. Clear, slow British English, six minutes.

Tip: watch once. Then write three sentences about your two quotes: "Riverport is cheaper than Grainline", "Grainline's delivery is faster", "The total is more important". Say each one out loud.

Everyday Grammar: Comparatives and Superlatives — VOA Learning English

The exact grammar of Lesson 6, in slow American English. The lesson shows the rule you use every time you compare two prices: short adjectives add -er, and words with more syllables take more. There is a short video and the full text below it, so you can read and listen together.

Tip: read the text and find five comparatives. For each one, decide: is it short (-er) or long (more)? Then use it in a sentence about a quote, and say it out loud.

Comparative Adjectives — engVid (Emma)

The teacher Emma builds the comparative step by step, with many examples: bigger, cheaper, more expensive, better. She shows the small spelling changes (big → bigger) and the common mistakes to avoid — "more cheap" and "expensiver" — the same mistakes from your class.

Tip: after the video, compare your two quotes in four sentences: the price, the delivery, the payment and the total. Read them out loud, as if you were on the phone with the supplier.
