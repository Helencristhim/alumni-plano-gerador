Chapter 4: Mixed Conditionals

If I had taken it, I would not be here...

the grammar that connects two times

Discovery

Listen and Notice

"If I had taken the safe job, I would not be here now."

"If we hadn't played the long game, we would not have this reputation today."

"If she weren't so honest, she would have hidden the mistake."

"If those choices had been different, everything would be different now."

"If I didn't believe in slow growth, I would never have turned it down."

TypeIf-clause (condition)Main clause (result)
Past → PresentIf + past perfect (had taken)would + base verb (would not be) — a past condition, a result now
Present → PastIf + past simple / were (weren't)would have + past participle (would have hidden) — a present condition, a past result

Mixed conditionals join two different times, so the two halves use different tenses. Never put "would" in the if-clause: say "If I had known", not "If I would have known".

Practice

Language Focus

Name the Half

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

If I would have listened to them, I would be somewhere else now.
If I had listened to them, I would be somewhere else now.
If we had started earlier, we would have been bigger today.
If we had started earlier, we would be bigger today.
If she didn't care so much, she wouldn't build the clinic back then.
If she didn't care so much, she would never have built the clinic back then.

A mixed conditional joins two different times. Never use "would" in the if-clause -- use the past perfect (had listened). For a PRESENT result, use would + base verb (would be), not would have. For a PAST result of a present condition, use would have + past participle (would have built).

Dialogue

The Advisor's Reflection

A
Eliane, I've been thinking about the early days. Do you ever wonder where you would be if you had taken that hospital job?
E
All the time. If I had taken it, I would not be running this clinic now. That refusal was the defining moment.
A
It really was the pivotal one. Half the room thought you were making a mistake.
E
I know. But that decision became the cornerstone the whole practice was built on.
A
And the course correction in year three? Closing that service could not have been easy.
E
It was hard-won. But if I weren't willing to admit mistakes early, I would have carried that failure for years.
A
One choice, and the ripple effect on everything after it -- the people, the research, the reputation.
E
Exactly. I played the long game, and now the work has come full circle.
A
That is the real legacy, Eliane. Not the building -- the decisions that still shape everyone here.
