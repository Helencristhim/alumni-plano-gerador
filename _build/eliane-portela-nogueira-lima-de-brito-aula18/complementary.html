Film
The Family Man (2000)

A Wall Street executive is shown the alternate present he would be living if he had made a different choice years earlier. It is a whole film built on one mixed conditional: "If I had stayed, I would be a different person now." Watch how a single formative, pivotal decision has a ripple effect on every part of the present -- exactly the grammar and the idea at the heart of Lesson 18.

Tip: after the film, say three mixed conditionals about the main character ("If he had stayed, he would be..."), then do the same for one decision in your own career.

Podcast
Choiceology with Katy Milkman

A Charles Schwab podcast about the decisions that shape our lives -- exactly this lesson's theme. Each episode tells the true story of a high-stakes choice and traces its ripple effect and knock-on effect on everything that followed. Listen for how the hosts frame decisions the way you learned today: the formative, pivotal moment, the hard-won course correction, and the long game that brings a story full circle.

Tip: pick one episode and note two mixed conditionals you could say about the decision-maker ("If they had chosen differently, they would be...").

YouTube
BBC Learning English -- "Grammar: Mixing conditionals" (English Masterclass)

A clear masterclass on exactly this lesson's key grammar: how to mix conditionals across two different times -- a past condition with a present result, and a present condition with a past result. It shows the forms side by side and the classic mistake of putting "would" in the if-clause. Direct connection: the grammar that lets you say how a past decision still shapes you today.

Tip: pause after each pattern and say one sentence about your own career using it, out loud -- one past-to-present, one present-to-past.
