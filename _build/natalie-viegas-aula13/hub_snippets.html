Negotiation Basics -- Finding Common Ground

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary

Vocabulary

These are the key words of negotiation. Listen to each one and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Leverage -- an advantage that gives you power in a negotiation
"Our early delivery gives us real leverage." (Vantagem / poder de barganha)
Trade-off -- a balance where you accept less of one thing for more of another
"A longer contract for a lower price is a fair trade-off." (Troca / contrapartida)
Bottom line -- the lowest price or limit you are willing to accept
"Twelve percent is our bottom line; we cannot go lower." (Limite mínimo)
Terms -- the conditions that both sides agree to in a deal
"We accepted the terms and signed the deal." (Condições / termos)
Concession -- something you give up in order to move closer to an agreement
"I made a small concession on the price." (Concessão)
Counteroffer -- a new offer you make in response to the other side's offer
"We rejected their first price and made a counteroffer." (Contraproposta)
Deadlock -- a situation where neither side will move and the talks are stuck
"We almost reached a deadlock over the timeline." (Impasse)
Walk away -- to leave a negotiation without reaching an agreement
"If the terms are bad, we are ready to walk away." (Desistir / sair sem acordo)
Settle -- to finally agree on something after discussion
"We settled on a fair number for both sides." (Fechar acordo)
Win-win -- a result in which both sides get something they value
"A longer contract was a true win-win for both companies." (Ganha-ganha)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Natalie is preparing for a tough negotiation with a government client. Their main concern is the price, so the bottom line will be hard. But Natalie has one big advantage: her company is the only vendor that can deliver on time, and that is real leverage. She plans a smart trade-off. "If they asked for a lower price, I would offer a longer contract," she thinks. She knows the rule: never make a concession without getting something back. If the talks reached a deadlock, she would suggest a short break instead of choosing to walk away. Her goal is not to win everything; it is to find common ground and settle on a deal that feels like a win-win for both sides.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Second Conditional

GRAMMAR

O segundo condicional fala de situações imaginárias ou hipotéticas: if + passado simples + would + verbo base. É a linguagem da negociação porque soa mais educada e estratégica. / The second conditional talks about imaginary or hypothetical situations: if + past simple, would + base verb.

FormExample (EN)Tradução (PT)
Affirmative"If they asked, we would offer more time."Se eles pedissem, nós ofereceríamos mais tempo.
Negative"If we lowered it, we would not make a profit."Se baixássemos, não teríamos lucro.
Advice (were)"If I were you, I would make a counteroffer."Se eu fosse você, eu faria uma contraproposta.
Question"What would you do if the talks reached a deadlock?"O que você faria se as conversas chegassem a um impasse?

Key point / Ponto-chave: nunca use would depois de if — a parte do "if" leva o passado simples (asked, lowered, found), e só a parte do resultado usa would + verbo base. Depois de "if", use were para todos os sujeitos: "If I were you." Compare com o primeiro condicional (real): "If they ask, we will offer" (possível) vs "If they asked, we would offer" (imaginário). / Never use would after if; use the past simple in the if-clause and were for all subjects.
