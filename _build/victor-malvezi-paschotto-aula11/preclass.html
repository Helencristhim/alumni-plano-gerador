Aula 11 -- Pre-class

Presenting to the Board -- Slides & Stories

Tell the growth story to the board: set the backdrop, build to a turning point, switch to the dramatic present for the key moment, signpost every step, and land one clear takeaway. New words: signpost, takeaway, walk through, compelling, backdrop, storyline, turning point, drill down, headline, land, flag, frame. New structure: narrative tenses (past perfect for the backstory, past continuous for the scene, past simple for the events) plus the dramatic / historic present to make one moment vivid.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

The language of presenting a story to a board. Tap Listen to hear each word.

Signpost -- to tell your audience clearly where the talk is going next
"Let me signpost the plan: headline first, then the detail."
Takeaway -- the single key point you want the audience to remember
"If there's one takeaway today, it's that the model scales."
Walk through -- to guide someone step by step through something
"Let me walk you through how we got to that number."
Compelling -- so convincing or interesting that people pay full attention
"A compelling story makes a board lean in, not check their phones."
Backdrop -- the background situation against which events happen
"Against a backdrop of a sliding market, our growth stood out."
Storyline -- the connected thread that holds a presentation together
"Each slide moved the storyline forward instead of repeating it."
Turning point -- the moment when a situation clearly changed direction
"The new route was the turning point of the whole year."
Drill down -- to move from the overview into specific detail
"Let me drill down into the two numbers behind that jump."
Headline -- to lead with your single most important number or point
"Headline the result first, then explain how you got there."
Land -- to make a message connect and stay with the audience
"Say the takeaway slowly so it really lands."
Flag -- to draw attention to something important, often in advance
"Let me flag one risk before we move on to the numbers."
Frame -- to present information from a particular angle so it makes sense
"He framed the cost as an investment, not a loss."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the prep note and answer the questions.

The Growth Manager had seven minutes with the board, so he decided to headline the result and then walk them through the story. First he set the backdrop: the market had been sliding for two quarters while the team was quietly rebuilding the pipeline. That context made the turning point hit harder. "In March we launched the new route," he said, and then, to make the moment vivid, he switched to the present: "So we go live, and within a week the orders double." He signposted every move so nobody got lost, paused to drill down into two numbers, and kept the storyline tight. He even flagged one risk early and framed it as manageable. The talk was compelling because it built to a single takeaway he wanted to land: the model scales to three more regions.

1. Why does the speaker say "the market had been sliding" before "we launched the new route"?
A The past perfect shows the backstory that happened BEFORE the main events.
B "Had been sliding" means it is still sliding now.
C The past perfect is only used in questions.
2. Why does he switch to "we go live, and the orders double" in the present?
A Because it is happening right now, during the presentation.
B The dramatic present makes one past moment feel vivid and immediate.
C He made a grammar mistake under pressure.
3. Which sentence correctly uses a finished past time?
A "We have launched the route last March."
B "We launched the route last March."
C "We are launching the route last March."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Narrative Tenses & the Dramatic Present

GRAMMAR

Use a different tense for each layer of the story.

Job in the storyTenseExample
The backstory (before)Past perfect: had + past participle"By March we had rebuilt the pipeline."
The scene (in progress)Past continuous: was/were + -ing"The market was sliding while we invested."
The key eventsPast simple"Then we launched the new route in March."
The vivid momentDramatic (historic) present"So we go live, and the orders double."
Watch the traps: with a finished past time (last March, in Q1) use the past simple, NOT the present perfect ("we launched", not "we have launched last March"); use "we/they were" + -ing (not "was"); use the dramatic present only for the turning point, and keep it consistent within that moment. PT: tempos narrativos -- past perfect ("had + participio") para o que veio ANTES, past continuous ("was/were + -ing") para o pano de fundo em andamento, past simple para os eventos; o presente dramatico (historic present) deixa UM momento vivo, como se acontecesse agora -- use so no ponto de virada.
