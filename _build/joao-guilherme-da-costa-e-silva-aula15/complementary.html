Lesson 15 -- Speaking Under Pressure: Handling Tough Questions in Real Time

TED Talk
Sian Leah Beilock -- "Why we choke under pressure and how to avoid it" (TED, ~13 min, free, plays in the browser)

This is the science behind your entire lesson. Beilock is a cognitive scientist who studies exactly what happened to you in the March interview: under pressure, the brain's working memory — the space you think in — gets eaten by worry, so the skill you have suddenly will not come out. She explains why, and gives concrete ways to stop it: rehearse under mild stress, focus outward instead of on yourself, and reframe the pressure. It is the mechanism under every phrase you drilled tonight: buy time, reframe, keep your cool.

Tip: watch once with ENGLISH subtitles and note the two or three techniques she names for not choking. Then write, in your own words, how each one applies to the Élise interview — what you will do the moment a question lands on the spot. Record a 90-second summary of the talk, out loud, as if briefing a colleague who freezes in interviews.

Podcast
Think Fast, Talk Smart (Stanford GSB) -- Ep. 107, "How to Speak Successfully When You're Put on the Spot" (~25 min, free on YouTube, plays in the browser)

Matt Abrahams teaches strategic communication at Stanford, and this episode is your lesson from the inside: how to answer well when you are put on the spot with no time to prepare. He gives the exact moves you practiced — slow down, buy yourself time, ask a clarifying question, reframe the challenge — and explains why trying to be "perfect" is what makes you freeze. Native English, real pace, and everything transfers straight into an interview or a supplier call.

Tip: listen once and write down every specific technique he gives for handling a question on the spot. Then take the three pressure questions from tonight's screening card and script your opening move for each — the phrase you use to buy time before you answer. Record all three, and make sure at least one answer uses would rather, had better or may as well.

YouTube
Would Rather vs Had Better -- the difference between WOULD RATHER and HAD BETTER (~10 min)

The exact grammar of tonight's lesson, at your level, from a clear native teacher. It puts would rather and had better side by side (and touches may as well), and shows why each one changes the tone of what you say — preference versus warning — which is precisely what keeps you sounding senior instead of cornered under pressure. Watch it AFTER the lesson to fix the two errors Brazilians make most: adding "to" after the modal, and saying "would better" instead of "had better".

Tip: after the video, write 9 sentences from your real work at Motiva -- three with would rather (a preference in a negotiation), three with had better (advice to a supplier), three with may as well (accepting a fixed constraint). Record them fast, collapsing would/had into a 'd, with a bare verb right after. Then say each one as if Élise had just pushed you on it.
