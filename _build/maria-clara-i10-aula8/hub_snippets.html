Lesson 08 -- Pre-class

More, Less, the Best -- Comparatives and Superlatives

Compare two campuses, two hotels, and two conferences. Key words: formal, efficient, comfortable, distant, significant, local, previous, current. Structure: comparatives (larger than / more formal than) and superlatives (the largest / the most important / the best). And two phrases for comparing: "I would rather..." and "The more, the better.".

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Take your time — listen twice, then say it out loud. These are the eight adjectives you will use to compare.

Formal -- serious and official, with polite rules to follow
"The official dinner was very formal, so everyone wore a suit."
Efficient -- able to work well and fast, with no waste of time
"The new campus is modern and efficient."
Comfortable -- nice and relaxed to use, to sit in, or to stay in
"Our campus is smaller, but it is more comfortable."
Distant -- far away in space, not near you
"Seoul is distant; it is very far from Brazil."
Significant -- large or important enough to matter
"This is the most significant conference in ten years."
Local -- close to you and in the same city or area
"I would rather stay in a local hotel, close to the campus."
Previous -- the one that came before this one
"This conference was more formal than the previous one."
Current -- happening now, at the present time
"My current English is better than last year."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

Last month, I visited two universities for the council. The campus in Seoul is much larger than ours, and it is more modern and more efficient. My own campus is older and more traditional, but in my opinion it is more comfortable. This year's conference was more formal than the previous one, and more significant too. The hotel was more comfortable, but it was also more expensive and more distant from the campus. The best moment was the final round table — the most important meeting of the week — and I spoke in English, with no interpreter. My current English is better than last year. The more I practice, the better I speak.

1. How is the campus in Seoul, compared with Maria Clara's?
A Larger, more modern, and more efficient.
B Smaller and older.
C Exactly the same.
2. What was the best moment of the week?
A The dinner at the hotel.
B The final round table, the most important meeting.
C The flight to Seoul.
3. How is Maria Clara's current English?
A Worse than last year.
B Better than last year.
C The same as last year.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Comparatives and Superlatives (larger than / more formal than / the best)

GRAMMAR

Use a comparative to compare two things (with than), and a superlative to point to the top one (with the). Short adjective (1 syllable): add -er / -est. Long adjective (2+ syllables): use more / the most.

AdjectiveComparative (two things)Superlative (the top one)
Short: largelarger thanthe largest
Short: bigbigger thanthe biggest
Long: formalmore formal thanthe most formal
Long: importantmore important thanthe most important
Irregular: goodbetter thanthe best
Irregular: badworse thanthe worst

Never mix the two: say bigger (not "more big"), and the best (not "the most best"). Always than after a comparative, and the before a superlative. Irregular: good → better → the best; bad → worse → the worst; far → farther → the farthest.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each real moment of comparing.

A colleague asks "Is Seoul bigger than Paris?" You answer:
A "Yes, Seoul is bigger than Paris."
B "Yes, Seoul is more big than Paris."
C "Yes, Seoul is the bigger than Paris."
You want to say the hotel was very good, number one. The best sentence is:
A "It was the most best hotel of the trip."
B "It was the best hotel of the trip."
C "It was the more good hotel of the trip."
You compare this conference with the last one. The best sentence is:
A "This conference is more formal than the previous one."
B "This conference is formaler than the previous one."
C "This conference is more formal that the previous one."
You want to say your preference about the hotel. The best sentence is:
A "I would rather to stay in a local hotel."
B "I would rather stay in a local hotel."
C "I would rather staying in a local hotel."

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself. There is no right or wrong answer — speak for about a minute, with no script. This is your rehearsal for a real comparison at an international table.

Compare two things you know well: your two doctorates (USP and UFSC), or two cities you have visited. Which one was more challenging? Which one was more formal? Which one was the best for your career? Speak in 4-5 sentences with comparatives and superlatives. Use: more... than..., the most..., I would rather... Take your time and don't read from a script.
