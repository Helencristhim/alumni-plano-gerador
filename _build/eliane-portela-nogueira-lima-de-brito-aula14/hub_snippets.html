Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Twelve words founders use in a tough Q&A. Listen and say each one. / Doze palavras que fundadores usam num Q&A difícil. Ouça e diga cada uma.

Pushback -- resistance or disagreement to your idea / resistência, oposição
"I expected some pushback on the numbers, and I welcomed it."
Grill -- to question someone hard and repeatedly / interrogar duramente, pressionar
"The investors grilled her for twenty minutes on the forecast."
Objection -- a stated reason for disagreeing / objeção, ressalva
"A sharp objection shows you where the risk really is."
Scrutiny -- careful, critical examination / exame criterioso, escrutínio
"Every claim in a pitch is put under close scrutiny."
Deflect -- to turn a question away without answering it / desviar, esquivar-se da pergunta
"Do not deflect a hard question; answer it directly."
Hedge -- to avoid giving a clear, committed answer / evitar uma resposta clara, ficar em cima do muro
"When she hedged on the timeline, the room lost trust."
Concede -- to admit that a point is fair or true / admitir, reconhecer um ponto
"I concede that the first year is the biggest risk."
Red flag -- a sign that warns of a possible problem / sinal de alerta
"A vague answer about cash is a red flag for any investor."
Worst-case scenario -- the worst possible outcome you can imagine / o pior cenário possível
"Name the worst-case scenario before the investor does."
Reassure -- to remove someone's doubt or worry / tranquilizar, dar segurança
"A good founder reassures the room with data, not promises."
Anticipate -- to expect something and prepare for it / antecipar, prever
"Anticipate the hardest question and rehearse your answer."
Follow-up -- a further question or action after the first one / pergunta ou ação de acompanhamento
"Her clear answer turned the follow-up into curiosity."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

After a tough Q&A, Eliane replayed every answer. If she had anticipated the hardest question, she would have stayed calm instead of hedging. If the team had shown the worst-case scenario first, the investors would not have pushed back so hard. Notice the pattern: the if-clause uses had + past participle (had anticipated, had shown) and the result uses would have + past participle (would have stayed, would not have pushed). This is the third conditional -- an imagined past that did not happen. In formal English, you can drop the if and invert: Had we prepared for the follow-up, we could have closed the round.

1. In the if-clause of the third conditional, which form is correct?
A If she had anticipated -- if + had + past participle
B If she would have anticipated -- if + would have
C If she anticipated -- if + past simple
2. In the result clause, which form is correct?
A they would not pushed back (no "have")
B they would not have pushed back (would + have + past participle)
C they would not push back (would + base verb)
3. What does "Had we prepared for the follow-up..." mean?
A The same as "If we had prepared..." -- a formal inversion.
B A question about whether we prepared.
C A command to prepare for the follow-up.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Third Conditional

GRAMMAR

We use the third conditional to imagine a different past -- something that did NOT happen -- and its result. It is the grammar of hindsight and regret. / Usamos o terceiro condicional para imaginar um passado diferente -- algo que NÃO aconteceu -- e o seu resultado. É a gramática do arrependimento e da análise em retrospecto.

Part / ParteForm / FormaExample / Exemplo
If-clause / condiçãoIf + had + past participleIf we had shown the risk...
Result / resultadowould / could / might + have + past participle...they would not have pushed back.
Meaning / sentidoimagined past, did NOT happen / passado imaginadohindsight · regret / arrependimento
Inversion / inversãoHad + subject + past participleHad we prepared, we could have...

Film
The Social Network (2010)

The whole film is built around two hostile legal depositions, where Mark Zuckerberg is grilled about past decisions under adversarial questioning. It is a master class in fielding pressure: the pushback, the sharp objections, the moments he deflects instead of conceding. And it is pure third conditional -- everyone in the room is arguing about what would have happened if the founding, the code, or the equity had been handled differently.

Tip: after a deposition scene, sum up one dispute in a third-conditional sentence ("If Eduardo had signed a different agreement, he would not have lost his shares"), then say how you would have answered one hard question.
