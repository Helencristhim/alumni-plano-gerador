Film
The Social Network (2010)

The whole film is built around two hostile legal depositions, where Mark Zuckerberg is grilled about past decisions under adversarial questioning. It is a master class in fielding pressure: the pushback, the sharp objections, the moments he deflects instead of conceding. And it is pure third conditional -- everyone in the room is arguing about what would have happened if the founding, the code, or the equity had been handled differently.

Tip: after a deposition scene, sum up one dispute in a third-conditional sentence ("If Eduardo had signed a different agreement, he would not have lost his shares"), then say how you would have answered one hard question.

Podcast
Acquired

Ben Gilbert and David Rosenthal take apart how the greatest companies were built, decision by decision. Again and again they run a calm post-mortem on the pivotal calls -- the fundraise, the pivot, the deal that almost died in the Q&A -- and ask what would have happened if a founder had answered a hard question differently. You will hear the vocabulary of this lesson (scrutiny, red flag, worst-case scenario, pushback) used by people analysing real high-pressure rooms.

Tip: pick one episode, then write two third-conditional sentences about a decision the hosts discuss ("If the founders had raised more, they would have...").

YouTube
BBC Learning English -- "The third conditional (Conditionals with Georgie)"

A clear, full BBC Learning English lesson on exactly this lesson's grammar: If + past perfect, would have + past participle, used to talk about an unreal past and about regret. It explains how to build both halves and when to use them. Direct connection to Lesson 14: the grammar that lets you say what would have happened if the Q&A had gone differently.

Tip: pause after each example and say one sentence about your own last presentation using the same pattern, out loud.
