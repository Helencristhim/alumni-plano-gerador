Series
The Diplomat — Season 1, Episode 1: “The Cinderella Thing” (Netflix)

Kate Wyler walks into rooms full of people who do not report to her — and she still has to get the decision to come out her way. It is influence without authority in its purest form, in the English of closed doors and high pressure. Connection to Lesson 4: notice HOW she builds an argument before she asks for anything — the data first, the ask at the end — and how she names the weakness in her own proposal before the other side can find it.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Write down THREE lines in which somebody gets a decision without the formal power to demand it, and ONE line that opens a presentation with a piece of data.

Podcast
HBR — “Building Influence Without Authority” (Coaching Real Leaders, Muriel Wilkins)

A real coaching session, recorded, with a leader who has to get an entire organization to adopt her strategy without a single direct report in the room. This is literally your August first, in executive English and at native speed. Connection to Lesson 4: the whole episode is the argument of this lesson — approval is not adoption, and a plan only moves when somebody in the room decides to carry it.

Tip: listen once, with no pauses. Then summarize the episode out loud in sixty seconds, opening with “What the data shows us is...” and using at least one inversion (“Never have I seen...”). Record it and listen back.

YouTube
Adam Galinsky — “How to speak up for yourself” (TED, 15 min)

The Columbia professor who studies power and influence explains the mechanics of positioning yourself when you have no formal authority in the room — and why the very same sentence sounds arrogant from one person and legitimate from another. Connection to Lesson 4: it is the theory behind what you practiced today, and the answer to the question that will come up in August: how to disagree with a Canadian director of twenty years without losing the room.

Tip: write down HOW he builds each argument (the data before the opinion). Bring two of his sentences that you would use in an executive presentation, and say each of them out loud in its inverted version.
