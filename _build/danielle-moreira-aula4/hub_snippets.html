Influencing Without Authority -- Presenting Your People Strategy to Canadian Leadership

Presenting your people strategy to five Canadian leaders who do NOT report to you: open with the data, answer the cost objection with the cost of doing nothing, name the trade-off before the room names it, and leave the meeting with an owner for every pillar. Key words: talent retention, engagement survey, performance cycle, succession planning, employer brand, people analytics, workforce planning, change management, psychological contract, diversity and inclusion, influence without authority, employee value proposition, to make a compelling case, to phase out, a double-edged sword. Structure: inversion for emphasis (Never have I seen... / Not only did we..., but we also... / Hardly had we... when...) + executive presentation language (What the data shows us is... / I would like to draw your attention to... / The rationale behind this approach is... / What this means in practice is...).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the vocabulary a people strategy is written in — and the only one in which a Canadian board is willing to fund it.

Talent retention -- keeping the people you cannot afford to lose, and knowing what it costs when you do not
"Talent retention is not a value. It is a number, and this year the number is four."
Engagement survey -- the instrument that measures how invested people are in the work
"The engagement survey had been running for four years, and nobody had ever read it region by region."
Performance cycle -- the yearly loop of goals, feedback and review a company runs on its people
"That region has not completed a performance cycle since the pandemic. Nobody noticed."
Succession planning -- deciding today who is ready to take a critical role tomorrow, before anyone resigns
"Succession planning is the question nobody asks until the answer is urgent."
Employer brand -- what the market says about you as a place to work, when you are not in the room
"Our employer brand is doing our recruiting for us. Unfortunately, it is doing it badly."
People analytics -- using workforce data to make a people decision defensible in a room full of finance people
"Retention as a value was ignored for four years. Retention as people analytics took eleven minutes."
Workforce planning -- deciding how many people, with which skills, you will need, and by when
"Workforce planning is the difference between hiring in September and hiring in a panic in November."
Change management -- the discipline of taking people through a change they did not ask for
"Nobody asked for this change. That is not an obstacle to change management. That is change management."
Psychological contract -- the unwritten expectations between a company and a person, the promises nobody signed
"We never broke her contract. We broke the psychological contract, and she resigned six weeks later."
Diversity and inclusion -- who is in the room, and whether they can actually speak once they are there
"Diversity is who you hire. Inclusion is whether they are still speaking in year two."
Influence without authority -- getting a decision made by people who do not work for you and never will
"Influence without authority is not a soft skill. In that room it is the only skill."
Employee value proposition -- the deal you offer: what a person actually gets in exchange for joining you and staying
"If our employee value proposition is only the salary, we will lose to whoever pays more."
To make a compelling case -- to argue something so well that saying no becomes the harder option
"Make a compelling case, and then stop talking. The silence after a strong argument is not awkward."
To phase out -- to retire an old practice gradually, on purpose, instead of stopping it overnight
"We phase out the old performance cycle over two quarters. We do not switch it off in one."
A double-edged sword -- a real advantage that carries a real cost, at the same time
"Flexible work is a double-edged sword: it widens the talent pool and it weakens the informal culture."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

When Marina Ortiz joined the Halden Group as its first Director of People and Culture, she was given a title, almost no budget, and no authority whatsoever. The five regional directors did not report to her, they had built the company, and they had never once been asked to adopt a people strategy written by somebody who had been in the building for six weeks. Her predecessor had presented a beautiful framework to the board, had watched it approved, and had watched nothing happen afterwards. The plan was never rejected. It was simply never carried by anyone. Influence without authority was not a soft skill in that room. It was the only skill available to her.

So Marina spent a quarter inside the numbers before she proposed anything. The engagement survey had been running for four years, and nobody had ever read it region by region. When she did, the story stopped being about culture and started being about money: one region was losing its senior teachers at three times the rate of the others, every departure was costing eleven months of hiring, and that region had not completed a performance cycle since the pandemic. Talent retention, presented as a value, had been ignored for four years. Talent retention, presented as people analytics, took eleven minutes.

The board adopted her strategy in a single meeting. Never had she seen a plan survive because it was right; plans survive because somebody in the room decides to carry them. So she made a compelling case, and then she named the trade-off before anyone else could. Not only did the flexible model widen the talent pool, she told them, but it also weakened the informal culture that had held the company together for twenty years. It was a double-edged sword, and she intended to manage it rather than deny it. Hardly had she finished when the regional director who had quietly killed her predecessor's framework asked to own the first pilot.

1. Why is it "Never had she seen a plan survive" and not "Never she had seen a plan survive"?
A Because, once the emphasis word (Never) moves to the front, the sentence borrows the word order of a question: the auxiliary comes BEFORE the subject.
B Because it is a rhetorical question in disguise.
C Because the subject is feminine and therefore follows the verb.
2. "Not only did the flexible model widen the talent pool..." — why widen and not widened?
A Because the sentence is in the present.
B Because did already carries the past — the main verb goes back to its base form, exactly as in "Did you widen it?".
C Because after "not only" the verb is never conjugated.
3. According to the text, why did the previous framework fail?
A The board rejected it because it was badly written.
B The board approved it, and then nobody in the room ever carried it.
C The regional directors were never shown the framework at all.
4. What made the board adopt Marina's strategy, according to the text?
A Her authority over the five regional directors.
B The fact that her strategy was more correct than her predecessor's.
C She argued it with data, named the trade-off first, and somebody in the room decided to carry it.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Inversion for Emphasis

GRAMMAR

The grammar of weight: the emphasis word moves to the FRONT of the sentence and the auxiliary steps ahead of the subject, exactly as it does in a question — but this is not a question, it is authority.

FormUseExample
Never / Rarely / At no point
+ auxiliary + subject		Conviction. You put your whole career behind a single statement.Never have I seen a strategy succeed without an owner.
Not only + did + subject + base verb
..., but ... also ...		Scale. Two achievements in one breath, without turning into a list.Not only did we redesign the cycle, but we also rebuilt the onboarding.
Hardly / Scarcely + had
+ subject + past participle + when		Speed. The result arrived before the doubt did.Hardly had we launched the pilot when the scores rose.
Only when / Only after
+ clause + auxiliary + subject		The only condition. This way, and no other way.Only when they owned it did the plan move.
NegativeThe inversion IS the negative — never add “not”At no point was succession planning discussed. (never “was not”)
QuestionInversion is a device for STATEMENTS. In a question, keep the normal word order.Have you ever seen a plan survive without an owner?
Presentation languageWhat the data shows us is... · I would like to draw your attention to... · The rationale behind this approach is... · What this means in practice is...

Watch out (slip number one): after the emphasis word, the subject cannot come before the auxiliary. “Never I have seen” is wrong — the correct form is “Never have I seen”. The sentence borrows the word order of a question, and only the word order: no question mark.

Watch out (slip number two): if the original sentence has no auxiliary, English borrows one: We redesigned it → “Not only did we redesign it”. And the main verb goes back to its base form (redesign, never redesigned).

Dosage: inversion is the seasoning, not the dish. Two or three of them in a ten-minute presentation sound like authority. Six sound like theater — and the room stops listening to the argument in order to watch the performance.

Collocation: it is make a compelling case (never “do a case”), draw your attention to (never “call your attention for”), and phase out a practice (never “phase off”).
