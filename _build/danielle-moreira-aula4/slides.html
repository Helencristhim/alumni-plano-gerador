Chapter 4: The Code

The Sentence That Stops the Room

Never have I seen · Not only did we · Hardly had we

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

“Never had she seen a plan survive because it was right.”

“Not only did we redesign the performance cycle, but we also rebuilt the onboarding.”

“Hardly had we launched the pilot when the engagement scores began to rise.”

“Never have I seen a people strategy succeed without an owner in the room.”

Look at the first three words of each one. Something has jumped in front of the subject — and the verb followed it.

TriggerFormExample
Never / Rarely / At no point+ auxiliary + subject + verbNever have I seen a plan survive without an owner.
Not only... but also+ did/do/does + subject + base verbNot only did we redesign it, but we also rebuilt it.
Hardly / Scarcely... when+ had + subject + past participleHardly had we launched the pilot when the scores rose.
Only when / Only after+ clause + auxiliary + subjectOnly when they owned it did the plan move.
The mechanicThe emphasis word goes to the front, and the sentence then borrows question word order — auxiliary before subject. It is not a question. It is weight.

If there is no auxiliary in the plain sentence, English borrows one: We redesigned it → Not only did we redesign it.

Register

Three Inversions, Three Jobs

Never have I...

Conviction. It puts your whole career behind one claim.

“Never have I seen a people strategy succeed without an owner in the room.”

Not only did we...

Scale. Two achievements in one breath, without a list.

“Not only did we redesign the performance cycle, but we also rebuilt the onboarding.”

Hardly had we...

Speed. The result arrived faster than the doubt did.

“Hardly had we launched the pilot when the engagement scores began to rise.”

Inversion is a spice, not the meal. Two or three in a ten-minute presentation sound like authority. Six sound like theater — and the room stops listening to the argument and starts listening to the performance.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Never I have seen a transformation succeed without buy-in."
"Never have I seen a transformation succeed without buy-in."
"Not only we redesigned the performance cycle, but we also rebuilt the onboarding."
"Not only did we redesign the performance cycle, but we also rebuilt the onboarding."

Once the emphasis word takes the front, the sentence must borrow question word order: auxiliary before the subject. And after Not only, if the plain sentence has no auxiliary, English inserts did — and the main verb goes back to its base form (redesign, never redesigned).

D
What the data shows us is that one senior departure costs us eleven months of hiring, and we have had four this year. And I would like to draw your attention to the second line of the dashboard: succession planning, two of nine critical roles.
N

D
I would not argue with the budget. Never have I seen a people strategy win that argument. I would put the cost of doing nothing next to the cost of the plan, in people analytics, and let them read both.
N
And the flexible model? Two of the directors will tell you it is destroying the culture they spent twenty years building.
D
They are half right, and I intend to say so before they do. Flexible work is a double-edged sword: not only did it widen our talent pool, but it also weakened the informal culture that used to do the work for us. That is change management, and I would rather manage it than deny it.
