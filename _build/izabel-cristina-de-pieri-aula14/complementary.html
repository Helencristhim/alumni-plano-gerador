Aula 14 -- The Case for the Deal -- Making an Argument with Emphasis

Film
Moneyball (2011)

Billy Beane builds a winning baseball team on a tiny budget by trusting the data instead of the scouts' opinions. It is Lesson 14 in a single film: a compelling case built on evidence, a room full of objections and bias, and a leader who has to substantiate every claim and rebut the assertion that "this is how it has always been done." Watch how the strongest argument is the one that answers its own counterarguments.

Tip: pick one scene where Beane defends his plan. Write three emphatic sentences about it -- "It was ... that ...", "What ... is ...", "The reason ... is that ..." -- separating the facts from the opinions.

Podcast
HBR IdeaCast -- Harvard Business Review

A weekly conversation with the leading thinkers in business and management. Many episodes are, in effect, a spoken business case: a guest states the bottom line, anticipates the objections, substantiates the merits with evidence, and rebuts the common bias. It is the exact register of Lesson 14 -- building and defending an argument out loud, with emphasis, for a sceptical audience.

Tip: choose one episode on a decision or strategy. Note two sentences where the guest emphasizes a key point, then record your own 90-second case for a real AVRT decision using at least two cleft structures.

YouTube
"CLEFT sentences -- how to use EMPHASIS in English" -- Mónica Redondo Arias

A clear walkthrough of exactly the code from Lesson 14: it-clefts ("It was X that ..."), what-clefts ("What ... is X"), and how emphasis moves one idea into the spotlight. It covers the traps you practised -- starting the it-cleft with "It", avoiding the extra "it" in a what-cleft, and using "the reason ... is that", never "is because".

Tip: pause after each example and say your own version about AVRT out loud. Then write four emphatic sentences defending one real business decision -- at least one it-cleft, one what-cleft, and one "The reason ... is that ...".
