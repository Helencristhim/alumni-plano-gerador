Chapter 3 -- The Code

Language Discovery

Present perfect vs simple past

Discovery

Listen and Notice

What is different about the orange verbs? Which ones have a finished date, and which ones connect to now?

"I have managed three subsidiaries for fifteen years."

"We have closed three deals this quarter."

"I led the restructuring in 2019."

"We signed the contract last year."

The Rule

Present Perfect vs Simple Past

UsePatternExample
Experience (no exact time)have/has + past participle"I have worked with US partners", "she has mentored a team"
Result in an open periodhave/has + past participle"we have closed three deals this quarter"
Finished, dated eventverb + -ed / irregular past"I led the restructuring in 2019", "we signed it last year"
Time markersfor / since / this year vs. in 2019 / last year / ago"since 2010" (perfect) vs. "in 2010" (past)

Key idea: use the present perfect (have/has + past participle) for experience and for results in a period that is still open (this year, this quarter). Use the simple past when the event is finished and has a clear time (in 2019, last year, two months ago). Quick test: if you can add a finished date, use the past; if the time is open or unstated, use the present perfect.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"I have led the restructuring in 2019."
"I led the restructuring in 2019."

A finished, dated time (in 2019, last year, two months ago) takes the simple past. Keep the present perfect for open time: "I have led several restructurings."
