Chapter 3 -- Experience & Results

Language Discovery

Present perfect vs simple past -- and the passive for the result

Discovery

Listen and Notice

What is the difference in time between the first two sentences and the last two?

"I have managed this holding for over ten years."

"We have closed three major contracts this quarter."

"I led the restructuring in 2019."

"We opened the Pittsburgh office last March."

The Rule

Present Perfect vs Simple Past

TenseFormUse & example
Present perfecthave / has + past participleexperience + results in an open period: "I have managed it for ten years"
Simple pastverb + -ed / irregularfinished event at a definite time: "I led the restructuring in 2019"
Signal words (perfect)for, since, this quarter, ever, already, so far"sales have grown since January"
Signal words (past)in 2019, last March, yesterday, ago"we signed it in June"

Key idea: use the present perfect when the period is still open and the result still counts today ("I have managed it for ten years" -- and I still do). Switch to the simple past the moment you add a finished time ("I led it in 2019"). One definite past time, one past tense.

Language Focus

Reporting the Result

When you report a result, use be / was / have been + past participle. The doer is often obvious, so you can leave it out.

"The target was exceeded last year."

"The groundwork has been laid for next year."

"The report has been approved by the board."

Pattern: subject + was / were or has / have been + past participle (exceeded, laid, approved). Add "by + doer" only if it matters ("approved by the board"). You tell your experience in the present perfect; you report the result in the passive.

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"I have led the restructuring in 2019."
"I led the restructuring in 2019."

A definite past time ("in 2019", "last March") needs the simple past, not the present perfect. Keep the present perfect for open periods ("for ten years", "this quarter"). And the passive still needs be: say "the target was exceeded", never "the target exceeded".

Comprehension

About the Summary

1. Why is her experience written in the present perfect?

Because it is still true today: she has managed the holding for over a decade and still does -- an open period, so the present perfect.

2. Why are the milestones written in the simple past?

Because each one has a definite past time -- restructured the fiscal area in 2022, opened the office last March, signed the agreement in June -- finished events, so the simple past.

3. Where is the passive used, and why?

In the results -- "three contracts have been closed", "every target has been surpassed", "the groundwork has been laid" -- because the result matters more than the doer.
