Vocabulary

Recipient -- the person who receives an email, message, or document
"Always check that the recipient is correct before you click send on a sensitive email."
Concise -- giving a lot of information clearly and in a few words
"Keep the update concise so the directors can read it in under a minute."
Prompt -- done quickly, without delay
"Thank you for your prompt reply; it helped us close the order on time."
Discrepancy -- a difference or inconsistency between two things that should match
"There is a discrepancy between the invoice and the quantity we actually received."
Reiterate -- to say or write something again to emphasize it
"Let me reiterate that the deadline for the LATAM report is this Friday."
Pertinent -- directly relevant and connected to the matter being discussed
"Please include only the pertinent figures so the message stays focused."
Courtesy -- polite behavior, or a polite and considerate action
"As a courtesy, she copied the regional team on the supplier update."
Liaise -- to communicate and coordinate with others to work together effectively
"Victor will liaise with the London office to align the security rollout."
Outstanding -- not yet resolved, completed, or paid
"There are two outstanding items from last week that still need a response."
Tactful -- careful not to offend or upset others; diplomatic in wording
"A tactful email asks for action without sounding like a demand."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

The Growth Manager opened his inbox and saw a problem. A supplier had missed a deadline, and there was a clear discrepancy between the order and the delivery. His first instinct was to write something direct: "Fix this now." But he knew a tactful message would get a better result. Instead of a demand, he chose a hedged, conditional request. He wrote: "I would appreciate it if you could confirm the corrected quantity by Thursday." He kept the email concise and included only the pertinent details, so the recipient could act quickly. As a courtesy, he copied his counterpart in London so she could liaise with the supplier directly. He also decided to reiterate the original requirements, because they had been agreed weeks earlier. At the end, he added a soft escalation: "If we do not receive the figures by Friday, we will raise the matter with the regional director." It was firm but polite. He asked for a prompt reply at the recipient's earliest convenience, and he closed the message warmly. Two outstanding issues remained, but the tone of the email made it far more likely that they would be resolved without friction.

1. Why did the Growth Manager NOT write "Fix this now"?
A Because he did not know who the recipient was.
B Because a tactful, hedged request gets a better result than a blunt demand.
C Because the problem was not important enough to mention.
2. What is the function of the sentence "If we do not receive the figures by Friday, we will raise the matter with the regional director"?
A It is an apology for the delay.
B It is a polite but firm escalation, using a first conditional to state the consequence.
C It is a question asking for permission.
3. Why did he include only the "pertinent" details and keep the email "concise"?
A So the email would look longer and more serious.
B Because he did not have time to write more.
C So the recipient could understand the issue and act on it quickly.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Register, Hedging & Conditional Requests

GRAMMAR

The same request can sound rude or professional depending on the register. Hedging softens it; conditionals make it diplomatic.

RegisterStructureExample
Direct (avoid)Imperative"Send me the report."
NeutralCan / Could you...?"Could you send me the report?"
Formal (hedged)I would appreciate it if you could..."I would appreciate it if you could send me the report."
TentativeI was wondering if..."I was wondering if you could send the report today."
EscalationFirst conditional: If + present, will + base"If we do not receive it by Friday, we will escalate."
Hedging devices: would / could / might / I was wondering if / it would be helpful if / perhaps / at your earliest convenience. Rule: the more important or sensitive the request, the more you hedge. Use "would appreciate it if you could + base verb" (NOT "to send"). PT: quanto mais delicado o pedido, mais voce suaviza -- evite o imperativo direto.
