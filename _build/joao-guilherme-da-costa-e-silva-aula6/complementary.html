Lesson 6 -- Holding Your Ground: The Supplier Approval Meeting

Documentary
Downfall: The Case Against Boeing (Netflix, 1h29)

This is your lesson, on the scale of a tragedy. Engineers raise concerns about the MCAS; the company downplays the failure ("it is a minor change, no training required"); responsibility is pushed onto the pilot and onto the supplier (passing the buck); and the root cause is admitted only after 346 deaths. Listen to how the investigators speak: almost everything they assert is in the passive voice — "the sensor was found to be defective", "the certification had not been completed" — because an impersonal finding is the only kind that survives a courtroom.

Tip: watch it with English subtitles and write down 8 passive sentences said by investigators or executives. Then classify each one: who disappeared from the sentence, and did the sentence get stronger or more cowardly because of it? That is the difference between hiding behind the passive and stating a fact with it.

Podcast
Causality (John Chidgey) — Episode 62 "OceanGate Titan" (39 min)

An Australian electrical engineer does, episode after episode, exactly what you do in a factory acceptance test: root cause analysis. In this one, specialists raised concerns about the hull and the materials, the company refused third-party certification (the sign-off it did not want to ask for), and the submersible imploded. The English is technical and native, at real speed — and the structure of the reasoning (symptom → cause → corrective action) is the structure of your own non-conformance report.

Tip: listen to all 39 minutes ONCE, without pausing. Then record 60 seconds summarizing out loud what was found, what had not been done and what should have been implemented. If the sentence does not come out whole on the first try, do it again until it does.

YouTube
HAVE / GET something DONE (CAUSATIVES) — English Grammar for B2 First (SMASH English)

The exact structure of this lesson, at the exact level: have something done and get something done. Pay attention to the point that trips up most engineers: the OBJECT comes before the participle — "have the report resubmitted", never "have resubmitted the report". And note the difference in meaning: I resubmitted it (I did the work) versus I had it resubmitted (I demanded it; somebody else did the work).

Tip: after the video, say 5 sentences about YOUR own signaling package — three with have something done (the report, the relay, the certificate) and two with the passive of a finding (was found to be..., had not been issued). Out loud, with nothing written down first.
