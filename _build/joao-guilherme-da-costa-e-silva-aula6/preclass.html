Lesson 06 -- Pre-class

Holding Your Ground -- The Supplier Approval Meeting

The safety relay failed the factory acceptance test and the supplier calls it a "minor deviation": how to state the failure without accusing anybody, how to demand the corrective action without doing the work yourself, and how to keep the hold point closed. Key words: deviation, submittal, approval workflow, inspection release, out of tolerance, calibration certificate, traceability, root cause, rework, sign-off, to downplay, to raise a concern, to hold your ground, to pass the buck. Structure: the advanced passive (was found to be... / had not been issued / must be implemented) + the causative have something done ("We need to have the report resubmitted by Friday").

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

In Lesson 5 you put a name to yourself in front of an interview panel. Here you put a name to the DEFECT — and to whoever pays for it. These are the words of the approval meeting: what was found, what had not been issued yet, and what the supplier will try to call "minor". Listen to every term and read the example.

Deviation -- a departure from what the approved specification requires
"The deviation was accepted in writing, so no rework is required."
Submittal -- a document the supplier formally sends to the client for review and approval
"The submittal was returned with comments on Tuesday."
Approval workflow -- the sequence of reviews a document must pass before it is accepted
"The drawing is stuck in the approval workflow, not on my desk."
Inspection release -- the formal authorization to ship, or to move to the next stage, after an inspection
"No inspection release has been issued, so nothing leaves the factory."
Out of tolerance -- outside the range of values the specification allows
"The relay was found to be out of tolerance during the functional test."
Calibration certificate -- the document that proves an instrument was verified against a reference
"The calibration certificate had not been issued at the time of the test."
Traceability -- being able to track a part back to its material, its test and its records
"In signaling, a component without traceability does not exist."
Root cause -- the underlying reason a failure happened, not the symptom you can see
"The root cause has not been identified yet, so the corrective action is premature."
Rework -- work that has to be done a second time to correct a defect
"The rework will be carried out at the supplier's own cost."
Sign-off -- the formal written approval that closes a stage and lets the project move on
"I cannot give you sign-off until the calibration certificate is on file."
To downplay -- to make a problem sound smaller than it really is
"He downplayed the failure: 'It is a minor deviation, nothing more.'"
To raise a concern -- to put a risk formally on the record, before it becomes a problem
"I would like to raise a concern about the traceability of that batch."
To hold your ground -- to keep your position when the other side pushes back
"He downplayed it twice, and twice I held my ground."
To pass the buck -- to shift responsibility to somebody else instead of owning it (informal)
"Blaming the subcontractor is passing the buck: our contract is with you."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text, then answer the questions below.

The factory acceptance test was carried out on a Tuesday morning, with João Guilherme watching on a video link from São Paulo. The safety relay was found to be out of tolerance: eleven milliseconds, against a specified range of five to seven. The test was witnessed, a hold point was triggered, and no inspection release was issued. Then the report arrived from the supplier, and every sentence in it had been written to make the failure disappear.

Two things had not been done at the time of the test. The calibration certificate for the reference instrument had not been issued, and the root cause had not been identified. The report calls the failure a minor deviation and asks for a waiver, which is the polite way of asking João to pay for somebody else's rework. On the call, the quality manager downplays it — "the unit works" — and then passes the buck: the calibration, she says, was performed by a subcontractor. João holds his ground. A deviation is approved before the work; this was found after it. The contract is with her company, not with the subcontractor.

So he states what has to happen, and he states it in the passive, because the obligation belongs to the procedure and not to him. "A corrective action must be implemented before the retest. We need to have the report resubmitted through the approval workflow by Friday, and we will have the relay retested with a valid certificate in place. I would also like to raise a concern about the traceability of that batch. Sign-off cannot be granted while the hold point remains open." Nobody was accused. Nothing was denied. And the relay is being retested on Thursday.

1. "The relay was found to be out of tolerance." Why does the engineer choose the passive here, instead of "Your technician calibrated it badly"?
A Because the passive is more polite, even though it is weaker.
B Because the passive takes the PERSON out of the sentence and leaves the FACT. An accusation can be disputed and defended; a finding can only be CLOSED — and that is why every inspection report is written this way.
C Because the active voice is not allowed in technical English.
2. "The calibration certificate had not been issued at the time of the test." What does the PERFECT passive do here that the simple passive would not do?
A It dates the absence: at the MOMENT of the test, that document did not exist yet — so it does not cover the test. Issuing it afterwards solves nothing.
B It only sounds more formal; the meaning is the same as "was not issued".
C It means the certificate will never be issued.
3. "We need to have the report resubmitted by Friday." WHO resubmits the report?
A I do, because have means "to have to do something".
B Nobody — the sentence only describes an intention.
C THE SUPPLIER. The causative have something done says the action is DEMANDED by me and CARRIED OUT by somebody else. I do not do the work; I require it.
4. Which sentence is correct?
A "We need to have resubmitted the report by Friday."
B "We need to have the report resubmitted by Friday."
C "We need to have the report resubmit by Friday."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Advanced Passive & Causative

GRAMMAR

How to state a failure without accusing a person — and how to demand the fix without doing the work yourself.

FormFunctionExample
Simple passive
be + participle		The object becomes the subject and the agent DROPS OUT — because it is obvious, because it is unknown, or because naming it would start a fight."The submittal was returned with comments."
Passive of a finding
be + found / reported / believed + to be		The register of every inspection report. You state the FACT, not the person. Note: is believed to have been caused is NOT the same as has been identified — the supplier reaches for the first one precisely in order not to commit to the second."The relay was found to be out of tolerance."
Perfect passive
had been + participle		It dates the ABSENCE: at the time of the test, that document did not exist yet. This is the sentence that stops the supplier from making the problem go away by issuing the certificate afterwards."The certificate had not been issued at the time of the test."
Modal passive
modal + be + participle		The obligation belongs to the PROCEDURE, not to you — and that is why nobody takes it personally. After the modal comes be, always."A corrective action must be implemented." · "Sign-off cannot be granted."
Causative
have + OBJECT + participle		You DEMAND the action; somebody else CARRIES IT OUT. The order is fixed: the thing comes before the participle. get something done is the same, only less formal."We need to have the report resubmitted." · "I will get the relay retested."
Causative with a person
have somebody + BASE FORM		For when you DO need to name who does the work. Then the verb goes back to the base form — no to, no participle."I will have your team resubmit the report."
Why it matters"You calibrated it badly" is an accusation — and everything that follows it will be a defense. "The relay was found to be out of tolerance" is the SAME fact with the person removed: nobody is attacked and nothing can be denied. Then the causative hands the work back to whoever owns it: "we need to have it retested" — not by us, by THEM, and by Friday.

Watch out (the two classic traps): (1) dropping the be or the participle after the modal — "The report must be resubmit" and "A corrective action must implemented" are both wrong; the right forms are "must be resubmitted" and "must be implemented"; (2) the word order of the causative — the temptation is to say "We need to have resubmitted the report", which in English is a different tense and a different meaning altogether. In the causative the OBJECT comes FIRST: "have the report resubmitted".

Collocations of the approval meeting: carry out a test, issue a certificate (NEVER "emit" — the classic false friend), trigger a hold point, identify the root cause, raise a concern, grant sign-off, resubmit a report through the approval workflow.

Stage 2: Put the Approval Meeting in Order

Order

Put the stages of a hold-point meeting in the right order — from the finding to the refusal of sign-off.

?Put the responsibility back where the contract is, when the supplier blames the subcontractor.
?Demand the work with the causative, and give the date: have the report resubmitted by Friday.
?Hold the gate: sign-off cannot be granted while the hold point remains open.
?Refuse the word: a deviation is approved before the work, so a failure found after it is a non-conformance.
?State the finding in the passive: the relay was found to be out of tolerance during the functional test.
?Date what is missing: the calibration certificate had not been issued at the time of the test.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for every real moment of a factory acceptance test meeting — including the moment when the supplier downplays the failure or shifts the blame onto the subcontractor.

The supplier's quality manager opens with "It is a minor deviation, the unit works." You answer:
A "A deviation is approved before the work. This was found after it, on a safety relay, so I am afraid that is a non-conformance."
B "You are wrong and you know it. That relay failed."
C "All right, if the unit works, I suppose we can look at it as a deviation."
She says the certificate was issued the Monday AFTER the test. The strongest reply is:
A "That is good news, we can close the point then."
B "The calibration certificate had not been issued at the time of the test, so it does not cover the test. The unit will have to be retested with a valid certificate in place."
C "Please send it to me and I will see what I can do."
"The calibration was performed by our subcontractor, not by us." You answer:
A "Then I will take it up with your subcontractor directly."
B "That is not my problem, and it is not an excuse."
C "To some extent, I understand your position. However, our contract is with you, so the root cause still has to be identified on your side."
She asks what you need before the unit can be released. You want the work done by THEM, with a date:
A "We need to have the report resubmitted through the approval workflow by Friday, with the root cause and a corrective action plan."
B "We need to have resubmitted the report by Friday."
C "I will resubmit the report myself once you send me the data."
She asks for a provisional sign-off so the shipment is not delayed. You say no without closing the door:
A "I cannot sign anything, please stop asking me."
B "I am afraid sign-off cannot be granted while the hold point remains open. As soon as the retest passes and the certificate is on file, the inspection release can be issued the same day."
C "All right, provisionally, but do not tell anyone."
