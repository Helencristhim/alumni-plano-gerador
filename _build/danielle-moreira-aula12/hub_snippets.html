Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the language of your first ninety days leading the Canadian division — the difference between arriving as a stranger and arriving as a listener.

Entry plan -- a deliberate 90-day plan for how a new leader will learn, decide, and act
"The entry plan she finally presented had three phases -- learn, decide, act -- and she was still, deliberately, in the first."
Listening tour -- a structured round of one-on-one conversations a new leader holds before changing anything
"Instead of restructuring, she spent the first quarter on a listening tour, sitting with the people who run the classrooms."
Ramp-up period -- the early stretch in a new role when you are still building context and cannot work at full speed
"The ramp-up period is not wasted time, even though it feels like it, because you are used to full speed and suddenly you cannot."
Learning curve -- how steep and how long the climb is before a new role feels natural
"That discomfort is the learning curve, not a sign that you are failing."
Credibility -- the trust you earn that makes people act on what you say, before any authority forces them to
"Credibility is not spent with the title. It is earned in the first quarter, one honest conversation at a time."
Mandate -- the clear authority and remit you are given to make a specific change happen
"You have a real mandate here, and the temptation will be to spend it fast to prove you deserve it."
Predecessor -- the person who held your role before you, whose decisions you inherit
"A predecessor who is honest about what went wrong is the most useful person you have in the first quarter."
Transition period -- the window between two leaders when a role is formally handed over
"The mistake almost every senior hire makes is to treat the transition period as an audition."
Frontline staff -- the people who do the core daily work directly with students, furthest from head office
"She sat with the frontline staff, not just the directors -- the people who actually run the classrooms."
Vantage point -- the position from which you see a situation, which shapes what you notice and miss
"It came from a particular vantage point -- the classroom floor -- that a new leader only reaches by asking early."
Handover -- the formal passing of a role, its context, and its unfinished work from one person to the next
"None of this was in the handover document, and none of it could have been."
To hit the ground running -- to be productive immediately in a new role, with no slow start
"Everyone expects a senior hire to hit the ground running, but the smartest ones slow down first to speed up later."
To take the reins -- to formally assume control and responsibility for something
"On Monday she takes the reins of the Canadian division, and the first decision is to make no big decision for ninety days."
To size up -- to assess a person or a situation quickly and shrewdly
"Size up the culture before you try to move it."
To lean on -- to rely on someone for support or expertise
"Lean on Gordon. He made the fast mistakes so you do not have to."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

On her last day in the role, Gordon left Danielle a voice note about the one thing the handover document could not carry. He admitted that he had restructured the academic team in his first month, and he told her that he had been wrong about almost all of it — because he had changed the answer before he understood the question. His advice was the opposite of his instinct: he urged her to decide nothing for ninety days and to spend the ramp-up period on a listening tour instead.

So she did. Over eleven weeks she sat with the frontline staff, not just the directors, and asked what was genuinely broken. One coordinator explained that the last three reorganizations had each been announced as urgent and then quietly reversed, so the team had learned to wait out any new plan. Another admitted that morale was low, but insisted that the cause was not the workload — it was that nobody in head office had ever asked them anything before deciding. A regional director warned her not to mistake their politeness for agreement, and pointed out that a Canadian team will rarely say no to your face.

None of this could have reached her except from one vantage point — the classroom floor. The temptation the whole time was to act, because a mandate feels like something you are supposed to spend, and every week she did not restructure anything felt like wasted authority. But credibility, she was learning, is not spent with the title. It is earned in the first quarter, one honest conversation at a time.

By day eighty-nine she had a smaller, sharper plan than the one she would have written on day one. She had learned to lean on Gordon, whose early mistakes were now her shortcuts, and to size up a culture before trying to move it. Her entry plan had three phases — learn, decide, act — and she was still, deliberately, in the first. When a stakeholder asked why she had not fixed anything yet, she no longer heard a threat. She heard her cue to say, plainly, that she was still listening, on purpose, and that the listening was the work.

1. Gordon “told her that he had been wrong” — why told her and not said her?
A Because tell takes a person object (tell someone), while say does not: you tell her that... but you say that... (or say to her).
B Because “said” cannot be used in the past tense at all.
C Because “told” is only correct when the sentence is a question.
2. The coordinator “explained that the reorganizations had been reversed”. Why had been and not were?
A Because “reorganizations” is plural, which forces the past perfect.
B Because reported speech backshifts one step: an event already past at the moment of speaking moves from were reversed to had been reversed.
C Because the passive voice always requires “had” in English.
3. Why is “He admitted that he had been wrong” stronger, in a debrief, than “He said that he had been wrong”?
A Because “admitted” is more formal and formality is always better.
B Because the reporting verb names how it was said: admit tells you he conceded a fault, which say would leave out. Choosing the verb is choosing the meaning.
C Because “said” is grammatically incorrect before “that”.
4. According to the text, how is credibility earned in a new senior role?
A It arrives automatically with the title and the mandate.
B By changing something visible in the first two weeks to look decisive.
C In the first quarter, one honest conversation at a time — by listening before acting, not by spending the mandate fast.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Reported Speech & Reporting Verbs

GRAMMAR

The grammar of the leader who relays what a room told her — precisely, and without turning someone else's words into her own claim. In a listening tour, reporting well IS the work.

FormUseExample
Backshift
verb + that + clause, one tense back		Reporting a past moment, each tense steps back: is→was, was→had been.“Morale is low” → She said that morale was low. · “It was reversed” → He explained it had been reversed.
Say vs telltell takes a person (tell me); say does not (say that).He told me that... · He said that... · NOT “he said me”.
Verb + object + to-infinitiveurge / advise / warn / ask / tell + someone + to + base verb. Negative: not to.He urged me to slow down. · He warned me not to restructure.
Verb + -ing / thatsuggest / recommend take a gerund or a that-clause — never to + verb.She recommended waiting. · She suggested that I wait. · NOT “recommended to wait”.
Choose the reporting verbadmit (concede a fault) · insist (hold firm) · point out (note a fact) · warn (flag a risk) · deny (reject) · explain (give the reason). The verb carries the attitude that say leaves out.
Why it sounds seniorRelaying a listening tour with the right reporting verbs shows you heard people precisely, without turning their words into your own verdict. “A director warned me not to mistake politeness for agreement” is exact and attributed; “they all agree” is your assumption — and the wrong one.

Watch out (the number one slip): say takes no person and tell requires one. “He said me” → “He told me” or “He said that”.

Watch out (the other slip): after recommend / suggest comes the -ing form or a that-clause, never to. “recommended to wait” → “recommended waiting”.

The line that wins the room: the precise reporting verb plus a clean backshift. “One coordinator explained that the reorganizations had all been reversed, so the team learned to wait — and another insisted that the real problem was never being asked.”

Stage 2: Put the First 90 Days in Order

Order

Put the five moves in the order a new leader runs the first quarter — listen first, decide last.

?Report back precisely what people said, using reported speech, without inventing a verdict of your own.
?On day ninety, present a smaller, sharper plan built on what you heard, not on what you assumed on day one.
?Build a simple entry plan with three phases — learn, decide, act — and commit to staying in phase one.
?Earn credibility before spending the mandate — resist changing anything visible just to look decisive.
?Run a listening tour with the frontline staff, not just the directors, and ask what you must not touch.
