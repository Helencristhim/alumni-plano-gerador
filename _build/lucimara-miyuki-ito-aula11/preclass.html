Lesson 11 -- Pre-class

Navigating Airports and Flights -- Check-in to Boarding (Consolidation)

A consolidation night at the airport: no new rule, just the words of the gate and the confidence to use what you already know. Key words: gate agent, overhead bin, aisle seat, window seat, boarding group, priority boarding, standby, customs declaration, tarmac, jet bridge, hold up, transit visa. Grammar review: the first conditional for real airport problems -- "If the flight is delayed, I'll ask for a voucher." Present simple after if, will in the result -- plus request modals (could, should, need to) for asking clearly at the desk.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Take your time -- listen twice, then say it out loud. These are the twelve words that live between the check-in desk and the boarding gate.

Gate agent -- the airline employee at the departure gate who checks tickets and helps passengers
"The gate agent scanned my boarding pass and pointed me to the jet bridge."
Overhead bin -- the closed storage space above the seats for your carry-on bag
"There was no room left in the overhead bin, so I put my bag under the seat."
Aisle seat -- the seat next to the walkway, easy to stand up and stretch
"I always book an aisle seat so I can stretch my legs on a long flight."
Window seat -- the seat next to the window, good for the view and for sleeping
"She prefers a window seat so she can sleep against the wall."
Boarding group -- the number or letter that tells you when it is your turn to board
"They called boarding group three, so it was finally my turn."
Priority boarding -- the right to get on the plane early, before most passengers
"My card gives me priority boarding, so I never fight for bin space."
Standby -- waiting for an open seat on a full flight, without a confirmed one
"I missed my flight, so they put me on standby for the next one."
Customs declaration -- the form where you list what you are bringing into a country
"I filled out the customs declaration before we landed in New York."
Tarmac -- the paved area outside where planes park, taxi, and take off
"We sat on the tarmac for an hour before we finally took off."
Jet bridge -- the covered walkway that connects the gate to the airplane door
"We walked down the jet bridge straight onto the plane."
Hold up -- to delay or stop someone, often used as “we were held up”
"We were held up at customs because of a long line."
Transit visa -- a document that lets you pass through a country on your way to another
"For a long layover in China, you may need a transit visa."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text slowly, twice, and then answer the questions.

Lucimara has done this trip many times, so she plans out loud. If her flight to Paris is delayed, she 'll ask the gate agent about her connection to Lyon. Notice the shape: present simple after if -- is, not will be -- and will in the result.

At the desk, she asks for what she wants, clearly and politely. "I'd like an aisle seat, please." If her carry-on is too big for the overhead bin, the gate agent will check it for free. If the flight is full, she 'll go on standby for the next one.

She listens for her boarding group, and she watches the board. If the gate changes, she 'll walk to the new one right away. When her group is called, she goes down the jet bridge with her boarding pass ready.

A year ago, a delay or a gate change would have frozen her. Now she has a plan for each one, in English. The rules are not new -- the first conditional, "could you," "should I" -- but the airport is where she finally uses them without stopping to translate.

1. "If the flight is delayed, I'll ask for a voucher." Which part is correct?
A Present simple in both parts.
B Present simple after if, and will in the result. That is the first conditional.
C Will in both parts.
2. Which sentence is correct?
A "If the flight will be delayed, I'll ask for a voucher."
B "If the flight is delayed, I'll ask for a voucher." The if part stays in the present.
C "If the flight is delayed, I ask for a voucher tomorrow."
3. Your carry-on is too big for the overhead bin. What is most likely to happen?
A You must leave the bag behind.
B The gate agent checks it for free, and you pick it up at baggage claim.
C You cannot board the flight.
4. The flight is full and you don't have a seat. What does the agent put you on?
A Priority boarding.
B Standby -- you wait for an open seat on that flight.
C A transit visa.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- First Conditional (Review)

GRAMMAR

You met the first conditional earlier in the program. Here it is again, for the airport: a real, likely future. Present simple after if, and will in the result.

PartFormExample
The conditionIf + present simpleIf the flight is delayed,
The resultwill / 'll + base verbI 'll ask for a voucher.
Either orderresult + if (no comma)I'll check the board if the gate changes.
The one ideaA real, likely future. Present simple after if, will in the result -- never will after if.

Watch out -- the classic mistake: putting will in the if part. It is "If the flight is delayed...", never "If the flight will be delayed...". The will belongs in the result only. To ask at the desk, add a modal: "Could you check my bag?", "Should I stay near the gate?", "I need to make my connection."

Stage 2: At the Gate, in Order

Order

This is how you get from the check-in desk to your seat -- in the order that actually happens, from arriving to boarding.

?Ask for your seat: "I'd like an aisle seat near the front, please."
?Give your destination: "I'm flying to Lyon, with a layover in Paris."
?Board: when your group is called, go down the jet bridge with your boarding pass ready.
?Sort out the bag: "If it doesn't fit in the overhead bin, could you check it?"

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer -- speak for about 90 seconds, with no script.

Imagine you are at the airport, checking in for your flight to Lyon with a layover in Paris. Narrate the whole thing out loud: give your destination, ask for an aisle seat, deal with your carry-on, and then plan for problems with the first conditional -- "If the flight is delayed, I'll...", "If I miss my connection, I'll...", "If the gate changes, I'll...". Use at least four of today's new words and at least three first-conditional sentences. Ask, don't demand. Take your time and do not read from a script.
