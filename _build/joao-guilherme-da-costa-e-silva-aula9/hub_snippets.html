Say That Again -- The Commissioning Call at Full Speed

A British project director, forty minutes and a commissioning schedule at native speed: how to FORECAST with a date attached (what will be DONE vs. what will be HAPPENING), where the tense hides in fast speech (the auxiliary collapses, the participle survives), and the three sentences that stop the call without losing face. Key words: integration test, site readiness, look-ahead schedule, open action, outstanding item, provisional acceptance, final acceptance, defects liability period, ramp-up, interface management, dry run, to phase out, to factor in, to run something by someone. Structure: future perfect and future continuous (By the twelfth we will have confirmed... / This time next week we will be running...).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

In Lesson 8 you wrote the question with all the time in the world. Now the last phase of the project arrives SPOKEN, fast, and at somebody else's pace. These are the words of the final stretch: who accepts, when, and what is still open. Listen to each term and read the example.

Integration test -- the test that proves the separate systems talk to each other -- not that each one works alone
"The integration test between the interlocking and the control center starts on the fifteenth."
Site readiness -- whether the place itself -- power, access, safety -- can actually receive the equipment and the crews
"I will confirm site readiness on the twelfth, once the power supply is energized."
Look-ahead schedule -- the short window of the plan: the next three weeks, activity by activity, in detail
"The three-week look-ahead schedule shows the integration test starting on the fifteenth."
Open action -- a task with a name on it and a date on it, and it is not closed yet
"By Friday we will have closed every open action from the last call."
Outstanding item -- something that was agreed, is still not done, and is still on the list at the end of the meeting
"Three outstanding items remain, and none of them can wait until the ramp-up."
Provisional acceptance -- the client takes the system into service -- with a list of defects still attached to it
"Provisional acceptance is planned for the end of the third quarter."
Final acceptance -- nothing is left open, the warranty period is over, and the last payment is released
"Final acceptance will not be granted until every outstanding item is closed."
Defects liability period -- the window after handover in which the supplier still fixes, at its own cost, whatever fails
"The defects liability period runs for twelve months between provisional and final acceptance."
Ramp-up -- the gradual climb from the first test run to full service, week by week
"By the time the ramp-up begins in November, we will have trained every operator."
Interface management -- making two suppliers' systems meet without a gap -- and owning the gap when there is one
"Interface management between signaling and rolling stock sits with me."
Dry run -- a full rehearsal of the real operation, with nothing at stake and everybody watching
"This time next week we will be running a dry run of the failover procedure."
To phase out -- to remove something gradually, on a planned date, until it is gone for good
"The manual workarounds will be phased out before provisional acceptance, not after."
To factor in -- to include something in the calculation BEFORE you commit to the date
"Have you factored in the night-shift restrictions on the site?"
To run something by someone -- to repeat or check something with somebody, so that they can follow it or approve it
"Could you run the acceptance dates by me one more time?"

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

The call starts at eight and Fiona Hartley, the project director in London, has another one at half past. She goes straight in. Where are we on site readiness? Because by the twelfth she needs the power supply energized, or the integration test does not happen. She speaks the way people speak when they are late: the auxiliaries collapse, will have becomes will'uv, will be almost disappears, and the dates arrive in the middle of long sentences with no emphasis at all.

So João Guilherme does the thing that feels wrong and is right: he stops her. I want to make sure I am following — the deadline you are referring to is the twelfth, and that is for site readiness, not for the test itself. She says "Correct" and carries on. Five seconds. Then he forecasts, and every sentence carries a date. By the fifteenth we will have energized the power supply and closed the two open actions on the cable route. We will be working nights from the eighth to hold that date. This time next week we will be running a dry run of the failover procedure. By the time the ramp-up starts in November, every operator will have been trained.

Then comes the part that decides the money. Provisional acceptance at the end of the third quarter; final acceptance twelve months later, with the defects liability period running in between. And the manual workarounds, Fiona says, get phased out BEFORE provisional acceptance, not after — she has seen too many projects where the temporary fix quietly becomes the design. João asks her to run the acceptance dates by him one more time, because he wants to factor them in before he sends the three-week look-ahead schedule. And he closes the call the way a call has to be closed: Just to confirm — are you saying that no workaround survives provisional acceptance? Then by the thirtieth we will have cleared every outstanding item on that list. He did not understand every word she said. He understood every date — and that is the entire job.

1. "By the fifteenth we will have energized the power supply." What exactly does this structure promise?
A That the energization will HAPPEN on the fifteenth.
B That the energization will be COMPLETE BEFORE the fifteenth — will have + past participle is the tense of the DEADLINE, and the deadline is what the contract reads.
C That the energization begins on the fifteenth and carries on after that.
2. "This time next week we will be running a dry run." Why will be running and not will run?
A Because will be running sounds more formal.
B Because the action will be IN PROGRESS at that moment — neither finished nor starting. Will run would sound like a decision taken on the spot.
C Because English does not allow the simple future after next week.
3. "By the time the ramp-up starts, the workarounds will have been phased out." Why starts and not will start?
A Because after by the time / when / before / as soon as, English writes the future in the PRESENT. "By the time it will start" does not exist.
B Because the ramp-up has already begun.
C Because start is an irregular verb.
4. Fiona drops the new date in the MIDDLE of a long sentence, with no emphasis at all. What is the professional reaction?
A Note down whatever you managed to catch and check it later by email, so you do not look slow.
B Stop her and confirm it out loud: "Just to confirm — are you saying that provisional acceptance has moved to the twenty-third?" Five seconds there save three weeks of work toward the wrong date.
C Carry on with the call and ask her to speak more slowly next time.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Future Perfect & Future Continuous

GRAMMAR

The two tenses a project is run with — and why they almost disappear in fast speech.

StructureWhat it promisesOn the call
will have + past participle
future perfect		DONE before that point in the future. This is the tense of the DEADLINE — the one the contract reads, and the one somebody can hold you to."By the twelfth we will have confirmed site readiness."
will be + verb -ing
future continuous		IN PROGRESS at that moment: not finished, not starting — happening. The simple future would sound like a decision taken on the spot."This time next week we will be running the dry run."
will have been + verb -ing
future perfect continuous		It measures the DURATION built up to that point. This is the one that justifies a cost or an extension of time."By September the crews will have been working nights for six weeks."
by vs. untilBY = the deadline (no later than). UNTIL = the whole duration. Portuguese covers both with a single word, which is why Brazilian engineers write "until Friday we will have finished" — a sentence that means nothing in English."By Friday" (deadline) · "We will be testing until Friday" (duration)
by the time + PRESENTAfter by the time / when / before / as soon as / until, the future is written in the PRESENT. "By the time the ramp-up will start" DOES NOT exist."By the time the ramp-up starts, the workarounds will have been phased out."
How it SOUNDSNobody says these tenses the way they are written. will have becomes /wɪləv/ ("will'uv"), we will have becomes "we'll've", and will be almost vanishes. The tense itself does NOT vanish with it: it survives in the PARTICIPLE, at the end of the sentence (...we'll've CONFIRMED it). Stop hunting for the auxiliary you were taught at school: listen for the LAST word.

Watch out (three classic mistakes): (1) "Until the twelfth we will have confirmed..." — it is BY the twelfth: until is duration, by is a deadline; (2) "By the time the test will start..." — after by the time you use the PRESENT: starts; (3) "At ten we will run the test" when you mean that the test will be under way — the right form is we will be running: the simple future sounds like a decision made at that very instant.

Commissioning collocations: confirm site readiness (never "guarantee") · close an open action (never "finish") · clear the outstanding items · grant provisional acceptance (the client GRANTS it; the supplier OBTAINS it) · phase out a workaround · factor in a constraint (NEVER "consider in") · run a dry run · manage the interfaces.

Clarifying under pressure (learn these three by heart): "Could you run that by me one more time?" · "Just to confirm — are you saying that...?" · "I want to make sure I'm following: the deadline you're referring to is..." — none of them is an apology. They are what a native speaker says when a date gets past him, and they cost five seconds.

Stage 2: Put the Conference Call in Order

Order

Put in the right order the steps of somebody who never loses a date on a fast call — from catching the information she buried to committing with the right tense.

?Catch the date she buried in the middle of the sentence: provisional acceptance has moved to the twenty-third.
?Say what will be happening, not finished: "We will be working nights from the eighth to hold that date."
?Factor in what she cannot see: the night-shift restrictions on site.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each real moment of the call — including the one where she buries the new date in the middle of a long sentence.

Fiona opens the call at full speed: "Where are we on site readiness? By the twelfth I need that power supply energized." You reply:
A "I want to make sure I'm following -- the deadline you're referring to is the twelfth, and that is for site readiness, not for the integration test itself. Is that right?"
B "Sorry, could you speak more slowly, please? My English is not very good."
C "Yes, yes, no problem, everything is on track."
She asks for the status. The strongest answer is:
A "We are working on it and I think we will finish before the test."
B "By the fifteenth we will have energized the power supply and closed the two open actions on the cable route, and we will be working nights from the eighth to hold that date."
C "The site will be ready, do not worry about that."
Halfway through a long sentence she says that provisional acceptance has moved to the twenty-third. You:
A Write it down and check it later against her email, so as not to interrupt her.
B Stop her: "Just to confirm -- are you saying that provisional acceptance has moved to the twenty-third? Then by the twenty-third we will have cleared every outstanding item."
C Assume you misheard the date and carry on with the call as usual.
"Let us just keep the manual workarounds until final acceptance, to be safe." You push back:
A "The workarounds are phased out before provisional acceptance, not after. A temporary fix with no end date quietly becomes the design, and the operator pays to maintain it for thirty years."
B "All right, if that is easier for your team, we can keep them."
C "I do not have the authority to decide that."
She asks whether you still hold the date, even with the night-shift restriction on site. You say:
A "Yes, of course, we will make it work somehow."
B "I need to factor in the night-shift restrictions before I commit to that date. You will have the three-week look-ahead schedule tonight, and the date will be in it."
C "That is impossible. Nobody can work with these restrictions."
