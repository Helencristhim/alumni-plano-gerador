Lesson 9 -- Say That Again: The Commissioning Call at Full Speed

Movie
Apollo 13 (1995, Ron Howard) — 140 min · available in Brazil (see where through the link)

The whole movie is your lesson: engineers listening to technical English, fast, over noise, with no right to get a date or a number wrong. Watch the PROTOCOL they use — the one you practiced today: nobody ever just says "ok". They READ BACK what they heard — "Say again?", "Copy that, two amps", "Just to confirm, we're powering down the LEM?" — and only then act. Watch, too, how mission control forecasts with a date attached, exactly the way Fiona does: "By the time they hit the atmosphere, we'll have burned the last of it". That whole room does, under maximum pressure, what you will be doing on your next call with Motiva.

Tip: watch it with ENGLISH subtitles and write down 10 moments where somebody confirms before acting. Then turn the subtitles off and watch the re-entry checklist scene again (from the 100-minute mark): how many numbers do you catch on the first listen? Finally, write THREE sentences of your own with will have + past participle and THREE with will be + -ing, about the commissioning schedule of Line 5 itself.

Podcast
Freakonomics Radio — "Here's Why All Your Projects Are Always Late (and What to Do About It)" (~45 min, free)

Native English, real pace, your subject: why EVERY project runs late — and why the fault lies not with the supplier, but with the way people FORECAST the future. They explain the "planning fallacy": the human brain estimates the best-case deadline and forgets to factor in everything that always happens. In other words, the antidote is exactly today's grammar — you stop saying "we will finish" and start saying "by the twenty-third we will have closed every outstanding item", with a date somebody can hold you to. This is the most valuable listening input of the week: native speed, no subtitles, about your own job.

Tip: listen ONCE with no pauses and no transcript, at 1x (never 0.75x — that is the whole point of this lesson). Write down only the NUMBERS and the DATES you managed to catch. Then listen again with the transcript from the website open, and count how many you missed. Finally, write 5 sentences applying the "planning fallacy" to your own Line 5 schedule, using will have + past participle in all five.

The grammar of this lesson at exactly your level, from the same channel as the videos in Lessons 6, 7 and 8. He puts will be + -ing side by side with will have + past participle and with the simple future, and shows why the choice changes what you are PROMISING — which is precisely what decides whether your commitment on the call is an intention or a date. Watch it AFTER the lesson: it will sound obvious, and that is the idea.

Tip: after the video, take the REAL schedule of your next three-week window at Motiva and write 8 sentences: four with will have + past participle (what will be DONE on each date) and four with will be + -ing (what will be HAPPENING on that day). Record them out loud, fast, running the auxiliary together ("we'll've closed'em by Friday") — producing the reduced form is what trains your ear to catch it on the next call.
