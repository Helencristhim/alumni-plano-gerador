Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the language of the People Analytics brief you present to the Canadian Academic Council — the difference between a chart and a decision.

Data storytelling -- turning a table of numbers into a claim a decision-maker can actually act on
"Data storytelling is not decoration. It is the discipline of making a number say the one true thing it can, and no more."
Correlation -- two things moving together, which is not yet a reason for one another
"Engagement rose while turnover fell, but that is a correlation. The mentorship program is not proven by it."
Causation -- one thing actually producing another, which a chart can hint at but never prove
"Causation is the claim a room of researchers will make you earn. A line on a slide is not enough to earn it."
Confounding variable -- a hidden third factor driving both numbers, and the first thing a researcher looks for
"The reorganization is the confounding variable here: it landed in the same quarter, and either event could be moving the numbers."
Statistical significance -- the test of whether a result is real or just the noise of a small sample
"At this sample size I would not claim statistical significance. I would call it directional and ask for a second read."
Sample size -- how many people the number actually rests on, and the fastest way to overclaim
"Before you defend the finding, defend the sample size. Thirty responses cannot carry a company-wide claim."
Outlier -- the single extreme case that quietly drags an average somewhere it should not go
"One team tripled its score. That team is an outlier, and it skews the mean more than it should."
Margin of error -- the honest band around a number that amateurs drop and researchers demand
"Keep the margin of error on the slide. It makes the number look smaller and makes you look like you have done this before."
Leading indicator -- a signal that moves before the outcome does, so you can act while it still matters
"Treat the onboarding window as a leading indicator, rather than waiting for turnover to confirm the problem a quarter too late."
Actionable insight -- a finding a manager can do something with on Monday, not just admire on a slide
"The actionable insight does not depend on the causal claim. It is the one thing a manager can act on this week."
To control for -- to hold a variable steady so it cannot secretly explain your result
"Once we control for tenure, the gap narrows but does not close, and it is that residual gap I am reporting."
To skew -- to pull a distribution to one side, usually through an outlier or a bad sample
"Drop the outlier and the effect shrinks but survives. Left in, it skews the whole story toward the flattering explanation."
To read too much into -- to draw a bigger conclusion than the data can honestly carry
"The fastest way to lose a research audience is to read too much into a chart that shows correlation and call it proof."
To tease out -- to separate a real signal from the noise around it
"After all the honesty, I can still tease out the one signal that survives every rival explanation."
To connect the dots -- to turn separate findings into one coherent story
"My job is to connect the dots into a single actionable insight, not to make each number say more than it can."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

The most dangerous sentence in any analytics deck is the confident one. Two lines move together on the screen — engagement climbs, turnover falls — and the temptation is to stand up and announce that the mentorship program did it. In front of a room of researchers, that is the sentence that ends the presentation. Two numbers moving together is a correlation, and a correlation is not yet a cause. It is estimated that turnover among new managers fell nine points last quarter, and it is tempting to read too much into that — but the honest analyst asks what else changed in the same window.

Almost always, the answer is a confounding variable. Here, the reorganization landed in exactly the quarter the mentorship launched, and it is thought to have shifted the workload of half the teams. Either event could be moving the numbers, and no chart can separate them. So you name the variable you could not control for out loud, before anyone else does. Turnover is believed to be linked to onboarding, you say — not caused by it. The distinction is the whole of your credibility.

Then comes the discipline. One team tripled its score after a change of manager; that team is an outlier, and it will skew the average until you set it aside. You check the sample size, and you keep the margin of error on the slide even though it makes the number look smaller. Statistical significance is reported to matter less to executives than it should — which is precisely why keeping it marks you as someone who has done this before.

And still there is something true to say. You tease out the one signal that survives every rival explanation, and you call it what it is: a leading indicator, a thing that moves before the outcome does, not a proof. New joiners, for instance, are twice as likely as tenured staff to leave in the first year, and that gap holds even after you control for the reorganization. You connect the dots into a single actionable insight — watch the onboarding window monthly, rather than waiting for turnover to confirm the problem a quarter too late. The number never speaks for itself. Your job is to make it say the one true thing it can.

1. Why do we say “It is estimated that turnover fell nine points” and not “It is estimated turnover to fall nine points”?
A Because the impersonal report takes that + a full clause with a finite verb (fell), not an infinitive. “It is estimated” is followed by a whole sentence, not by “to fall”.
B Because “estimate” cannot be used in the passive voice in English.
C Because the number is plural, which forces the infinitive.
2. “Turnover is thought to be linked to onboarding.” — why is this stronger, in that room, than “Onboarding causes turnover”?
A Because the passive is always more polite than the active, regardless of meaning.
B Because the reporting passive states a finding without claiming causation as fact — it attributes the link to the evidence, which is exactly what a research audience demands.
C Because “causes” is grammatically incorrect after the word onboarding.
3. According to the text, why keep the margin of error on the slide when it makes the number look smaller?
A Because researchers cannot read a chart without one printed on it.
B Because keeping it marks you as someone who has done this before, rather than someone overclaiming a fragile number.
C Because the margin of error is what proves causation once and for all.
4. What does the writer say is left to present, once you have refused to overclaim?
A Nothing — an honest analyst has no conclusion to offer.
B The raw correlation, presented louder and with more confidence.
C The one signal that survives every rival explanation — a leading indicator you connect into a single actionable insight a manager can act on.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Reporting Passives & Data Comparatives

GRAMMAR

The grammar of the analyst who states a finding without owning it as fact: you attribute the claim to the evidence, which is exactly the register a room of researchers trusts.

FormUseExample
Impersonal report
It is + reported/estimated/thought + that + clause		State a finding as the field's, not yours. Note: that + a full clause with a finite verb.It is estimated that turnover rose by twelve percent.
Personal report
subject + is thought/believed to + base verb		Raise the subject to the front. After to comes the base verb: be, reflect, drive.Turnover is thought to be linked to onboarding.
Perfect report
is + reported to have + past participle		The result reaches back into the past — it already happened.Retention is reported to have improved since March.
Why it sounds senior“Onboarding causes turnover” claims causation as fact — and a researcher will destroy it. “Turnover is thought to be linked to onboarding” states the same finding while conceding it is a reading of evidence, not a proof. That concession IS your credibility.
Data comparativesCompare two groups precisely, without overclaiming size.New joiners are twice as likely as tenured staff to leave · a threefold increase · significantly higher than the benchmark
Collocationcontrol for a variable · tease out a signal · read too much into a chart · an outlier skews the average · connect the dots into an insight

Watch out (the number one slip): the impersonal report needs that + a finite clause. “It is estimated turnover to rise” is a word-for-word import; the English is “It is estimated that turnover rose”.

Watch out (the other slip): after “is thought to” comes the base verb, not the -ing form. “is thought to reflecting” → “is thought to reflect”.

The line that wins the room: a reporting passive plus the honest caveat, in one sentence. “The drop is believed to be linked to the reorganization, which I could not fully control for — so I am reporting it as a leading indicator, not a cause.”

Quiz

Dr. Prescott says: “Your two lines move together, so the mentorship program raised engagement. Yes?” The most senior answer is:
A “Yes. The data clearly proves the program caused the improvement.”
B “It is a correlation I cannot yet attribute — the reorganization landed in the same quarter. What I can defend is a leading indicator, not a cause.”
C “I am not sure. Maybe it did, maybe it did not.”

A “That team is an outlier and it skews the average on this sample size. Set it aside and the effect shrinks but survives — that is the version I would act on.”
