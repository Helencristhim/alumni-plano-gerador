Film
Moneyball (2011, Netflix)

Billy Beane rebuilds a baseball team on data that everyone else in the room dismisses as noise — and the whole film is the fight you will have at the Academic Council: a number-driven finding versus a room that trusts its gut. Watch how the analyst wins not by overclaiming, but by being precise about what the data can and cannot say. Connection to Lesson 10: notice every time someone confuses a correlation with a causation, and every time Beane refuses to read too much into a single game — the outlier — and holds the pattern instead.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Write down THREE moments where a number changes a decision, and rewrite each one as a reporting passive for your own council (“It is estimated that...”, “is thought to be linked to...”).

Podcast
Freakonomics Radio — “How to Be Less Terrible at Predicting the Future”

The show that made a career out of the sentence “correlation is not causation” turns to the researchers who forecast honestly: the ones who state a probability, keep the margin of error visible, and refuse to overclaim. It is the exact register Dr. Prescott is testing you for. Connection to Lesson 10: listen for how the best forecasters attribute their claims to the evidence (“it is estimated that...”, “is thought to...”) instead of asserting them as fact — and how naming what they cannot know is what makes them trusted.

Tip: listen once with no pauses. Then summarize one idea out loud in ninety seconds, using TWO reporting passives and one data comparative (“twice as likely as”, “a threefold increase”). Record it and check: did you claim a cause, or report a link?

YouTube
Hans Rosling — “The best stats you've ever seen” (TED, 20 min)

The definitive lesson in data storytelling: Rosling takes numbers that everyone assumes they understand and makes them say something true and surprising — without ever overclaiming what a single data point means. This is the skill of Lesson 10 in its purest form: turning a table into a story a room can act on. Connection to Lesson 10: watch how he separates a real signal from noise, teases out the one pattern that matters, and hands the audience a leading indicator rather than a verdict.

Tip: watch it twice. On the second pass, write your own thirty-second “numbers talk” about one finding from your work — state the correlation, name the confounding variable you could not control for, and end with one actionable insight. Bring it to Lesson 11.
