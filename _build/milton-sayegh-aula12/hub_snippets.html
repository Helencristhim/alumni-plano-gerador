Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the briefing and answer the questions. / Leia o briefing e responda.

Before a trip to New York, a buyer briefed Milton on local etiquette. "Punctuality is strict," she said. "You are supposed to be seated when the meeting starts, so you should arrive five minutes early." She explained that the small talk is short, and that directness is read as courtesy, not rudeness: "You ought to state your price clearly." Then a warning: "You had better not undersell your work — be assertive about its value." She added that hierarchy is less visible there, and that good networking turns into real rapport only if you follow up fast.

1. Why does she say "You are supposed to be seated" (not "you should be seated")?
A It is an expected cultural rule, not just her personal advice.
B "Supposed to" and "should" mean exactly the same thing.
C "Supposed to" is only used for the past.
2. What does "You had better not undersell your work" add to the advice?
A Nothing — it is the same as "you can undersell".
B A warning — there is a bad consequence if he ignores it.
C A polite request for permission.
3. Which sentence has the correct form?
A "You should to arrive early."
B "You ought to arrive early."
C "You had better to arrive early."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Modals of Advice & Expectation

GRAMMAR

Use should / ought to for advice, be supposed to for an expected rule, and had better for advice with a warning.

FormMeaningExample
should / ought to + baseAdvice"You should confirm the time." / "You ought to be early."
be supposed to + baseExpected rule"You're supposed to RSVP before Friday." (the norm)
had better (not) + baseAdvice + warning"You'd better not be late — they will notice."
Watch the traps: after "should" and "had better" use the base verb with NO "to" ("you should go", not "should to go"). "ought TO" and "supposed TO" keep the "to". "should/ought to" = a good idea; "be supposed to" = what is expected of you; "had better" = strong advice with an implied consequence. PT: use SHOULD / OUGHT TO para conselho ("Voce deveria chegar cedo"); BE SUPPOSED TO para uma regra esperada ("Voce deve / e esperado que confirme presenca ate sexta"); HAD BETTER para conselho com aviso ("E melhor voce nao se atrasar -- eles vao reparar"). Nunca use "to" depois de should ou had better; mas mantenha o "to" em ought to e supposed to.

Lesson 12 -- Cultural Intelligence: Brazilian vs American Business

Series
Suits (USA Network) -- any boardroom negotiation scene

A masterclass in American business directness: short small talk, then straight to the point, with people stating their value assertively and without apology. Watch how courtesy and bluntness coexist — exactly the balance you need with a New York buyer.

Tip: pause on one direct line and rewrite it as advice: "You should...", "You're supposed to...", "You'd better...".

Podcast
HBR IdeaCast (Harvard Business Review) -- any episode on cross-cultural business or Erin Meyer's "The Culture Map"

Practical, research-based talk on how trust, directness, and time work differently across cultures — including Brazil and the U.S. The exact framework behind this class: when directness is courtesy, and how relationship-first and results-first cultures meet in the middle.

Tip: note one difference between your culture and an American one, then say the advice aloud with a modal.

YouTube
BBC Learning English -- "should, ought to and had better" explained

A short, clear lesson on exactly the grammar from this class: the difference between giving advice (should / ought to) and warning someone (had better), with the base verb and no extra "to". Native speakers model it in natural contexts.

Tip: after watching, give five pieces of advice out loud about your own export work — mix should, ought to, supposed to, and had better.
