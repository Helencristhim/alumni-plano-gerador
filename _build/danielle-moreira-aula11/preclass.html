Lesson 11 -- Pre-class

The Turnaround Story -- Narrating What Had Already Changed

Telling the board the turnaround you led in Brazil — and proving it was a build, not luck. The tool is the past perfect: it lets you narrate the SEQUENCE, so your action lands against a problem that was already there and your results arrive AFTER the groundwork was already laid. Key words: legacy, groundwork, tipping point, precedent, turnaround, entrenched, status quo, in hindsight, wake-up call, uphill battle, to inherit, to trace back to, to come to a head, to gain momentum, to reverse a trend. Structure: past perfect (had + past participle) and past perfect continuous (had been + -ing) — By the time..., had already... · had been climbing for two years · had traced back to onboarding before anyone named it. The simple past says WHAT happened; the past perfect proves it was a sequence, and the sequence is the proof.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the vocabulary of someone who narrates a recovery and makes the board believe she caused it.

Legacy -- what an earlier situation or leader leaves behind for you to deal with, good or bad
"The disengagement was not something I created; it was the legacy I inherited on my first day."
Groundwork -- the unglamorous preparation you do before anything visible can happen
"By the time the numbers moved, the groundwork had already been in place for two quarters, and it was the only reason the turnaround held."
Tipping point -- the moment a slow change suddenly becomes visible and hard to reverse
"The resignation was the tipping point: after it, the culture problem could no longer be treated as a rumor."
Precedent -- an earlier case that sets the pattern for how the next one is judged
"There was no precedent for a culture role in that office, so the first year was about proving the function should exist at all."
Turnaround -- the point at which a declining situation reverses and starts to recover
"The board wants to know whether the turnaround was leadership or luck, and the answer is in the sequence, not in the final number."
Entrenched -- so deeply established that it resists any attempt to change it
"The pattern was entrenched: it had been building for two years before anyone was willing to name it."
Status quo -- the existing state of things, especially the one people quietly prefer not to disturb
"Every turnaround is a fight with the status quo, and the status quo always had a head start."
In hindsight -- seeing clearly, after the fact, what was not obvious at the time
"In hindsight, I should have set the baseline earlier, before the culture had already deteriorated."
Wake-up call -- a shock that finally forces people to face a problem they had been ignoring
"Her exit interview was the wake-up call the leadership team had been avoiding for a year."
Uphill battle -- a struggle that is hard the whole way because everything is working against you
"Changing an entrenched culture is an uphill battle, and the first months move nothing you can measure."
To inherit -- to take over a situation, a team or a problem that somebody else created
"I did not build that crisis. I inherited it, and then I gave it a name nobody had dared to use."
To trace back to -- to follow a problem to its true origin, however far back that is
"The attrition traced back to onboarding, a decision made two years before the resignations started."
To come to a head -- (of a building problem) to reach the point where it can no longer be ignored
"The tension had been growing for months, and it came to a head the week our most senior teacher resigned."
To gain momentum -- to start moving faster and become harder to stop, once it has begun
"Once the new onboarding gained momentum, engagement climbed on its own, and my job became protecting it."
To reverse a trend -- to turn a line that was going the wrong way and make it go the right way
"We did not just slow the decline; we reversed the trend, and the exit interviews changed from warnings to thank-you notes."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Renata Alencar lost the room in the first minute, and she lost it by telling the truth in the wrong order. She had led a genuine turnaround in her last company — attrition halved, engagement recovered, a toxic pattern broken — and when the new board asked her to walk them through it, she said: “I joined, I redesigned onboarding, and within a year the numbers turned around.” Every word was true. It also sounded exactly like luck. Three events in a line, no cause between them, and a chair who had funded that story before and watched it die in a second country.

There is a way to tell the same three facts that proves the opposite, and it is not charisma — it is a tense. By the time she was even asked to fix it, the attrition had been climbing for two years, and nobody had traced it back to onboarding yet. That is the legacy she inherited; she did not create the crisis, she named it. Long before the numbers moved, she had rebuilt the onboarding, she had set up a quarterly pulse survey, and she had trained the leads on psychological safety. So when the most senior teacher resigned and the whole thing came to a head, the groundwork had already been in place for two quarters. The wake-up call did not catch her unprepared. It caught her ready.

Notice what the past perfect is actually doing, because it does the same job every time. It puts what was already there — the failing, the building, the preparing — before the simple-past event that everyone can see. “I redesigned onboarding and engagement rose” is two events that might be a coincidence. “I had redesigned onboarding before engagement rose” is a cause and an effect, in that order, and the order is the whole argument. The status quo was entrenched and it had a head start; changing it was an uphill battle; and the only way to prove you won it on purpose is to show the sequence — what had been failing, what you had built, and only then, what finally moved.

In hindsight, Renata said afterwards, the mistake was never the data. It was that she had handed the board a list when she should have handed them a timeline. A turnaround told in the simple past is a series of happy accidents. The same turnaround told with the past perfect underneath it is a method — and a method is the only thing that survives the trip to a country where you have not lived the culture yet, and where the groundwork will have to be laid again, from the very beginning.

1. Why does “I had redesigned onboarding before engagement rose” prove more than “I redesigned onboarding and engagement rose”?
A It does not prove more — the two sentences carry the same information, so they make the same claim.
B Because the past perfect fixes the ORDER: the cause (your action) is completed BEFORE the effect (the result). Two simple-past events side by side could be a coincidence; a past perfect before a simple past is a claim about cause.
C Because the past perfect makes the sentence more polite and less arrogant.
2. The number one mistake of this lesson: why is “By the time the board met, we already lost thirty teachers” wrong?
A Because an event completed BEFORE another past moment needs the past perfect: “we had already lost thirty teachers”. “By the time” is the classic trigger, and the simple past drops the sequence the whole story depends on.
B Because “by the time” can only be followed by the present tense.
C Because “lost” should be “loosed” in this context.
3. And mistake number two: why is “engagement had been dropped for eighteen months” wrong?
A Because past perfect continuous does not exist in American English.
B Because the correct form is “engagement was dropped for eighteen months”.
C Because “had been dropped” is a PASSIVE (somebody dropped it). For an action in progress over time you need the past perfect CONTINUOUS: “engagement had been dropping for eighteen months” — had been + -ing.
4. According to the text, what did Renata actually do wrong with the new board?
A She lied about the numbers, and the board found out the attrition had not really halved.
B She told three true facts as a flat list of simple-past events, with no sequence between them — so a real turnaround sounded like luck. She handed them a list when she should have handed them a timeline.
C She was arrogant and took too much personal credit for the recovery.
5. What does the text mean when it says a method — not a story of happy accidents — is what survives the trip to a second country?
A That the exact same actions, in the same order, will produce the same numbers anywhere.
B That what transfers to Canada is the SEQUENCE — diagnose the legacy, lay the groundwork, wait for the tipping point, reverse the trend — not the luck. In a culture she has not lived in, the groundwork has to be laid again from the beginning.
C That she should simply repeat the Brazil presentation word for word to the Canadian team.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Past Perfect & Past Perfect Continuous

GRAMMAR

The past perfect is the “before the before”. It puts the thing that was already true — the failing, the building, the preparing — in front of the simple-past event everyone can see. In a turnaround story, that ordering is not decoration: it is the difference between a sequence you caused and a coincidence you were standing next to.

StructureFormExample (your Brazil turnaround)
1. By the time + past simple, + past perfectBy the time X happened, Y had (already) + past participleBy the time the board reviewed it, attrition had already fallen.
2. Past perfect continuous (duration up to a past point)had been + -ing + for / sinceEngagement had been dropping for eighteen months.
3. Past perfect + before + past simpleS had + past participle ... before + past simpleI had rebuilt onboarding before the numbers moved.
4. already / never / just + past perfecthad already / never / just + past participleThe groundwork had already been in place for two quarters.
The three layers of a turnaroundDeep past (had been failing / had been building) → your build (had rebuilt, had set up) → the visible result (attrition fell, engagement rose). The past perfect owns the first two layers; the simple past owns the third.

Watch out (mistake number 1): after By the time, the earlier event needs the past perfect. “By the time the board met, we already lost thirty teachers” is WRONG → “we had already lost thirty teachers”. A first language may let you drop that earlier layer in speech; English does not, and dropping it here erases the very sequence your argument depends on.

Watch out (mistake number 2): had been + -ing (active, in progress) is not had been + past participle (passive, done to it). “Engagement had been dropped for eighteen months” says somebody dropped it. You mean it had been dropping. And use for + a period (for two years), since + a starting point (since 2023) — never “since two years”.

Why this matters to you: a turnaround told entirely in the simple past — “I arrived, I changed onboarding, retention improved” — is a to-do list, and a to-do list reads as luck. The same story with the past perfect underneath — “attrition had been climbing for two years; long before it moved, I had rebuilt onboarding” — is a method. The board is not testing whether your Brazil numbers are real. It is testing whether you can show the order, because the order is the only proof that it will happen again in Canada.

The three-move story: BACKDROP (what had already been failing, in the past perfect continuous) → BUILD (what you had put in place, in the past perfect, before any result) → REVERSAL (what finally moved, in the simple past). In that order, and the order is the argument. Never lead with the result.

Stage 2: Put the Turnaround Story in Order

Order

Put the five moves in the order in which you will narrate the Brazil turnaround to the board.

?Show the groundwork you had already laid, completed before any result appeared: I had rebuilt onboarding and set up a pulse survey months before the numbers moved.
?Close on the reversal and the method, not the luck: the trend reversed, and what I am bringing to Canada is the sequence, not the coincidence.
?Set the backdrop in the past perfect continuous: for two years before I arrived, attrition had been climbing and engagement had been falling.
?Mark the tipping point as a simple-past event against everything that was already ready: when it came to a head, the new system had already been running for a term.
