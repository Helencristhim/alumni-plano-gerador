Film
Moneyball (2011) — official trailer & film

This is your board review as a movie. Billy Beane runs a turnaround that everyone else calls luck, and the whole film is an argument about causation versus coincidence — the exact question Marcus Reid asks you tomorrow. Watch how the story is TOLD: the season that had already been lost, the system he had already built before the wins came, the tipping point in the middle. Connection to Lesson 11: the wins at the end mean nothing without the sequence in the middle. That sequence is the past perfect — what had been failing, and what he had built before anything moved.

Tip: watch the trailer here, then find the full film on your streaming service. As you watch, write ONE turnaround sentence about Beane in the past perfect (“By the time they won twenty in a row, he had already...”). Then rewrite it about your own Brazil year.

Podcast
Hidden Brain — “The Story of Your Life”

Shankar Vedantam and psychologist Dan McAdams show that the WAY you sequence your own history — not the facts of it — decides whether people hear growth or hear luck. It is the theory behind the most important idea in Lesson 11: the same three true events, told as a flat list or told as a build, deliver opposite verdicts. Connection to Lesson 11: what McAdams calls a redemption narrative, grammar calls the past perfect — the tense that puts the struggle BEFORE the recovery and makes the recovery earned.

Tip: listen once with no pauses. Then narrate your Brazil turnaround out loud in ninety seconds using THREE past-perfect structures (“By the time... had already...” / “had been climbing for...” / “long before the numbers moved, I had...”). Record it and check: did the backdrop come before the result, or after it?

YouTube
Tim Harford — “Trial, error and the God complex” (TED, 18 min)

The economist Tim Harford explains why real progress almost never looks like a single confident decision, and why the honest version of a success story is a messy sequence of things that had already been tried and had already failed before anything worked. It is the intellectual permission to tell your turnaround as a build rather than a triumph. Connection to Lesson 11: Harford is arguing, in economics, exactly what the past perfect argues in grammar — that the credible story is the one that shows the groundwork and the failures that came before the result.

Tip: watch it twice. On the second pass, write your own two-minute answer to Marcus Reid’s question (“how do you know it was you, and not the market?”) using the past perfect for everything you had built, and the words groundwork, to trace back to and to reverse a trend. Bring it in writing to Lesson 12.
