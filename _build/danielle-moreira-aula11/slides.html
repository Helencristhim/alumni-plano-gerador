Two Tellings, One Defeat

Both of These Lose the Board

THE FLAT LIST

“I joined, I redesigned onboarding, and within a year the numbers turned around. Attrition halved and engagement recovered.”

Every word is true. It is also three events in a line with no cause between them — and that is indistinguishable from luck.

THE OVERCLAIM

“It was entirely me. Before I arrived it was chaos, and I personally turned the whole culture around single-handedly.”

In a room of directors, certainty reads as inexperience. And a hero story does not survive the trip to a country she has never worked in.

The third version does not list the wins and does not claim the miracle. It tells the sequence: what had been failing before she arrived, what she had already built before anything moved, and only then, what finally turned. That sequence has a grammar, and it is tonight's.

to inherit

Chapter 4: The Code

The Tense That Proves the Sequence

By the time... had already... · had been climbing for two years · long before it moved, I had built...

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

By the time the board reviewed the numbers, attrition had already fallen for two quarters.

When I inherited the team, engagement had been dropping for eighteen months, and nobody had traced it back to onboarding yet.

Long before the numbers moved, I had rebuilt onboarding and set up a quarterly pulse survey.

When it came to a head, the new system had already been running for a term, so the wake-up call caught us ready.

Four sentences. In every single one, TWO things happened in the past — and the OLDER one is marked differently. Look at which event carries had. It is always the one that was already true before the visible moment.

StructureThe earlier event (had...)The visible moment (past simple)
By the time + past simple, + past perfectattrition had already fallenthe board reviewed the numbers.
had been + -ing (duration up to a past point)engagement had been dropping for 18 monthswhen I inherited the team.
had + past participle + before + past simpleI had rebuilt onboardingbefore the numbers moved.
already / never / just + past perfectthe system had already been runningwhen it came to a head.
The three layersDeep past (had been failing) → your build (had rebuilt) → the result (attrition fell). The past perfect owns the first two; the simple past owns the third.

And here is the part nobody teaches you. The past perfect is not about the past — it is about order. “I redesigned onboarding and engagement rose” is two events that might be a coincidence. “I had redesigned onboarding before engagement rose” is a cause and an effect, in that order. You are not decorating the sentence. You are making the claim that it happened because of you, and the tense is the only proof of the order.

Register

Same Facts, Luck or a Build

Nothing has been added. Nothing has been removed. Only the tense has changed.

This sounds like luck

“I joined, I changed the onboarding, and attrition fell.”

Three simple-past events in a line. No order, no cause. It could just as easily be a recovering market that lifted everyone.

This sounds like a build

“Attrition had been climbing for two years. Long before it moved, I had rebuilt onboarding. Only then did attrition fall.”

The board hears the order: problem first, your build second, result third. The market cannot claim a result that started before it did.

Under pressure, the instinct is the flat list — it is faster and it feels modest. It is also, structurally, a confession of luck. Put what was already there first, in the past perfect. Then let the result land last, in the simple past, as the thing your build caused.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"By the time the board met, we already lost thirty teachers."
"By the time the board met, we had already lost thirty teachers."
"Engagement had been dropped for eighteen months."
"Engagement had been dropping for eighteen months."

Both errors erase the sequence. After By the time, the earlier event needs the past perfect (had lost, not “already lost”) — a first language may let you drop that layer in speech, and English does not. And had been dropped is a passive (somebody dropped it); for an action in progress over time you need the continuous, had been dropping. Use for + a period, since + a starting point — never “since two years”.

The Move

Backdrop · Build · Reversal

One turnaround. Thirty seconds. Three moves, always in this order.

1. BACKDROP — what had already been failing (past perfect continuous)

“For two years before I arrived, attrition had been climbing and engagement had been falling, and nobody had traced it back to onboarding.”

2. BUILD — what you had put in place, before any result (past perfect)

“Long before the numbers moved, I had rebuilt onboarding, I had set up a quarterly pulse survey, and I had trained the leads on psychological safety.”

3. REVERSAL — what finally moved (simple past)

“Only then did engagement turn, and attrition followed it two quarters later. We did not slow the decline; we reversed the trend.”

AND THE CLOSE THAT TRANSFERS TO CANADA

“So what I am bringing to Canada is not the luck. It is the sequence, and the groundwork will have to be laid again from the beginning.”

Notice what the order does. Lead with the reversal and it sounds like a boast. Lead with the backdrop and the build, and the reversal arrives as a consequence you earned — and a consequence is the only thing a second country will trust.

0:000:00

He is not going to challenge them. He has read them, they are real, and they are good. His question is a different one: whether they are repeatable.

The sequence in the middle. Every turnaround looks like leadership after the fact, and half of them were a recovering market. If she can say what had already been failing before she touched it, and what she had put in place before the results appeared, he is looking at cause, not coincidence.
3. What does he say about Canada specifically?
Canada is not Brazil. The status quo she is about to inherit is entrenched in a culture she has read about and not yet lived in. He wants to know she understands the groundwork has to be laid again from the beginning, and that last time it took her the better part of a year before anything moved.
