Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

These are the key social expressions for this lesson. Tap Listen to hear the pronunciation.

Break the Ice -- to start a conversation in a relaxed, friendly way
"I always break the ice with a simple question."
Small Talk -- light, informal conversation about general topics
"We made small talk about the weather before the meeting."
Mingle -- to move around and talk to many different people at an event
"She likes to mingle at networking receptions."
Common Ground -- a shared interest, opinion, or experience
"We found common ground in our love of finance."
Acquaintance -- someone you know a little, but not well
"He is a business acquaintance from a conference."
Strike Up -- to start a conversation with someone
"I struck up a conversation with the person next to me."
Get Along -- to have a friendly, easy relationship with someone
"I get along well with my international colleagues."
Outgoing -- friendly and confident in social situations
"You need to be outgoing at networking events."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

Grammar

It is the first evening of the Restructuring Summit, and Eduarda is at the networking reception. She is a little nervous, but she knows how to break the ice. She walks up to an acquaintance and asks, "Do you come here every year?" Soon they are making small talk about the city. Eduarda likes to mingle, so she strikes up a conversation with a delegate from London. "What brings you to São Paulo?" she asks. "Have you been here before?" They quickly find common ground: they both work on cross-border deals. Eduarda is outgoing tonight, and she gets along well with everyone she meets.

1. What does Eduarda do to start conversations?
A She waits for people to talk to her.
B She breaks the ice and strikes up conversations.
C She talks only about deals.
2. Which question uses the present perfect (Have + been)?
A "Do you come here every year?"
B "Have you been here before?"
C "What brings you to São Paulo?"
3. What do Eduarda and the London delegate have in common?
A They both work on cross-border deals.
B They both live in London.
C They are old friends.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip

Grammar

Question forms = in English, the auxiliary (do/does, am/is/are, have/has) comes before the subject (em inglês, o auxiliar vem ANTES do sujeito na pergunta)

TypeStructureExample
Yes/No (be)Am/Is/Are + subject + ...?"Are you enjoying the event?"
Yes/No (do)Do/Does + subject + verb?"Do you work in finance?"
Wh- questionWh- + auxiliary + subject + verb?"What do you do?"
Present perfectHave/Has + subject + past participle?"Have you been here before?"

Common Mistake:

"What you do for work?" / "You are from where?"

"What do you do for work?" / "Where are you from?"

Nunca esqueça o auxiliar (do/are/have): sem ele, a pergunta soa como tradução direta do português.
