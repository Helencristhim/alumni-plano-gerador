Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Last week you ran the milestone review. Tonight the milestone is missed — by a supplier who is now blaming you. These are the words of a conflict you win without burning the bridge: how you stay firm, how you give ground, and how you keep the relationship. Listen to each term and read the example.

Assertive -- confident and direct without being aggressive or passive -- you say the hard thing clearly and respectfully
"Assertive is not aggressive: I stated our position plainly, but I never attacked the person."
To defuse -- to reduce the tension in a situation before it escalates into a fight
"When he got defensive, I defused it: 'I'm not blaming you, I'm reading the schedule.'"
A sticking point -- the single issue that is blocking an agreement while everything else is settled
"We agreed on the schedule; the only sticking point was who pays for the recovery shifts."
An impasse -- a deadlock where neither side will move and the discussion cannot go forward
"To break the impasse, I proposed we table the cost question and agree the delivery sequence first."
Common ground -- the points both sides already agree on, used as a starting place to rebuild the talk
"Before arguing about the cause, we found common ground: we both wanted Line 4 delivered."
To meet halfway -- to compromise so that each side gives up something to reach a deal
"I couldn't accept full blame, but I offered to meet them halfway on the schedule."
To hold someone accountable -- to make someone answer for what they were responsible for
"The plan holds both sides accountable: their slip, and our nine-day drawing delay."
Remediation plan -- an agreed plan of actions to fix a failure and recover the schedule
"We left the call with a remediation plan, not a winner and a loser."
To draw the line -- to set a firm limit that you will not cross, however much pressure you are under
"I conceded the drawing delay, but I drew the line at signing sole responsibility."
To table a proposal -- in American English, to postpone discussion of a proposal until a later point
"Let's table the penalty clause for now and agree the recovery sequence first."
Goodwill -- the trust and positive relationship two parties have built and want to protect
"A formal dispute would win the point and burn two years of goodwill."
To smooth things over -- to repair a relationship after friction or a disagreement
"The start was tense, but we smoothed things over and both signed the plan."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

A German supplier, GleisTech, has missed three delivery milestones on Line 4, and this morning a letter arrived claiming the whole delay was Motiva's fault: the technical drawings were approved nine days late. João Guilherme is the engineer who has to answer it — this afternoon, on a call. His manager's brief is exact: do not go in swinging, and do not roll over. The goal is not to win an argument; it is to leave with a remediation plan and a supplier who still wants the next contract. In other words, protect the goodwill.

The grammar of that call is the grammar of concession. Watch how a firm point is built on top of a fact you accept. "Although we were nine days late on the January revision, that does not explain a three-month slip." The although-clause gives ground; the main clause holds it. "While I accept the drawing was late, the delay after approval is yours to resolve." "Our records show March, whereas the baseline called for January." And when the connector takes no clause: "Despite the late approval, we kept the line moving" — despite + a noun, never despite we were late. Each sentence concedes something true, then draws a line exactly where the evidence draws it.

So João runs the call the assertive way, which is neither aggressive nor passive. He defuses the defensiveness — "I'm not saying you dropped the ball; I'm saying the schedule shows a gap" — and looks for common ground before arguing about cause. When they reach a sticking point over who pays for the recovery shifts, he does not let it become an impasse: he offers to meet them halfway on the schedule while he draws the line on sole responsibility. He tables the hardest clause so nobody has to lose face, keeps both sides accountable to a revised sequence, and smooths things over before the call ends — the point made, the bridge intact. "Despite the tension, I'd rather meet you halfway than lose a month to a dispute."

1. "Although we were nine days late on the January revision, that does not explain a three-month slip." What is the main point of the sentence?
A That Motiva caused the whole delay.
B That the nine-day delay is real but does NOT explain the rest — the although-clause concedes a fact, and the MAIN clause carries the weight.
C That the January revision was never late.
2. Which sentence is grammatically correct?
A "Despite we were late on the drawings, we kept the line moving."
B "Despite the late approval, we kept the line moving."
C "Despite of the late approval, we kept the line moving."
3. "Our records show delivery in March, whereas the baseline called for January." What does whereas do here?
A It draws a clean contrast between two facts placed side by side.
B It apologizes for the March delivery.
C It means 'because', giving the reason for the delay.
4. The manager says the goal of the call is to leave with a remediation plan and a supplier who still wants to work with Motiva. What is he protecting?
A The penalty clause.
B The goodwill — the working relationship, so pushing back must never become burning the bridge.
C His own promotion.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Concession & Contrast

GRAMMAR

Concede a true point, then hold your line. What changes is the grammar AFTER the connector — a clause, a noun, or a new sentence.

StructureWhat follows itExample
Although / Even though+ a FULL CLAUSE (subject + verb). Concede a fact, then make your real point in the main clause. Even though is the more emphatic version."Although we were late on one revision, that does not explain the slip."
While / Whereas+ a FULL CLAUSE. While = "although" or "at the same time as"; whereas draws a clean contrast between two facts."Our records show March, whereas the baseline called for January."
Despite / In spite of+ a NOUN or an -ing form — NEVER a full clause. "despite the delay", "despite being late" — not "despite we were late"."Despite the late approval, we kept the line moving."
However / NeverthelessJoin two SEPARATE sentences: full stop before, comma after. They pivot the reader from one sentence to the next."We accept the drawing delay. However, the schedule tells a different story."
Why it mattersConcession is how you push back without burning the bridge. You give ground on a small, true point — "although we were nine days late" — and that concession buys the credibility to hold firm on the big one. The engineer who admits nothing is believed on nothing. Conceding first is not weakness; it is the move that keeps the room open.

Watch out (habits carried over from Portuguese): (1) "Despite we were late..." — after despite / in spite of comes a NOUN or -ing, not a clause: "despite the delay", "despite being late". For a full clause, use although; (2) "Despite of the delay..." — it is despite (no "of") or in spite of (with "of"), never "despite of"; (3) "Although late, but we kept moving" — English uses although OR but, never both in the same sentence.

Conflict collocations: defuse a situation, reach an impasse, find common ground, meet someone halfway, hold someone accountable, draw the line, table a proposal, preserve goodwill, smooth things over, push back on a claim (not on a person).

The question to ask before you reply in a conflict: "What can I honestly concede first?" Find the true point you can give away, concede it with although, and only then draw your line. Concede, then contrast — every time.

Quiz

The supplier opens by blaming your late drawings for the entire delay. Your strongest first move is:
A "That's completely wrong, the delay is entirely your fault."
B "Although we were nine days late on the January revision, and I accept that openly, the baseline schedule shows the rest of the delay began on your side."
C "You're right, it's all our fault, let's move on."
They get defensive and stop listening. To defuse it without giving up your position, you say:
A "You clearly dropped the ball on this one."
B "I'm not saying you dropped the ball -- I'm saying the schedule shows a gap, and I'd like us to close it together."
C "Fine, forget it, I'll escalate this to your director."
You reach a sticking point over who pays for the recovery shifts. To avoid an impasse, you:
A Refuse to discuss anything else until they admit fault.
B Offer to meet them halfway on the schedule while you draw the line on sole responsibility, and table the cost clause for later.
C Agree to pay everything just to end the call.
They ask you to sign a document that assigns Motiva sole responsibility. You protect Motiva and the relationship by saying:
A "I'll sign whatever ends this argument."
B "I can commit to a joint remediation plan; however, I can't sign a document that assigns us sole responsibility. Let's table that clause and agree the sequence first."
C "No, and I'm ending this call now."
The call was tense but productive. To close it and keep the goodwill, you say:
A "Despite a difficult start, I think we've smoothed things over -- I'll send the remediation plan today so we hold each other accountable."

William Ury — "The Walk from No to Yes" (TEDx, 18 min)

Ury wrote Getting to Yes and spent his career in the hardest conflicts on earth. In eighteen minutes he gives you the two moves you rehearsed tonight: go to the balcony before you react, and look for the common ground instead of the win. His whole thesis is that the point of a difficult conversation is not to defeat the other side but to reach an agreement both can live with — which is exactly what "pushing back without burning bridges" means when the supplier is blaming you for a delay.

Tip: watch once. Then take the GleisTech scenario and write, in English, one sentence for each of Ury's moves — one that "goes to the balcony" (defuses the heat) and one that names the common ground. Those two sentences are the opening of your next real conflict call.
