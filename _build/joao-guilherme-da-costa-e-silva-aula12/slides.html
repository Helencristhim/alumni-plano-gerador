Impact

Three Ways to Answer the Same Blame

Rank them, say why, then click to compare

A. "That is completely wrong. The delay is entirely your fault, and you know it."
Aggressive. The moment you say "your fault", the other side stops listening and starts defending. You may be right, but you will never reach a remediation plan through a closed door. Winning the argument here loses the project.
B. "You are right, I am sure it is our fault. Let's just move on and forget it."
Passive. You protected the mood and gave away Motiva's position in one sentence. Rolling over is not diplomacy — it is misinformation about who owes what, and nobody respects the engineer who admits everything.
C. "Although we were nine days late on the January revision — and I accept that openly — the baseline schedule shows the rest of the delay began on your side."
Assertive. You concede the small, true point first, which buys the credibility to hold firm on the big one. The bridge stays open, and the line is drawn exactly where the evidence draws it. This is the whole skill.

The Tool

The Assertive Move

1 — Defuse

Take the heat out first. "I'm not here to win an argument — I'm here to fix the schedule." Separate the person from the problem.

2 — Concede

Give ground on the small, true point. "Although we were nine days late on one revision..." A real concession buys credibility.

3 — Contrast

Now hold the line. "...whereas the two milestones after approval are yours to resolve." The evidence draws the line, not you.

4 — Bridge

Leave a way forward. "Let's agree a remediation plan and hold both sides accountable." Nobody signs a confession.

Most engineers skip step 2 and jump straight to the line — and that is why they sound aggressive. Concede first, then contrast. The small point you give away is what earns the right to hold the big one.

Chapter 3: The Code

Concede, Then Contrast

The grammar of pushing back: give ground on one clause, hold your line in the next.

Grammar Discovery

Four Ways to Concede a Point

"Although we were nine days late, that does not explain a three-month slip."

"Our records show March, whereas the baseline called for January."

"Despite the late approval, we kept the line moving."

"We accept the drawing delay. However, the schedule tells a different story."

Every sentence gives ground, then holds a line. Look at what comes after the red word: a full clause, a noun, or a new sentence. What is the pattern?

ConnectorWhat follows itExample
although / even thoughA full clause (subject + verb). Concede a fact, then make your real point in the main clause. even though is more emphatic."Although we were late, that does not explain the slip."
while / whereasA full clause. while = "although" or "at the same time as"; whereas draws a clean contrast between two facts."Records show March, whereas the baseline called for January."
despite / in spite ofA noun or an -ing form — NEVER a full clause. "despite the delay", "despite being late", not "despite we were late"."Despite the late approval, we kept the line moving."
however / neverthelessJoin two separate sentences: full stop before, comma after. They pivot the reader from one sentence to the next."We accept the delay. However, the schedule differs."
Why it mattersConcession is how you push back without burning the bridge. You give ground on a small, true point — "although we were nine days late" — and that concession buys the credibility to hold firm on the big one. The engineer who admits nothing is believed on nothing. Conceding first is not weakness; it keeps the room open.
Meaning

Four Connectors, Four Grammars

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Despite we were nine days late, we kept the line moving."
"Despite the nine-day delay, we kept the line moving."
"Although the approval was late, but the slip began on their side."
"Although the approval was late, the slip began on their side."

After despite / in spite of, use a noun or -ing: "despite the delay". For a full clause, use although. And English uses although OR but — never both in the same sentence, however natural "embora... mas" feels in Portuguese.

Comprehension

About the Call

1. What is Petra's opening claim, and what does she blame for the delay?
She claims Motiva's notice ignores the real cause — that Motiva approved GleisTech's drawings nine days late, and their production line could not start without that approval.
2. Which part does João concede, and which part does he refuse?
He concedes the drawing approval was nine days late (the January milestone). He refuses to accept blame for the February and March milestones, which slipped after the approval was already in hand.
3. When Petra pushes back ("your team approved the schedule"), how does João hold the line without attacking her?
He defuses it — "I'm not saying your team dropped the ball; I'm saying the schedule shows a gap" — then uses "even though" to concede the tight schedule while pointing to the documented later slips. He separates the person from the problem.
4. What is the final sticking point, and how does João propose to move past it?
Who pays for the recovery shifts. He proposes tabling the cost for now, agreeing the delivery sequence first, meeting halfway on the January shifts while GleisTech covers February and March — a joint remediation plan that holds each side accountable and protects the goodwill.

1. What does Sofia say GleisTech is claiming, and how much of it does she say is true?
That the whole delay was caused by Motiva's late drawing approval. She says part of it is true — Motiva was nine days late on one revision in January — but nine days does not explain a three-month slip, and the baseline schedule proves it.
2. What are the two things she tells João NOT to do?
Do not go in swinging (too aggressive), and do not roll over (too passive). Acknowledge the drawing delay because it is real, then hold the line on the rest with the schedule in front of him.
3. What does she say the real goal of the call is?
Not to win an argument, but to leave with a remediation plan and a supplier who still wants to work with Motiva on the next project. Push back on the claim, not the people; find common ground first, then draw the line where the evidence draws it.

Three Rules Before You Sit Down

A negotiation advisor. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What is the first rule, and what does he say happens the moment you say "you failed"?
Separate the person from the problem. The moment you say "you failed", the other side stops listening and starts defending — so say "the schedule shows a gap", not "you dropped the ball". Same fact, but one keeps the room open.
2. What is the second rule, and why does he say conceding is not weakness?
Concede early and concede something real. Giving ground on a small, true point buys enormous credibility when you hold firm on the big one — the engineer who admits nothing is never believed on anything.
3. What is the third rule about the other side, and how does he define "assertive"?
Always leave the other side a way to save face — nobody signs a plan that reads like a confession. Assertive is not aggressive: you say the hard thing clearly, in a way the other person can still walk back from. Draw the line, and build the bridge across it at the same time.
