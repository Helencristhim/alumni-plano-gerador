Lesson 12 -- Navigating Conflict: Pushing Back Without Burning Bridges

Talk
William Ury — "The Walk from No to Yes" (TEDx, 18 min)

Ury wrote Getting to Yes and spent his career in the hardest conflicts on earth. In eighteen minutes he gives you the two moves you rehearsed tonight: go to the balcony before you react, and look for the common ground instead of the win. His whole thesis is that the point of a difficult conversation is not to defeat the other side but to reach an agreement both can live with — which is exactly what "pushing back without burning bridges" means when the supplier is blaming you for a delay.

Tip: watch once. Then take the GleisTech scenario and write, in English, one sentence for each of Ury's moves — one that "goes to the balcony" (defuses the heat) and one that names the common ground. Those two sentences are the opening of your next real conflict call.

Podcast
HBR IdeaCast — "Learn to Disagree More Effectively" (Ep. 1071, ~26 min)

Harvard's Julia Minson has studied for years what people get wrong in disagreement. Her finding is the spine of tonight's lesson: the goal of a good disagreement is not to win — it is to keep the conversation going. She shows how "receptiveness" is built from specific language choices, the same concede-then-contrast move you drilled: acknowledge the other side's point out loud before you make your own. Listen for how often the strongest move is to give ground first.

Tip: as you listen, note three "receptive" phrases she models. Then rewrite your firmest line to GleisTech ("the post-approval slip is yours") so it opens with one of them — "I can see why you'd frame it that way; however..." Say the new version aloud until it sounds assertive, not aggressive.

YouTube
BBC Learning English — "Linking words of contrast" (English Class, ~7 min)

The exact grammar of tonight, from native teachers at your level. They set the concession words side by side and drill the one thing that trips up every B2 speaker: what comes AFTER the connector. although / even though / while take a full clause; despite / in spite of take a noun or -ing — so "despite the delay", never "despite we were late". Watch it after the lesson: it will sound obvious, and that is the point.

Tip: after the video, write six sentences about the GleisTech dispute — two with "although", two with "despite" (+ noun), and two with "however" (between two sentences). Read them aloud. Those six sentences are the backbone of the email you will write for homework.
