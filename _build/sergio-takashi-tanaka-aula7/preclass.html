Lesson 07 -- Pre-class

Past & Present -- Talking About What Changed

Reviewing a year of change on the risk desk: what happened last year and what has changed since. Key words: trend, climb, dip, steady, peak, sharp, improve, deteriorate, rebound, track record. Structure: the past simple (finished time) against the present perfect (a change up to now).

Vocabulary

Trend -- the general direction in which something moves over time

To climb -- to rise steadily, step by step
"Exposure climbed sharply after the market shock."
To dip -- to fall a little for a short time, then recover
"The buffer dipped in April, but it recovered fast."
Steady -- staying at about the same level; not changing much
"The trend has been steady for six months now."
Peak -- the highest point something reaches
"Exposure hit a new peak in the first quarter."
Sharp -- sudden and large
"There was a sharp fall in volatility after March."
To improve -- to become better
"The risk profile has improved a lot since March."
To deteriorate -- to become worse, step by step
"Last year the numbers deteriorated for two quarters."
To rebound -- to recover after a fall
"The trend rebounded quickly after the April dip."
Track record -- a history of how well someone or something has performed
"We have kept a clean track record with the regulator."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Last year was a hard one for the desk. In the first quarter, exposure climbed to a new peak after a market shock, and the numbers deteriorated for two quarters. Then, in the spring, the desk cut the biggest positions. Since March, the trend has improved month by month. The limit breaches have dropped to zero this year, and so far we have kept a clean track record with the regulator. There was one dip in April, but the trend rebounded fast, and it has been steady ever since.

1. Why do we say "Exposure climbed to a new peak last year"?
A "Last year" is a finished time, so we use the past simple.
B The change reaches up to now, so we use the present perfect.
C It is a plan for next year, so we use "will".
2. Which sentence is correct?
A "Since March, the trend has improved month by month."
B "Since March, the trend improved month by month."
C "Since March, the trend improves month by month."
3. Why do we say "The breaches have dropped to zero this year"?
A "This year" is still in progress, so we use the present perfect.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Past Simple vs Present Perfect

GRAMMAR

How to separate a finished time that is over from a change that reaches up to now.

FormUseExample
Past simple
verb + -ed (rose, cut, climbed)		A finished time that is over: last year, in March, in 2024, yesterday.Exposure climbed to a peak last year.
Present perfect (positive)
have / has + past participle		A change up to now, or a result still true. Words: since, so far, this year, already.The trend has improved since March.
Present perfect (negative)
have / has not + participle		Something that has not happened yet, up to now.The figures have not arrived yet.
The signalFinished time word (last year, in March) → past simple. Up-to-now word (since, so far, this year, yet, for) → present perfect. Never put last year with have.
