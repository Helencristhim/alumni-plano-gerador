Talk
"The best stats you have ever seen" -- Hans Rosling (TED)

A famous, energetic talk that turns years of data into a clear story of how the world has changed over time. Connection to Lesson 7: this is exactly what you do in a year-in-review -- you describe a trend, a peak, a sharp rise, and how the numbers have improved. Notice how he moves between what happened in the past and what has changed up to now.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Then say two sentences about one chart: one in the past simple ("The numbers rose in the 1960s") and one in the present perfect ("The trend has improved since then").

Podcast
"Planet Money" -- NPR

Short, clear episodes that explain money and markets through real stories, in American English. Connection to Lesson 7: many episodes review what happened to a company or a market and what has changed since -- the same past-and-present story you tell about the desk. Great listening practice for a strong reader who wants to rebuild the ear.

Tip: pick one episode, listen once for the main idea, then say what happened in the past simple and what has changed in the present perfect: "The price rose in 2021, but it has fallen since."

Read
BBC News -- The Economy

Free, clearly written stories about the economy and markets, a lighter read than the Financial Times you already know. Connection to Lesson 7: reading is your strength, so use it to feed your speaking. Most stories describe how a number has climbed, dipped, or improved -- the exact language of change from this lesson.

Tip: read one short story, then close it and describe the trend out loud: "Last quarter, prices rose, but they have been steady since." Mix the past simple with the present perfect.
