Impact

Three Ways to Open the Same Update

Rank them, say why, then click to compare

A. "Let me explain everything from the beginning — in January we ran the factory test, then the firmware, then the shipping, then the inspection, and then..."
Detail dump. You have lived inside this project for three months, so the story is obvious to you; to a busy client it is noise. The engineer who unloads the whole investigation loses the room in ninety seconds — the decision-maker never hears the ask.
B. "I'm really sorry, this is a serious delay, I take full responsibility, it's been very difficult on our side..."
Apology first. It sounds humble, but it gives away the narrative and never states what happened, why, or what you need. The client is left anxious and no clearer — you have spent your opening minute on feeling, not information.
C. "Headline first: commissioning has slipped twelve weeks, recoverable to four. The cause is one late component; the decision I need today is night-shift access."
Executive summary. What happened, the cause, and the ask — three sentences, with the headline figure in the first minute. A busy client decides in that first minute whether to trust you. This is the whole skill: structure before detail.

The Tool

The Presenter's Move

1 — Summary

Open with the executive summary and the headline figure. "Twelve weeks lost, four to recover." The busy client decides in the first minute.

2 — Findings

Now the cause, in the order they need. "One late component; two units failed inspection." State the finding, then the evidence.

3 — Recommendation

What you advise. "A three-workstream recovery run on night shifts." Draw attention to the one number in the supporting data.

4 — Call to Action

Close with ONE ask, not a list. "Approve night-shift access on this call." Then take questions.

Most engineers start with the findings and never reach a clean ask — and that is why decisions do not get made. Summary first, detail after, one call to action. Structure is what makes it easy for a busy person to say yes.

Chapter 3: The Code

The Clause That Shrinks

Reduced relative clauses: how "the equipment which was delivered late" becomes "the equipment delivered late".

Grammar Discovery

What Got Removed?

"The equipment delivered late pushed commissioning back three months."

"The team running the factory test flagged the firmware issue."

"Every figure supporting this recommendation comes from the site logs."

"The units still in transit arrive next week."

Each green phrase is a relative clause with who / which / that + be removed. What form is left — a past participle, an -ing, or a phrase? And what decides which?

Full relative clauseReduced formWhy
the equipment which was delivered latethe equipment delivered lateThe noun receives the action → keep the past participle (passive).
the team that is running the testthe team running the testThe noun does the action → keep the -ing form (active).
the units that are still in transitthe units still in transitA state or a phrase → drop that + be, keep the description.
the supplier that we choseno reduction → use chosen by usHere that is the object, not the subject. You can only reduce a subject relative clause.
Why it mattersReduced relative clauses are what make a briefing note read as dense and precise. "The recommendation set out below", "the action requested from the client", "the data attached to this note" — a professional writes them without thinking. Half the noun phrases in a real technical document are reduced relatives.
Meaning

One Question Decides

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"The data presenting on this slide comes from the site logs."
"The data presented on this slide comes from the site logs."
"The supplier chosen for Line 4, it missed the deadline."
"The supplier chosen for Line 4 missed the deadline."

If the noun receives the action, use the past participle ("data presented"), not the -ing form. And once you reduce the clause, do not repeat the subject with "it" — "the supplier chosen for Line 4 missed the deadline", however natural the extra pronoun feels in Portuguese.

About the Call

Did You Follow It?

1. What does Ingrid say her board wants from the call?
One thing: how bad the slip is, and whether it can be recovered. She wants the headline first, not the detail.
2. What hard question does Ingrid ask about the firmware, and why is it difficult?
Her engineers flagged a firmware mismatch in January, so she asks why that did not stop the shipment. It is difficult because it points to an internal process failure — João does not have the exact answer ready.
3. How does João handle the firmware question without freezing or inventing an answer?
He names the silence — "that's a good question, let me think about that for a moment" — gives the part he is sure of (the note didn't reach the team handling the order), promises to circle back to the exact date after the call, and reassures her the fix is already approved.
4. What is João's single call to action, and what does it protect?
Approve the night-shift access. It lets the integration testing postponed in March run at night, clawing back eight weeks and holding the Line 4 opening date.
Listening

The Manager's Brief

Just before the presentation. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does Sofia say João should open with, and in what order should the presentation go?
Open with the executive summary — one sentence on what happened, one on the cause, one on the ask — leading with the headline figure (twelve weeks, recoverable to four). Then findings, then recommendation, in that order every time.
2. What does she tell him to do — and not do — with the supporting data?
Do not read the table out loud. Draw attention to the one number that matters and let the slide carry the rest. Keep the call to action to a single decision, not a list.
3. What does she say to do when a hard question interrupts him?
That is good, not bad. Take it, answer the part he can, and if he needs a moment, say so out loud — silence you name is confidence; silence you hide is panic.
Listening 2

Three Rules Before You Present

A presentation coach. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What is the first rule, and what does he say the engineer who dumps the whole investigation loses?
The audience is not you — the cause is obvious to you but new to them, so walk them through it in the order they need: finding, then evidence, then meaning. The engineer who dumps the whole investigation loses the room in ninety seconds.
2. What is the second rule about the number, and how does he say to highlight a key finding?
The number does the work, not the sentence. Put one figure on the slide and say it slowly — draw attention to the headline figure and let everything else sit in the handout. A recommendation supported by one clear number is believed; one buried under twelve is not.
3. What is the third rule about silence, and what should you say when you don't have the answer ready?
Manage the silence. Say "that is a good question, let me think about that for a moment", then actually think — a pause you control reads as competence. If you need to check, say you will circle back, and then actually circle back before the call ends.
