Lesson 14 -- Pre-class

Making Your Case -- Presenting Technical Findings

A three-month commissioning slip on Line 4 has to be presented to a European client's senior engineer. This is how you make the case: structure before detail -- an executive summary and a headline figure first, then findings, recommendation, and one clear call to action -- delivered under hard questions without freezing. Key words: executive summary, findings, recommendation, supporting data, headline figure, takeaway, to walk someone through, to highlight, to take questions, to circle back to, call to action, to wrap up. Structure: reduced relative clauses -- "the equipment (which was) delivered late", "the team (that is) running the test".

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Last week you read the fine print of a contract. Tonight you stop reading other people's cases and make your own -- a structured technical update a busy client can act on in minutes. These are the words of a presentation that gets a decision: how you open, how you point to the numbers, how you take questions, and how you close with a clear ask. Listen to each term and read the example.

Executive summary -- a short opening section that states the key points for a busy decision-maker
"The executive summary is three sentences: what happened, the cause, and what I'm asking for."
Findings -- the results or conclusions your investigation has produced
"The findings point to a single component: one late axle-counter delivery."
Recommendation -- the course of action you advise, based on the findings
"My recommendation is a three-workstream recovery plan run on night shifts."
Supporting data -- the figures and evidence that back up a claim
"The supporting data attached to the note shows a realistic four-week recovery."
Headline figure -- the single most important number you want the audience to remember
"Twelve weeks lost, four to recover -- four weeks is the headline figure I lead with."
Takeaway -- the one main point you want people to leave the room with
"If they remember one thing, the takeaway is: recoverable to four weeks."
To walk someone through -- to guide someone step by step through something
"Let me walk you through the recovery plan, one workstream at a time."
To highlight -- to draw attention to the most important point
"I'll highlight one figure on this slide and let the handout carry the rest."
To take questions -- to invite and answer questions from the audience
"I'll take any questions now -- and if you can approve the access, we hold the date."
To circle back to -- to return to a topic mentioned earlier
"That question comes a little early -- can I circle back to the cost in two slides?"
Call to action -- a clear statement of what you want the audience to do next
"The call to action is one decision: approve night-shift access on this call."
To wrap up -- to bring a point or a presentation to a close
"To wrap up: one slip, one cause, one plan, and one decision I need from you."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

The interlocking commissioning on Line 4 has slipped by three months, and this afternoon João Guilherme presents it to Nordic Rail's senior engineer. His manager's brief is exact: do not open with the apology or the detail. Open with the executive summary -- one sentence on what happened, one on the cause, one on the ask -- and lead with the headline figure: a twelve-week slip, recoverable to four. A busy client decides in the first minute whether to trust you.

The grammar of a technical briefing is dense, and one structure does most of the work: the reduced relative clause. Watch how a full relative clause loses its who / which / that + be. "The equipment which was delivered late" becomes "the equipment delivered late" -- a past participle, because the equipment receives the action. "The team that is running the test" becomes "the team running the test" -- an -ing form, because the team does the action. So the findings read like this: "the axle counters manufactured in Shenzhen arrived late, and two of the units delivered failed the inspection carried out at our depot."

Then the recommendation, and only then the ask. João will walk the client through the recovery plan, highlight the one number in the supporting data, and close with a single call to action: approve the night-shift access. If a hard question interrupts him and the answer is not ready, he will not freeze or invent -- he will say, "that's a good question, let me think about that for a moment", give the part he is sure of, and promise to circle back to the detail. The takeaway he wants them to leave with is one line: recoverable to four weeks. Then he will take questions and wrap up.

1. "The equipment delivered late was the cause of the slip." Why is it delivered and not delivering?
A Because -ing and past participle mean the same thing here.
B Because the equipment RECEIVES the action (it was delivered) -- passive, so the past participle. The full clause was "which was delivered".
C Because delivered is a noun here.
2. Which reduced relative clause is correct?
A "The team ran the test flagged the issue."
B "The team running the test flagged the issue."
C "The team who running the test flagged the issue."
3. The manager tells João to open with the executive summary and the headline figure. Why?
A Because the client wants a long, detailed story first.
B Because a busy client decides in the first minute whether to trust you -- structure before detail.
C Because the summary replaces the recommendation.
4. A hard question interrupts and João's answer is not ready. What does he do?
A Invents a number so he doesn't look unprepared.
B Names the silence -- "let me think about that for a moment" -- gives the part he is sure of, and promises to circle back to the detail.
C Stops the presentation until he finds the answer.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Reduced Relative Clauses

GRAMMAR

A relative clause loses its who / which / that + be. What is left is a participle -- and one question decides which one: does the noun DO the action, or RECEIVE it?

Full relative clauseReduced formWhy
the equipment which was delivered latethe equipment delivered lateThe noun RECEIVES the action → past participle (passive).
the team that is running the testthe team running the testThe noun DOES the action → -ing form (active).
the units that are still in transitthe units still in transitA state or a phrase → drop that + be, keep the description.
the supplier that we choseno reduction → use "chosen by us"Here that is the OBJECT. You can only reduce a subject relative clause.
Why it mattersReduced relative clauses are what make a briefing note read as dense and precise: "the recommendation set out below", "the action requested from the client", "the data attached to this note". Half the noun phrases in a real technical document are reduced relatives -- and producing them is what makes your English sound professional, not just correct.

Watch out (habits carried over from Portuguese): (1) "The data presenting on this slide..." -- the data RECEIVES the action, so use the PAST PARTICIPLE: "the data presented on this slide". Ask: does it present, or is it presented? (2) "The supplier chosen for Line 4, it missed the deadline" -- once you reduce the clause, do NOT repeat the subject: "the supplier chosen for Line 4 missed the deadline"; (3) "the engineer who presenting..." -- drop who/which/that AND be together: "the engineer presenting...".

Presentation collocations: present findings, outline a recommendation, support a claim with data, walk someone through a plan, draw attention to a figure, highlight a key finding, take questions, circle back to a point, wrap up with a call to action.

The question to ask before you write a technical noun phrase: "Does this noun DO the action or RECEIVE it?" Does it → -ing. Receives it → past participle. That one test gives you the whole grammar.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer for every real moment of the presentation -- the opening, the data, the hard question, the timing, and the close.

You open the status update to a busy senior engineer. Your strongest first move is:
A "Let me explain everything from the beginning -- in January we ran the factory test, then..."
B "The headline is a twelve-week slip, recoverable to four; one late component caused it, and I need one decision today."
C "I'm so sorry, this is a serious delay and it's been very difficult on our side."
The slide shows a table with twelve numbers. To highlight the key finding, you:
A Read every row of the table out loud in order.
B Draw attention to the one figure that matters and let the handout carry the rest.
C Skip the data entirely and hope no one asks.
A hard question interrupts you and you don't have the exact answer ready. You say:
A Anything, quickly, to fill the silence -- even a number you're not sure of.
B "That's a good question -- let me think about that for a moment," give the part you're sure of, and promise to circle back.
C Nothing -- you go silent and lose the thread.
A question belongs later in your presentation. Without losing your thread, you say:
A "That's exactly where I'm heading -- can I circle back to the cost in two slides, once the plan is on the table?"
B "I don't know, let's talk about it now instead."
C Ignore the question and keep going as if it wasn't asked.
You reach the end of the update. To close with a clean call to action, you say:
A "To wrap up: one slip, one cause, one plan -- and one decision I need from you today, which is night-shift access."
B "So, that's more or less everything, I think. Any thoughts?"
C "There are about ten things I'd like you to approve, in no particular order."
