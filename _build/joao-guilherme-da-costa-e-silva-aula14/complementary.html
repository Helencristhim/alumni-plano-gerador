Lesson 14 -- Making Your Case: Presenting Technical Findings

Talk
Nancy Duarte — "The secret structure of great talks" (TEDxEast, ~18 min)

Duarte reverse-engineered hundreds of the best presentations ever given and found they all share one shape. It is the same move you drilled tonight: start where the audience is, show them where you want them to be, and make the gap between the two impossible to ignore. Watch it as a template for your own status update — the executive summary is your "what is", the recommendation is your "what could be", and the call to action is the leap you ask the client to take.

Tip: watch once, then map your Line 4 update onto her structure. Write the one sentence that is your "what is" (the twelve-week slip) and the one that is your "what could be" (recoverable to four). Those two sentences are your opening.

Podcast
Think Fast, Talk Smart (Stanford) — Ep. 193: "Conquer Questions" (~25 min)

Matt Abrahams from Stanford takes apart the exact moment you rehearsed tonight: the hard question you did not see coming. His finding is that Q&A feels dangerous only because we treat it as an interruption instead of the point. He gives a simple structure for answering under pressure — answer first, then one detail, then why it matters — and shows why naming a pause ("let me think about that for a moment") reads as control, not weakness. It is the spine of the firmware question you handled in the dialogue.

Tip: as you listen, write down his answer structure. Then take the two hardest questions a client could ask about your Line 4 delay and draft a three-part answer for each — answer, detail, why it matters. Say them aloud until the pause feels comfortable.

YouTube
Victoria's Lingua Lounge — "Participle Clauses & Reduced Relative Clauses (B2/C1)" (~12 min)

The exact grammar of tonight, at your level. She drills the one decision that trips up every B2 writer: after you drop who / which / that + be, is it a past participle or an -ing form? Her test is the same one you learned — does the noun do the action or receive it — and she shows how these clauses are everywhere in formal, technical writing. Watch it after the lesson and the briefing note you read will suddenly look transparent.

Tip: after the video, rewrite three sentences from your Line 4 briefing as full relative clauses, then reduce them back — "the equipment which was delivered late" → "the equipment delivered late". Producing them by hand is what moves the grammar from recognised to owned.
