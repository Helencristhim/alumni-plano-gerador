Take the integration past the plan and lead the people through it. Build buy-in, face resistance, run a town hall, protect the momentum, and set clear expectations for the team. New vocabulary: change management, mandate, resistance, buy-in, momentum, town hall, engagement, escalate. Grammar focus: modals of obligation and advice (must, have to, should, need to).

"At the town hall, you answer the hard questions in the open." (reunião geral)
Engagement -- how involved and committed the people are
"Engagement rises when people feel heard." (engajamento)
Escalate -- to raise a problem to a higher level so it gets help fast
"If you cannot solve it, you have to escalate it early." (escalonar / elevar)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read about leading the change and answer the questions.

The plan is ready, so now Carlos leads the change management. He has a clear mandate from the board. "Every manager must own the message," he says. "Nobody can stay silent." He knows there is some resistance, so he works for buy-in: "I do not have to win every argument, but I should listen and explain the why again." The teams have to hit the milestones, but people need to feel safe to escalate a blocker early. At the town hall, Carlos answers the hard questions in the open to build engagement. To protect the momentum, the team should celebrate every small win out loud.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Modals of Obligation & Advice

GRAMMAR

Para definir expectativas, use quatro formas. Must + verbo base = obrigação forte que VOCÊ impõe ("Every manager must own the message"). Have to + verbo base = obrigação que vem de FORA (plano, regra, diretoria): "The teams have to hit the milestones". Should + verbo base = conselho / recomendação: "You should walk the floor". Need to + verbo base = necessidade: "People need to feel safe". Depois de must e de should, nunca use "to" (must own, não "must to own"). Cuidado: must not = proibido, mas do not have to = não é necessário. / Use must for strong rules you set, have to for outside obligations, should for advice, and need to for a necessity. All take the base verb; "must not" means forbidden, "do not have to" means not necessary.

FormExample
Must (my strong rule)"Every manager must own the message."
Have to (outside rule)"The teams have to hit the milestones."
Should (advice)"You should walk the floor."
Need to (necessity)"People need to feel safe to speak up."
