Lesson 11 -- Milestone Review: Consolidating What You Know

Movie
Moneyball (2011, Bennett Miller) — 133 min · available in Brazil (see where through the link)

A film built out of exactly the two things this lesson trains: leverage and the counterfactual. Billy Beane runs a whole season on one insight nobody else will name — the elephant in the room — and every negotiation in the movie is a debrief under pressure: he owns his side, he anchors his position in the data he cannot be argued out of, and he refuses the shortcut everyone offers him. Listen for the conditionals, because the whole story is one long "if we had done it the old way, we wouldn't be here" — the mixed conditional you learned today, spoken by native professionals under real stakes. The trade-negotiation scenes are a masterclass in pushing back without burning the bridge.

Tip: watch with ENGLISH subtitles and write down 8 sentences where somebody uses a conditional to explain a decision. Mark which are pure third conditional (both halves in the past) and which are MIXED (past cause, present result). Then take one real fact from your Line 5 project and say it five ways — cleft, inversion, causative, future perfect, mixed conditional — out loud, the way the director in tonight's listening asked you to.

Talk
William Ury — "The Walk from No to Yes" · TED (~19 min, free, subtitled)

The co-author of Getting to Yes on the exact move you practiced in the role-play: how to hold your position in a hard negotiation without turning it into a fight. His central idea — go to the "balcony," name the real problem, and give the other side a way to say yes without losing face — is the debrief with Camille in one talk. Native English, clean and measured pace, the register of a professional who never once raises his voice. Watch it and you will hear the difference between attacking a supplier and pushing back on one.

Tip: listen once with no subtitles and write down the three moves he says a negotiator has to make. Then listen again with English subtitles on. Finally, map each move onto tonight's call: where did João "go to the balcony," where did he name the elephant in the room, and where did he give Camille a way to say yes? Write one sentence for each, using a structure from the lesson.

YouTube
All Conditionals (0, 1, 2, 3 and MIXED) — English Grammar · Arnel's Everyday English (~30 min)

The one new structure of the lesson, taught inside the full family you already know. Arnel puts the mixed conditional side by side with the second and third conditionals, and shows exactly the trap tonight's Common Mistake slide warned about: when the result is NOW, you drop "would have" and say "would." Her examples are natural, adult and native-paced. Watch it AFTER the lesson — it will feel like confirmation of something you already used in the role-play, which is the whole point of a milestone review.

Tip: after the video, write 6 mixed conditionals about your real work at Motiva — three "past cause, present result" (If we had released the schedule on time, we wouldn't be...) and three "present truth, past result" (If I weren't so strict on compliance, I would have...). Record them out loud, then check each one against the two-mistake rule from the Grammar Tip: is the present-result half using "would + base," and is the present-truth condition using the past simple?
