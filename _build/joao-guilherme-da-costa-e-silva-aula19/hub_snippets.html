Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Last week you read an international standard for what it must and may require. This week the hiring manager reads YOU the same way -- so you build the language of self-positioning: the words that make a case sound senior rather than rehearsed. Listen to each term and read the example.

Domain expertise -- deep, specialized knowledge of one particular field
"My domain expertise is railway signaling — specifically CBTC commissioning."
Hands-on experience -- practical experience of doing something yourself, not just the theory
"I have hands-on experience taking signaling systems from factory tests to live running."
Proactive -- acting in advance to prevent problems, rather than reacting after they happen
"I'm proactive: I raise a risk before it becomes a delay, not after."
Technical liaison -- the person who connects the technical team with clients or suppliers
"I acted as the technical liaison between the operator and three overseas suppliers."
To take ownership of -- to accept full responsibility for a task or outcome
"I took ownership of the interface management on the São Francisco project."
To make a compelling case -- to argue for something so convincingly that people are persuaded
"In an interview you have to make a compelling case for why you're the right hire."
To demonstrate initiative -- to show you can act on your own without being told
"I demonstrated initiative by proposing a risk register no one had asked for."
To add measurable value -- to make a difference that can be shown in real numbers or results
"I want a role where I can add measurable value, not just fill a seat."
To address challenges head-on -- to deal with difficulties directly instead of avoiding them
"I prefer to address challenges head-on rather than let them escalate."
To pursue an international role -- to actively seek a job position abroad
"I'm pursuing an international role in railway signaling in Europe."
To implement best practices -- to put proven, high-standard methods into use
"I implemented best practices from the IEC standards on the commissioning plan."
To contribute to the broader goals -- to help achieve the wider objectives of the whole organization
"I don't just deliver my scope; I contribute to the broader goals of the programme."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

It's March, and in 45 minutes João has an interview with a European railway signaling company -- a real chance to pursue an international role. The hiring manager, Claire Dubois, already has his CV, so she does not want it read back to her: she wants a case. That is the whole shift tonight. A nervous candidate recites a flat list -- "I worked at Motiva, I did commissioning, I did supplier meetings." A senior one leads with what he is: a signaling engineer with domain expertise in CBTC and hands-on experience from factory tests to live running.

Then he proves it with one project, and he chooses where the spotlight falls. It was the São Francisco commissioning that gave me my first exposure to international standards -- a cleft, not a list. There he was the technical liaison between the operator and three overseas suppliers, and he did not just manage the scope. Not only did I manage the technical scope, but I also coordinated all three at once -- an inversion that makes the achievement lean forward. He demonstrated initiative by proposing a risk register no one had asked for, and it caught two faults before the track. That is measurable value: the manager remembers the number, not the adjective.

The hardest question is a gift. When Claire asks about a conflict with a supplier, João does not deflect -- he addresses it head-on, takes ownership of his part, and shows what he changed. And he turns his past into the reason to hire him: Had I not worked on CBTC commissioning, I wouldn't have the judgment I bring now. A mixed conditional -- a past cause, a present result. None of this is new grammar; every structure he already owns. What is new is the purpose: in an interview, inversion, cleft and the conditional are not grammar, they are emphasis -- and he is here to make a compelling case, add measurable value, and contribute to the broader goals of a company that works across borders.

1. "Not only did I manage the scope, but I also coordinated three suppliers." Why did I and not I managed?
A Because "not only" can only be used in questions.
B Because fronting the negative "not only" triggers inversion -- the subject and auxiliary swap into question order (did I), which makes the achievement lean forward for emphasis.
C Because "manage" has no past form.
2. "It was São Francisco that gave me my exposure to international standards." What does this cleft do?
A It hides which project mattered.
B It splits the sentence to spotlight one project as THE turning point -- so the manager remembers exactly the fact João wants, instead of it being buried in a list.
C It changes the meaning to the future.
3. "Had I not worked on CBTC, I wouldn't have this experience today." What kind of link is this?
A A mixed conditional -- a PAST cause ("had I not worked") with a PRESENT result ("I wouldn't have"), which turns his past into the reason to hire him.
B A simple present habit.
C A future prediction with "will".
4. Why does the text say the manager "remembers the number, not the adjective"?
A Because measurable value is proof -- "caught two faults before the track" sticks, while words like "dedicated" slide off. A concrete result makes the case.
B Because managers dislike adjectives grammatically.
C Because numbers are easier to pronounce.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Three Structures for Authority (integration)

GRAMMAR

No new grammar tonight -- these three structures you already own. What changes is the PURPOSE: in an interview they are emphasis, the tool that decides where the spotlight falls.

StructureWhat it doesIn the room
Inversion
not only / never before + auxiliary + subject		Front a negative word and swap subject and auxiliary into question order (did I, had the team). The achievement leans forward and lands first."Not only did I manage the scope, but I also ran the interface."
Cleft sentence
it was … that / what … was		Split one sentence in two to spotlight the part that matters. You choose which fact the manager remembers, instead of burying it in a list."It was São Francisco that taught me international standards."
Mixed conditional + modal perfect
had I (not) … I would(n't) have		A past cause with a present result ("Had I not worked on CBTC, I wouldn't have this experience"), or a past regret with a past result ("If I'd been given the lead, I would have implemented a register"). Turns your past into the reason to hire you."Had I not worked on live commissioning, I wouldn't have this judgment."
Why it sounds seniorA nervous candidate flattens everything into "I did this, I did that." A senior one chooses where the spotlight falls. Same experience, opposite impression -- because emphasis is exactly what pressure makes you forget.

Watch out (three classic mistakes): (1) "Not only I managed the scope" -- inversion needs the question order: Not only did I manage; (2) "It was São Francisco gave me exposure" -- a cleft needs its that / who; (3) "If I not worked on CBTC, I don't have this" -- a mixed conditional links a past cause to a present result: Had I not worked … I wouldn't have.

Interview collocations: to leverage expertise · to take ownership of · to make a compelling case · to demonstrate initiative · to add measurable value · to address challenges head-on · to implement best practices.

The reflex to learn by heart: lead with your expertise → prove it with one project (a cleft: it was … that) → make the coordination lean forward (an inversion: not only did I) → turn your past into the reason to hire you (a mixed conditional: had I not … I wouldn't have). A case, not a CV.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each moment of the interview -- the reply that makes a case instead of reciting a CV.

Claire opens: "I've read your CV -- tell me about yourself and why you're right for this role." The strongest reply is:
A "I'm a signaling engineer with domain expertise in CBTC and hands-on experience from factory tests to live running -- and it was São Francisco that gave me my international exposure."
B "Well, I worked at Motiva, I did signaling, I did commissioning, and I did supplier meetings."
C "It's all on my CV, so I think you already know everything."
You want the coordination of three suppliers to stand out, not trail. You say:
A "Not only did I manage the technical scope, but I also coordinated three overseas suppliers at once."
B "Not only I managed the scope, but I also coordinated three suppliers."
C "I managed the scope. I coordinated some suppliers. That's about it."
She asks about a conflict with a supplier. You stay in control by:
A "I'll take that head-on: our records had a gap, I took ownership of it, we settled without prejudice, and I changed how I log approvals."
B "Honestly, it was entirely the supplier's fault and nothing to do with us."
C "I'd rather not talk about the difficult parts, if that's alright."
She asks about your greatest challenge right now. You turn it into strength with:
A "Communicating my judgment in English at full pace -- but had I not pushed into international projects, I wouldn't be addressing it as directly as I am now."
B "I don't really have any challenges; everything is fine."
C "If I not worked internationally, I don't have this problem now."
She asks why you want an international role. You make the case with:
A "I'm not looking to leave a job -- I'm pursuing an international role where I can add measurable value and contribute to the broader goals of a cross-border company."
B "Mostly because I want to leave my current company as soon as possible."
C "I'm not really sure, it just seemed like a good idea to apply."
