The Reframe

Reciting vs Making a Case

Reciting the CV (rehearsed)

"I worked at Motiva. I did signaling. I did commissioning. I did some supplier meetings. I have a post-graduation in project management."

A flat list of tasks. Nothing leans forward, nothing is proof, and the manager forgets it before you finish.

Making a case (senior)

"I'm a signaling engineer with domain expertise in CBTC. It was the São Francisco commissioning that gave me my first exposure to international standards — and I took ownership of the supplier interface, catching two faults before the track."

Lead with expertise, prove with one project, name the measurable result. The manager remembers the number.

A hiring manager asks for your case, not your history. Lead with your expertise, prove it with one project, and let the number carry the value. Same experience, opposite impression.

Chapter 3: The Code

Structures You Already Own

Inversion, cleft, mixed conditional — not new grammar, but the emphasis a nervous candidate forgets.

Grammar Discovery

Notice the Emphasis

Not only did I manage the technical scope, but I also coordinated three international suppliers.

It was the São Francisco commissioning that gave me my first exposure to international standards.

Had I not worked on CBTC commissioning, I wouldn't have the hands-on experience I have today.

Each of these could be flat. Instead something is moved or split: Not only did I (inversion), It was … that (cleft), Had I not … I wouldn't have (mixed conditional). None of it is new — it is emphasis.

StructureWhat it doesIn the room
Inversion
not only / never before + auxiliary + subject		Front a negative word and swap subject and verb, as in a question (did I, had the team). The achievement leans forward and lands first."Not only did I manage the scope, but I also ran the supplier interface."
Cleft sentence
it was … that / what … was		Split one sentence in two to spotlight the part that matters. You decide which fact the manager remembers instead of burying it in a list."It was São Francisco that taught me international standards."
Mixed conditional + modal perfect
had I (not) … I would(n't) have		A past cause with a present result ("Had I not worked on CBTC, I wouldn't have this experience"), or a past regret with a past result ("If I'd been given the lead, I would have implemented a register"). Turns your past into the reason to hire you."Had I not worked on live commissioning, I wouldn't have this judgment."
Why it sounds seniorNone of these is new grammar. What changes is the PURPOSE: in an interview they are emphasis, not decoration. A nervous candidate flattens everything into "I did this, I did that"; a senior one chooses where the spotlight falls. Same experience, opposite impression.

Three Tools, One Purpose

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Not only I managed the scope, but I also ran the interface."
"Not only did I manage the scope, but I also ran the interface."
"It was São Francisco gave me my exposure to international standards."
"It was São Francisco that gave me my exposure to international standards."
"If I not worked on CBTC, I don't have this experience today."
"Had I not worked on CBTC, I wouldn't have this experience today."

Inversion needs the question word order after the fronted negative (did I, not I managed). A cleft needs its that / who. A mixed conditional links a past cause to a present result — had I not … I wouldn't have. Get the frame right and the emphasis does the rest.

Sound First

What You Read vs What You Hear

The fronted auxiliary (did / had) is the clue that emphasis is coming.

You hear: "nod-only-did-eye-manage-it"
"Not only did I manage it."

You hear: "it-wuz-São-Francisco-thut-taught-me"
"It was São Francisco that taught me."

You hear: "had-eye-not-worked-there"
"Had I not worked there."

Compare (flat): "I-managed-it-and-I-also-ran-it"
"I managed it, and I also ran it." — no fronted auxiliary: just a plain list, no emphasis.

The clue is the fronted auxiliary: did I = inversion; it was … that = a cleft spotlight; had I = a mixed conditional. When you hear the auxiliary jump to the front, the speaker is choosing where the emphasis falls.

1. "CBTC commissioning" is essential. Prove it with one project, using a cleft for emphasis.
"It was the São Francisco commissioning that gave me my hands-on CBTC experience — I took the system from factory acceptance tests all the way to live running." One project, framed as the turning point, not a list.
2. "Managing international suppliers" is essential. Position yourself with an inversion.
"Not only did I manage the technical scope, but I also acted as the technical liaison between the operator and three overseas suppliers at once." The inversion makes the coordination lead, not trail.
3. The role is essential-heavy and you meet it. Make the case for why they should hire you, using a mixed conditional.
"Had I not spent those years on live commissioning, I wouldn't have the judgment I bring now — and that judgment is exactly what an international role rewards. I'm here to add measurable value on a bigger stage." Past cause, present value.

The Interview

J
I'm a signaling engineer with domain expertise in CBTC and hands-on experience from factory tests to live running. It was the São Francisco commissioning that gave me my first real exposure to international standards — and there I took ownership of the supplier interface.

J

J
I'll take that head-on. One supplier filed a delay claim; our own approval records had a gap. I took ownership of that gap, put the dated evidence on the table, and we settled without prejudice to either side. Had I not owned it early, the dispute would have cost us months — so I now log every approval the same day.
C
Last one. Why an international role, and why now?
J
I'm not looking to leave a job — I'm pursuing an international role where my expertise adds the most measurable value. Had I not spent those years on live commissioning, I wouldn't have the judgment I bring now. I want to implement best practices across borders and contribute to the broader goals of a company that works the way this one does.
Comprehension

About the Interview

1. What does Claire ask João NOT to do, and what does she want instead?
She has already read his CV, so she asks him not to read it back to her — she wants a case: who he is and why he's right for the role, not a list of past jobs.
2. How does Claire react to João's opening answer?
She says "that's a case, not a CV" — approval. Leading with expertise and framing one project with a cleft landed as a case, which is exactly what she asked for.
3. When Claire asks about a conflict with a supplier, what does she specifically warn him against?
"Don't sand off the edges" — she wants the real conflict, not a polished non-answer. She is testing whether he can address a hard question head-on and own his part.
4. What is João's stated reason for wanting an international role?
Not to escape his current job, but to add measurable value on a bigger stage — to implement best practices across borders and contribute to the broader goals of a cross-border company. He frames his past as the reason he can.
Listening

How a Senior Engineer Answers

A model "Tell me about yourself". Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. In what order does the engineer structure the answer, and what does he lead with?
He leads with what he IS — his domain expertise and hands-on experience — not where he was born. Then one project as proof, then why he's in the room. Lead with the case, give it a spine, close with the reason.
2. Why does he say the manager remembers the number, not the adjective?
Because "measurable value" is proof, not a claim: his risk register caught two faults before the track. A concrete result sticks, while words like "hardworking" or "dedicated" slide off. Show the number.
3. What does he say to do with the hard question, and why?
Address it head-on — the candidate who owns the difficult part is the one they trust with the easy part. Don't deflect, don't shrink; own your part and show what you changed.
Listening 2

The Coach's Three Rules

An interview coach, right before you go in. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does the coach say the interview is really testing, and what should every answer do?
Not your English, but whether you can make them confident. So stop translating your CV and make a case: every answer should say I've done this, here's the proof, here's what it does for you. That's the difference between rehearsed and senior.
2. Which three structures does the coach tell you to use on purpose, and what does each do?
Inversion ("not only did I manage") makes the sentence lean forward like leadership; the cleft ("it was São Francisco that taught me") puts the spotlight where you want it; the conditional ("had I not worked on commissioning, I wouldn't have this judgment") turns your past into the reason to hire you. Emphasis is what a nervous candidate forgets.
3. How does the coach say you should treat the hardest question?
As a gift. Don't deflect and don't shrink — address it head-on, take ownership of your part, and show what you changed because of it. The engineer with scars and lessons is stronger than the one with none.
