Lesson 19 -- Interview Simulation: Positioning Yourself as a Railway Signaling Expert for International Roles

TED Talk
Amy Cuddy -- "Your body language may shape who you are" (TED, ~21 min, free, plays in the browser)

Before the words come the nerves, and this is the most-watched talk on carrying yourself into a high-stakes room. Cuddy's argument is simple and useful for your interview: how you hold yourself before you speak changes how confident you feel when you do -- and confidence is exactly what a hiring manager is reading. You cannot fake the case, but you can walk in composed enough to make it. Watch it the night before, then practise your two minutes standing tall, the way you will actually sit across from Claire.

Tip: watch once with English subtitles and note what she says happens in the two minutes before a high-pressure moment. Then record your "Tell me about yourself" answer standing up, shoulders back, and compare it to the version you record slouched at your desk -- you will hear the difference in your own voice.

Podcast
Think Fast, Talk Smart (Stanford GSB) -- Ep. 151, "Get Hired: How the Right Communication Can Advance Your Career" (~25 min, free, plays in the browser)

This is your lesson from the recruiter's side of the table. The guest, a Stanford career expert, answers the exact question behind tonight's simulation: in an interview you are not being tested on your CV, you are being tested on whether you can make the room confident in you. She breaks down how to turn experience into a case -- lead with what you are, prove it with one story, and make the difficult question the moment you look most in control. Native English, real pace, and it maps straight onto the mock interview with Claire.

Tip: listen once and write down every phrase she gives for framing your own experience as value. Then take your three strongest facts -- CBTC commissioning, the São Francisco project, the supplier interface -- and turn each into one sentence a hiring manager would remember. Record all three, and make one of them a cleft ("it was ... that ...") and one a mixed conditional ("had I not ..., I wouldn't have ...").

YouTube
Linda Raynier -- "Tell Me About Yourself: A Good Answer to This Interview Question" (~9 min)

The exact question you drilled tonight, answered by a career coach who has trained thousands of candidates. She walks through the structure you practised -- lead with who you are, prove it with the highlights of your experience, and close with why you want this role -- and shows the difference between reciting a CV and making a case. Watch it AFTER the lesson to lock in the framework, then map it onto your real story: Motiva, signaling, São Francisco, CBTC, and the post-graduation in project management.

Tip: after the video, write your own 90-second "Tell me about yourself" in three sentences -- one leading with your domain expertise, one proving it with São Francisco (as a cleft), and one on why an international role. Record it three times: as you practised, then slightly nervous recovering mid-sentence, then faster as if time is short. Send all three.
