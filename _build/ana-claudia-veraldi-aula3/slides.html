Chapter 3: The Code

Quieter Than · The Quietest

Two things, or all of them

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"My town is quieter than the city."

"It is the most peaceful place I have ever lived."

"The city was far more convenient."

"That was the worst year, and this is the best one."

Why blue when there are two, but green when there is a winner?

FormUseExample
Short word + -er ... thanTwo things, one or two syllablesquiet → quieter than
more + long word ... thanTwo things, three syllables or moreconvenient → more convenient than
the + word + -est / the mostThe winner out of all of themthe quietest / the most peaceful
Irregulargood → better / the best · bad → worse / the worst · far → further / the furthest

To make it stronger: much / far / a lot + comparative ("much cheaper"). To soften: a bit / slightly.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"My town is more quiet than the city."
"My town is quieter than the city."
"It is the most cheapest place I know."
"It is the cheapest place I know."
"The air here is cleaner that in the city."
"The air here is cleaner than in the city."

Short words take -er, long words take more — never both. A superlative already means "the most", so the most cheapest says it twice. And the comparison word is than, not "that".

Practice

Comparative or Superlative?

Say it first, then click to check

"The air here is ___cleaner than in the city." (clean)
"This is ___the most peaceful place I have ever lived." (peaceful)
"The public transport there was ___better than here." (good)
"That was ___the worst winter of my life." (bad)
"Rent here is ___much cheaper." (cheap, and make it strong)
Detail

How Much Bigger?

much / far / a lot = strong · a bit / slightly = soft

"Rent is ___much cheaper here." (half the price)
"The hospital is ___a bit smaller." (only slightly)
"The city was ___far more convenient." (a big difference)
"Winters are ___slightly colder up here." (just a little)
