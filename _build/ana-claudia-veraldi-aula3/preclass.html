Lesson 03 -- Pre-class

Life in a Town of 40,000 -- Small Town vs Big City

Comparing the two lives you have lived. Key words: cost of living, pace of life, convenient, public transport, fresh air, pollution, wide range, nightlife, safe, isolated, facilities, affordable. Structures: comparatives (-er than / more ... than), superlatives (the -est / the most), irregulars (better, worse), and intensifiers (much, far, a bit).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Cost of living -- the money you need for daily life in a place
"The cost of living here is less than half of the city."
Pace of life -- how fast or slow daily life feels in a place
"The pace of life here is much slower, and I like that."
Convenient -- easy and quick to reach or use, saving you time
"The city was far more convenient at eleven at night."
Public transport -- the buses, trains and subways that everyone shares
"The public transport there was excellent, honestly."
Fresh air -- clean outdoor air, with nothing dirty in it
"I open the window for the fresh air, not for the view."
Pollution -- dirty air, water or noise made by human activity
"The pollution was the thing my body noticed first."
Wide range -- a large variety of different options to choose from
"There was a much wider range of restaurants there."
Nightlife -- the bars, restaurants and events that are open late
"Do I miss the nightlife? Honestly, not really."
Safe -- free from danger, where nothing bad is likely to happen
"I walk home at night here, which I never did before."
Isolated -- far away from other people and places
"Some people find it isolated. That is why I chose it."
Facilities -- the hospitals, schools and services that a place offers
"The facilities are worse here, and that is the real cost."
Affordable -- cheap enough for you to pay without difficulty
"A house with a backyard is affordable here. There, never."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

People often ask Ana which life is better, and she never gives a simple answer. Sao Paulo was far more convenient. The public transport was better than anything she has now, and there was a much wider range of restaurants, cinemas and nightlife. But it was also the most stressful period of her life. Today her town is much quieter, the pace of life is slower, and the cost of living is less than half. The air is cleaner, there is almost no pollution, and a house with a backyard is affordable here in a way it never was there. She feels safer too: she walks home at night, which she never did before. It is not perfect. The facilities are worse, the hospital is smaller, and some people would call the town isolated. But for her, it is the most peaceful place she has ever lived, and that is the best answer she has.

1. Why is it "the most stressful period" and not "the more stressful period"?
A Because she is comparing it with all the other periods of her life, not just one.
B Because "stressful" is an irregular adjective.
C Because the sentence is in the past.
2. The text says "much quieter", not "very quieter". Why?
A Because "quiet" is a long adjective.
B Because comparatives take much / far / a lot to get stronger, never "very".
C Because "very" cannot be used in the present tense.
3. Which sentence about the facilities is correct?
A "The facilities are more bad than in the city."
B "The facilities are worse than in the city."
C "The facilities are the most worst than in the city."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Comparatives and Superlatives

GRAMMAR

How to put two things side by side, and how to crown a winner.

FormUseExample
Short adjective + -er ... thanTwo things. One or two syllables.quiet → quieter than
more + long adjective ... thanTwo things. Three syllables or more.convenient → more convenient than
the + adjective + -est
the most + long adjective		The winner out of all of them.the quietest / the most peaceful
Irregularsgood → better / the best · bad → worse / the worst · far → further / the furthest
Stronger / softermuch, far, a lot + comparative (much cheaper) · a bit, slightly + comparative (a bit smaller). Never "very cheaper".
Spellingbig → bigger (double the consonant) · easy → easier (y becomes i)

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer.

Someone who has always lived in a big city asks you: "Honestly, is it better out there?" Compare the two lives completely - the cost, the pace, the air, the facilities, how safe you feel - and then give your verdict and say why. Use comparatives, at least one superlative, and much or a bit to show how big each difference is. Take your time.
