What Would You Like to Order? -- Eating Out in a European Restaurant

Ask for what you want politely, and compare two dishes before you choose: we would like a table for two, the fish is cheaper than the beef. Key words: menu, appetizer, main course, dessert, sparkling water, to order, to recommend, bill, tip, allergic to. Structure: would like for polite requests (and how it is different from want), plus comparatives with -er / more ... than. Expression: I used to...

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to every word and read the example. Five words live on the menu, and five live around the table. Together they are your dinner in Porto.

Menu -- the card with all the food and the prices of the restaurant
"The menu is long, and I am reading it slowly."
Appetizer -- the small first dish, before the big one
"We would like two tomato soups for the appetizer."
Main course -- the big dish, the center of the meal
"For the main course, the grilled fish is the special tonight."
Dessert -- the sweet dish at the end of the meal
"For dessert, I would like the orange tart."
Sparkling water -- water with gas and bubbles in it
"We would like a bottle of sparkling water, please."
To order -- to tell the waiter what you want to eat
"Before you order, let me tell you about our specials."
To recommend -- to say which dish is good, when somebody asks you
"What do you recommend?"
Bill -- the paper with the price of everything you ate
"Could we have the bill, please?"
Tip -- the extra money you leave for the waiter, on top of the bill
"She leaves a tip -- ten percent."
Allergic to -- when a food makes you sick, and you cannot eat it
"My husband is allergic to nuts."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

It is eight o'clock in Porto. A waiter is coming to the table with two menus. "Good evening. Would you like something to drink?" Rosângela does not look at her husband. "Good evening. We would like a bottle of sparkling water, please." The menu is long. "What do you recommend for the main course?" she asks. "The grilled fish," the waiter says. "It is fresher than the meat, and it is cheaper." "Perfect. We would like two tomato soups for the appetizer, and then the grilled fish for two. One more thing: my husband is allergic to nuts." The waiter goes to the kitchen, and he comes back: "No nuts. It is safe." For dessert, she would like the orange tart, because it is lighter than the chocolate cake. At the end she raises her hand: "Could we have the bill, please?" She reads it, and she leaves a tip -- ten percent. She used to wait for her husband to order for her. Tonight she ordered.

1. "We would like a bottle of sparkling water." Why not "We want a bottle"?
A Because "want" does not exist in English.
B Both are correct English, but "would like" is the polite one. With a waiter, a stranger or a hotel, you use "would like".
C Because there are two people at the table.
2. The waiter says the fish is "fresher than the meat". Why "fresher" and not "more fresh"?
A Because "fresh" is a short word, and short words take -er. Long words take "more" in front.
B Because fish is always fresh.
C Because the sentence is a question.
3. "The tart is lighter than the chocolate cake." What does "than" do here?
A It is the same as "that", and both work here.
B It shows the time of the meal.
C It joins the two things you are comparing. When you compare, it is always "than", never "that".
4. The husband does not speak English. Who tells the waiter about the nut allergy?
A Nobody. They order something else and say nothing.
B Rosângela does, before the waiter goes to the kitchen. It is safety, and she is the one who can say it.
C The husband writes it on the menu.
5. "She used to wait for her husband to order for her." Does she wait for him today?
A Yes. "Used to" is a habit she still has today.
B No. "Used to" is a habit from the past that is finished. Tonight she ordered herself.
C It is not possible to know from the text.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Would Like and Comparatives

GRAMMAR

In Lesson 5 you learned to say the plan: "We are going to take the metro." Now you learn to ask for what you want — politely — and to compare two things before you choose.

FormUseExample
I / we would like + a thingAsking for something, politely. The same words for everybody: I, you, he, she, we. Never "he would likes".I would like the fish. · We would like the bill.
I would like to + verbWith a verb, "to" comes in the middle. Only before a VERB — never before a thing.I would like to pay. (never "I would like to the fish")
Question
Would you like ...?		"Would" jumps to the front. This is the question the waiter asks you. Answer "Yes, please" or "No, thank you".Would you like a dessert? — "Yes, please."
would like vs wantSame meaning, different room. "I want" is for family and friends; "I would like" is for a waiter, a hotel, a stranger."I want the fish" → direct. "I would like the fish, please" → polite.
Comparative
short word + -er than		Short words add -er. If it ends in one vowel + one consonant, double the last letter.cheap → cheaper than · light → lighter than · big → bigger than
Comparative
more + long word + than		Long words do not take -er. Put "more" in front. And "good" is irregular: better.The beef is more expensive than the fish. · good → better than
I used to + plain verbA habit from the past that is finished today. The verb never changes: used to eat, never "used to ate".I used to eat only meat, but now I love fish.

The one rule to remember: "would like" costs one extra word and changes everything — it is the difference between an order and a request. And when you compare, the little word is always than, never "that".

Speaking

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer for these real moments at the table.

The waiter meets you at the door. You are two people, and you want the table by the window. You say:
A "Two. Window."
B "Good evening. We would like a table for two, please. By the window, if possible."
C "I would like to the table of the window."
"Would you like a dessert?" You do want one. The best answer is:
A "Yes, please. I would like the orange tart."
B "Yes. I want tart."
C "Yes, I would like to the orange tart."
Your husband cannot eat nuts. When do you tell the waiter?
A After the food arrives, if there is a problem.
B Never. It is not polite to talk about it in a restaurant.
C Before he goes to the kitchen: "My husband is allergic to nuts. Is there anything with nuts in it?"
The fish is 17 euros and the beef is 24. Which sentence is right?
A "The fish is more cheap that the beef."
B "The fish is cheaper than the beef."
C "The fish is cheaper that the beef."
Dinner is finished and you want to pay. You say:
A "I want to pay now."
B "Give me the bill."
C "Excuse me. Could we have the bill, please?"

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt. There is no right or wrong version. Speak for 60 seconds, with no script.

Think about your dinners in Italy, in Paris, in London -- the real ones. Who used to order for you? What did you use to eat, and what do you love now? Then put yourself at a table in Porto tonight: what would you like for the appetizer, the main course and the dessert, and why? Compare two dishes and choose one. Use "I used to..." at least twice, "I would like..." at least twice, and one comparative with "than". Take your time and do not read from a script.
