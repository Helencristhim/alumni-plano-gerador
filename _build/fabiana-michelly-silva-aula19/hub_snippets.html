Indemnity -- a promise to cover the other party's losses or damages
"The supplier must give an indemnity against any third-party claim." (Indenização / garantia de ressarcimento)
Bargaining chip -- something you can offer or trade to gain an advantage in a deal
"The faster payment terms are our main bargaining chip." (Moeda de troca / trunfo de negociação)
Redline -- a marked change or objection to the wording of a draft contract
"We sent back the draft with a redline on the penalty wording." (Marcação de objeção / alteração no rascunho)
Liability cap -- a limit on how much one party must pay for damages
"Unless we agree a liability cap of two million per incident, I cannot sign." (Limite de responsabilidade)
Default -- a failure to fulfill a contractual obligation, such as a deadline
"Missing a confirmed deadline counts as a default, and the penalty applies." (Inadimplência / descumprimento)
Binding -- legally enforceable; you must follow it
"The concession must be binding, not just a verbal understanding." (Vinculante / obrigatório)
Terminate -- to end a contract before its natural close
"Either party may terminate on ninety days notice." (Rescindir / encerrar)
Waiver -- a formal voluntary giving up of a right
"The penalty applies in full, with no waiver." (Renúncia / dispensa)
Force majeure -- unforeseeable events that excuse a party from a duty
"The force majeure clause stays standard." (Força maior / caso fortuito)
Amendment -- a formal change made to an existing contract
"I will send the amendment for signature today." (Aditivo / emenda contratual)
In good faith -- honestly and fairly, with sincere intentions
"If they accept those points in good faith, we have a deal." (De boa-fé)
Penalty clause -- a fixed charge for failing to meet a contract term
"Should the supplier miss a deadline, the penalty clause applies." (Cláusula de multa / penalidade)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

In a contract negotiation, the plain word if often sounds too casual. Formal English offers sharper, more powerful variations. To set a real condition, you can invert with Should: "Should the supplier miss a deadline, the penalty clause applies." Here Should + the supplier + miss (base verb) replaces "If the supplier misses". To make a hypothetical offer, you invert with Were: "Were you to bring the lead time down to ten days, we would halve the penalty." This means "If you brought it down". To attach a strict condition, use provided that, as long as, or on condition that: "We hold the rate provided that you commit to the volume." And unless means "if not": "Unless we agree a liability cap, I cannot sign." Notice: the inverted forms already contain the "if", so you never write "if were you to"; and "unless" already means "if not", so you never write "unless if". When the condition comes first, a comma separates it from the result.

1. "Should the supplier miss a deadline, the penalty applies." This condition is:
A real and formal -- an inverted way of saying "If the supplier misses a deadline".
B a pure hypothesis that will never happen.
C a command telling the supplier what to do.
2. Which sentence is correct?
A "Unless if we get a cap, we cannot sign."
B "Unless we get a cap, we cannot sign." -- "unless" already means "if not".
C "If unless we get a cap, we cannot sign."
3. Why use "Were you to bring the lead time down..." instead of "If you brought..."?
A It is a more formal, hypothetical way to make a conditional offer in a negotiation.
B It means the opposite of "If you brought it down".
C It is only used to talk about the past.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best response for each professional situation.

You want to attach a penalty to a missed deadline in a formal, precise way. You say:
A "Should the supplier miss a confirmed deadline, the penalty clause applies, with no waiver." (inverted real condition)
B "If they are late, maybe we charge them something, I am not sure." (vague, weak)
C "We will see what happens if they miss it." (no binding term)
You want to offer a conditional concession in exchange for a faster lead time. The best version is:
A "If were you to cut the lead time, we would halve the penalty." (incorrect -- double "if")
B "Were you to bring the lead time down to ten days, we would halve the penalty." (inverted hypothetical)
C "We halve the penalty and you do whatever you want with the lead time." (no condition attached)
A supplier asks you to drop the liability cap. You want to hold firm. You say:
A "Unless we agree a liability cap, I cannot recommend this to the board." (firm condition, "if not")
B "Unless if we get a cap, no deal." (incorrect -- "unless if")
C "Fine, we can drop the cap, no problem." (gives away a key protection)
