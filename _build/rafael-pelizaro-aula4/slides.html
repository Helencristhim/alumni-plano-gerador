Renewing the Software Contract — A Vendor Negotiation

Every year, Rafael has to renew the licenses for his team's project software. This time, the first quote from the supplier is fifteen percent over his budget, so he cannot simply accept it. Before the call, he prepares his proposal carefully. He decides which terms matter most to him and the one price he would be happy with. A good manager never opens a negotiation without a plan.

On the call, Rafael stays calm and polite. He thanks the supplier for the quote, but explains that the price is a little high for this year. "Would you consider a discount," he asks, "for a two-year commitment instead of one?" The supplier is flexible. She offers ten percent off for the longer deal, but she asks for faster payment in return. Rafael makes a counteroffer: he would accept the longer term and the faster payment, but only if support is included at no extra cost.

After a few minutes, they reach an agreement that works for both sides. Rafael gets a better price and full support; the supplier gets a two-year deal. Nobody has to lose. The secret is simple: know your terms, stay polite, and always be ready to offer something in return. A negotiation is not a fight — it is a conversation where both people look for a deal they would both sign.

Adapted for class use from an internal IT vendor-management note

Chapter 4: The Code

Polite Proposals with Would

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Find the Pattern

"Would you consider a discount for a longer deal?"

"We would accept these terms with full support."

"I would suggest a two-year contract."

"That would work for both sides."

FormUseExample
would + base verba polite, softer proposal or offerWe would accept these terms.
Would you + base verb?a polite request in a negotiationWould you consider a discount?

Use would to make an offer or request softer and more polite. After would, use the base verb with no to (consider, accept, work). In speech we often say I'd, we'd, that'd.

Common Mistake

Wrong vs Right

I would to suggest a discount.
I would suggest a discount.

After would, use the base verb with no "to" (would suggest, not would to suggest).

We would accepted the offer.
We would accept the offer.

After would, use the base form (accept), never the past (accepted).

The Negotiation Call

Dialogue

Closing the Deal

S
Thanks for your interest, Rafael. As you saw, the quote for one year is twelve thousand dollars.
R
Thank you, Sofia. The proposal looks good, but the price is a little over our budget this year.
S
I understand. What did you have in mind?
R
Would you consider a discount for a two-year commitment instead of one?
S
For a two-year commitment, I could offer ten percent off, but I would need faster payment.
R
That would work. We would accept the longer term and faster payment, but only with support included.

About the Call

1. What is the price of the one-year quote?
Twelve thousand dollars.
2. What does Sofia offer for a two-year commitment?
Ten percent off the price.
3. What does Sofia ask for in return?
Faster payment.
