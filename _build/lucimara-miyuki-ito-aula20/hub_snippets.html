Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Reduction -- a shortened, relaxed form of words in fast speech, like 'gonna' for 'going to'
"In fast speech, 'want to' becomes the reduction 'wanna'."
Contraction -- two words joined and shortened, like 'don't' or 'let's'
"'Do not' turns into the contraction 'don't' in normal speech."
Connected speech -- the way words blend together when people talk quickly
"In connected speech, 'what are you' can sound like 'whatcha'."
Intonation -- the rise and fall of the voice that adds meaning
"Her intonation went up, so I knew it was a question."
Word stress -- the syllable you say louder and longer in a word
"Word stress on the important words helps you catch the meaning."
Inference -- a smart guess about meaning based on clues and context
"I did not hear the price, but I made an inference from her gesture."
To get the gist -- to understand the general meaning without catching every word
"I did not catch every word, but I got the gist of the sale."
To tune in -- to start listening closely and follow what is said
"When the clerk spoke, I tried to tune in to the keywords."
To tune out -- to stop paying attention to a sound or a voice
"The store was noisy, so I had to tune out the background music."
To catch on -- to begin to understand something after a moment
"At first it was fast, but I started to catch on."
To pick up -- to learn words informally, just by hearing them
"I picked up a few phrases just by shopping in New York."
To lose the thread -- to stop following what someone is saying
"He spoke so fast that I lost the thread for a second."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Lucimara's listening has changed a lot. She used to freeze the moment someone spoke fast. She used to translate every single word, and she often lost the thread. She didn't use to notice reductions like "gonna" or "wanna". Now everything is different: she listens for keywords, she gets the gist, and she picks up new phrases just by hearing them. What used to feel impossible now feels normal.

1. In "She used to freeze", what does "used to" tell us?
A It was a past habit that is no longer true.
B It is something she does now.
C It will happen in the future.
2. Which sentence is correct?
A She didn't use to notice reductions.
B She didn't used to notice reductions.
C She doesn't used to notice reductions.
3. How do you turn "I used to translate every word" into a question?
A Did you use to translate every word?
B Did you used to translate every word?
C Used you to translate every word?

GRAMMAR

FormStructureExample

Negativedidn't use to + base"I didn't use to understand reductions."
QuestionDid ... use to + base?"Did you use to translate every word?"
Meaningpast habit, no longer true"I used to tune out, but now I tune in."
