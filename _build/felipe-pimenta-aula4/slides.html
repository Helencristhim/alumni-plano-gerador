Chapter 4: The Code

The Grammar of Regret

I wish I had... · If only we had...

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I wish I had more time before the announcement."

"I wish I had listened to the two directors."

"If only we had offered a longer severance package."

"I wish the board would decide faster."

Two are about now, two are about the past, and one is a complaint. The tense always steps one pace backwards. Why?

FormUseExample
wish + past simpleA regret about NOW. The reality is the opposite of what you say.I wish I had more time. (I don't.)
wish + past perfectA regret about the PAST. Your main tool tonight.I wish I had listened. (I didn't.)
wish + would + verbA complaint about someone else's behavior — never about yourself.I wish the board would decide faster.
If only...The same structures, but stronger and more emotional. Use it once, not five times.If only we had managed it through attrition.
wish + couldA regret about ability, present or past.I wish I could have protected that team.

The tense lies on purpose: it steps back one pace to signal "this is not real." That is why it is called the unreal past — the same trick behind if I were you.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I wish I would have listened to the two directors."
"I wish I had listened to the two directors."
"I wish I have more time before the announcement."
"I wish I had more time before the announcement."
"If only we would offer a longer severance package."
"If only we had offered a longer severance package."

After wish, the tense always steps BACK: had for now, had + participle for the past. wish + would exists, but only to complain about someone else — never about yourself.

Chapter 5: The Conversation

Your Head of People Wants a Word

Three months after the cuts

Dialogue

What We Wish We Had Done

N
Felipe, the numbers recovered. But I lost four of my best people this quarter, and none of them were on the layoff list.
F
I know. And I wish I had listened to you in March, when you told me the morale problem would come from the silence, not from the cuts.
N
It was not only the silence. The severance was legal, but it was thin. People talk.
F
If only we had offered three months instead of one. We had the cash. That was a trade-off I got wrong — I protected the runway and I spent ten years of goodwill to do it.
N
That is the most candid thing you have said all year. So what now?
F
Now I say this out loud, to the whole company, in exactly these words. I wish we had told them earlier and paid them better. And then I fix the workload of the people who stayed, because that is the part I can still change.
Comprehension

About Nadia

1. What is Nadia's problem, three months after the cuts?
She lost four of her best people — and none of them were on the layoff list.
2. What does she say about the severance?
It was legal, but thin — and people talk.
3. How does she react to Felipe's admission?
She calls it the most candid thing he has said all year, and asks what he will do now.

0:000:00

Role-Play

The Conversation with Nadia

Scenario: Your Head of People tells you the company is bleeding talent that was never on the layoff list. Do not defend the decision. Name what you wish you had done, own the trade-off you got wrong, and say what you will change for the people who stayed.

Keywords:

I wish I had listened when...If only we had offered...That was a trade-off I got wrong.What I can still change is...
Role-Play

The Person You Laid Off

Scenario: Six months later, at a conference, you meet someone you laid off. She is polite. She asks, quietly: "Was there really no other way?" Answer her honestly, as a person — not as a company.

Keywords:

Honestly, I wish we had...There was a trade-off, and I...What I got wrong was...You deserved to hear it earlier.
Role-Play

The Speech You Owe Them

Scenario: Address the whole company, three months after the restructuring. Be candid: say what you wish you had done differently, name the trade-off you got wrong, do not blame the board or the headwinds, and finish with the one thing you can still change — the workload of the people who stayed.
