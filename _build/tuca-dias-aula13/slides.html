Chapter 3 -- Second Conditional

If I Had..., I Would...

Talking about imaginary situations and dreams

Listen & Repeat

Second Conditional in Action

"If I had more money, I would travel every year."

"If the fee were lower, I would change money here."

"If I lost my card, I would call the bank at once."

"If I were you, I would use a card, not cash."

Grammar Rule

Second Conditional: If + Past, Would

StructureUseExample
If + past, would + basean imaginary or unlikely situation nowIf I had more time, I would travel.
If I were you, would + basepolite advice in an imaginary caseIf I were you, I would use a card.
Compare: first conditionala real future possibility (last class)If I have cash, I will pay now.

Key idea: the past in the if part does not mean past time -- it shows the situation is imaginary: "If I had a million" (but I do not). After would, use the base verb: would travel, would call. With to be, use were for all persons: "if I were you".

Common Mistake

Watch Out

"If I would have more money, I would travel."
"If I had more money, I would travel."

In the if part, use the past simple. The would goes only in the result: if I had more money, I would travel.

About the Message

1. What is the daily limit at the cash machine, and what do you do for more?

The daily limit is five hundred dollars. For more, you have to come to a counter and show your passport.

2. What should you check before you accept an exchange?

You should check the exchange rate and the fee, because there is a small fee for every exchange.

3. What must you do if you lose your card?

You must call the number on the back of your bank statement at once, so they can block the card.
