Lesson 11 -- Pre-class

Should I or Shouldn't I? -- Modals for Advice

Give and receive advice before the delegation flies to Seoul: Bruno asks for your advice, and you answer. Key words: recommend, suggest, consider, advise, avoid, risk, option, prepare. Structures: should and shouldn't for advice, could for a suggestion, might for what is not sure — always with the base verb (no "to", no "-s"). Question for advice: Should I...? Expression: "In my opinion..." Idiom of the lesson: "Better to be safe than sorry."

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. These are the eight words you use when you give advice.

Recommend -- to tell someone that something is a good idea
"I recommend you confirm the hotel today."
Suggest -- to give someone an idea to think about
"I suggest we prepare the agenda first."
Consider -- to think carefully about something before you decide
"You should consider the risk before you choose."
Advise -- to tell someone what you think is the best thing to do
"I advise you to bring a second copy."
Avoid -- to stay away from something because it is a problem
"You should avoid a long trip without your documents."
Risk -- the chance that something bad can happen
"There is a small risk, so we should prepare."
Option -- one thing you can choose from a list of choices
"We have two options for the flight."
Prepare -- to get everything ready before an event
"We should prepare everything before November."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Bruno has a few doubts before Seoul, so he asks Maria Clara for advice. "My visa might not be ready. Should I travel anyway?" he asks. "No, you shouldn't travel without it," she says. "You could call the embassy today." In her opinion, the rector is important, so she recommends that Bruno invite him — but he should confirm the date first. The interpreter might cancel again, so Maria Clara advises Bruno to prepare a second option. "You shouldn't take a big risk before an important trip," she says. "Better to be safe than sorry." Bruno writes everything down. Now he knows what he should do, and what he should avoid.

1. Why is it "you shouldn't travel" and NOT "you shouldn't to travel"?
A Because the sentence is negative.
B Because after a modal (should, could, might) you use the base verb — never "to".
C Because "travel" is a special word.
2. Bruno says: "The rector is important." Maria Clara gives strong advice. Which is correct?
A "You should invite him." (strong advice = should)
B "You might invite him." (might = just maybe, not advice)
C "You shouldn't invite him."
3. The interpreter "might cancel again." What does "might" mean here?
A She will definitely cancel.
B Maybe she cancels — it is possible, but not sure.
C She should cancel.
4. Which sentence gives advice correctly?
A "She should to confirms the date and we could to wait."
B "She should confirm the date, and we could wait one more day."
C "She shoulds confirm and we coulds wait."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Should, Could, Might

GRAMMAR

Advice is simple in English: a modal + the base verb. This is how you say what someone should, could, or might do.

ModalUse it forExample
shouldAdvice — a good idea, the right thing to do.You should confirm the date.
shouldn'tAdvice NOT to do — a bad idea.You shouldn't travel without a visa.
couldA suggestion — one possible option.We could invite the rector.
mightMaybe — you are not sure it will happen.I might arrive late.
The form: modal + base verbNo to, no -s — even with she/he. Always the base verb.She should go (NOT "should to go", NOT "shoulds").
Question & expressionAsk for advice with Should I...? Start softly with In my opinion...Should I bring an interpreter? · In my opinion, we should wait.

Common mistake: many people say "should to go" or "she shoulds go". Remember: after a modal, always the base verb — "should go", "could invite", "might arrive". No "to", no "-s". The idiom of the lesson: "Better to be safe than sorry."

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Choose the best answer for each moment when Bruno asks you for advice.

Bruno asks: "My visa might not be ready. Should I travel anyway?" You answer:
A "Yes, you should to travel without it."
B "No, you shouldn't travel without it. You could call the embassy today."
C "You shoulds wait for the visa."
He asks: "Should I invite the rector?" The best answer is:
A "You might to invite him."
B "Yes, I recommend you invite him, but you should confirm the date first."
C "He should invites him."
The interpreter may cancel. You are not sure. You say:
A "The interpreter should cancel again."
B "The interpreter might cancel, so we should prepare a second option."
C "The interpreter shouldn't cancels."
Bruno wants to put his passport in his luggage. You give advice:
A "You shouldn't put it in your luggage. You should keep it with you."
B "You should to put it in your luggage."
C "You might put it in your luggage, no problem."
You want to ASK Bruno for advice about the meeting. You say:
A "I should start the meeting?"

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer — speak for one or two minutes, with no script. Every attempt counts.

A new colleague is traveling to Seoul for the first time and asks you for advice. Give at least five pieces of advice, using should, shouldn't, could, and might: about the documents (what they should carry and where they shouldn't put it), the schedule (what they should confirm), the money (what they could take), the interpreter (what might happen and what they should prepare), and one thing they should avoid. Start with "In my opinion..." and close with the idiom of the lesson: "Better to be safe than sorry." Take your time and do not read from a script.
