Impact

Three Ways to Report the Same Fault

The fault: a relay dropped out during the functional test. Rank the reports, say why, then click to compare

A. "The relay fails. We look. It is broken. The system is not good now."
All present tense, all vague. It names nothing precisely and reconstructs no sequence — it sounds like someone guessing in real time. The supplier reads this as panic, not analysis, and you have told them nothing they can act on.
B. "The relay dropped out during the test and the system went into degraded mode."
Correct simple past, but it only describes the symptom in the order you noticed it. It does not say what had happened first — so it leaves the root cause invisible and the timeline flat. Better than A, but still not an inspector's report.
C. "By the time the alarm came up, the relay had been dropping out since the night before, so the system had been running in degraded mode for six hours."
The past perfect reconstructs the sequence: the cause came first, the symptom came later. This is the whole of root cause analysis in one sentence — it sounds precise and in control, exactly what a witness test needs. This is tonight's skill.

The Tool

The Reporter's Move

1 — Reconstruct

What had happened before the symptom showed? Use the past perfect to put the cause first — that is root cause analysis.

2 — Name It Precisely

Defect or snag? What is the failure mode? Do not soften a defect or inflate a snag — the word is your competence.

3 — Hold the Function

Work will not resume on the affected function until the fix has passed its own functional test. Flag it before anyone proceeds.

4 — Commit

Close with a plan, not a worry. Say what you will have done and by when — the future perfect turns a defect into a managed risk.

Under pressure the untrained instinct is to narrate the symptom in the present. The inspector does the opposite: reconstruct what had happened, name it precisely, hold the function, and commit to what you will have done. That is field English in control.

Chapter 3: The Code

Tenses You Already Own

past perfect = what had happened · past perfect continuous = how long · future perfect = what you will have done

Grammar Discovery

Which Tense Does Each Job?

"By the time we identified the fault, the system had been running in degraded mode for six hours."

"The supplier had not completed the pre-commissioning checklist before we arrived."

"The relay had been dropping out intermittently since the night before."

"By Friday we will have verified all the interlocking tables."

Four green verbs, three jobs. One reconstructs what had happened before a moment in the past; one adds how long it had been going on; one commits to what will be finished by a future deadline. Which is which — and which one turns a defect into a managed risk?

TenseFormJob on the floor
Past perfecthad + past participleReconstructs what had happened before a past moment. Puts the cause first, the symptom second — root cause analysis.
Past perfect continuoushad been + -ingAdds how long it had been going on before the fault showed — the duration behind a failure mode.
Future perfectwill have + past participleCommits to what will be finished by a named future point — turns a worry into a managed plan.
Why it mattersNone of this is new grammar — you already own it. Under field pressure the instinct is to flatten everything into the simple past and present. The past perfect separates cause from symptom; the future perfect closes the finding with a deadline. Same facts, opposite level of control.
Meaning

Three Tenses Do the Work

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"The relay failed and then the system went into degraded mode."
"By the time we saw the alarm, the relay had already been failing and the system had fallen back to degraded mode."
"By Friday we verify all the interlocking tables."
"By Friday we will have verified all the interlocking tables."

A flat simple past hides the sequence — it makes the symptom look like the whole story and leaves the root cause invisible. And a bare present for a deadline sounds unfinished: "by Friday we verify" could mean started, not done. The past perfect reconstructs the cause; the future perfect commits to a finished result. Read the fault by its tenses.

The Fault Call

Reporting It Live

P
João, we had an alarm on the ATP logic at 10:14, mid functional test. My team says it's just a glitch and we can carry on. Can we?
J
Not a glitch. By the time the alarm came up, the affected relay had been dropping out intermittently since the night before — the system had been running in degraded mode for about six hours. The alarm is the symptom; the intermittent contact is the cause.
P
All right — but is this a real defect, or one of the small snags we can just note and move past?
J
It's the defect — the failure mode is an intermittent contact on one relay. The loose label and the missing cable tag, those are snags for the punch list. But the interlocking had already fallen back to degraded mode as designed, so no unsafe command was issued — and the type approval stays valid, because it's an installation fault, not a design fault.
P
Fine. We'll swap the relay. Can my team keep testing that function in parallel to save the schedule?
J
I have to hold that one. Work will not resume on the affected function until the replacement relay has passed its own functional test. I'd like to flag this before we proceed — I'll log the non-conformance now and escalate it to the project manager.
P
Understood. And what do I tell my director about the witness test — where will we be by Friday?
J
Tell them it's a managed non-conformance, not a crisis. By the close of this shift we will have re-tested the ATP logic, and before the witness test on Friday we will have verified all the interlocking tables end to end. Four hours of downtime today, and the test back on track for Friday.

Listening

The Manager's Brief

Just before the witness test. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Why does the manager tell João NOT to report a fault in the simple past?
Because the simple past only narrates the symptom in the order it was noticed and hides the cause. He wants João to reconstruct the sequence with the past perfect — say what had already happened before the problem showed — because that is the whole of root cause analysis and it makes João sound precise and in control, not guessing.
2. What does he say about naming what you see, and about committing to the future?
Name it precisely: a defect is a defect, a snag is a small one, a failure mode is the specific way it broke — don't soften a defect or inflate a snag, because the supplier reads your vocabulary as your competence. And commit with the future perfect: not "we will verify the tables" but "we will have verified all the tables before the witness test on Friday" — it tells everyone the work will be finished, not just started.
3. What does he tell João to do when he can't understand a heavy accent over the noise?
Do not nod and hope — ask. "Can you walk me through this? Is this documented anywhere? Who has authority to sign this off?" Repair the communication out loud, because a witness test is no place to guess.
Listening 2

Three Rules for the Floor

A commissioning mentor. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What is the first rule — and what tense does the mentor say to reach for?
Slow the moment down before you speak: the pressure makes your English collapse into the present tense and vague words. Take one breath and reconstruct the timeline with the past perfect — what had happened before the symptom showed, whether the equipment had already passed the functional test or had been failing quietly the whole time. On a witness test, that distinction between cause and effect decides who is responsible.
2. What is the second rule about not understanding the supplier?
Protect your right to not understand. A supplier's engineer speaking fast, with a heavy accent, over workshop noise is hard for anyone. The weak move is to nod and pretend; the strong move is to ask, calmly, every time — "Can you walk me through this? Is this documented anywhere? We need to put this on hold until we clarify." Asking is what a serious inspector does, and it protects you when the report is written later.
3. What is the third rule — how do you close a finding?
Close every finding with a plan, not a worry. Don't leave a defect hanging. Say what you will have done and by when, using the future perfect: "I will have logged the non-conformance today, and we will have re-run the functional test before the witness test on Friday." A defect with a deadline is a managed risk; a defect without one is a crisis.
