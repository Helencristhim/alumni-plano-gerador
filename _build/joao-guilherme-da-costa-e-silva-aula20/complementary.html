Lesson 20 -- Field English Under Pressure: Managing a Factory Acceptance Test and an On-Site Crisis

Video
BBC Learning English — "Narrative tenses: BBC English Class" (YouTube, ~9 min)

Telling a story and reporting a fault are the same grammar problem: you have to show what happened before the moment you noticed it. Dan walks through the narrative tenses — past simple, past continuous and, crucially, the past perfect — using a real story about how he got a scar. Watch it as the grammar under tonight's field report: the past perfect is what lets you say the relay had been dropping out long before the alarm made the defect visible.

Tip: after watching, retell today's fault out loud in three tenses — what had been happening (past perfect continuous), what happened (past simple), and what you will have done by Friday (future perfect). Record yourself and check the sequence is clear.

Article
Grammarly — "Past Perfect Tense: How to Use It, With Examples" (~8 min read)

The clear reference for the tense that carries a root-cause report. It shows exactly what you drilled tonight: the past perfect (had + past participle) exists to mark a level of the past that is earlier than another past moment — and it lives with the very time expressions you use on site, by the time and before. That is precisely what separates the cause from the symptom when you reconstruct a fault for a supplier.

Tip: take one real finding from a Motiva inspection and write two versions — a flat simple-past line, and a past-perfect line that puts the cause first. Notice how the second one sounds like an inspector, not a bystander.

Reference
Grammarly — "Future Perfect: How to Use It, With Examples" (~7 min read)

The other half of tonight's lesson: the tense that closes a finding with a deadline. It makes the one rule that matters on a witness test explicit — the future perfect (will have + past participle) is only for an action that will be complete before a specified future point. That is why "we will have verified all the tables before Friday" is a managed plan, while "we verify the tables" leaves everyone unsure whether the work is finished or merely started.

Tip: write three commitments for your next test in the future perfect — each with a named deadline (by the end of the shift, before the witness test, by Friday). Read them aloud until the "will have + participle" pattern is automatic under pressure.
