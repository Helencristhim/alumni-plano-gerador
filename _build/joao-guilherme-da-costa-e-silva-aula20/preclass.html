Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Last week you sat the interview. Tonight you are on a factory floor, and a fault appears mid-test. These are the words of the field: what you name when something breaks, how you describe the way it broke, and the safe state a system falls back to. Listen to each term and read the example.

Defect -- a fault or flaw found in equipment during a test or inspection
"The intermittent relay was the defect that caused four hours of downtime."
Snag -- a small, minor defect that can be fixed later, put on a punch list
"A loose label and a missing cable tag are snags — they don't block completion."
Failure mode -- the specific way in which a component or system fails
"The failure mode was an intermittent contact on a single trackside relay."
Degraded mode -- a reduced but safe state a system falls back to after a fault
"The interlocking fell back to degraded mode as designed, so no unsafe command was issued."
Witness test -- a test the client watches in person to confirm the result
"We carry out the witness test on Friday, with the client's inspector on the floor."
Functional test -- a test that checks the equipment does what it is meant to do
"Work won't resume until the replacement relay has passed its own functional test."
Type approval -- official certification that a product's design meets the required standard
"The type approval stays valid — the fault is an installation fault, not a design fault."
ATP (Automatic Train Protection) -- the safety system that automatically stops a train to prevent a collision
"An alarm was raised on the down-line ATP logic at 10:14."
Relay -- an electrically operated switch used in trackside signaling circuits
"The failure mode was an intermittent contact on a single relay."
Trackside equipment -- the signaling hardware installed along the railway line
"The trackside equipment around the faulty relay was unaffected."
Downtime -- the period when a system is out of service and not operating
"The fault caused four hours of test downtime before the relay was replaced."
Pre-commissioning -- the checks done before a system is formally tested and handed over
"The supplier had not completed the pre-commissioning checklist before we arrived."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

João Guilherme is on the factory floor in Germany for a witness test on a relay-based interlocking. The functional test ran normally for the first hour. At 10:14 an alarm was raised on the down-line ATP logic. The report is not written in the simple past, like a story — it is written in the tenses that reconstruct what really happened.

By the time the inspection team arrived, the supplier had not completed the pre-commissioning checklist. Analysis of the event log showed that the affected relay had been dropping out intermittently since the night before — the system had been running in degraded mode for roughly six hours before the alarm made the defect visible. The past perfect does the real work here: it puts the cause first and the symptom second, which is the whole of root cause analysis.

The confirmed failure mode was an intermittent contact on one relay; the trackside equipment around it was unaffected, and because the interlocking had already fallen back to degraded mode, no unsafe command was issued — only four hours of downtime. Two smaller items, a loose label and a missing cable tag, were noted as snags for the punch list. The unit's type approval stays valid, since the defect is an installation fault, not a design fault. And João closes with a commitment, in the future perfect: by the close of the shift the team will have re-tested the ATP logic, and before the witness test on Friday they will have verified all the interlocking tables end to end.

1. "The system had been running in degraded mode for six hours before the alarm." Why the past perfect continuous?
A Because it is happening right now, as João speaks.
B To show how long the fault had already been going on before a moment in the past — the duration behind the failure mode.
C Because it is a promise about the future.
2. The supplier "had not completed" the pre-commissioning checklist before the team arrived. What does the past perfect tell us?
A The checklist was finished on time.
B The missing preparation came first — it is the root cause that was already true before the team got there.
C The checklist will be done next week.
3. Was an unsafe command issued to the trackside equipment?
A Yes — the fault made the system dangerous.
B No — the interlocking had already fallen back to degraded mode as designed, so no unsafe command was issued.
C The text does not say.
4. "Before the witness test on Friday they will have verified all the tables." What does the future perfect commit to?
A That they are starting the tables now, with no end date.
B That the work will be finished by a named future point — a managed plan, not a worry.
C That the tables were verified last week.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Three Tenses on the Floor

GRAMMAR

None of this is new grammar — you already own it. What changes under field pressure is that you have to use it fast, on your feet. One question decides the tense: are you reconstructing what had happened, or committing to what will be done?

TenseFormJob on the floor
Past perfecthad + past participleReconstructs what had happened before a past moment — the cause before the symptom.
Past perfect continuoushad been + -ingAdds how long it had been going on before the fault showed — the duration behind a failure mode.
Future perfectwill have + past participleCommits to what will be finished by a named future point — turns a worry into a managed plan.
Why it mattersThe past perfect separates cause from symptom; the future perfect closes a finding with a deadline. Flatten everything into the simple past and present, and you sound like a bystander, not an inspector.

Watch out (habits carried over from Portuguese): (1) narrating a fault in the flat simple past — "the relay failed and the system went to degraded mode" hides that the relay had been failing first. (2) using the present for a deadline — "by Friday we verify the tables" sounds unfinished; say "we will have verified". (3) leaving a defect without a plan — always close with what you will have done and by when.

The question to ask before you report: "Am I reconstructing what had happened, or committing to what will be done?" That one test picks the tense — and makes you sound in control.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer for every real moment on the floor -- reconstructing the fault, naming it, holding the function, and committing to the fix.

A fault appears mid-test and Petra asks what happened. The strongest report is:
A "The relay fails. The system is not good. We look now."
B "By the time the alarm came up, the relay had been dropping out since the night before — the system had been running in degraded mode for six hours."
C "The relay will break and we will fix it, probably."
Petra calls a loose label and a missing cable tag "a crisis". You say:
A "Yes, those are serious defects that block completion."
B "Those are snags, not blockers — they go on the punch list. The defect is the intermittent relay."
C "Let's just ignore them, they don't matter at all."
Petra wants her team to keep testing the affected function in parallel to save time. You say:
A "Work will not resume on that function until the replacement relay has passed its own functional test — I'll flag this before we proceed."
B "Sure, go ahead — we'll sort out the test later."
C "It should probably wait, but a bit of pressure is fine this once."
The director asks whether the fault costs the unit its certification. You say:
A "Yes, the type approval is gone — the design failed."
B "No — the type approval stays valid. It's an installation fault on one relay, not a design fault."
C "I'm not sure certification matters here."
The director asks where you'll be by Friday. The best commitment is:
A "We work on the tables. Maybe done, maybe not."
B "Before the witness test on Friday, we will have verified all the interlocking tables end to end — and by the close of this shift, we will have re-tested the ATP logic."
