American English Podcast — Ep. 146: Up in the Air

One expression that is the whole of Lesson 4 in three words: up in the air = nothing is decided yet, everything can still change. It is exactly the state of your night when the board turns red — and it is what you say to a colleague back home when he asks what time you land: “Everything is still up in the air. If they rebook me tonight, I’ll land at six.” The host speaks slowly and repeats the expression in several contexts.

Tip (do this in the car, on the way there): listen once, all the way through, without pausing. Then build 3 sentences of your own with up in the air + the first conditional, about September: one about the flight, one about the meeting, one about the fair. Bring all three to Lesson 5.

American English Podcast — Ep. 117: Between a Rock and a Hard Place

The expression for the exact moment at the desk in Miami: two options, and both of them bad — sleep at the airport or miss the meeting. “I was between a rock and a hard place: unless I took the Charlotte flight, I’d miss the meeting.” An American uses this in a business meeting without thinking twice, and it opens a whole conversation about a hard decision — which is your home ground.

Tip (on the way back): listen twice. The second time, PAUSE every time you hear an if or an unless and repeat the whole sentence out loud. The mouth needs the repetition, not only the ear — and the first conditional will only come out automatically at the desk if it comes out automatically in the car.

The grammar of Lesson 4, explained in clear English and focused on the MISTAKES — including the two we saw in class: the will that slips inside the if, and the double negative after unless. It is worth it precisely because he does not stop at the rule: he shows the rule being BROKEN, which is how you will recognize your own mistake when it comes out of your mouth.

Tip: watch it once with English subtitles. Then close your eyes and listen again, audio only, repeating each example out loud. And every time he shows a mistake, PAUSE and say the correct version BEFORE he does — if you catch both mistakes from Lesson 4, slide 24 (Spot the Error) is already won.
