Lesson 05 -- Pre-class

Getting Around -- Plans, Transportation, and Asking for Directions in a European City

Leave the hotel and cross the city alone: buy your ticket at the machine, find the right platform, and stop a stranger to ask the way. Key words: metro, tram, bus stop, ticket machine, one-way ticket, round trip, platform, fare, transfer, ride-share. Structure: "going to" for your plans (We are going to take the tram), in the affirmative, the negative and the question. Expression: I would rather ... than ...

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to every word and read the example. These are the ten words that move you around a European city. Four of them are on the machine and on the signs — you are going to read them before you say them.

Metro -- the train that goes under the city, fast, from station to station
"We are going to take the metro to the city center."
Tram -- the small old train that runs on the street, on rails, up the hills
"Tram twenty-eight goes up to Alfama."
Bus stop -- the place on the street with a sign, where you wait for the bus
"The bus stop is in front of the hotel."
Ticket machine -- the machine with a screen and buttons, where you buy your ticket alone
"I bought two tickets at the ticket machine."
One-way ticket -- a ticket to go, but not to come back
"I only need a one-way ticket, because we are going to walk back."
Round trip -- one ticket to go and to come back, on the same day
"Two round trips to Belém, please."
Platform -- the place where you stand and wait for the train to arrive
"Is this the right platform for Belém?"
Fare -- the price of one trip on the metro, the tram or the bus
"The fare is one euro and eighty."
To transfer -- to change from one line to another, in the middle of your trip
"I am going to transfer at Baixa-Chiado."
Ride-share -- a car you call with an app on your phone, and you pay on the app
"At night we are going to take a ride-share back to the hotel."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

It is nine in the morning, and Rosângela is leaving the hotel. Today she has a plan. "We are going to visit the tower in Belém," she says, "and we are not going to take a taxi. The traffic here is terrible — I would rather take the tram." At the station she finds the ticket machine, she chooses English on the screen, and she looks at the price: the fare is one euro and eighty. "Are you going to come back today?" the agent asks her. "Yes, we are," she answers, "so I am going to buy a round trip, not a one-way ticket." The agent says something very fast about the platform, and she does not catch it. She does not guess. "I am sorry, could you say it more slowly, please?" He repeats it: platform two, and she is not going to transfer — tram fifteen is direct. At eleven at night, tired and happy, she looks at the closed metro. "We are going to take a ride-share," she says to her husband. And her husband, who does not speak one word of English, says nothing at all. He does not need to.

1. "We are going to visit the tower." Why not "We visit the tower tomorrow"?
A Because "visit" is an irregular verb.
B Because in English a plan always needs am / is / are + going to. The present cannot talk about tomorrow, like it does in Portuguese.
C Because the tower is far away.
2. "I am going to buy a round trip." Why is it "buy" and not "bought"?
A Because "going to" already says it is later, so the verb goes back to normal.
B Because "buy" has no past form.
C Because she bought the ticket yesterday.
3. Why is she going to buy a round trip and not a one-way ticket?
A Because a one-way ticket is more expensive.
B Because she is not going to come back to the hotel.
C Because they are going to come back the same day, and a round trip is one ticket to go and to come back.
4. The agent speaks very fast about the platform and she does not catch it. What does she do?
A She guesses, and walks to any platform.
B She asks him to say it more slowly — and he repeats it.
C She asks her husband to translate it for her.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- "Going To": Your Plan

GRAMMAR

In Lesson 4 you said what is happening now. Now you say what is going to happen — the plan you decided before.

FormUseExample
Yes
am / is / are
+ going to + verb		A plan: you decided it before, and you are going to do it later.I am going to take the metro. We are going to visit the tower.
The verb
stays normal		"Going to" already says it is later, so the verb does not change. Same idea as "did" in Lesson 3: one time per sentence.We are going to take the tram. (never "going to took")
No
am / is / are + not		The "not" goes next to am / is / are, exactly like in Lesson 4.I am not going to take a taxi. We are not going to transfer.
Question
Are you going to...?		Turn it around: am / is / are comes to the front.Are you going to come back today? — "Yes, I am." / "No, I am not."
Never the
present alone		In Portuguese the present can talk about tomorrow. In English it cannot: a plan always needs am / is / are + going to."I am going to visit Belém tomorrow." (never "I go to visit Belém tomorrow")
You already
know the pieces		Lesson 4 gave you am / is / are + verb-ing. Put going to in the middle, and now it is later, not now.I am waiting (now) · I am going to wait (later)

The one rule to remember: a plan is never the present alone. It is am / is / are + going to + verb — and the verb after "going to" always relaxes: "We are going to take the tram", never "going to took".

Stage 3: Pronunciation

Speaking

Listen to the model, then record yourself. Take your time: listen twice before you speak. In fast speech "going to" often sounds like "gonna" — you are going to hear it that way. Say the full form: it is clearer, and it is always correct.

We are going to take the tram to Belém.
Excuse me. Could you tell me how to get to Belém, please?
Is this the right platform for the airport?
How long does it take to get to the city center?
I would rather take the metro than a taxi.

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt. There is no right or wrong version. Speak for 60 seconds, with no script.

Think about your next trip to Europe, with your husband. Tell me the plan: where are you going to go, and when? How are you going to get around the city — the metro, the tram, a ride-share? What are you going to visit on the first day? Use at least six sentences with "going to", one negative sentence ("We are not going to..."), and one sentence with "I would rather... than...". And finish with one thing you are going to do yourself, in English, with nobody to translate for you. Take your time and do not read from a script.
