Chapter 4: The Code

Hedging & Diplomatic Disagreement

"I'm not sure that's quite right — could we look again?"

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I'm not sure that's quite right." (soften the claim)

"That might be overstating the risk a little." (modal + downtoner)

"While I take your point on cost, I'd argue the timing is wrong." (concede → counter)

"Could we be underestimating the downside here?" (disagree as a question)

Every line above disagrees — and not one of them says "wrong". What are the pieces doing the work?

MoveStructureExample
Soften with a modalmight / could / would · I'd tend to think"That might be too optimistic."
Downtone the claimnot quite / not entirely / a little / somewhat"I'm not entirely convinced it holds."
Concede, then counterWhile I take your point, ... · I see the logic, but ..."While I agree on the goal, I'd question the timing."
Disagree as a questionCould we be...? · Is it possible that...?"Could we be moving too fast?"
Register a reservationI have one reservation... · My hesitation is..."I have one reservation about the assumptions."
Hold your ground, warmlyI hear you, and I'd still... · That may be so, but..."I hear you — and I'd still flag the covenant."

The order is the whole trick: concede what is true, hedge what is uncertain, and be unequivocal only on the one line that matters. Say no by first saying yes to everything you honestly can.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"No. That is completely wrong."
"I'm not sure that's quite right — could we look at it again?"
"I disagree completely with the timing."
"While I take your point, I'd argue the timing might be too early."
"You are underestimating the risk."
"Could we be underestimating the risk here?"

The word "wrong" ends the conversation. A hedge keeps it open. Turn the accusation into a question, soften the certainty with might / could, and disagree without ever attacking the person.

Chapter 5: The Table

Disagreeing with the Chair

Same room. One reservation. No fight.

Dialogue

One Reservation, Said Well

D
So we're all aligned: we close the acquisition this quarter and fund it with debt. Felipe, you're happy with that?
F
I'm with you on the strategic logic, Diana — I genuinely am. But I'd gently push back on the timing. I have one reservation, and I think it's worth a minute.
D
Go on. Though I'll say the board is keen to move.
F
I take that point, and I'm not trying to slow us down for its own sake. But could we be underestimating what this debt does to our covenants? While I concede the target is right, I'd argue the timing might be a quarter too early.
D
That's a fair challenge. What would change your mind?
F
If we secured the refinancing first, I'd be fully behind it. That's my sticking point — not the deal, the sequence. On everything else we have common ground; on this one figure, I'd ask to stand my ground.
Comprehension

About Diana

1. What does Diana want the board to agree to?
To close the acquisition this quarter and fund it with debt.
2. How does she react to Felipe's pushback?
She calls it a fair challenge and asks what would change his mind.
3. What does she say the board is keen to do?
To move — the board is keen to move quickly on the deal.

0:000:00

The sound of everyone agreeing — every head nodding.

To disagree with her out loud, in front of the others.

Because a board of yes-people has no brakes.
Listening 2

Disagree Without a War

A communication coach, on the order that keeps the room calm. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What does he say most people wrongly lead with?
The disagreement itself — the word "no" — which makes the other person stop listening and start defending.
2. What is the order he teaches instead?
Concede what is true first, then hedge what is uncertain, and only then name the one thing you cannot move on.
3. How does he define diplomatic disagreement?
Holding your ground on what matters while giving way on what does not — and leaving the relationship stronger.
