Chapter 4: The Language of Certainty

How Sure Are You?

Strong claims and careful claims

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"The figures clearly show a rise in sales."

"The data suggests a small change."

"It seems to indicate steady growth."

"This report proves the new trend."

LanguageHow certain?When to use
clearly show / proveStrong (100% sure)Only with solid, complete data: "The figures clearly show a rise."
suggests / seems toSoft (careful)When the data is partial: "The data suggests a change."
may / might point toSoftest (possible)For an early signal: "This may point to a new trend."

Match the strength of your words to the strength of your data.

Language Focus

Strong or Soft?

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"It seems to indicates growth."

✓ RIGHT

"It seems to indicate growth."

✗ WRONG

"The data clearly show, no doubt at all."

✓ RIGHT

"The data suggests a likely change."

After "seems to", use the base verb (indicate, show). And do not over-claim: if the data is partial, choose careful language.

Dialogue

The Quarterly Report

J
Helen, did you get a chance to read the draft of the quarterly report?
H
I did. The overview on page one is very clear. Good work.
J
Thanks. What did you think of the sales figures?
H
They look strong. There is a clear upward trend in the south region.
J
Exactly. That growth was our main milestone for the year.
H
I agree. But the summary at the end feels a little short.
J
Fair point. I will add the client feedback and a note on the final outcome.
H
Perfect. Then the report is ready to send to the board.
J
Great. I will send you the final draft this afternoon.
