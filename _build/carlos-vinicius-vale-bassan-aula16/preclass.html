Block 3 -- Handling Tough Questions After the Pitch

Field the board's pushback after the pitch: stay composed under scrutiny, treat a pressing question as valid, and answer with a calm hypothetical. New vocabulary: pushback, pressing, scrutiny, valid, caveat, defensible, stand by, composed. Grammar focus: the second conditional (if + past simple, would + base verb) -- "If we delayed the deal, we would lose the window", "If I were on the board, I would ask the same question".

Valid -- fair and reasonable, worth taking seriously
"That is a valid concern, and here is the answer." (válido / pertinente)
Caveat -- a condition or warning you add to a statement
"With one caveat on timing, the case still holds." (ressalva / condição)
Defensible -- able to be supported and justified with good reasons
"A defensible answer is short, calm, and tied to the numbers." (defensável / sustentável)
Stand by -- to keep supporting your recommendation, not walk it back
"I stand by the recommendation: buying is the smarter spend." (manter / sustentar)
Composed -- calm and in control, especially under pressure
"Stay composed -- scrutiny is normal, not an attack." (calmo / sereno)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read about handling tough questions and answer the questions.

Carlos delivered the pitch, and the board liked it. But the pitch is the easy part. Now the directors push back, and a coach prepares him. Stay composed: a pressing question is not an attack, it is scrutiny, and scrutiny is normal. Treat each question as valid before you answer it. To stay calm, use a hypothetical -- the second conditional: "If we delayed the deal, we would lose the window." When a director worries about cost, Carlos says: "If I were on the board, I would ask the same question." He can add a caveat -- "with one caveat on timing" -- but he must stand by the recommendation, not walk it back. A defensible answer is short, calm, and tied to the numbers. The pushback is expected; the answer just has to be steady.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Second Conditional

GRAMMAR

Sob pressão, a resposta mais calma é uma hipotética: você mostra o que ACONTECERIA, sem prometer nada. Isso é o second conditional: If + verbo no PASSADO SIMPLES, ... would + verbo base ("If we delayed the deal, we would lose the window"). Use para situações imaginadas / improváveis. Cuidado: NUNCA use would depois de if, e depois de would use o verbo BASE (would lose, não would lost). No registro formal, com I/he/she/it usamos were: "If I were on the board...". Ð diferente do first conditional da aula 9 (if + present + will), que é para algo REAL e provável. / The second conditional imagines an unreal situation: past simple after if, would + base verb in the result. Never put would after if; use were in the formal "if I were".

PartFormExample
If-clauseIf + past simple"If we delayed the deal..."
Resultwould + base verb"...we would lose the window."
Formal "were"If I / he / it were"If I were on the board, I would ask too."
vs. first conditionalreal plan: if + present, will"If we settle today, I will sign." (real)
