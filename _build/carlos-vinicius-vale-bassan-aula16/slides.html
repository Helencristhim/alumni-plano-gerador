Chapter 3 -- If It Were...

Answer with a Hypothetical

Second conditional: "If we delayed, we would lose the window."

Listen and Notice

What Is the Pattern?

Each answer imagines a situation that is not real. Notice the orange words.

"If we delayed the deal, we would lose the window."

"If the cost were lower, we would not even debate it."

"If I were on the board, I would ask the same question."

"If we walked the case back, the board would stop trusting it."

The Rule

The Second Conditional

PartFormExample
If-clauseIf + past simple"If we delayed the deal..."
Resultwould + base verb"...we would lose the window."
Formal "were"If I / he / it were"If I were on the board, I would ask too."
vs. first cond.real plan: if + present, will"If we settle today, I will sign." (real)

Tip: the second conditional imagines a situation that is not real -- it is the calm, hypothetical answer under pressure. Use past simple after if and would + base verb in the result: "If we delayed, we would lose the window." Save the first conditional (if + present, will) for a real, likely plan.

Common Mistake

No "Would" After "If"

"If we would delay the deal, we would lost the window."
"If we delayed the deal, we would lose the window."

No would after if: the if-clause uses the past simple ("if we delayed"). And after would, use the base verb: "we would lose", never "would lost".

Comprehension

About the Brief

1. What does the coach say about a pressing question and scrutiny?

A pressing question is not an attack -- it is scrutiny, and scrutiny is normal. Stay composed; if you looked nervous, the board would doubt the case.

2. How should you use the second conditional under pressure?

To stay calm and hypothetical: "If we delayed the deal, we would lose the window." It keeps the answer measured, not emotional.

3. What makes an answer defensible, and what should you not do?

A defensible answer is short, calm, and tied to the numbers. Add a caveat if you must, but stand by the recommendation -- do not walk it back.

PROJECT ATLAS -- Q&A PREP SHEET

Presenter: Carlos Vinicius Vale Bassan -- Accenture Strategy  ·  Stay composed under scrutiny

Cost
Q: "The upfront cost is too high."  A: "That is a valid concern. If the cost were lower, we would not even debate it -- but it is the return that pays for it. I stand by the recommendation."
Timing
Q: "Why not wait a quarter?"  A: "If we delayed the deal, we would lose the window. With one caveat on staffing, the case still holds."
Risk
Q: "What if integration fails?"  A: "If I were on the board, I would ask the same question. The plan is defensible: a phased rollout limits the downside."
Trust
Q: "Are you sure?"  A: "If we walked the case back now, the board would stop trusting it. I stand by it, and the numbers are defensible."

Under pressure: stay composed, treat the question as valid, answer with the second conditional (if + past, would + base verb), add a caveat only if you must, and stand by the recommendation.
