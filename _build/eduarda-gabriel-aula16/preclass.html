Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

These are the key email and tone terms for this lesson. Tap Listen to hear the pronunciation.

Recipient -- the person who receives your message
"Always check the recipient before you hit send."
Salutation -- the opening greeting line of an email
"Use a formal salutation: Dear Mr. Carter."
Concise -- short and clear, with no extra words
"Keep the body concise: three short paragraphs."
Acknowledge -- to confirm that you received or read something
"Acknowledge their email before you make a request."
Attach -- to add a file to an email
"Please find the report attached."
Forward -- to send a received email on to another person
"I will forward you the client's reply."
Prompt -- quick and without delay
"Thank you for your prompt reply."
Sign-Off -- the closing line of an email (Best regards, ...)
"End with a warm sign-off, not just thanks."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

Grammar

Eduarda starts her email with a formal salutation. Before any request, she acknowledges the client's last message. Then she states her purpose: "I am writing to confirm the timeline." She keeps the body concise. Instead of "send the numbers", she softens the request: "Could you please send the figures by Friday?" and "I would appreciate the report by end of day." She names the file: "Please find attached the deck", so the recipient does not miss it. She closes with a warm sign-off and thanks the client for a prompt reply.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip

Grammar

Use the formal register to make an email polite and professional. A direct command can sound rude to a client; a softened request gives them a choice. O registro formal suaviza o pedido: em vez de "Send it", escreva "Could you please send it?". Use formulas fixas para abrir, pedir e apontar anexos.

FunctionFormal formulaExample
State purposeI am writing to + verb"I am writing to confirm the timeline."
Soft requestCould you please + verb"Could you please send the figures?"
Point to a filePlease find attached + noun"Please find attached the deck."

Common Mistake:

"Give me the numbers by Friday."

"Could you please send the figures by Friday?"

Para um cliente, um pedido precisa de um suavizador ("could you please" / "I would appreciate") — nunca uma ordem seca.
