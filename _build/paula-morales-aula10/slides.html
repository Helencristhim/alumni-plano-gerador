Chapter 3: The Code

Three Verbs, Two Rules

like · want · would like

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

O que vem logo depois de cada verbo?

I like working with brokers.

I want to grow in this company.

I'd like to learn the underwriting side.

Sofia likes meeting clients.

Depois de like, o verbo ganha -ing. Depois de want e de would like, vem o to.

VerboDepois vemO que dizExemplo
likeverbo + -ingo que você já faz e gostaI like working with brokers.
wantto + verboo que você quer — diretoI want to grow.
would like
(I'd like)		to + verboo que você quer — de gravataI'd like to grow.
Perguntado / would pula na frenteigual às aulas 8 e 9Do you like working here? · Would you like to join?
Duas regras, e uma você já sabe. want e would like querem a mesma coisa — muda só a roupa. É exatamente o truque da aula 9: can → could. Hoje: want → would like. E o like é outro assunto: ele não fala do futuro, fala do que você já faz — e por isso o verbo dele vem com -ing.
Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I want grow in this company."
"I want to grow in this company."
"I like work with brokers."
"I like working with brokers."
"She want to improve her English."
"She wants to improve her English."

O primeiro erro é culpa nossa, e você está sendo coerente: na aula 9 o verbo ia pelado depois do can. Agora o to voltou. O motivo é simples: can é um ajudante — ele não é verbo de verdade, e ajudante não aceita to. Já want é um verbo normal, igual a work e a like — e verbo normal precisa do to para puxar outro verbo. O terceiro erro é o mesmo assunto pelo outro lado: can nunca leva -s (“she can”), mas want leva (“she wants”) — porque um é ajudante e o outro é verbo normal.

Two Registers

Same Want, Different Clothes

 Com o colega — wantCom o diretor — would like
CrescerI want to grow in this company.I'd like to grow in this company.
AprenderI want to learn underwriting.I'd like to learn underwriting.
MarcarI want to schedule a meeting.I'd like to schedule a meeting.
PerguntarI want to ask you something.I'd like to ask you something.
AjudarI want to help.I'd like to help.
Duas colunas, uma vontade. A da direita é mais formal — mas a da esquerda não é grosseira. Ninguém nunca se ofendeu com um I want to grow. Você não precisa escolher entre ser educada e dizer o que quer: essa escolha não existe. Aula 9: can → could. Aula 10: want → would like. O mesmo movimento — e você já sabe fazer.

Dialogue

Thirty Minutes with Greg

G
Paula, thanks for the time. Thirty minutes, one question, and then I let you go. What do you want?
P
Nobody asks me that. Can I think for a second?
G
Take your time. Let's start with the easy one: what do you like about your work?
P
I like working with brokers. Twenty years, and I still like it.
G
Good. Now the other one. In twelve months — what do you want?
P
I want to grow in this company. I want a challenge — I'd like to work with the international clients, not only the Brazilian ones.
G
That's the first time today somebody said it in one sentence. And what would you like to learn?
P
I'd like to improve my English. This conversation is in English, and I am still here.
G
Paula, your English is not the problem. Your English got you this meeting. Send me two lines: your goal, and what you'd like to learn. That's all I need.
P
One more thing. This is a great opportunity for me, and I want it.
G
I know. You just said it out loud. That's the whole meeting, Paula.
Comprehension

About Greg

1. How much time does Greg have, and how many questions does he ask?
Thirty minutes, and one question: "What do you want?" He says it in the first line — "one question, and then I let you go".
2. What does Greg say when Paula answers in one sentence?
"That's the first time today somebody said it in one sentence." He asked other people the same question today — and they did not answer it.
3. Does Greg say anything about Paula's English?
One thing: "Your English is not the problem. Your English got you this meeting." He does not correct her, and he never mentions it again.
4. What does Greg ask Paula to send him?
Two lines: her goal, and what she would like to learn. "That's all I need."

Listening

David, the Day Before

Sound first — no text. Leia as perguntas antes de ouvir.

0:000:00
1. What question is Greg going to ask? And what does David say Paula is going to answer?
"What do you want?" — and David says she is going to answer: "I'm happy, everything is fine, thank you." And then go back to her desk and be angry for a year.
2. David says "I want to grow" is not rude in English. So what is it?
It is the answer to the question. It is what Greg asked for. And if she wants it softer, she has the same trick from last week: "I would like to grow" — same want, with a tie on. What is not correct is saying nothing.
3. What are David's two grammar warnings at the end?
One: like is about what you already do — "I like working with brokers". Two: after want, the verb needs the to — "I want to grow", never "I want grow". Last week the verb went naked after can; this week it wears the to.

Free Practice

Two Minutes — No Screen, No “But”

Scenario: Two minutes, out loud, alone. The question is: “Why do you want to work here, and what are your goals?” Use what you like (like + verb-ing), what you want (want to), what you are asking for (I'd like to), what you can do (Lesson 9), and how often you do it (Lesson 7). No screen, no notes, no help.

One rule, and it is the whole lesson: not one sentence with a “but” about your English. Two times: with the bank, then alone. The second one is the one that counts.
