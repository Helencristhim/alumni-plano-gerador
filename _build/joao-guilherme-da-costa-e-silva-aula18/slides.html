Impact

Three Ways to Read the Same Clause

The clause: "The supplier should provide the test records in English." Rank the readings, say why, then click to compare

A. "It says provide the records in English, so it's mandatory — if they're in German, we're non-conformant."
Reading a should as a shall. It looks safe, but it over-specifies the contract: you have turned a recommendation into a binding obligation the standard never made. Now you are enforcing something you cannot actually hold the supplier to.
B. "It's only a should, not a shall — so it doesn't matter, we can just ignore it."
The opposite error. Should is not binding, true, but it is still the standard's intent, strongly advised good practice. Treating it as "optional, therefore delete" quietly lowers quality and leaves the departure undocumented — which is exactly what an auditor flags.
C. "Should is a recommendation, not a shall — so they don't lose conformance, provided that the departure is recorded and justified in the safety case."
Reading the modal correctly. You know exactly how much force the clause has: recommended, not mandatory, and a justified departure keeps conformance. This is the whole skill of tonight — the modal, not the noun, tells you what binds.

The Tool

The Reader's Move

1 — Find the Scope

Does this clause even apply to my equipment? A requirement only binds inside its scope of application.

2 — Read the Modal First

Shall, should, may, or must not? Underline it before the technical noun — the modal is what tells you if the line binds.

3 — Follow the Cross-Reference

A clause rarely stands alone. If it points to an annex or another clause, read them together before you decide.

4 — Decide Conformance

Every shall met and proven by the verification method? Conformant. A shall you cannot meet? A deviation permit — before work proceeds.

Most engineers skim a clause for the technical noun and miss whether it says shall or should — and those two words decide whether it binds. Find the scope, read the modal, follow the cross-reference, decide conformance. That is what reading a standard actually looks like.

Chapter 3: The Code

Four Words That Set the Force

shall = mandatory · should = recommended · may = permitted · must not = forbidden

Grammar Discovery

What Does Each Modal Demand?

"The interlocking shall fail to a safe state on loss of power."

"The supplier should provide the test records in English."

"The contractor may apply for a deviation permit, provided that safety is preserved."

"Access to the vital core must not be granted without dual authorization."

Four green modals, four different forces. One is a binding requirement; one is advice; one is a permission; one is an absolute prohibition. Which is which — and which two can break conformance if you get them wrong?

ModalForce in a specWhat it means
shall / shall notmandatory requirement / prohibitionBinding. Must be met and demonstrated for conformance. NOT the future — not "will".
should / should notrecommendationAdvised good practice, not binding. A justified departure keeps conformance.
maypermission / optionAllowed, not required. A choice, not a duty — and not "might" (mere possibility).
must / must notnecessity / absolute prohibitionStrong necessity, usually safety. "Must not" is a wall no recommendation can soften.
Why it mattersIn a standard the modal is the contract, not the style. Read shall as "will" and you miss an obligation; read should as shall and you over-specify; read may as a duty and you invent an obligation. The modal decides whether the line binds — read it before the noun.
Meaning

Four Modals Set the Terms

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"The interlocking should fail to a safe state on loss of power."
"The interlocking shall fail to a safe state on loss of power."
"The contractor must apply for a deviation permit if the part changes."
"The contractor may apply for a deviation permit if the part changes."

A safety-critical fail-safe is a mandatory requirement — it takes shall, not the softer should. And applying for a deviation permit is a permission — may, not must. The reflex to soften an obligation into a recommendation, or to harden a permission into a duty, comes straight from reading the noun instead of the modal.

The Clarification Call

Reading It Together

A
João, thanks for making time. I want to close the conformance statement for Clause 7 today. Let's start with 7.1 — it says the interlocking shall fail to a safe state and shall log every command. Are those two firm, or is there room?
J
They're firm. Both are written with shall, so each is a mandatory requirement — there's no room. We can only claim conformance once each one is met and demonstrated by the stated verification method, the type test.
A
Understood. Now 7.2 — the test records in English. We can only deliver them in German this week. Does that break conformance?
J
No. That line uses should, so it's an optional requirement — recommended, not binding. You don't lose conformance, provided that the departure is recorded and justified in the safety case. Send them in German for now, log the reason, and we're fine.
A
Good. One more — we'd like to swap the axle counter for an equivalent unit. Is that allowed, and does it change anything?
J
You may propose an equivalent component — it's a permission, provided that it holds the same safety integrity level. But the fail-safe behavior is a mandatory clause, so if the swap touches it, work shall not proceed on that function until we approve a deviation permit in writing.
A
Fair. My test engineer also asked whether her team can get into the vital core to speed up the integration test.
J
That one I have to stop. Access to the vital core must not be granted without dual authorization — it's an absolute prohibition, whatever the pressure on the schedule. And notwithstanding the rest, the safety case must be approved before energization. So: 7.1 mandatory, 7.2 recommended, the swap permitted under a permit, and the vital core off limits. Listening

The Manager's Brief

Just before the call. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Why does the manager say "shall" is a trap for a Portuguese speaker, and what does it really mean?
Because the document looks like plain English and "shall" reads like a future ("will"). In a standard, shall is not a prediction and not a suggestion — it is mandatory: a requirement you have to meet and demonstrate, or you cannot claim conformance.
2. What are the two opposite ways people get "should" wrong?
Reading a should as if it were a shall — over-specifying the contract by making a recommendation binding — and reading it as "optional, therefore ignore it", which misses the intent. Should is advised good practice: not binding, but a departure has to be recorded and justified.
3. What does she tell João to do as he reads, and what to do if a change touches a "shall"?
Mark every clause: is it a shall, a should, a may, or a must not. Confirm the mandatory requirements, flag the recommendations, and if a change touches a shall, tell the supplier a deviation permit is needed before any work proceeds.
Listening 2

Three Rules for Reading a Standard

A standards auditor. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. What is the first rule — and what does the auditor tell you to underline in every clause?
Read the modal before anything else, because the modal is the requirement. Underline it in every clause before you argue about the content: shall / shall not are mandatory, should / should not are recommended, may is permitted, must / must not carry necessity and prohibition. If you cannot say which a clause is, you have not read it yet.
2. What is the second rule about scope and cross-references?
Respect the scope of application: a requirement only binds inside the scope the clause names, and a cross-reference tells you which other clause you must read with it. People quote a shall from a section that does not apply, or read one clause alone when it points to an annex that changes it. Find the scope, follow the cross-reference, then decide what conforms.
3. What are the two honest ways out of a requirement you cannot meet?
Either the product is deemed to conform because it passed the stated verification method (type test, inspection, or analysis), or, if it genuinely cannot meet a mandatory clause, you apply for a deviation permit and do not proceed on that function until the permit is approved in writing. Conformance is every shall met and demonstrated, every deviation documented.
