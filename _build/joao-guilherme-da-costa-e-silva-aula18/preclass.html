Reading the Standard -- Interpreting an International Signaling Specification

A German supplier sends the interface specification for the Line 4 interlocking, and before you sign a conformance statement you have to read it the way the standard means it. This is the grammar that carries a specification: shall for a mandatory requirement, should for a recommendation, may for a permission, must not for a prohibition. Key words: clause, mandatory requirement, optional requirement, conformance, deviation permit, applicable standard, scope of application, cross-reference, verification method, to deem, provided that, notwithstanding. The move: read the modal before the noun.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Last week you presented the numbers. Tonight a supplier sends you a specification, and a conformance statement is waiting for your signature. These are the words of a standard: how it names a requirement, how it proves one, and how it lets you out of one you cannot meet. Listen to each term and read the example.

Clause -- a single numbered section of a standard that states one requirement
"Clause 7.1 says the interlocking shall fail to a safe state on loss of power."
Mandatory requirement -- a condition the supplier must meet, signalled by the word "shall"
"Every 'shall' in the standard is a mandatory requirement, not a suggestion."
Optional requirement -- a condition that is recommended but not binding, signalled by "should"
"The 'should' clauses are optional requirements — good practice, not obligations."
Conformance -- the state of fully meeting what a standard requires
"We can only claim conformance once every mandatory clause is met and demonstrated."
Deviation permit -- a formal, written approval to depart from a requirement
"If we cannot meet clause 7.4, we apply for a deviation permit before any work proceeds."
Applicable standard -- the specific standard that governs a piece of work
"The applicable standard for this interlocking software is EN 50128."
Scope of application -- the range of situations a clause or standard actually covers
"The scope of application excludes the operator workstation, so clause 5 does not apply there."
Cross-reference -- a pointer from one clause to another that must be read with it
"Clause 7.2 has a cross-reference to Annex B — read them together."
Verification method -- the agreed way of proving a requirement has been met (test, inspection, or analysis)
"For each 'shall', the standard names a verification method — type test, inspection, or analysis."
To deem -- to officially treat something as meeting a requirement
"A unit that passes the type test is deemed to conform to the clause."
Provided that -- on the condition that; the condition under which a permission applies
"The contractor may substitute the part, provided that it holds the same safety integrity level."
Notwithstanding -- despite a named clause; it overrides what another clause says
"Notwithstanding clause 7.3, the safety case must be approved before energization."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below.

A German supplier has sent João Guilherme the interface specification for the Line 4 interlocking, and a conformance statement is waiting for his signature. The document looks like plain English — and that is the trap. In a standard, the modal verbs are not style; they are the contract.

The force of every clause is set by one word. Shall is a mandatory requirement: "The interlocking shall fail to a safe state on loss of power." It is not a prediction about the future, the way will would be, and it is not a polite suggestion — it is binding, and a product is deemed to conform only when each shall is met and demonstrated by the stated verification method. Should is an optional requirement: recommended good practice, but not binding. "The supplier should provide the records in English" — a departure keeps conformance, provided that it is recorded and justified. May is a permission, not a duty: "The contractor may apply for a deviation permit." And must not is an absolute prohibition: "Access to the vital core must not be granted without dual authorization."

So João reads by the modal, not the noun. He checks the scope of application before he applies a clause, follows every cross-reference to the annex it points to, and confirms which lines are mandatory against the applicable standard. Where a change touches a shall, work shall not proceed until a deviation permit is approved; and notwithstanding the rest, the safety case must be approved before energization.

1. "The interlocking shall fail to a safe state." In a specification, what does shall mean here?
A A prediction about the future — the same as "will".
B A mandatory requirement — binding, and demonstrated by the stated verification method for conformance.
C A polite suggestion the supplier can take or leave.
2. The records can only be delivered in German this week. According to the text, does the supplier lose conformance?
A Yes — the specification says the records shall be in English.
B No — that line uses "should", so it is recommended, not binding; conformance holds provided that the departure is recorded and justified.
C It cannot be decided from the specification.
3. "The contractor may apply for a deviation permit." What force does may carry?
A An obligation — the contractor has to apply.
B A permission — the contractor is allowed to, but does not have to.
C A mere possibility — the same as "might".
4. A change touches a mandatory ("shall") function. What does the specification require before work continues?
A Nothing — the contractor may keep working and document it later.
B Work shall not proceed on the affected function until a deviation permit is approved in writing.
C The supplier must simply switch the clause from "shall" to "should".

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- The Modals of a Standard

GRAMMAR

In a specification, the modal verb is the requirement. One question decides how much a clause binds: is this an obligation, a recommendation, a permission, or a prohibition? Read the modal before the technical noun.

ModalForce in a specWhat it means
shall / shall notmandatory requirement / prohibitionBinding — met and demonstrated for conformance. NOT the future ("will").
should / should notrecommendationAdvised good practice, not binding. A justified departure keeps conformance.
maypermission / optionAllowed, not required — a choice, not a duty. Not "might" (mere possibility).
must / must notnecessity / absolute prohibitionStrong necessity, usually safety. "Must not" is a wall no recommendation softens.
Why it mattersThe modal decides whether the line binds. Read shall as "will" and you miss an obligation; read should as shall and you over-specify; read may as a duty and you invent an obligation. Read the modal before the noun.

Watch out (habits carried over from Portuguese): (1) reading shall as a future — in a standard, "the system shall comply" is a requirement, not a forecast. (2) softening a mandatory shall into a polite should — a safety-critical fail-safe is shall, not should. (3) hardening a permission into a duty — "the contractor may apply", not must apply.

The question to ask before you read a clause: "Is this an obligation, a recommendation, a permission, or a prohibition?" The modal answers it — and that one test gives you the whole force of the line.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer for every real moment of reading the specification -- the mandatory line, the recommendation, the permission, and the prohibition.

A clause reads: "The interlocking shall fail to a safe state." The supplier asks if it is negotiable. You say:
A "It says 'shall', so it'll probably happen — let's not worry about it."
B "No — shall makes it a mandatory requirement. We can only claim conformance if it's met and demonstrated by the verification method."
C "It's flexible — we can treat it as a recommendation if you're short on time."
The spec says the records "should" be in English; the supplier can only deliver German this week. The best reading is:
A "That breaks conformance — the records must be in English."
B "Should is a recommendation, so conformance holds — provided that the departure is recorded and justified in the safety case."
C "It's only a should, so it doesn't matter at all — ignore it."
The supplier wants to swap in an equivalent axle counter. The specification says the contractor "may" propose one. You say:
A "You may propose an equivalent component, provided that it holds the same safety integrity level — it's a permission, your option."
B "You must propose an equivalent component now that the original is unavailable."
C "You might propose one — but there's no way to know if it's allowed."
A mandatory function cannot be met as specified. The correct route is:
A "Keep working and note the problem in the final report."
B "Apply for a deviation permit — and work shall not proceed on the affected function until it's approved in writing."
C "Rewrite the clause from 'shall' to 'may' and move on."
The supplier's team asks to enter the vital core to save time. You say:
A "Access to the vital core must not be granted without dual authorization — that's an absolute prohibition, whatever the schedule."
B "Sure, go ahead — we can sort out the authorization later."
C "It should probably be authorized, but a bit of pressure is fine this once."
