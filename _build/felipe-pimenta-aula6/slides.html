Chapter 4: The Code

The Causative

I had it done · I got her to do it

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"I had the numbers audited before the board met."

"We got the payroll outsourced in six weeks."

"I had my controller rebuild the model."

"I got her to take the monthly close off my desk."

In the first two, who did the work? We are not told, and we do not care. In the last two, we name the person — but look closely: rebuild vs to take. Why the difference?

FormUseExample
have + object + past participleThe work got done. WHO did it is invisible — and that is the point. The result matters, not the hands.I had the numbers audited.
get + object + past participleSame meaning, slightly more informal, often with a hint of effort or difficulty.We got the payroll outsourced.
have + PERSON + bare infinitiveYou name the person. After have, NO to. It sounds like authority.I had my controller rebuild the model.
get + PERSON + TO + infinitiveYou name the person. After get, you MUST use to. It sounds like persuasion.I got her to rebuild the model.
The nuancehave = you have the authority. get = you had to convince. Choose on purpose.I had him do it. / I got him to do it.

The whole trick: after have + person, drop the to. After get + person, keep it. And with a past participle, the person disappears entirely — which is exactly how a CFO reports work he did not do.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"I had my controller to rebuild the model."
"I had my controller rebuild the model."
"I got her rebuild the model."
"I got her to rebuild the model."
"I audited the numbers before the board met."
"I had the numbers audited before the board met."

After have + person: no to. After get + person: to is obligatory. And if you did not do the work yourself, do not say you did — use the causative and let the result speak.

Chapter 5: The Handover

The Conversation You Have Been Avoiding

The handover begins

Dialogue

Handing Over the Close

P
Felipe, you asked to see me. Is this about the monthly close again?
F
It is. I am handing it over to you — the whole thing, not the parts I find boring. From March, the close is inside your remit.
P
The whole thing. Including signing it off, or including preparing it so that you sign it off?
F
You sign it. That is the difference between this time and the last three times. I will have the audit trail reviewed once a quarter, and I want to be looped in only if a number moves more than five percent.
P
Then I need two things: I need you to get finance systems to give me access, and I do not have the bandwidth unless something comes off my desk.
F
Agreed. I will get the access sorted this week, and we are outsourcing payroll — that is four days a month back for you. The deliverable is the March close, signed by you, on the fifth working day.
Comprehension

About Priya

1. What is Priya's first question about the handover?
Whether she will sign the close off herself, or only prepare it for him to sign.
2. What two things does she say she needs?
Access from finance systems — and something taken off her desk, because she has no bandwidth.
3. Why is she skeptical at the start?
Because he has tried to hand the close over three times before, and always kept the sign-off.
