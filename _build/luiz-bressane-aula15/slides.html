Chapter 2: Words of the Higher Court

The Words of the Appeal

Vocabulary

Words 1-4

Appeal
A formal request asking a higher court to review and change a decision
"Counsel lodged an appeal against the conviction within the deadline."
Appellant
The party who brings the appeal to the higher court
"As appellant, the defendant carries the burden of showing an error."
Respondent
The party who answers the appeal and defends the original decision
"The respondent urged the court to leave the verdict untouched."
Grounds
The legal reasons on which an appeal is based
"He has strong grounds: the judge admitted evidence he should have excluded."
Vocabulary

Words 5-8

0 / 4 words revealed

Overturn
To cancel a lower court's decision and decide the opposite
"The appellate court overturned the conviction on a point of law."
Uphold
To confirm a lower court's decision and let it stand
"The court upheld the verdict, finding no error in the trial."
Quash
To wipe out a conviction or order as if it never happened
"The judges quashed the conviction and ordered the appellant released."
Remit
To send a case back to the lower court for a fresh hearing
"Rather than acquit, the court remitted the case for a new trial."

Chapter 3: Wish & Third Conditional

The Unreal Past

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

I wish the judge had excluded that confession.

If only trial counsel had objected at that moment.

If the judge had excluded it, the jury would never have heard it.

Had the evidence been disclosed, my client would have been acquitted.

StructurePatternMeaning
wish + past perfectI wish + had + past participleregret about the past
if only + past perfectIf only + had + past participlestronger regret
third conditionalIf + had + p.p., would have + p.p.imagined past + result
inverted (formal)Had + subject + p.p., would have...formal third conditional

All three describe an UNREAL past: it did not happen. On appeal you argue the case that should have been — if a ruling had been different, the outcome would have been different. The inversion "Had the evidence been disclosed..." drops "if" and is the formal register of the appellate court.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"If the judge would have excluded it, ..."

✓ RIGHT

"If the judge had excluded it, ..."

✗ WRONG

"I wish the judge excluded it."

✓ RIGHT

"I wish the judge had excluded it."

The "would have" belongs only in the RESULT clause, never after "if" or "wish". To talk about an unreal past, the condition takes the past perfect: had + past participle.

Chapter 4: Before the Appellate Bench

Arguing the Grounds

Dialogue

The Appeal Hearing

J
Counsel for the appellant, you have leave to appeal. State your grounds.
L
My Lord, the grounds are simple. The trial judge admitted a confession taken without counsel. That ruling was erroneous.
L
Had that confession been excluded, the jury would never have heard it, and my client would have been acquitted. I say this conviction is a miscarriage of justice.
J
The respondent says the other evidence was overwhelming. Why should we not uphold the verdict?
L
Because the confession was the heart of the case. If only the rest had stood on its own, the Crown would have led with it. It did not. I ask the court to quash the conviction.
L
And if the court is not minded to acquit outright, I ask, in the alternative, that you remit the case for a fresh trial before a different judge.
J
The court finds an error of law. The conviction is quashed, and the matter is remitted for retrial.
Comprehension

The Hearing

1. What was the appellant's main ground of appeal?
The trial judge wrongly admitted a confession taken without counsel.
2. What did counsel say would have happened if the confession had been excluded?
The jury would never have heard it, and the client would have been acquitted.
3. What two outcomes did counsel ask for?
To quash the conviction, or in the alternative to remit the case for a fresh trial.
4. What did the appellate court decide?
It found an error of law, quashed the conviction and remitted the matter for retrial.

Chapter 5: Drill

Sharpen the Grounds

Detective · Transformation · Building

Spot the Error

Fix the Conditional

"If the judge would have excluded it, the jury would not have heard it."
If the judge had excluded it, the jury would not have heard it. (had, not would have)
"I wish counsel objected at that moment."
I wish counsel had objected at that moment. (past perfect for past regret)
"Had the report disclosed, my client would have been acquitted."
Had the report been disclosed, my client would have been acquitted. (passive past participle)
"If only the court would have granted leave."
If only the court had granted leave. (past perfect after if only)
Transformation

Imagine the Past

"The judge admitted the confession, and the jury convicted." → third conditional
If the judge had not admitted the confession, the jury would not have convicted.
"Counsel did not object — it was a mistake." → wish
I wish counsel had objected.
"The report was not disclosed." → if only
If only the report had been disclosed.
"The evidence was weak, but the jury was not warned." → inverted third conditional
Had the jury been warned, they might not have convicted.
