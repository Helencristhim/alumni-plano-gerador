Chapter 4: The Code

The Passive Voice

be + past participle · when the action matters most

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"This dish is cooked at home."

"The whey is simmered for hours."

"It is eaten on bread every morning."

"The recipe has been passed down for generations."

Each blue phrase is be + past participle. The food comes first; the cook disappears.

TensePassive formExample
Presentis / are + participleThe bread is baked every morning.
Pastwas / were + participleThe dish was invented here.
Present perfecthas / have been + participleIt has been eaten for centuries.

Use the passive when the action matters more than who does it. Add by + agent only when it matters: "a dish loved by everyone".

Language Focus

Active or Passive?

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

✗ WRONG

"This dish served with rice."

✓ RIGHT

"This dish is served with rice."

✗ WRONG

"The cheese is age for months."

✓ RIGHT

"The cheese is aged for months."

The passive always needs be + a past participle — never drop the "be", and never leave the verb in its base form.

Dialogue

At the Market

L
Welcome to the food market! This little stall is where the best brown cheese is made. It is our great specialty here — have you tried it?
M
Not yet. It looks fascinating. How is it actually made?
L
The whey is simmered very slowly for hours, until the sugar turns brown and sweet. Then it is pressed into blocks. The milk is all locally sourced, from farms just up the valley.
M
A friend once told me it was sweet and savory at the same time. Is it eaten on its own?
L
Usually it is served on thick bread, for breakfast. For a first-timer it is an acquired taste, but the whole country is renowned for it. Here, try a slice.
M
Mmm. Strange and wonderful. Back home, we have a cheese that is aged in caves — I will have to bring you some.

Listening 1

Linnea's Recipe

How her grandmother's fish soup is made. Sound first, no text.

0:000:00
1. How is the broth for the fish soup made?
The fish heads and bones are simmered very slowly for a whole afternoon, until the broth turns rich and golden.
2. What vegetables are added, and when?
Whatever is in season, added after the broth, with a little cream at the end.
3. Why has the recipe never been written down?
It is only passed down from one cook to the next, by watching.
