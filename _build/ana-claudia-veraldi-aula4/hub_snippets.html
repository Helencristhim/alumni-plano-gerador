Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Loyal -- always staying with the people it loves, and never leaving them
"She is so loyal that she follows me to the bathroom."
Stubborn -- refusing to change its mind, no matter what you say
"He is too stubborn to walk in the rain. He just sits down."
Playful -- full of energy and always wanting to have fun
"The young one is playful from six in the morning."
Calm -- quiet and relaxed, never getting upset about anything
"Mel is calm about everything except the doorbell."
Curious -- wanting to know about everything, and going to look
"She is too curious. Every open door is an invitation."
Lazy -- doing as little as possible, and sleeping most of the day
"She is a big lazy creature who owns my sofa."
Skinny -- very thin, in a way that looks unhealthy
"She was skinny and filthy when I found her."
Filthy -- extremely dirty, covered in mud or dust
"He comes back filthy from every single walk."
Frightened -- feeling afraid because of something that happened
"She was the most frightened animal I have ever seen."
Exhausting -- making you very tired, taking all your energy
"The young one is completely exhausting before breakfast."
Well-behaved -- behaving well and doing what it is told
"He is well-behaved until food appears on the table."
Gentle -- kind and soft, never rough or aggressive
"She is enormous, but she is gentle with children."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Ana lives with two dogs, and they could not be more different. Mel is a big old brown dog: calm, lazy, and gentle with everybody who walks through the door. She sleeps most of the day and complains if dinner is late. The other one is a small noisy young dog: playful, curious and completely stubborn. He refuses to walk in the rain - he simply sits down in the middle of the street and waits. He is not well-behaved, and he is honestly exhausting. After the morning walk Ana is exhausted, and that is exactly the point: HE is exhausting, SHE is exhausted. Mel was a stray. When Ana found her under a parked car she was skinny, filthy and completely frightened. It took three days and a lot of cheap sausages. Now she is the most loyal animal Ana has ever had, and she has clearly decided that the house belongs to her.

1. Why is it "a big old brown dog" and not "a brown big old dog"?
A Because English fixes the order: size, then age, then color.
B Because "brown" cannot start a description in English.
C Because the dog is old, so age always comes first.
2. The text says HE is exhausting and SHE is exhausted. What is the difference?
A They mean the same thing; it is just style.
B The -ing form is what causes the feeling; the -ed form is how the person feels.
C The -ing form is present and the -ed form is past.
3. Which sentence about the two dogs is correct?
A "They are two browns dogs."
B "They are two brown dogs."
C "They are two dogs browns."

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Adjective Order and the -ed / -ing Pair

GRAMMAR

How to stack adjectives, and how not to call yourself boring.

FormUseExample
Adjective orderopinion → size → age → shape → color → origina lovely big old brown dog
How manyTwo or three sounds natural. More than that sounds like a list.a big old dog
-ed adjectiveHow the person FEELS.I am exhausted / bored / frightened
-ing adjectiveWhat CAUSES the feeling.the puppy is exhausting / the film is boring
Never pluralAn adjective in English never takes an -s: "two black dogs", never "two blacks dogs".
Careful"I am boring" means YOU are the boring thing. You almost always want "I am bored".

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the question below. There is no right or wrong answer.

Someone who has never met your dogs asks: "So tell me about them." Introduce both: what they look like (use two or three adjectives in the right order), what they are like, how they are different from each other, and how you found them. Somewhere in there, use one -ed adjective for how you feel and one -ing adjective for what causes it. Take your time.
