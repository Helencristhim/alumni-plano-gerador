Chapter 1: The Read

Revenue Was Ninety Million. Good or Bad?

You cannot answer that yet — and neither can the analyst. A number alone says almost nothing. Which way is it moving, how fast, and against what?

Same Number, Two Reads

A Photograph vs the Film

"Revenue was ninety million."

A still frame. Up or down? Fast or slow? Against what?

"Ninety million — up eleven per cent year on year, or five like-for-like."

Now the analyst can price it.

The difference

is direction, speed, and comparison — the trend, not the figure.

The best finance people never present a figure naked. They dress every number in a trend and a comparison.

Chapter 3: The Code

The Language of Trends and Comparison

Direction, speed, and comparison — the three things a bare number is missing

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

"Margin edged up a point, from twenty-one to twenty-two."

"Sign-ups surged after the launch."

"Revenue is up eleven per cent year on year."

"Growth plateaued in the fourth quarter."

The orange words all describe movement. So tell me: how do they differ, and what do they compare against?

ToolChoicesExample
Verb (small → big)edged up · rose · climbed · surged"Margin edged up; sign-ups surged."
Verb (flat / down)plateaued · broadly flat · dipped · fell"Growth plateaued in December."
Adverb of degreemarginally · steadily · sharply"Revenue fell marginally, not sharply."
Preposition of changeup FROM... TO... · BY 9% · now AT 22%"Up from twenty-one to twenty-two."
Comparison frameyear on year · like-for-like · ahead of plan"Up eleven per cent year on year."

Match the verb to the size of the move, use from... to... or by to carry the change, and always anchor it to a comparison. A number with none of these is a photograph; with all three, it is the film.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"Revenue was ninety million." (a number with no direction and no comparison)
"Revenue was ninety million, up eleven per cent year on year, five like-for-like."
"The margin surged." (it moved a single point — the verb is too big)
"The margin edged up a point, from twenty-one to twenty-two per cent."
"Profit is up strongly." (it was flattered by a one-off that will reverse)
"The beat is a one-off; underlying profit was broadly flat."

The senior move is never to inflate. Match the verb to the size of the move, anchor it to a comparison, and separate the headline from the underlying figure. An analyst forgives a soft quarter. They do not forgive a number dressed up to look bigger than it is.

Chapter 4: The Review

The Analyst Who Wants the Trend

She will not accept a figure without direction and comparison

Dialogue

Walking Through the Quarter

D
So, give me the quarter. How did the top line do?
F
The top line was ninety million, up eleven per cent year on year — though on a like-for-like basis, stripping out the acquisition, it is closer to five.
D
Good, you gave me both. And the margin?
F
Gross margin edged up a point, from twenty-one to twenty-two per cent. Small, steady, driven by mix — not a surge, and I would not call it one.
D
Your profit line looks wonderful, though. Too wonderful.
F
You are right to push. That beat is a one-off — a tax gain that will not repeat. Underlying profit was broadly flat; higher costs were largely offset by higher volumes.
D
That is the honest read. So where is the story going?
F
We outperformed the sector on revenue, but cash collection is lagging the plan by two weeks. The momentum is real, and the one thing I am watching is that lag going into the fourth quarter.
