Expressions

Five That Cross a Border

to bridge cultural gaps

to build the crossing between two ways of working, instead of picking one

“My job is not to choose a culture. It is to bridge cultural gaps between two of them.”

to read between the lines

to hear what was meant, when it was never actually said

“In Brazil you learn to read between the lines. In Canada you learn to say the line.”

to account for

to include a factor in your reasoning, deliberately, because it changes the result

“Any diagnosis that does not account for power distance will read the silence as agreement.”

to set out

to state expectations clearly, at the start, so that nobody has to guess later

“I would rather set out the expectations in week one than defend them in month six.”

to take into consideration

to weigh a factor before deciding, not after

“We should take into consideration that this team has never been asked for its opinion before.”

Chapter 3: Two Rooms, Two Countries

Compare Without Generalizing

One could argue that · More often than not · It would appear that · For the most part

Phrase Bank

The Language of a Careful Claim

TO OPEN A CLAIM

“One could argue that our feedback culture is not weak. It is simply spoken in a different register.”

TO STATE A TENDENCY

“More often than not, difficult feedback in our Brazilian teams is delivered indirectly.”

TO READ THE DATA

“It would appear that the silence in those sessions has more to do with hierarchy than with language.”

TO GENERALIZE SAFELY

“For the most part, our Canadian colleagues expect the expectations to be set out in writing.”

TO PROTECT THE CLAIM

“That said, I would be careful not to overgeneralize. There is more variation inside each country than between them.”

Chapter 4: The Code

Point the Spotlight

It was the power distance that silenced them · What surprised me was the directness

Grammar Discovery

Listen and Notice

It was the lack of psychological safety that caused the disengagement, not the workload.

What I found most challenging was navigating the difference in communication style.

It is the relational culture in Brazil that makes direct feedback harder to implement.

What this team needs is not another framework. It is a leader who sets out the expectations in week one.

Every one of these could have been said in five plain words. The speaker spent twelve on purpose — to move one piece of the sentence into the spotlight, and leave everything else in the dark.

StructureFormWhat it does
It-cleftIt was + X + that / who + restIsolates the CAUSE and rules the others out: “It was the power distance that silenced them, not the language.”
Wh-cleftWhat + clause + is / was + XBuilds suspense, then lands the point: “What I found most challenging was the feedback culture.”
Always It wasEven with a plural: “It was the academic directors who pushed back” — never “It were”.
Why it sounds seniorA plain sentence reports a fact. A cleft makes a CHOICE between competing explanations — and a room full of PhDs is paid to notice which explanation you chose.

Pair it with a hedge and it becomes diplomacy: “It would appear that it was the power distance, rather than the language, that kept them quiet.”

Register

A Knife and a Stage

It-cleft — the knife

Use it when the room is blaming the wrong cause. It cuts one explanation away from the others.

“It was the power distance, not the workload, that produced that silence.”

Wh-cleft — the stage

Use it to open a recommendation. It holds the room until your last three words.

“What this academic team needs is not a framework. It is to be asked first.”

Neither one raises your voice. Both make you harder to interrupt — because the sentence has not landed yet, and everyone in the room can hear that.

Common Mistake

Right vs Wrong

"It was the power distance what made them silent."
"It was the power distance that made them silent."
"What surprised me most it was the directness of the feedback."
"What surprised me most was the directness of the feedback."

Both errors are the same error: Portuguese, arriving on time. In the it-cleft the link is that (or who for people), never "what". In the wh-cleft, What ... was already contains the verb — adding "it was" says it twice.

Chapter 6: Your Turn

Face the Faculty

Guided > Semi-free > Free

Dialogue

The First Session

M
I read your brief. You write that our teams are harder to read than the Brazilian ones. Are you not at risk of overgeneralizing about two entire countries?
D
I would be, if I were describing people. I am describing tendencies. More often than not, difficult feedback in our Brazilian teams is delivered indirectly — and there is more variation inside each country than between them.
M
Fair. Then explain the finding I disagree with. You say our last engagement session failed. Nobody objected to anything in that room.
D
That is the finding. It was the power distance in that room that produced the silence, not agreement. Two of your professors have run their faculties for twenty years. Any diagnosis that does not account for that will read silence as consent.
M
And your solution is a Brazilian culture program, translated into English, for a faculty of doctors who did not ask for one.
D
What this faculty needs is not a program. It is to be asked before it is told. So I will set out the expectations on day one, and then I have a question nobody here has asked you: what do you think the problem is?
Comprehension

About Marc

1. What is the first objection Marc raises to the brief?
That she is at risk of overgeneralizing about two entire countries.
2. What evidence does he use to argue that the engagement session did not fail?
That nobody objected to anything in the room.
3. How does he describe the faculty he is defending?
As a faculty of doctors who never asked for a culture program — and, in his account, would be handed a Brazilian one translated into English.
