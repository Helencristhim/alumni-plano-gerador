Series
The Chair — Season 1, Episode 1 (Netflix)

Ji-Yoon Kim takes over an academic department and finds out that formal authority buys nothing in a room of PhDs with twenty years in the building — people who disagree by email, three days later, never in the meeting. It is your August meeting room, filmed. Connection to Lesson 5: watch HOW the silence works in the department meetings — nobody contradicts anyone on the spot, and the disagreement lands later, in writing. That is power distance and a low-context culture in the same scene.

Tip: watch with English subtitles. Write down TWO lines where someone disagrees without raising their voice, and ONE where the silence in the room is clearly NOT agreement.

Podcast
HBR IdeaCast — “How Authority and Decision-Making Differ Across Cultures” (Erin Meyer, INSEAD)

Erin Meyer, the author of The Culture Map, explains why hierarchy and decision-making are TWO different things — and why a foreign leader gets it wrong precisely by collapsing them into one. It is the theory behind power distance, in the executive English you will need to use it in. Connection to Lesson 5: it is exactly the argument of your matrix — a room can be egalitarian and still nobody contradicts the director; the silence is not agreement.

Tip: listen once, with no pauses. Then summarize it out loud in sixty seconds, opening with “What surprised me most was...” and using ONE it-cleft (“It was the... that...”) and ONE hedge (“More often than not...”). Record it and listen back.

YouTube
Erin Meyer — “The Culture Map” (INSEAD)

The author herself presenting the eight scales of the culture map: communication (high-context vs. low-context), feedback, leadership, decision-making, trust, disagreement, time. It is the entire vocabulary of this lesson, spoken by the person who built it. Connection to Lesson 5: her scales are exactly the rows of your Cultural Comparison Matrix — and she shows that Brazil and Canada land on different points of almost all of them.

Tip: watch the first half. Pick THREE scales where Brazil and Canada sit far apart and write ONE sentence about each, with a hedge and a cleft, to bring to Lesson 6.
