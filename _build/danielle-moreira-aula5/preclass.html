Lesson 05 -- Pre-class

Brazil vs. Canada -- Articulating Cultural Differences with Academic and Executive Teams

Explaining the difference between Brazilian and Canadian culture to a room of PhDs and directors — with no stereotype, no sweeping generalization, and no retreat from the observation itself: name the dimension, back it with evidence, and put the right cause under the spotlight. Key words: cultural intelligence, high-context culture, low-context culture, power distance, individualism and collectivism, indirect communication, assertiveness, cultural humility, work-life integration, inclusive leadership, to bridge cultural gaps, to read between the lines, to account for, to set out, to take into consideration. Structure: cleft sentences (It was the power distance that... / What this team needs is...) + hedging for comparative cultural analysis (One could argue that... / More often than not... / It would appear that... / For the most part...).

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each term and read the example. This is the vocabulary the Canadian academics already use to describe you — from August on, it is the vocabulary you use to describe the room.

Cultural intelligence -- the ability to read a culture you were not raised in, and adjust inside it without losing yourself
"Cultural intelligence is not knowing many cultures. It is functioning well inside one you do not know yet."
High-context culture -- a culture where the meaning lives around the words: in the tone, the pause, the relationship
"In a high-context culture, we will look into it can mean no, and everyone in the room knows it."
Low-context culture -- a culture where the meaning lives in the words themselves: say it, write it down, and it is settled
"Canada leans low-context: if it was not said out loud, do not assume the room heard it."
Power distance -- how far the boss feels from the room, and how safe it is to contradict one in public
"It was the power distance, not the language, that kept them silent in that meeting."
Individualism and collectivism -- whether a person is measured on their own result, or on what the group delivered together
"Our reward system rewards the individual. Our culture rewards the group. That gap is the problem."
Indirect communication -- saying the difficult thing by making it easy to infer, never by naming it
"Indirect communication is not dishonesty. It is a different address for the same message."
Assertiveness -- stating your position clearly and holding it, without attacking the person who disagrees
"Assertiveness travels badly. What reads as clear in Toronto can read as harsh in Sao Paulo."
Cultural humility -- assuming your way is one way, not the way, and staying curious once you are in the room
"I am not arriving with a Brazilian model to install. That is cultural humility, and it is also strategy."
Work-life integration -- a life where the work and the rest of it share the same day, on purpose, not in two sealed halves
"Two days at the office and two at home is work-life integration. Managing it is a skill, not a perk."
Inclusive leadership -- leading so that the quietest person in the room still ends up speaking
"Everyone was invited and three people spoke. That was not inclusive leadership. That was a guest list."
To bridge cultural gaps -- to build the crossing between two ways of working, instead of picking one of them
"My job is not to choose a culture. It is to bridge cultural gaps between two of them."
To read between the lines -- to hear what was meant, when it was never actually said
"In Brazil you learn to read between the lines. In Canada you learn to say the line."
To account for -- to include a factor in your reasoning, deliberately, because it changes the result
"Any diagnosis that does not account for power distance will read the silence as agreement."
To set out -- to state expectations clearly, at the start, so that nobody has to guess later
"I would rather set out the expectations in week one than defend them in month six."
To take into consideration -- to weigh a factor before deciding, not after
"We should take into consideration that this team has never been asked for its opinion before."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

The first session went beautifully, and that was the problem. Nadia Ferraz had flown in to run a cultural assessment with the academic faculty of a bilingual school group, and for ninety minutes not one professor objected to anything she proposed. She wrote in her notes that the room was aligned. Six weeks later, the plan was quietly dead. It was not the plan that failed, her director told her afterwards. It was the way she read the room that failed, and the two are not the same thing.

She had walked into a low-context culture carrying the instincts of a high-context one. In the country she came from, you read between the lines: the pause tells you the answer, and a polite silence in a meeting is rarely a yes. Here, silence meant something else entirely. Two of those professors had run their faculties for twenty years, and they did not contradict a visitor in public. They contradicted her on Friday, in an email, in three careful paragraphs, copied to her director. Any diagnosis that fails to account for that kind of power distance will read a quiet room as consent.

What she learned that quarter was not a technique. It was a posture. One could argue that her mistake had been a sentence in the first draft of her brief, a sentence with no hedge in it at all: they avoid conflict here. More often than not, she would write later, difficult feedback in that faculty was delivered indirectly — and it would appear that it was the hierarchy, rather than any dislike of conflict, that explained it. For the most part, her second brief said the same things as the first. The difference was that this one could be argued with, and so it was read. What that faculty had needed all along was not another framework. It was cultural humility, and one question nobody had ever asked them: what do you think the problem is?

1. Why is it “It was the power distance that produced the silence” and not “...what produced the silence”?
A Because in an it-cleft the link is always that (or who, with people). The “what” is a direct transfer from your first language.
B Because “what” only exists in questions.
C Because the subject is abstract and takes no relative pronoun.
2. “What she learned that quarter was not a technique.” — why do we NOT say “What she learned that quarter it was not a technique”?
A Because “it” can never appear in a sentence that opens with “What”.
B Because the wh-cleft already has its verb (What ... was). Adding “it was” says the verb twice — it is your first language leaking into the English.
C Because the subject of the sentence is feminine.
3. According to the text, why was the first session a problem because it went beautifully?
A Because the professors had not read the brief before the meeting.
B Because nobody objected in the room, and she recorded that silence as alignment — when the disagreement was simply going to arrive later, in writing.
C Because the faculty agreed with the plan and then changed their minds six weeks later.
4. What made the second brief succeed where the first one failed?
A It contained different findings, based on a second round of data.
B It was shorter, and it avoided any comparison between the two cultures.
C It said the same things, but as tendencies with hedging rather than as rules — so the faculty could argue with it, and therefore read it.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Cleft Sentences & Hedging

GRAMMAR

The grammar of the spotlight: the sentence splits in two to push ONE piece of it into the light — and hedging is what keeps that light from turning into a stereotype.

FormUseExample
It-cleft
It was + X + that / who		The knife. Picks ONE cause and rules the others out.It was the power distance that produced the silence, not the language.
Wh-cleft
What + clause + is / was + X		The stage. Holds the room until the last word, then lands it.What this faculty needs is to be asked before it is told.
Always It wasEven with a plural subject. Never “It were”.It was the academic directors who pushed back.
Hedging: a tendencyTurns a rule into a tendency.More often than not, feedback here is delivered indirectly.
Hedging: reading the evidenceAn interpretation, not a verdict.It would appear that the silence has more to do with hierarchy.
Hedging: open and protectOpens the claim, then shields it.One could argue that... · For the most part... · That said, I would be careful not to overgeneralize.
Collocationtake into consideration (never “take in consideration”) · account for a factor (never “account the factor”) · set out the expectations (never “set the expectations out of”) · bridge cultural gaps (never “make a bridge in the culture”)

Watch out (slip number 1): in the it-cleft the link is that, never “what”. “It was the power distance what made them silent” is your first language arriving intact. With people, use who.

Watch out (slip number 2): in the wh-cleft the verb is already there. “What surprised me most it was the directness” says the verb twice. The correct form is “What surprised me most was the directness”.

Dosage: hedging is not insecurity — it is PRECISION. “Brazilians avoid conflict” is a stereotype, and a PhD will take it apart in ten seconds. “More often than not, difficult feedback in our Brazilian teams is delivered indirectly” is an observation, and he will argue with you instead of dismissing you.

The combination you want: hedge + cleft in the SAME sentence. “It would appear that it was the power distance, rather than the language, that kept them quiet.” It is the most senior sentence in this lesson.
