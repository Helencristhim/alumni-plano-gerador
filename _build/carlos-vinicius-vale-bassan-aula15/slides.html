Chapter 3 -- Emphasis Frames

Point at the One Thing That Matters

Cleft sentences: "What the board needs is..." / "It is the return that..."

Listen and Notice

What Is the Pattern?

Each sentence puts the emphasis on one idea. Notice the orange words.

"What the board needs is one number, the payback."

"It is the return that pays for the upfront cost."

"What matters here is the rationale, not the slides."

"The reason we should buy is the quicker payback."

The Rule

Three Emphasis Frames

FrameExample
What + subject + verb + is ..."What the board needs is one number."
It is + X + that ..."It is the return that pays for the cost."
The reason ... is ..."The reason we should buy is the payback."
What matters (here) is ..."What matters here is the rationale."

Tip: a cleft does not add new facts -- it re-orders the same fact to point at one idea. The frame verb is always is, and the part you stress comes after it: "What the board needs is one number." Use it once or twice in a pitch, on the idea that matters most -- not on every sentence.

Common Mistake

Keep the Frame "Is" and Don't Drop "That"

"What the board need are one number, and it is the return pays for the cost."
"What the board needs is one number, and it is the return that pays for the cost."

The frame verb is always is: "What the board needs is...". And the IT-cleft always needs that: "It is the return that pays for the cost."

Practice

Add the Emphasis

"The board needs one number." → _____What the board needs is one number. (What-cleft)
"The return pays for the cost." → _____It is the return that pays for the cost. (It-cleft)
"We should buy because the payback is quicker." → _____The reason we should buy is the quicker payback. (The reason ... is)
"The rationale matters, not the slides." → _____What matters here is the rationale, not the slides. (What matters)

Comprehension

About the Brief

1. How should the pitch open, and why?

With the recommendation, not the background -- what you say first is what they remember.

2. What does the coach say about the rationale and the emphasis?

Keep the rationale concise -- two or three reasons -- and use emphasis to point at the one number that matters, like the payback.

3. How should the pitch close, and what makes it compelling?

Close with the upshot, the one-line conclusion. A compelling pitch is not louder, it is clearer -- advocate for one option and end with the upshot.

The Two-Minute Pitch Script

PROJECT ATLAS -- BOARD PITCH (2 MIN)

Presenter: Carlos Vinicius Vale Bassan -- Accenture Strategy  ·  Recommendation: Buy

Open
"My recommendation is to buy from the vendor."
Rationale
"The rationale is concise: the return is the highest, and the payback is the quickest. The reason we should buy is the quicker payback."
Emphasis
"What the board needs is one number: the payback. What matters here is the return, not the slides."
Reinforce
"Yes, the upfront cost is high. But it is the return that pays for the cost. I advocate for buying because the case is compelling."
Upshot
"The upshot is simple: buying is the smarter spend, so we approve the budget and we move."

Structure: open with the recommendation, give a concise rationale, use an emphasis frame on the one number, reinforce before the objection, and close on the upshot. Two minutes, not ten.

1. What did Diana like about the opening, and what did she say about the rationale?

He led with the recommendation, so the board knew in one sentence what he was asking for. But he gave three reasons -- one too many; the most compelling pitch is the most concise, so give the two that matter most.

2. What did Diana say about the emphasis and the close?

He reinforced his point well, but he apologized for the upfront cost first. He should not apologize -- he should advocate for the return.
