Block 3 -- Presenting the Business Case: Delivering the Recommendation

Turn the business case into a short, structured pitch. Open with the recommendation, give a concise rationale, and close with the upshot -- using emphasis to point at the one thing that matters. New vocabulary: recommendation, rationale, compelling, concise, advocate, endorse, upshot, reinforce. Grammar focus: emphasis frames (cleft sentences) -- "What the board needs is one number", "It is the return that pays for the cost", "The reason we should buy is the quicker payback".

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. Tap Listen to hear it.

Recommendation -- the option you advise the board to choose, said up front
"My recommendation is to buy from the vendor." (recomendação)
Rationale -- the reasoning behind a recommendation, the "why"
"The rationale is simple: the payback is quicker." (justificativa / lógica)
Concise -- short and clear, with no wasted words
"Keep the pitch concise: two reasons, no more." (conciso / objetivo)
Compelling -- so strong and convincing that people agree with you
"A compelling pitch is not louder, it is clearer." (convincente)
Advocate -- to argue in favor of one option, to speak for it
"I advocate for buying, because of the return." (defender / advogar a favor)
Endorse -- to formally support or approve a recommendation
"If you can say it in two minutes, the board will endorse it." (endossar / aprovar)
Upshot -- the final result or conclusion, said in one clear line
"The upshot is simple: buying is the smarter spend." (conclusão / resultado final)
Reinforce -- to make a point stronger, especially before an objection
"Reinforce your point before they raise the objection." (reforçar)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read about delivering the pitch and answer the questions.

Carlos has the business case. Now he must present it. The board does not want ten slides; they want the recommendation in two minutes. A coach gives him the structure. Open with the recommendation, not the background: "My recommendation is to buy." Then give the rationale, but keep it concise -- two reasons, no more. Use emphasis to point at the one number: "What the board needs is one number, the payback." Then reinforce the point before the objection: "It is the return that pays for the upfront cost." Carlos must advocate for one option, not list them all. Finally, he closes with the upshot: "Buying is the smarter spend." A compelling pitch is not louder, it is clearer. If Carlos can say it in two minutes, the board will endorse it.

1. How should the pitch open?
A With the recommendation, not the background.
B With all ten slides of detail.
C With an apology for the upfront cost.
2. What does the coach say about the rationale?
A Give as many reasons as possible.
B Keep it concise -- two reasons, no more.
C Skip the reasons and go straight to the slides.
3. What makes a pitch compelling?
A It is louder than the others.
B It lists every option equally.
C It is clearer -- it advocates for one option and ends with the upshot.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Emphasis Frames (Cleft Sentences)

GRAMMAR

Num pitch, você não enfatiza tudo -- você aponta para a UMA coisa que importa. Em inglês, isso é o cleft sentence (frame de ênfase): você reescreve a frase normal para destacar uma parte. Três padrões: What ... is ... ("What the board needs is one number"); It is ... that ... ("It is the return that pays for the cost"); The reason ... is ... ("The reason we should buy is the quicker payback"). O verbo do frame é sempre is (singular), e a parte destacada vem DEPOIS do is. NUNCA use are no frame, e nunca esqueça o that no it-cleft. / Use a cleft sentence to put the spotlight on one idea: "What X needs is Y", "It is Y that does Z", "The reason ... is ...". The frame verb is always is, and you never drop that in the it-cleft.

FrameExample
What + subject + verb + is ..."What the board needs is one number."
It is + X + that ..."It is the return that pays for the cost."
The reason ... is ..."The reason we should buy is the payback."
What matters (here) is ..."What matters here is the rationale."
