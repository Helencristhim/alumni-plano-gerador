Lesson 18 -- Pre-class

The Rain Plan -- Presenting Upcoming Launches and the Backup Plan to Global

The Global team never wants only the plan — they want the plan behind the plan. This is the lesson of the first conditional: "If the launch is delayed, we will postpone it." You use it to present your backup plan — what you will do if the numbers change, if you are behind schedule, or if it rains on launch day. Key words: backup plan, setback, delay, postpone, reschedule, in advance, ahead of schedule, behind schedule. New grammar: the first conditional (if + present simple, will + base verb), next to going to, the plan you already decided.

Stage 1.1: Vocabulary Cards

Vocabulary

Listen to each word and read the example. These are the eight words of a plan that can change — the delays, the backups, and the schedule.

Backup plan -- a second plan you use if the first plan does not work
"If the launch is delayed, we start the backup plan."
Setback -- a problem that stops progress for a while
"A short delay is a small setback, not a disaster."
Delay -- extra time before something happens; when something is late
"There is a small delay in the product approval."
Postpone -- to move something to a later date
"If approval is late, we will postpone the launch."
Reschedule -- to give something a new date or time
"We will reschedule the congress for November."
In advance -- before the time something happens; early, with time to spare
"Send the slide in advance of the meeting."
Ahead of schedule -- earlier than the planned time
"If demand is high, we will launch ahead of schedule."
Behind schedule -- later than the planned time
"If we are behind schedule, we will use the backup plan."

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions.

Emmanuele presents the upcoming launches to the Global team. The normal plan is clear: "We are going to launch the new product in Q3, on schedule." But Marc asks the hard question the board always asks: "And what is your backup plan if something changes?"

Emmanuele is ready. She has a Rain Plan. "If the product approval is late, we will postpone the launch by one month. If we are behind schedule, we will start with the private market first, and we will reschedule the congress for November. And if it rains on launch day, we will move the event online." Notice the shape of every sentence: if + the present (is, are, rains), then will + the simple verb (will postpone, will start, will move).

This is not being negative — it is the opposite. A manager who has a backup plan for every setback is a manager the Global team trusts. A delay is not a disaster if you already know your next move. Marc is happy: "A plan with a backup plan. I like this." The normal plan shows what you hope; the Rain Plan shows that you are ready.

1. Emmanuele says, "If the product approval is late, we will postpone the launch." Why this form?
A First conditional: after "if" the present (is), then "will" + the simple verb (will postpone).
B It is the past — the approval was late last year.
C Both verbs should use "will": "If the approval will be late, we will postpone."
2. What does the Global team want, besides the normal plan?
A Nothing — the normal plan is enough for them.
B The backup plan: what she will do if something changes.
C A promise that nothing will ever change.
3. In "If it rains on launch day, we will move online", where does "will" go?
A After "if": "If it will rain..."
B Only in the second half: "we will move" (after "if", the present: "it rains").
C In both halves of the sentence.
4. According to the text, why is a backup plan a good thing?
A Because it means the launch will definitely fail.
B A manager who is ready for every setback is a manager the Global team trusts.
C Because it lets you avoid ever presenting a normal plan.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- the first conditional

GRAMMAR

Use the first conditional for a real future condition — your backup plan. It has two halves: if + the present simple, then will + the base verb. Keep going to for the plan you already decided, and use if... will... for what you will do if things change.

UseStructureExample
A real future conditionIf + present simple, ... will + base verb"If the launch is delayed, we will postpone it."
Order can switch... will + base verb ... if + present"We will postpone it if the launch is delayed."
Negative (−)If + present, ... will not (won't) + base"If we are on track, we won't need the plan."
if... will... vs going togoing to = a plan already decided ("We are going to launch in Q3"). if... will... = the backup plan ("If the launch is delayed, we will postpone it").

Common mistake: after if, use the present — never will. Not "If the launch will be delayed", but "If the launch is delayed". And do not forget the will in the second half: not "If we are behind schedule, we postpone it", but "If we are behind schedule, we will postpone it".
