Grammar Discovery

All Five Are Yours

"I booked the flight two weeks ago."

"The flight is boarding at gate twenty-two."

"We are going to take the tram at nine."

"One kilo over? All right — I will move it."

"The bins are full, so they might check my bag."

Every one of these is correct, and every one of these is already yours. But they do five different jobs. Which one is finished? Which one is happening right now? Which one was decided last night, and which one one second ago? And which one is a maybe?

LessonStructureThe job it does
1Present simple
I live, I travel		Always true, or true again and again. "I live in São Paulo. We travel once in a while."
2Past simple
I booked, I chose		Finished, and the time is over. "I booked the flight two weeks ago."
3Past — no and ?
did not / did you...?		The verb goes back to plain after did. "I did not bring liquids." · "Did you pack it yourself?"
4Present continuous
is boarding		Happening right now, while I speak. "The flight is boarding." · "He is sitting on a bench."
5Going to
am going to take		The plan. You decided it BEFORE, and it is on the paper. "We are going to take the tram at nine."
6Would like
I would like...		Want, but polite. The one you use at every counter and every table. "I would like a window seat."
7Superlatives
the most / the best		Number one of the group. "What is the most popular dish?" · "It is the best thing we make."
8Will + how much / how manyDecided one second ago, on the spot. "I will move it." And: can I count it? Museums = many. Time = much.
9Should · could · mightAdvice · an option or a polite question · a maybe. "You should ask." · "Could you check?" · "It might be late."

Nine lessons, nine tools. You are not missing any of them. The only question left is which one, and that is not grammar — that is practice.

The Contrast

Three Futures, One Portuguese Word

This is the only place where you still slip — and there is a reason. In your language, one little word does all three jobs. In English there are three, and the difference is when you decided.

Going to — the plan

Decided last night. It is on the paper.

"We are going to take the tram at nine."

Will — the decision

Decided one second ago, on the spot.

"One kilo over? I will move it."

Might — the maybe

Nobody knows yet. Fifty-fifty.

"The bins are full, so they might check my bag."

The plan is going to. The surprise is will. The maybe is might. Ask yourself one question before you open your mouth: when did I decide this?

Common Mistake

Four Mistakes in Ten Lessons

"I am agree with you, but we drank water."
"I agree with you, but we drank water."
"Sixty euros? No. I will to move one kilo."
"Sixty euros? No. I will move one kilo."
"How much bags are you checking today?"
"How many bags are you checking today?"
"I am looking forward to see Amsterdam."
"I am looking forward to seeing Amsterdam."

Agree is already a verb. After will the verb is plain. Bags you can count. And the to in "looking forward to" is not an infinitive — it is a preposition, so the verb after it takes -ing. Four mistakes in ten lessons is a very short list.

Listening

Gate Twenty-Two

Lisbon, nine in the morning. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. Who boards first, and what should they do now?
Families with small children, and passengers who need a little more time. They should come to the desk now. After them: rows twenty to thirty-five, and then rows one to nineteen.
2. The bins might be full. What could happen to your carry-on, and what should you take out of it first?
They might have to check it at the gate. It is free, but you should take your medicine, your documents and your phone out of it first — because you will not see that bag again until Amsterdam.
3. Your bag arrives damaged. Where should you go, and when?
To the desk in the baggage hall in Amsterdam, before you leave the airport. After you leave, they cannot help you.
Listening 2

She Started at Sixty-Six

Yuko is standing behind you in the line. Sound first — no text.

0:000:00
1. When did Yuko start English, and what did she do before that?
She started at sixty-six, after she retired. At school she learned nothing. She used to read a little, once in a while, but she never spoke — not one sentence, for fifty years.
2. What did her children say to her, and how did she feel about it?
They said: Mother, we are busy, we have our own lives, go with your sister. She was angry for one whole week. And then she was not angry any more, because they were right.
3. What happened on the third day in Seoul, and why does she still remember it?
She ordered her own dinner, asked the waiter what he would recommend, and understood his answer. It is a piece of cake for her now — but that night it was the biggest thing in the world. On the first night she had cried in the hotel.
4. A taxi driver in Lisbon laughed at her English yesterday. What does she say about that?
She laughed too. She would rather laugh with him than say nothing at all — and she says that is the whole secret, and it took her sixty-six years to learn it. People tell her it is too late at seventy-one. She disagrees: it is worth it, every single time.

Comprehension

About the Man With the Menu

1. What does Bram recommend, and what does he say straight after it?
The sea bass — the best thing they make, in his opinion. But then he gives the choice back: it depends on what you like, and if you would rather have meat, the beef is very good too.
2. She says she is allergic to nuts. What does Bram answer, and what does he do about it?
He says there is nothing in the sea bass — nothing at all. But he does not stop there: he says he will check with the kitchen to be sure.
3. What is wrong with the bill, and how does Bram react?
There is a bottle of wine on it that they did not drink — it belongs to table nine. He says she is right, he apologizes, and he says he will fix it right now.
4. At the very beginning, what does Bram offer while they are choosing?
Something to drink. She asks for sparkling water for two, and for a few more minutes.
