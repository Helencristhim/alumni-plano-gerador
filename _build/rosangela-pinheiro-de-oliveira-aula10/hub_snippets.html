Stage 1.1: Your Nine Expressions

Vocabulary

Nothing on this page is new. Every expression here comes from a lesson you have already done -- one from each, from Lesson 1 to Lesson 9. Read the card, listen to the audio, and before you read the meaning, try to remember the lesson it came from. Watch number nine: agree is already a verb, so it is "I agree" and "I disagree" -- never "I am agree".

Once in a while -- not often, but sometimes -- a few times a year, not every day
"We travel to Europe once in a while." (Lesson 1)
It depends on -- the answer is not always the same: it changes with the situation
"Do I speak English well? It depends on the day!" (Lesson 2)
It is a piece of cake -- it is very easy -- so easy that you do not even think about it
"The check-in? It is a piece of cake now." (Lesson 3)
I am looking forward to -- I am happy about something in the future, and I am waiting for it
"I am looking forward to seeing Amsterdam." (Lesson 4)
I would rather -- I prefer this one, not that one -- said politely, when you choose between two things
"I would rather have a window seat, if you have one." (Lesson 5)
I used to -- I did it many times in the past, but I do not do it any more
"I used to need my son at every counter." (Lesson 6)
In my opinion -- this is what I think -- not a fact, my own idea, said politely
"In my opinion that is a lot of money for one kilo." (Lesson 7)
It is worth it -- what I get is bigger than what I pay -- in money, in time, or in effort
"The tour is nine euros, but it is worth it." (Lesson 8)
I disagree -- the polite way to say you think something different, and it is a verb
"You were lucky." -- "I disagree. I was ready." (Lesson 9)

Stage 1.3: Grammar in Context

GRAMMAR

Read the text and answer the questions below. Every structure in it is one you already have.

It is nine in the morning at the Lisbon airport. Rosângela is standing at the check-in counter, and her husband is sitting on a bench behind her with the coffee. He does not speak English, and this morning he does not need to.

"Good morning. I booked this flight two weeks ago, and I would like a window seat, please." The agent puts the bag on the scale: twenty-four kilos. "I am afraid the limit is twenty-three. You are one kilo over." Four years ago, this is the exact moment she used to look for her son. She does not. "What could I do?" -- "You could pay the fee, or you could move one kilo into your carry-on." -- "How much is the fee?" -- "Sixty euros." -- "In my opinion that is a lot of money for one kilo. I will move it."

She opens the bag on the floor and moves two books into her carry-on. Thirty seconds. "Twenty-two point eight," he says. At the gate she calls her son and tells him everything. He is quiet for a second. Then: "Mom, you were lucky." -- "I disagree," she says. "I was not lucky. I was ready." Next month she is going to be sixty-nine years old, and she is looking forward to Amsterdam.

1. "I booked this flight two weeks ago." Why the past simple here?
A Because it is finished and the time is over: "two weeks ago" is a closed moment in the past.
B Because she is at the airport right now.
C Because booking a flight is always in the past.
2. She says "I will move it", and not "I am going to move it". Why?
A There is no difference: both are the future, so both are correct here.
B Because "will" is the decision she makes on the spot. Nobody planned the twenty-four kilos: she decided it one second ago, at the counter.
C Because "will" is more polite than "going to".
3. "She used to look for her son." What does that tell you about today?
A That she looks for her son every time there is a problem.
B That her son is at the airport with her this morning.
C That it was true many times in the past, and it is finished. She does not do it any more.
4. Her son says she was lucky, and she disagrees. What is the text really saying?
A That nothing about that morning was luck: ten lessons are the reason she could solve it alone.
B That her son does not want her to travel without him.
C That the agent was kind to her because she is sixty-eight.

Stage 1.4: Grammar Tip -- Your Nine Structures

GRAMMAR

There is nothing new in this table. It is the whole map of Lessons 1 to 9 on one page — and the only question left is never how, it is which one.

LessonStructureThe job it does
1Present simpleAlways true, or true again and again. "I live in São Paulo." · "We travel once in a while."
2Past simpleFinished, and the time is over. "I booked the flight two weeks ago."
3Past — negative and questionAfter did, the verb goes back to plain. "I did not bring liquids." · "Did you pack it yourself?" (never "did you packed")
4Present continuousHappening right now, while you speak. "The flight is boarding." · "He is sitting on a bench."
5Going toThe plan you made BEFORE. It is on the paper. "We are going to take the tram at nine."
6Would likeWant, but polite. The one you use at every counter and every table. "I would like a window seat."
7SuperlativesNumber one of the group. "What is the most popular dish?" · "It is the best thing we make."
8Will + how much / how manyDecided one second ago, on the spot. "I will move it." And: can I count it? Bags = many. Time = much.
9Should · could · mightAdvice · an option or a polite question · a maybe. "You should ask." · "Could you check?" · "It might be late."

The one rule to remember: the three futures are the only place where you still slip, and there is a reason for it — in Portuguese, one little word does all three jobs. So before you speak, ask yourself: when did I decide this? Last night = going to. One second ago = will. I did not decide, and nobody knows = might.

Stage 3: Pronunciation

Speaking

Listen to the model, then record yourself. Take your time: listen twice before you speak. Two things to watch. In real speech "I will" almost always sounds like "I'll", and "would" loses its L completely: say WUD. And in "looking forward to seeing", the words run together into one long sound -- do not stop between them.

I booked it two weeks ago, and I would like a window seat, please.
What could I do? All right, then I will do that.
In my opinion that is a lot of money, but it is worth it.
I am looking forward to seeing Amsterdam. I have never been.
I used to need help with this. Now it is a piece of cake.

Stage 4: Situational Quiz

Quiz

Pick the best answer. Every one of these is a real moment from one day of travelling.

You wrote the plan last night: the tram at nine. Your husband asks what the plan is. You say:
A "We will take the tram at nine." (Did you decide that one second ago?)
B "We are going to take the tram at nine."
C "We take the tram at nine." (Is that your routine every day?)
The scale says twenty-four kilos and the limit is twenty-three. Nobody planned this. You say:
A "All right. I will move one kilo into my carry-on."
B "All right. I am going to move one kilo into my carry-on."
C "All right. I will to move one kilo into my carry-on."
The agent asks you a question at the counter. Which one is correct?
A "Did you packed the bag yourself, madam?"
B "Did you pack the bag yourself, madam?"
C "Did you packing the bag yourself, madam?"
Your friend says the guided tour is too expensive. You think it is good value. You say:
A "I am disagree. It is worth it."
B "I am not agree. It is worth it."
C "I disagree. It is nine euros, but in my opinion it is worth it."
Nobody knows yet if the bins are going to be full. The agent says:
A "We might have to check your carry-on at the gate."
B "We should have to check your carry-on at the gate." (That is advice, not a maybe.)
C "We might to check your carry-on at the gate."
You are excited about the trip next month. You say:
A "I am looking forward to see Amsterdam."
B "I look forward Amsterdam."
C "I am looking forward to seeing Amsterdam."

Stage 5: Free Production

Reflection

Record yourself answering the prompt. There is no right or wrong version. Speak for two minutes, with no script. Keep this recording: in Lesson 20 you are going to listen to it again.

Think about the woman who sat down for Lesson 1. Same passport, same husband, same son. What can you do now that you could not do then? Be specific: name one moment, in one lesson, when something changed. Then tell me about your next trip -- the real one. Where are you going to go, and what are you going to do on the first day? What did you use to do differently when you travelled before? What are you looking forward to the most? Name one thing that could go wrong, and say what you will do about it, right there, on the street. And finish with this: some people say that at sixty-eight it is too late to learn a language. Do you agree, or do you disagree? Say why. Take your time, and do not read from a script.

Lesson 10 — Halfway There -- Ten Lessons, One Traveler

Film
The Best Exotic Marigold Hotel (2011) — Judi Dench, Bill Nighy, Maggie Smith

Seven British retirees fly to India to live in a hotel that looks nothing like the photographs. Watch Evelyn, the one played by Judi Dench. She is a widow of forty years, her husband did every practical thing in her life, and she has never worked, never travelled alone, never opened a bank account. She is about seventy. In the first ten minutes she cannot finish a phone call. By the end she has a job, a salary, and a life — in a country where she knew nobody. She is not brave in a big loud way. She is brave once a day, in small rooms, with strangers.

Tip: watch with English subtitles, and watch Evelyn only — ignore the other six stories the first time. Every time she starts a sentence with "I would rather", "In my opinion" or "I used to", say it out loud after her: they are your expressions, in her mouth. One warning: this cast is very British and they speak fast. Use the subtitles without guilt, and remember that in Europe you are going to hear exactly this.
